      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Three bodies in seven days. One city still waiting.

      Written September 5, 2025 (Revised September 5, 2025)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 5, 2025

      Just before 1 PM on Aug 31, at 30 St and 39 Ave, an SUV making a right turn hit a woman in the crosswalk. She died at the scene. NYC Open Data

      This Week

      • Early Aug 30, a taxi going straight on York Ave struck a man near E 72 St. He was killed. NYC Open Data
      • Aug 23, a sedan going straight hit a pedestrian near United Nations Ave S. Another death. NYC Open Data

      One Story, Citywide

      • They were one of 1,118 people killed on New York City streets since Jan 1, 2022. NYC Open Data
      • Year to date, 194 people have been killed, compared with 207 at this point last year. Serious injuries have risen from 506 to 522. The bodies still come. NYC Open Data
      • Pedestrians take the hit: since 2022, SUVs have killed 205 pedestrians and sedans 96, with thousands more injured. NYC Open Data

      Names and Asphalt

      • On the Queensboro Bridge over Labor Day weekend, police said a driver left the scene after striking a pedestrian. “A driver was arrested,” the report read. amNY
      • In East Flatbush, an officer directing traffic was hit from behind by a fleeing driver. “Police are looking for the hit-and-run driver,” reporters wrote. ABC7 | amNY
      • On the Upper East Side, the Daily News reported a man was struck on York Ave near E 72 St around 5 AM. He did not survive. NY Daily News

      Streets That Don’t Forgive

      • In Queens, an SUV turning right at 30 St and 39 Ave hit a pedestrian in the crosswalk. The crash report lists “Failure to Yield Right-of-Way.” She died. NYC Open Data
      • In Manhattan, a taxi going straight on York Ave struck a pedestrian near E 72 St. He died. NYC Open Data
      • In Brooklyn last month, two pedestrians were killed near 19 Ave and 42 St in a multi-vehicle crash. The toll spread across boroughs. NYC Open Data

      What Hasn’t Changed Yet

      • The city still records thousands of serious injuries each year. Through Sep 5, there have been 2,569 serious injuries and 196,304 injuries overall since 2022. NYC Open Data
      • The drivers and the vehicles are known. The pattern is known. The funerals are known. NYC Open Data

      What Can Change Now

      • The tools exist. Lower the default speed limit. Require speed limiters for repeat offenders. Both steps are laid out with actions you can take here.

      It was late morning on 30 St and 39 Ave. A turn, a strike, and a life ended. We do not have to keep writing this. Start here: Take action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How many people have been killed on NYC streets since 2022?
      According to NYC’s crash database, 1,118 people were killed citywide from Jan 1, 2022 through Sep 5, 2025. Source: NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets.
      ▸ Are things getting better this year?
      Year to date, 194 people have been killed compared to 207 at this point last year, while serious injuries rose from 506 to 522. Source: NYC Open Data comparative year-to-date stats.
      ▸ Which vehicles most often kill pedestrians in the data?
      Since 2022, SUVs are linked to 205 pedestrian deaths and sedans to 96 in NYPD crash records. Source: NYC Open Data (pedestrian injury/death rollups by vehicle type).
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions tables: Crashes (h9gi-nx95), Persons (f55k-p6yu), and Vehicles (bm4k-52h4). We filtered for New York City crashes dated 2022-01-01 through 2025-09-05. We tallied deaths, injuries, serious injuries, and pedestrian impacts using the fields provided by NYPD in those tables. Data as of Sep 5, 2025. You can reproduce the query starting here and applying the same date window.
