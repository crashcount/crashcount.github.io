      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      No Time Left: City Hall Stalls, New Yorkers Die

      Written July 9, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Toll This Week

      The city bleeds. In the last twelve months, 241 people died and 701 suffered serious injuries on New York’s streets. The dead do not get second chances. The injured carry scars no policy can erase. Just days ago, a 90-year-old man lay on the pavement in Brooklyn, his head split open after a masked moped rider hit him and fled. “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open,” said an eyewitness. The rider did not stop. The city keeps moving.

      A day before, Dov Broyde, 70, was crossing Avenue I. A Smart Car driver plowed into him. Broyde died at the hospital. The driver stayed. No charges filed. Fifty-five pedestrians have died this year. The number is lower than last year. It is not low enough. The report confirms it.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      The city has power now. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. New York can set its own speed limits. Some streets have dropped to 20 mph. But the citywide limit remains unchanged. The law sits. The council waits. The mayor waits. Lives do not wait.

      Some leaders fight for safety. Others rip it out. The city tried to remove the Bedford Avenue protected bike lane. DOT warned: “Removing the protected bike lane won’t remove cyclists—it will only make the street less safe” testified DOT. The fight drags on. Cyclists and pedestrians pay the price.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do the most harm. In three years, they killed 301 people and injured 26,959. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers are not just numbers. They are bodies in the street. They are families left waiting for someone who will not come home.

      What Now

      Every day the council delays, someone else dies. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand action. The city belongs to the living, not to the dead.

      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?

      Hoboken Council Unanimously Cuts Speed Limit To 20 MPH
      ▸

      Hoboken wiped out traffic deaths for four years. Now, city leaders slash speed limits to 20 mph. They target injuries next. Streets get daylighted corners, curb extensions, and raised intersections. No ticket blitz. Just slower cars. Safety comes first.

      On July 14, 2022, Hoboken’s city council unanimously approved a new law to lower the citywide speed limit from 25 to 20 mph. The measure, part of Hoboken’s Vision Zero push, aims to end all traffic violence by 2030. The city’s matter summary reads: officials 'began the process of lowering speed limits from 25 miles per hour to 20, as part of its effort to end all forms of traffic violence.' Mayor Ravi Bhalla backed the move, saying, 'that extra time could very well end up saving the life of a child or senior citizen.' Ryan Sharp, parking and transportation director, stressed the need to keep pushing for safer streets. The city focuses on infrastructure—daylighting, curb extensions, raised intersections—over enforcement. Police will educate, not ticket. Hoboken’s model shows small cities can make streets safe for walkers, cyclists, and all vulnerable road users.

      Report: Bad Road Design Fuels U.S. Pedestrian Death Crisis
      ▸

      A new report exposes the deadly cost of American road design. Even as driving fell, pedestrian deaths soared. Wide, fast arterials kill most. The toll climbs, year after year. The fix is clear: redesign streets for people, not speed.

      On July 12, 2022, Smart Growth America released its annual 'Dangerous by Design' report. The report, covered by Streetsblog NYC, finds that 'it all starts with bad design.' Pedestrian deaths rose 4.7 percent in 2020, hitting a 40-year high in 2021. Beth Osborne, director of Transportation for America, highlights that over 70 percent of urban pedestrian fatalities occur on multi-lane arterial roads, which make up only 15 percent of streets. Osborne urges systemic change: 'Safety has to be the default.' The report calls for better data, stronger design standards, and smarter use of federal funds. No council members are named; this is a national policy report, not a local bill. The findings underline a simple truth: dangerous roads kill, and only bold redesign will save lives.

      Study: SUVs Kill Children, Black Pedestrians at Alarming Rate
      ▸

      SUVs are deadly. Children hit by SUVs die eight times more often than those struck by cars. Black residents face a higher risk. The rise of large vehicles brings more death. Federal action is missing. The danger is clear. The toll is rising.

      On July 7, 2022, a University of Illinois study revealed the lethal impact of SUVs on vulnerable road users. The report, titled 'SUVs Are Indeed Death Machines for Children and Black Road Users,' found that SUVs, though involved in only 16.9% of crashes with children, caused 40% of childhood fatalities. Passenger cars, by contrast, made up 62% of such crashes but only 19% of deaths. The study states, 'Children are eight times more likely to be killed if struck by an SUV as opposed to a standard passenger car.' Black residents, just 14.2% of the population, made up 27% of crash victims. The study highlights systemic danger: the size and poor visibility of SUVs, rising fatalities since 2009, and federal inaction. Proposals to increase registration fees and redesign SUV fronts have stalled. Tom Flood, a former auto marketer, blames manufacturers for fueling demand for these deadly vehicles.
