      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Another Child Dead. How Many More Before City Hall Acts?

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, cleaned citations, and tightened prose.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      The Toll This Month

      The city bleeds. In the last twelve months, 243 people died on New York streets. 708 were left with serious injuries. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers—none are spared. Just last week, an eight-year-old boy was killed crossing Eastern Parkway in Crown Heights. His sister watched. His mother grieves. “He was a boy who loved life,” said his mother.

      Scooter riders, teens on mopeds, and cyclists have all fallen. On June 26, a 39-year-old man was thrown from his scooter and died on 116th Avenue. No arrests. No comfort. The street is unchanged.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In the last three years, they took 299 lives and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. The numbers are cold, but the pain is not. Every crash is a family torn open.

      Leaders Act—But Not Fast Enough

      The city has moved, but the pace is slow. Speed cameras in school zones are now extended through 2030. “It’s simple: speed cameras save lives and keep New Yorkers safe,” said Governor Hochul. But cameras alone are not enough. Streets need to be rebuilt. Speed limits must drop. The city has the power to set a 20 mph limit. It has not used it.

      Some leaders speak plain. “At this point, there are no questions, just facts: speeding kills, and speed cameras save lives,” said State Senator Gounardes. But others drag their feet, or worse, rip out bike lanes and leave riders exposed.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand that repeat speeders are stopped. Do not wait for another child’s name to be written in chalk on the street.

      Act now. Or count the dead.

      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in traffic crashes in New York City recently?
      In the last twelve months, 243 people died and 708 suffered serious injuries on New York City streets.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      SUVs and Cars: SUVs and cars killed 299 pedestrians; trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 10 deaths. Bikes: 11 deaths. These numbers show the greatest danger comes from cars and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or are they just accidents?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speeds, protected bike lanes, and better street design save lives. Policy choices, not fate, decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What have local leaders done to address traffic violence?
      Leaders extended speed cameras in school zones through 2030 and have the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph, but have not yet done so citywide.
      ▸ What can local politicians do right now to make streets safer?
      They can set a 20 mph citywide speed limit, build more protected bike lanes, and crack down on repeat speeders. They can act now, or more will die.

      NYC Lags Behind: Car-Free School Streets Far Fewer Than Paris, London
      ▸

      New York trails global cities in banning cars near schools. Paris, London, and Tirana have hundreds of car-free school streets. NYC has just 41. Streets near schools remain dangerous. City leaders lack urgency. Children, especially the most vulnerable, pay the price.

      This policy comparison, published August 1, 2022, highlights New York City's failure to match global peers on car-free school streets. The report notes, 'A movement to ban cars from streets near schools is taking off globally, with cities like London, Paris, and Tirana implementing hundreds of car-free school streets to protect children from traffic and air pollution.' In contrast, NYC has only 41 such streets. Advocates like Emily Stutts and Simon Battisti criticize the city’s lack of political will, funding, and urgency. Battisti points to Tirana’s swift removal of parking for safety, while David Belliard in Paris calls for cities to prioritize children and vulnerable people over cars. The report underscores that New York’s program is underfunded, limited, and absent from top officials’ agendas. Streets near schools remain hazardous, especially for children of color and low-income families. The city’s inaction leaves vulnerable road users exposed to daily danger.

      Senate GOP Bill Seeks Repeal of Drunk-Driving Tech Mandate
      ▸

      Three Republican senators want to scrap a law forcing new cars to detect drunk drivers. Their bill would erase a rule meant to stop deadly crashes. Safety advocates warn this move could cost lives. The senators cite privacy. Victims get no say.

      On August 1, 2022, Senators Mike Rounds, Mike Braun, and John Cornyn introduced a bill to repeal a key safety measure. The proposal targets a Bipartisan Infrastructure Law provision that requires automakers to install passive drunk-driving detection systems in all new vehicles by 2024. The bill is not yet assigned to a committee. The senators have not issued public statements, but sources cite concerns over driver privacy. The bill’s summary states it would 'overturn a key provision in the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law that requires automakers to install passive drunk-driving detection mechanisms.' Safety advocates argue the technology could prevent up to 94 percent of alcohol-related crash deaths. They say removing the mandate puts vulnerable road users—pedestrians, cyclists, passengers—at greater risk. The debate pits privacy against public safety, but the cost will be measured in lives lost on the street.

      DOT Converts Doyers Street to Car-Free Plaza in Chinatown
      ▸

      Doyers Street in Chinatown will close to cars and become a permanent pedestrian plaza. The city will add gravel, markings, and planters. The move follows years of temporary closures. Locals and businesses welcome the change. Streets once deadly for walkers now belong to them.

      On July 19, 2022, the NYC Department of Transportation announced the full pedestrianization of Doyers Street in Chinatown, formalizing its status as a car-free plaza. The project builds on the success of Covid-era open streets and seasonal closures. DOT spokesman Vin Barone said, "DOT is proud to build on the success of our Open Streets with proposals to formalize spaces like the Doyers Open Street as a permanent pedestrian plaza."
