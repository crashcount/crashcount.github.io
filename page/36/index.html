      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      This week on our streets: four killings, one city

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 14, 2025

      Just after 2 AM on Sep 7, a driver in a 2024 Toyota sedan going straight hit and killed a 30-year-old man on Mosholu Parkway. Police recorded driver inattention by the driver. NYC Open Data

      This Week

      • Aug 31, Astoria: a driver in an SUV turned right at 30 St and 39 Ave and hit a 38-year-old woman in a marked crosswalk; police recorded failure to yield by the driver. NYC Open Data
      • Aug 30, Upper East Side: a taxi driver going straight hit and killed a pedestrian at York Ave and E 72 St. NYC Open Data
      • Aug 23, East Midtown: a driver in a sedan going straight hit and killed a pedestrian near United Nations Ave S. NYC Open Data

      The count does not stop

      Since Jan 1, 2022, 1,123 people have been killed and 197,257 injured on New York City streets, with 2,592 seriously injured. NYC Open Data

      This year through Sep 14: 199 deaths, down from 213 by this time last year; serious injuries are 545 vs 523; recorded crashes are 56,795 vs 64,441. The line still runs through bodies. NYC Open Data

      Speed and turns, then silence

      On Mosholu Parkway, police logged driver inattention. In Astoria, police logged a failure to yield on a right turn. The result is the same. A person on foot. A driver who does not stop. NYC Open Data

      We know what slows cars. As the State Senate put it, “speed cameras have cut speeding by over 60% in locations where installed.” New York State Senate

      Make the next decision

      City Hall can set safer speeds. Lower the default to 20 MPH under Sammy’s Law. Albany can curb the worst repeat offenders. Pass the Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045C/A2299C) to require speed limiters for drivers who rack up violations. The tools are on the table. Take action

      What happens on Mosholu, on 39 Ave, on York, happens again tomorrow. It stops when we make it stop. NYC Open Data

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4), filtered for New York City between 2022-01-01 and 2025-09-14. Totals (deaths, injuries, serious injuries, and crashes) and the recent case details come from these tables. Data were extracted as of Sep 13, 2025. You can view a reproducible query of the crashes table here.
      ▸ Why highlight these specific incidents?
      They are the most recent fatal crashes involving people walking, across multiple boroughs, in the covered period. Each is documented in the city’s open data with location, time, and contributing factors recorded by police.
      ▸ What policies could prevent crashes like these?
      Lower the city’s default speed limit to 20 MPH under Sammy’s Law and pass the Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045C/A2299C) to require speed limiters for habitual violators. Details and contacts are listed on our Take Action page.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
