      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      York Avenue, 5 AM

      Written September 2, 2025 (Revised September 2, 2025)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 2, 2025

      About 5 AM on Aug 30 on York Avenue near E. 72nd St, a Toyota RAV4 driver hit 36‑year‑old James Mossetty and kept going, police said. He died in the street. NY Daily News.

      They are one of 1,114 people killed on New York City streets since Jan 1, 2022, in records drawn from city crash data. NYC Open Data.

      The count keeps moving

      Year to date, the city logged 54,837 crashes, 190 deaths, and 34,234 injuries. Serious injuries rose to 517 from 499 last year. NYC Open Data.

      The bodies are citywide. A pedestrian killed on United Nations Avenue South in Manhattan on Aug 23. NYC Open Data. A motorcyclist dead at Atlantic and Classon in Brooklyn on Aug 28. NYC Open Data. Two pedestrians and a driver killed at 19th Avenue and 42nd Street in Brooklyn on Aug 12. NYC Open Data.

      Trucks, SUVs, and human bodies

      Pedestrians take the hit. Since 2022, SUVs are linked to 204 pedestrian deaths, sedans to 96, trucks to 77, with thousands more injured. NYC Open Data.

      On Maspeth and Morgan, a box truck going straight struck and killed a 46‑year‑old pedestrian on Aug 6. NYC Open Data. On the BQE ramp two days later, a box truck and a motorcycle collided; the rider was killed. NYC Open Data.

      After the sirens, an arrest

      On the Queensboro Bridge, police said a driver hit a pedestrian and left. They arrested a 71‑year‑old man in the case. amNY.

      An NYPD sergeant directing traffic at Schenectady and Church Avenues in East Flatbush was struck from behind by a driver who fled. ABC7.

      High‑speed chases bring their own harm. “The evidence is clear: police vehicle pursuits and high‑speed car chases can be dangerous and even fatal, and it is time for a change.” Times Union.

      Slow the cars. Stop the worst repeaters.

      There are tools on the table. New York City can lower default speeds, and lawmakers can force chronic speeders to use speed‑limiters. The steps are set out here.

      Speed is the common thread. Lower it. Keep the repeat offenders from weaponizing their cars. The next name on the list does not have to be yours.

      Take one step today. Tell City Hall and Albany to act. Here’s how.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We pulled NYC’s official Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets — Crashes (h9gi‑nx95), Persons (f55k‑p6yu), and Vehicles (bm4k‑52h4) — for incidents citywide from 2022-01-01 through 2025-09-02. We counted total deaths, injuries, and serious injuries from the Persons table and used crash totals from the Crashes table. We matched specific cases using CrashIDs in all three tables. You can view the datasets here. Data was accessed Sep 2, 2025.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ Which recent cases does this story reference?
      James Mossetty’s fatal hit-and-run on York Avenue near E. 72nd St (Aug 30, 2025); a pedestrian killed on United Nations Ave S (Aug 23, 2025); a fatal motorcycle crash at Atlantic Ave and Classon Ave (Aug 28, 2025); a triple-fatal crash at 19th Ave and 42nd St (Aug 12, 2025); an NYPD sergeant struck while directing traffic at Schenectady Ave and Church Ave (Aug 31, 2025). All are documented in NYC Open Data or press reports cited above.
      ▸ Why highlight SUVs and trucks?
      Because the city’s records for this period attribute 204 pedestrian deaths to SUVs, 96 to sedans, and 77 to trucks — a clear share of the toll on people walking. Source: NYC Open Data causes-of-injury rollups for 2022‑01‑01 to 2025‑09‑02.
      ▸ What can I do right now?
