      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Survived Nazis, Killed by a Van: NYC Streets Are Failing Our Most Vulnerable

      Written July 19, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 19, 2025

      Children, Elders, and the Relentless Toll

      Just yesterday, an 11-year-old boy was hit by a car near a playground in Sheepshead Bay. He survived. Many do not. In the last twelve months, 258 people died on New York City streets. 732 suffered serious injuries. The dead include children, elders, and everyone in between. The city counts the bodies. The city moves on.

      A 95-year-old woman, Mayya Gil, was killed crossing Cropsey Avenue. She survived the Nazis and Chernobyl. She did not survive a Brooklyn van. The driver was charged with failure to yield and failure to exercise due care. “Police have arrested a man they say struck and killed a beloved 95-year-old Brooklyn woman with a cargo van as she crossed the street near her home,” reported Gothamist.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Over 51,000 people were injured in the last year. Children under 18 made up 11 deaths and more than 4,200 injuries. The old are not spared. People 75 and older: 20 dead, 697 injured. The city’s streets are a gauntlet. Cars and SUVs are the main killers—311 deaths in the last three years. Trucks and buses killed 95. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      The city has taken steps. Speed limits are dropping in some zones. Speed cameras are here to stay. But the pace is slow. “The 32 percent drop in traffic fatalities that we have seen this year is historic… further proof that our Vision Zero efforts are working,” said Mayor Adams. But the work is not done. Every day of delay is another day of risk. After a six-month investigation, police charged a driver who killed a 95-year-old woman. “Members of the NYPD’s Collision Investigation Squad charged him with failure to yield and failure to exercise due care, both misdemeanors,” reported NY Daily News.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat offenders. Join forces with groups like Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Every day you wait, someone else pays the price.

      Do not wait for another name to become a number. Act now.

      Helicopter Breaks Apart Over Hudson River
      ▸

      Six died when a sightseeing helicopter shattered midair over the Hudson. The fuselage, rotor, and tail tore loose. Loud bangs echoed. The craft plunged. No black box. No warning. Only fragments and silence left behind.

      NY Daily News reported on May 7, 2025, that federal officials released images showing a sightseeing helicopter breaking apart in midair before crashing into the Hudson River, killing six. The National Transportation Safety Board’s preliminary report details how the Bell 206L-4 split into three pieces: 'the fuselage, the main rotor system, and the tail boom.' Witnesses heard 'several loud 'bangs'' before the breakup. The helicopter had flown eight tours that day, all with the same pilot, who had less than 50 hours in this model. The aircraft had a prior maintenance issue with its transmission assembly and lacked flight data recorders. The NTSB noted the pilot wore video-capable sunglasses, but they remain missing. The crash highlights gaps in oversight and the risks of repeated tour flights without robust recording or inspection requirements.
