      Speed Kills. Albany Stalls. Another Family Mourns.

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      In the last twelve months, 260 people died on New York City streets. More than 51,000 were injured. Seven hundred thirty-four suffered wounds so grave they will never be the same. The dead are not numbers. A 76-year-old woman, struck by an SUV while crossing White Plains Road. A 32-year-old woman, crushed by a Jeep on Marcus Garvey Boulevard. A 79-year-old man, found dead in a parked car on West 77th. Each one leaves a hole that cannot be filled. NYC Open Data

      Most of the killing is done by cars and trucks. In three years, they have taken 298 lives and left 498 with serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10, bikes 11. The rest is steel, speed, and neglect.

      Leadership: Words, Delays, and the Fight for Action

      City leaders say the right things. “Speeding kills, and speed cameras save lives,” said State Senator Andrew Gounardes. But the law that keeps speed cameras running is set to expire. If Albany fails, the cameras go dark. Streets go back to the way they were—fast, brutal, and unforgiving. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez warns, “an extremely reckless few continue to endanger themselves and everyone around them.” Patch

      There is talk of a new law for “super speeders.” There is talk of lowering the speed limit to 20 mph. There is always talk. But families keep burying their dead.

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      The city cracks down on cyclists and e-bike riders, not the drivers who do the killing. Community boards call it out. “Holding cyclists to a far higher standard than motor vehicle drivers is unjust, puts immigrant workers at grave risk, and sends the wrong message about the actual danger on our streets,” said Manhattan Community Board 7. The real threat rolls on four wheels, not two.

      What You Can Do: Make Them Hear You

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand the renewal of speed cameras. Join Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Every day you wait is another day someone dies.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 298 deaths and 498 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 10 deaths and 44 serious injuries. Bikes caused 11 deaths and 52 serious injuries. Other vehicle types caused fewer deaths and injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      They are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement save lives. Policy choices—not fate—decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, renew and expand speed camera programs, redesign streets for safety, and stop policies that punish cyclists and pedestrians instead of dangerous drivers.

The piece states: “New York City's network of school-zone speed-enforcement cameras—which now operate 24/7—is a good start for reducing the epidemic of traffic deaths of the last 20 years.” The authors, including researchers from Columbia University, cite evidence that doubling the number of speed cameras would save hundreds of lives and over $1 billion in health and productivity costs. They call for cameras on more dangerous roads, not just near schools, and for lower speed limits, road diets, and pedestrian infrastructure. Sharing violation data with insurers is suggested as a fix. The message is clear: more cameras and safer streets mean fewer deaths.

      Federal Safety Chief Backs Idaho Stop for Cyclists
      ▸

      NHTSA’s Ann Carlson called for legalizing the Idaho Stop. She said it boosts cyclist safety, cuts exposure, and encourages riding. Data shows no rise in conflicts. Carlson stressed equity and a safe system. States, not feds, must act.

      On October 20, 2022, National Highway Traffic Safety Administration Acting Administrator Ann Carlson publicly supported legalizing the 'Idaho Stop' at the Vision Zero Cities conference. The Idaho Stop lets cyclists treat stop signs as yield signs and red lights as stop signs. Carlson stated, 'The Idaho stop allows cyclists to manage risks to their advantage. It increases their visibility to drivers and reduces their exposure.' She cited data showing no increase in conflicts with pedestrians or other cyclists. Carlson emphasized, 'Safety and equity has to go hand in hand.' The NHTSA fact sheet noted that stop-as-yield laws decriminalize common cycling behavior and may boost ridership. Carlson highlighted the need for a holistic, equitable 'safe system' approach. Despite federal support, only states can enact these changes. Efforts in New York have stalled.

      Vision Zero Cities: Fix Deadly Arterials, Save Lives
      ▸

      Arterial roads kill. They are wide, fast, and deadly for walkers and cyclists. Most are state-owned. Cities and advocates demand lower speeds, urban design, and local control. Changing these streets is urgent. Lives hang in the balance.

      This policy advocacy statement, published October 18, 2022, by Streetsblog NYC, highlights the urgent need to fix deadly arterial roads. The statement, titled 'Vision Zero Cities: How to Fix Our Most Dangerous Roads,' calls for context-sensitive speed limits, urban street design standards, and transferring state-owned roads to city control. Streetsblog and Transportation Alternatives urge, 'Cities and advocates should push their states to transform arterial streets into safe spaces for all modes.' The statement notes that arterials make up 15 percent of roads but see 67 percent of pedestrian deaths. It stresses that speed is the main factor in fatal crashes and that state DOTs often prioritize fast driving over safety. The call is clear: redesign streets for people, not cars, and give cities the power to act.
