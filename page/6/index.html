      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Third Avenue Bleeds: City Stalls, Pedestrians Pay

      Written July 12, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      The Week the Streets Went Red

      Two men crossed Third Avenue in Sunset Park. A BMW sped through the light. The car did not stop. The men, Kex Un Chen and Faqiu Lin, were thrown to the pavement. They died before sunrise. They were walking to a food pantry. City Harvest expressed deep sadness over the deaths, noting the men “may have been on their way to our Mobile Market simply trying to access food like so many New Yorkers in need.” The driver fled. Police later made an arrest. The street stayed the same.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      In the last 12 months, 261 people died on New York City streets. Over 52,000 were injured. 747 suffered serious injuries. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. Cars and SUVs killed 311. Trucks and buses killed 95. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers do not care about excuses. They only count the bodies. NYC Open Data tracks every crash.

      Leadership: Promises, Delays, and Missed Chances

      The city has the power to lower speed limits. It can redesign streets. It can stop the killing. But the work drags. On Third Avenue, the city paused a safety redesign after business owners complained. Julio Peña warned, “It was only a matter of time before somebody else died on this corridor and here we are. Every death is preventable, and once again we are in a place of frustration where we have this incredibly dangerous corridor and the city just refusin,”. Since the redesign was halted, 96 crashes have injured 80 people. The city knows which streets kill. It knows how to fix them. It waits.

      What You Can Do

      Every day of delay is another family broken. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand street redesigns where people keep dying. Demand action, not words. The dead cannot wait.
