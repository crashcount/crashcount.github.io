      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Right turn, no mercy

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 9, 2025

      A driver making a right turn hit a 38-year-old woman in a marked crosswalk at 30 St and 39 Ave. Police recorded failure to yield by the driver. She died. NYC Open Data

      This Week

      • The next day, a taxi driver going straight on York Ave hit and killed a pedestrian near E 72 St. NYC Open Data
      • Earlier that week (Aug 23), a driver in a sedan going straight hit and killed a pedestrian along United Nations Ave S. NYC Open Data

      Numbers, not accidents

      She was one of 1,122 people killed on New York City streets since Jan 1, 2022. Police have logged 197,054 injuries and 2,583 serious injuries in the same period. NYC Open Data

      This year to date, 198 people have been killed, compared with 209 at this point last year. Lives are still ending in the road. NYC Open Data

      Who does the harm

      Since 2022, drivers of SUVs and cars have killed 338 pedestrians citywide. Trucks and buses add to the count. The pattern is plain. NYC Open Data

      The spread is citywide. In the Bronx, a 30-year-old man was struck and killed near Mosholu Parkway; police said it was a hit-and-run. ABC7

      Tools on the shelf

      “When regular people band together and demand safer streets, speed limits get lowered, laws get passed, streets get redesigned, and lives are saved.” Families for Safe Streets

      New York City can set safer speeds. The city can lower residential limits and redesign streets to slow turns at corners like 30 St and 39 Ave. The State has a bill on repeat speeders — the Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045C/A2299C) — that would put speed limiters on the worst offenders. We do not need more names. We need the switches flipped. /take_action/
