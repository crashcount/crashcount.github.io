      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Another Red Light. Another Body. Still No Action.

      Written July 24, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 24, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      Just last week, two men crossed Third Avenue in Sunset Park. They had the light. A sedan ran the red, struck them both, and kept going. Both men died. Since 2018, drivers have killed or seriously injured 80 people on this two-mile stretch. The city’s answer? A sign that says, “be careful.” State Sen. Andrew Gounardes said: “We should all be offended. Angry. Insulted. That the response from our city to two deaths blocks away from here is a sign that says: ‘be careful.’”

      In Chinatown, a stolen car tore through Bowery and Canal. A cyclist and a pedestrian died. The driver tried to run. The street stayed the same. CBS New York reported the crash “is raising questions about a bigger safety problem at an intersection of Canal Street.”

      The Numbers Under the Blood

      In the last 12 months, 276 people died on New York City streets. Over 52,000 were injured. 756 suffered serious injuries—the kind that end a life even if the heart keeps beating. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers are the ones who pay. Cars and SUVs do most of the killing: 322 deaths. Trucks and buses: 98 deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds: 11 deaths. Bikes: 11 deaths. See the data.

      Promises, Delays, and the Price of Inaction

      Plans to fix Third Avenue started in 2014. It’s now 2025. The redesign is still on hold. Assemblymember Marcela Mitaynes asked: “We started talking about a plan in 2014 and it’s now 2025. What is going on?”

      The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has the power to redesign streets. It has the power to act. Every day of delay is another day for a mother to lose her son, for a child to never come home from school.

      What You Can Do

      Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns, not more signs.

      Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Streets can be redesigned, speed limits lowered, and dangerous drivers stopped. Waiting is a choice.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, and pass laws to keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road. They can act now, not after more deaths.
