      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Night work on South Conduit. Then a body in the road.

      Written August 30, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 30, 2025

      Just after 2:30 AM on Aug 13, 2025, a driver hit a 52‑year‑old man at 155th St and South Conduit Ave near JFK, then fled. “The operator of the vehicle fled the scene,” police said. ABC7.

      He is one of 1,111 people killed on New York City streets since Jan 1, 2022, with 195,514 injured in 346,041 crashes. NYC Open Data.

      The toll keeps coming

      This year isn’t done, and the bodies already tell a story. By Aug 30, 2025: 187 deaths and 33,813 injuries citywide. By the same point last year: 199 deaths and 35,953 injuries. Serious injuries ticked up to 511 from 489. NYC Open Data.

      Numbers slip a little, then rise somewhere else. The road stays hard. The pattern holds.

      Names lost to corners and corridors

      Queens: two men on foot died at 42nd St and 19th Ave on Aug 12. A driver, 84, died too. NYC Open Data.

      The Bronx: a 32‑year‑old woman was killed in the crosswalk at Bruckner Blvd and Leggett Ave just after midnight on Aug 26. NYC Open Data.

      Manhattan: a pedestrian died on United Nations Ave S on Aug 23. The listed factor was driver distraction. NYC Open Data.

      Staten Island–Brooklyn span: a 21‑year‑old motorcyclist died on the Verrazzano Bridge on Aug 25; speed was cited. NYC Open Data.

      Queens again: a 24‑year‑old passenger died on the Cross Island Parkway on Aug 26; speed was cited. NYC Open Data.

      The hit‑and‑run near JFK sits in that line. “No arrests have been made.” NY Daily News.

      We know what slows the ambulance

      The city is moving pieces. A 14th Street redesign aims “to improve the pedestrian experience.” NY1. Police brief the press on parade security. amNY.

      But the deaths above follow a simpler law. Speed. It shows up again and again in the record. It kills quickly and without warning. NYC Open Data.

      The tools are on the table

      Lower the default speed. Rein in repeat speeders. Those levers exist. Use them. Our guide is here: take action.

      City Hall knows the count. So do we. The man near JFK should be alive. The next one still can be.
