      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 77?

      No More Dead Kids: Make Streets Safe or Own the Blood

      Written June 29, 2025

      Precinct 77: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      The Toll: Children, Riders, and the Unseen

      In Precinct 77, the street does not forgive. In the last twelve months, 370 people were injured. Five suffered injuries so grave they may never walk the same. One did not survive. The dead are not numbers. They are children crossing with their sisters, riders thrown from mopeds, elders in the wrong place at the wrong time.

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy was crossing Eastern Parkway with his sister. A black Honda Pilot struck him. The driver stayed. The boy did not. “They were walking, he was crossing the street with his sister. She’s 17-years-old,” said a neighbor. The police towed the SUV away. No arrest. The street stayed open. The blood washed away.

      A few days before, a 17-year-old on a moped collided with a car. He died at the hospital. His passenger survived. The driver stayed. No arrest. The city moved on. “The teenager was riding a gas moped with a passenger on the back… when it slammed into a Genesis G80 vehicle operated by a 71-year-old man making a left turn,” police said.

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and the Cost of Delay

      Most injuries come from cars and SUVs. Out of all pedestrian injuries and deaths here, SUVs and sedans caused the most harm—over 240 incidents, including one death. Trucks and buses killed one and injured more. Motorcycles and mopeds, bikes, and even taxis left their own scars. No one is safe when the street is built for speed.

      Leadership: Promises, Silence, and the Role of Police

      The police in Precinct 77 have the tools. They can ticket speeders. They can crack down on failure to yield. They can watch the corners where the blood pools and the glass glitters. But action is a choice. The numbers do not lie. The dead do not speak. The silence is loud.

      Local leaders have the power to demand more. They can push for lower speed limits, more enforcement, safer crossings. They can reward courage and call out delay. But every day without action is another day the street claims its own.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Thoughts and Prayers

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Do not wait for another child to die in the crosswalk. The street will not wait. Neither should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 77 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 36, assembly district AD 43 and state senate district SD 20.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 77?
      It includes the Prospect Heights, Crown Heights (North), Lincoln Terrace Park, and Brooklyn CB8 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 35, District 36, and District 41, Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 55, AD 56, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 20 and SD 25.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 77?
      Cars and Trucks: SUVs and sedans caused 241 injuries and 1 death. Trucks and buses killed 1 and injured 10 more. Motorcycles and Mopeds: Involved in 8 injuries. Bikes: Involved in 9 injuries. No group is spared. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 77 police can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and issue failure-to-yield tickets. They can target crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. They have the tools. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. Lower speeds, better enforcement, and safer streets can prevent deaths and injuries. Every crash is a policy choice.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can push for lower speed limits, demand more enforcement, and fund safer crossings. They can hold police and agencies accountable for action, not just words.
