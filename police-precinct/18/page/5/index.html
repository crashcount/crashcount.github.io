      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 18?

      Blood on Their Hands: Demand Action for Precinct 18’s Deadly Streets

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, and cleaned citations.)

      Precinct 18: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Precinct 18, the numbers do not lie. Twelve people killed. Thirty-three left with serious injuries. Over 1,300 hurt since 2022. The dead do not get a second chance. The injured carry scars you cannot see.

      Just this year, the carnage continues. Six killed and 235 injured since January. Among them, the old and the young. Three people over 65 lost their lives. Two more over 75. A cyclist, age 67, died on Fifth Avenue. An 83-year-old woman was struck crossing with the signal at 58th and Sixth. A 39-year-old man was killed by a bus on East 51st. These are not numbers. These are lives ended in the street.

      The Crashes No One Stops

      The pattern is clear. Cars and SUVs are the main killers. They caused three deaths and nearly 300 injuries to pedestrians and cyclists. Trucks and buses killed one and hurt dozens more. Bikes and mopeds injured, but did not kill. The street does not care who you are. It only matters who is behind the wheel.

      The news does not stop. On July 2, two buses collided at Port Authority, injuring fourteen. “One bus made contact with the rear of the other bus,” said FDNY Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. “It required some extrication, meaning they had to use some equipment to free the patient” reported ABC7. The tunnel filled with sirens. The ramp was blocked. The city kept moving, but the wounded did not.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      The city has tools. Speed cameras. Lower speed limits. New laws. But the work is not done. The police in Precinct 18 can enforce speed limits, ticket reckless drivers, and target the corners where people keep dying. They can make it stop. They just need to act.

      Every crash is preventable. Every delay is a choice. The dead cannot call for change. The living must do it for them.

      Call to Action

      Demand more. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Tell them the deaths must end. Tell them to enforce the law, slow the cars, and protect the people who walk and bike these streets. Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 18 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, assembly district AD 67 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 18?
      It includes the Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown-Times Square, Manhattan CB4, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, and District 6, Assembly Districts AD 67, AD 73, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 18?
      Cars and SUVs: 3 deaths, 5 serious injuries, 294 total injuries. Trucks and Buses: 1 death, 3 serious injuries, 37 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 53 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 24 injuries. (2022–2025)
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 18 police can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and issue failure-to-yield tickets. They can target known crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. They have the tools. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Every crash is preventable. Speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving can be stopped with enforcement and safer street design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, and demand police focus on the most dangerous drivers and intersections. They can push for safer street designs and hold agencies accountable for results.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
