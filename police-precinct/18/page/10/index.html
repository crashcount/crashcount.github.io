      Who Dies Next on Fifth Avenue?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, cleaned and deduped citations, tightened prose, and ensured correct citation formatting.)

      Precinct 18: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Bodies in the Crosswalk

      A woman, 83, crossing with the signal at Sixth and 58th. Struck by an SUV making a left. She died there, in the cold February dusk. A man, 39, not in the roadway, killed by a bus and a boom lift on East 51st. Two cyclists, both 67, thrown from their bikes at Fifth and 51st. Both dead. The street does not care about age or reason. It only takes.

      In the last twelve months, seven people died in Precinct 18. Eight more were seriously injured. The dead are mostly old. The young bleed but live. Crashes do not stop.

      The Numbers That Do Not Lie

      SUVs killed two. Taxis killed one. Buses killed one. Cars and trucks left bodies and broken bones. Bikes injured, but did not kill. In three years: 12 dead, 30 seriously hurt, 1,296 injured. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, fathers, children, friends. They are the price of waiting.

      The Leaders and Their Promises

      Mayor Adams calls Fifth Avenue a place where “more people walk down the street every hour than fill Madison Square Garden during a sold-out Knicks game” and promises to make it “more walkable, greener, and safer” said Mayor Adams. The city will spend $400 million to widen sidewalks, plant trees, and slow cars. But there will be no new bike lanes. No busway. “Two mayors have now disregarded city workers’ plans for a busway, throwing riders under the bus to favor luxury boutiques,” said Danny Pearlstein.

      The police have the tools. They can ticket speeders. They can crack down on failure to yield. They can stand at the corners where people die and make it stop. But the dying does not stop.

      The Call

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call the precinct. Tell them: slow the cars, protect the people, end the waiting. Every day of delay is another name on the list. Demand action. Demand life.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 18 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan. It overlaps Council Districts District 3, District 4, and District 6, Assembly Districts AD 67, AD 73, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 47.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 18?
      It includes the Hell’S Kitchen neighborhood. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, and District 6, Assembly Districts AD 67, AD 73, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 18?
      Cars and Trucks: 2 deaths, 102 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 7 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 17 moderate injuries, 4 serious injuries. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 18 police can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous street conditions. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and focus on known crash hotspots. The tools exist. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The pattern is clear. These deaths and injuries are preventable. They are the result of policy, street design, and enforcement choices.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and demand police enforce traffic laws. They can push for protected bike lanes, busways, and more space for people on foot. They can act now, or answer for the next death.
