      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 110?

      Precinct 110: deaths at the edges, danger in the daylight

      Written August 27, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Precinct 110: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 27, 2025

      Precinct 110 covers Elmhurst, Corona, and Flushing Meadows. Since 2022, 16 people are dead and 2,760 are hurt in 4,910 crashes here, with 26 seriously injured, per city data (NYC Open Data). Pedestrians shoulder the harm: eight killed and 583 injured (NYC Open Data). SUVs and cars lead the body count among walkers (NYC Open Data).

      Highways take lives

      • Van Wyck Expressway, Dec 3, 2024: a 41-year-old man was killed off the roadway by a southbound SUV; the dataset lists driver distraction and alcohol involvement among contributing factors (CrashID 4776236).
      • Long Island Expressway bleeds often. It tops local hotspots with two deaths and 246 injuries since 2022 (NYC Open Data). Grand Central Parkway adds one death and 260 injuries. These are not streets. They are cuts through the precinct.

      On July 24, 2025, a 24-year-old motorcyclist died on the LIE during a westbound chain of movements that included a lane change and contact with an SUV and tractor trailer, per the city record (CrashID 4830329).

      The avenues are not safe

      Roosevelt Avenue ranks high for harm: one death, 106 injured. Broadway: one death, 49 injured. Both sit in the top local corridors for trauma since 2022 (NYC Open Data).

      A 70-year-old man was killed by a bus at Hoffman Drive off Woodhaven Blvd on Jun 13, 2025. The record lists “Other Actions in Roadway” for the pedestrian location; the bus was going straight eastbound (CrashID 4820244).

      Across the city, the pattern repeats. In Queens South this year through Aug 10, 17 people died in traffic crashes, up from 13 a year earlier, police told reporters after a predawn hit-and-run killed a man near JFK on Aug 13, 2025 (Gothamist).

      When the hurt comes

      The clock is blunt. The most injuries stack up around school let-out and the commute. About 3 PM sees a local spike, with 201 injuries and three serious injuries; 2 PM and 4 PM are close behind. Night does not spare us: just after 10 PM and towards midnight, deaths appear across the hours (NYC Open Data hourly profile).

      Most-listed contributing factors in local deaths and injuries include “other,” driver inattention/distraction, and alcohol involvement. Failure to yield appears in the injury rolls too (NYC Open Data).

      The precinct’s job, the city’s duty

      Precinct 110 officers know the map. The LIE, Grand Central Pkwy, Roosevelt, and Broadway are repeat hotspots. Targeted speed enforcement and failure-to-yield crackdowns on those corridors would meet the moment. Daylight the corners. Add hardened turns. Give leading pedestrian intervals at signals. Keep trucks in lanes designed to hold them. The hours around 2–4 PM and late night need a presence. The data says where and when.

      Citywide, the tools exist. Albany renewed New York City’s 24-hour school-zone speed-camera program through 2030. That buys time but not absolution; our own published guidance is blunt: cameras save lives, but lower speeds save more. Use the new authority to set safer limits and tame repeat offenders (Take Action).

      “Speed kills. You know it. Albany knows it. The city knows it too, yet here we are again—waiting.” (Take Action)

      “A pedestrian hit at 30 mph is five times more likely to die than at 20 mph.” (Take Action)

      One more lever is on the table. The proposed Stop Super Speeders Act would put speed limiters on cars driven by the worst repeat offenders. Our call is simple: pass it, and make the streets breathe again (Take Action).

      What breaks a heart is small

      On Aug 13, 2025, two men buying food on a Queens sidewalk were killed when a car jumped the curb. The driver also died. Police told reporters the man had been advised not to drive after a stroke two weeks earlier. Names given: Joaquin Venancio-Mendez, 41, and Santiago Baires, 70 (amNY).

      “I have never seen anything like this,” an eyewitness said, head in his hands (amNY).

      This is the pace of our loss. Slow. Constant. Avoidable.

      Make the change now

      • Precinct 110: focus enforcement on Roosevelt, Broadway, and highway edges; protect 2–4 PM and late-night hours.
      • DOT: daylight and harden turns at signalized corners; install LPIs on the busy crossings.
      • City Hall: lower the default speed limit; fit repeat speeders with intelligent speed assistance.

      The tools are in hand. The bodies are on the ground. Act now. Join the push and tell leaders to slow the streets (Take Action).
