      Elmhurst Bleeds: How Many Lives Before City Hall Acts?

      Precinct 110: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll on Elmhurst and Corona

      A woman steps into the crosswalk. A truck turns. She does not make it to the other side. In Precinct 110, 13 people have died and 2,393 have been injured since 2022. Five were left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. The dead include pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers. The living carry scars.

      SUVs and trucks hit hardest. Three pedestrians killed by SUVs. One by a truck. One by a moped. One by a bike. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old. In the last year, 63 children were hurt. One was eight years old, struck crossing 111th Street. She survived. Others did not.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city says it is acting. Speed cameras now run all day and night. Red-light cameras are multiplying. The mayor calls traffic violence a crime. “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many,” said Mayor Adams. The police commissioner promised, “NYPD officers will be highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action.”

      But the blood dries on the street. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program lapsed. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph, but most streets still wait. Cameras catch speeders, but only where they are installed. The rest is luck.

      The Role of Precinct 110

      Precinct 110 can do more. They can enforce the speed limit. They can ticket drivers who fail to yield. They can target the corners where people die. They have the tools. They need to use them.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. Every crash is a choice, a policy, a silence. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand lower speed limits. Demand enforcement. Demand streets where a child can cross and live.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 110 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens. It overlaps Council Districts District 21, District 24, District 25, and District 30, Assembly Districts AD 27, AD 30, AD 35, and AD 39, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, SD 13, and SD 16.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 110?
      It includes the Elmhurst, Corona, Flushing Meadows-Corona Park, Queens CB81, and Queens CB4 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 21, District 24, District 25, and District 30, Assembly Districts AD 27, AD 30, AD 35, and AD 39, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, SD 13, and SD 16.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 110?
      SUVs and Cars: 3 pedestrian deaths, 69 moderate injuries, 9 serious injuries. Trucks: 1 pedestrian death, 5 moderate injuries. Mopeds: 1 pedestrian death, 2 moderate injuries. Bikes: 1 pedestrian death, 6 moderate injuries.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 110 can enforce speed limits, ticket reckless drivers, and crack down on failure to yield. They can target crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. The NYPD commissioner said officers will be ‘highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action.’
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by policymakers, by those who enforce or ignore the law. Every crash is a chance to act.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand camera enforcement, and push for stronger laws. They can demand action from the police and DOT. They can listen to residents and act before another life is lost.
