      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 69?

      Precinct 69: Crosswalks, sirens, silence

      Written August 24, 2025 (Revised August 24, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Precinct 69: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 24, 2025

      Another driver. Same ending.

      • A 66-year-old man died at Rockaway Parkway and Seaview Ave at 7:25 p.m. He was crossing when a Toyota SUV going straight hit him. He died there. The data lists him as crossing against the signal. Only one person didn’t get to go home. NYC Open Data
      • Four days earlier in 2024, a 68-year-old man was killed at East 105th St and Glenwood Rd, 6:51 p.m. The SUV went straight. The record flags alcohol and tinted windows. He died at the scene. NYC Open Data
      • In 2025, at Avenue L and E 88th St, 6:46 p.m., a pedestrian was struck during a left turn by a Honda SUV. The victim is recorded as killed in the crosswalk. NYC Open Data

      The roll call of the dead in the 69th Precinct since 2022: 8 pedestrians, 1 cyclist, 2 vehicle occupants. SUVs and sedans do most of the harm. Trucks and buses kill too. Evenings cut deepest. Between 6 p.m. and 10 p.m., deaths stack up, with the worst at 6 p.m. and 10 p.m. hours. NYC Open Data

      “Speed kills.” The city knows it. Albany knows it. We know it. Take Action

      Three corners. One fix.

      The worst corridors keep repeating.

      • Rockaway Parkway logs two deaths and 138 injuries. NYC Open Data
      • Glenwood Road logs two deaths and 65 injuries. NYC Open Data
      • Avenue L shows one death and 63 injuries. NYC Open Data

      What the records show: left turns that cut through crosswalks, straight‑through hits at dusk, SUVs doing the damage. Contributing factors stack under “other,” with listed cases of alcohol and distraction. NYC Open Data

      Fixes are not theory. They are hardware:

      • Daylight every corner on Rockaway Pkwy and Glenwood Rd. Harden the turns. Give leading pedestrian intervals.
      • Narrow lanes on Avenue L. Protect the crossings.
      • Targeted evening enforcement where deaths cluster, 6–10 p.m.

      The killing hours

      In this precinct, injuries pile up all day. The deaths come at night. 6 p.m.: four deaths. 10 p.m.: two. 5 p.m., 9 p.m., 11 p.m.: one each. People on foot take the worst of it: 300 injured, 8 killed. A cyclist is gone too. NYC Open Data

      One boy, 14, died at East 81st St and Glenwood Rd. Multiple vehicles in the record. The field says “other actions in roadway.” He did not make it home. NYC Open Data

      Officials know what works — do they?

      Cameras stay on. Albany renewed 24‑hour school‑zone speed cameras through 2030. The tools exist. Use them. Take Action

      City Hall now holds the key to slower streets. Sammy’s Law lets NYC set lower limits. Set 20 mph on residential streets. Every mile per hour matters. Take Action

      The worst drivers are a small slice, but they do big harm. The Stop Super Speeders Act would force repeat offenders to use speed‑limiters after 16 camera or 11‑point records. Keep their cars from breaking the limit by more than 5 mph. Take Action

      Another night. Another family.

      Since 2022, this precinct logged 3,130 crashes, 2,325 injuries, and 11 deaths. SUVs and sedans dominate the toll. Trucks and buses add weight. People on foot pay the price. NYC Open Data

      Lower speeds. Harden turns. Protect crossings. Then do the citywide fixes.

      One call today can push it. Act now.
