      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 1?

      No Deaths, Hundreds Hurt: Our Streets Bleed While Leaders Stall

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 20, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      No one died in Precinct 1 this year. But the blood still runs. In the last twelve months, 278 people were hurt in crashes here. Five were left with serious injuries. Children, elders, cyclists, workers—no one is spared.

      Last August, an 11-year-old girl was cut across the face by an e-scooter on South Street. The crash report lists “unsafe speed” and “aggressive driving.” She was not in the roadway. She was just there.

      A city worker fixing a sign on Broadway was slashed by a cyclist after a near miss. The DOT called it an “abhorrent assault of a NYC DOT employee who performs critical work to keep our city moving”. The worker bled in the street. The cyclist fled. No arrest.

      A van crashed in Midtown. Police found 76 propane tanks and 75 gallons of gasoline inside. A woman and her child sat in a parked car, struck by a runaway food cart. They went to the hospital. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment. The air stank of fuel. Firefighters forced entry into the van, removing 76 20-pound propane cylinders and 15 five-gallon fuel containers.

      Who Pays the Price

      Cars and SUVs hurt the most. In three years, they injured 180 people here. Trucks and buses, 23. Motorcycles and mopeds, 9. Bikes, 20. The numbers are cold. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises and Gaps

      The city lowered speed limits near schools. Cameras catch speeders day and night. But the carnage continues. Police crack down on “ghost cars” and reckless drivers. Yet the streets stay dangerous for the ones on foot, on bikes, at work.

      When police and leaders act, people live. When they wait, people bleed. The tools are there: speed enforcement, failure-to-yield tickets, targeting crash hotspots. The will is not always there.

      Call to Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand speed enforcement. Demand safe crossings. Demand action before the next child, the next worker, the next neighbor is hurt.

      Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, assembly district AD 61 and state senate district SD 26.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 1?
      It includes the Financial District-Battery Park City, Tribeca-Civic Center, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, Soho-Little Italy-Hudson Square, and Manhattan CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council District District 3, Assembly Districts AD 61, AD 65, and AD 66, and State Senate Districts SD 26 and SD 27.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 1?
      Cars and SUVs: 180 injured by cars and SUVs, Trucks and Buses: 23 injured, Motorcycles and Mopeds: 9 injured, Bikes: 20 injured. No pedestrian deaths reported in the last year. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 1 police can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and issue failure-to-yield tickets. They can target known crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. The tools are there. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, reckless driving, and unsafe streets can be changed. Policy and enforcement save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, and demand enforcement that protects people walking and biking. They can push for more speed cameras and hold agencies accountable for results.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
