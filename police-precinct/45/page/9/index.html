      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 45?

      Left for Dead on the Parkway: How Many More?

      Written June 22, 2025 (Revised June 22, 2025 – Cleaned up body text, tightened FAQ, improved citation formatting, and deduped citations.)

      Precinct 45: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 22, 2025

      The Bodies on the Parkway

      A woman lies in the road at dawn. She is eighteen. She was thrown from the back of a scooter on the Hutchinson River Parkway. The driver left her there. Police say, “An 18-year-old was in critical condition after being thrown from a moped in the hit-and-run” (CBS New York). Six others went to the hospital. The cars kept moving. The city did not stop.

      In the last twelve months, 533 people have been injured in crashes in Precinct 45. Six were hurt so badly they may never walk the same. Two are dead. The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do.

      Patterns of Harm

      The young are not spared. Thirty-six children were hurt in the last year. The old are not spared. One man, sixty-five, died. The city records the numbers. The numbers keep rising.

      SUVs and sedans do most of the damage. They killed two. Trucks killed one. Mopeds, bikes, buses—they leave bruises, but the big machines break bodies. The police know this. They see the wreckage. They write the reports.

      Leadership: Words and Waiting

      The mayor says, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many” (ABC7). The police commissioner promises, “NYPD officers will be highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action” (ABC7). But the parkways run fast and the tickets come slow. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program has lapsed. The speed cameras are not everywhere. The silence is louder than the sirens.

      What the Precinct Can Do

      Precinct 45 can act. They can enforce speed limits. They can ticket drivers who fail to yield. They can target the corners where children cross and the old men shuffle. They can make it hurt to drive reckless. They just need to know it matters.

      Call to Action

      Do not wait for another body in the road. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand speed enforcement. Demand action at crash hotspots. Demand that the mayor and the NYPD treat every injury as a failure, not a fact of life. The numbers will not stop unless you make them.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 45 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, overlaps District 12 and District 13 in the City Council, AD 82 in the Assembly, and SD 34 and SD 36 in the State Senate. See NYC Open Data for details.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 45?
      It includes the Westchester Square, Throgs Neck-Schuylerville, Pelham Bay-Country Club-City Island, Co-Op City, Hart Island, Ferry Point Park-St. Raymond Cemetery, Pelham Bay Park, Bronx CB10, and Bronx CB28 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 12 and District 13, Assembly District AD 82, and State Senate Districts SD 34 and SD 36. See NYC Open Data for more.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 45?
      Cars and Trucks: SUVs and sedans killed 2 pedestrians and caused most injuries. Trucks killed 1. Motorcycles and Mopeds: Caused minor injuries. Bikes: Caused only minor injuries, no deaths. Data: NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 45 can enforce speed limits, issue failure-to-yield tickets, and target known crash hotspots. They can crack down on reckless driving and respond to dangerous conditions. As the NYPD said, “NYPD officers will be highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action” (ABC7).
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Speeding, reckless driving, and failure to yield can be stopped with enforcement and safer street design. Every injury is a failure, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can push for more speed enforcement, expand speed cameras, restore programs like the Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program, and demand action from NYPD and DOT at crash hotspots.
