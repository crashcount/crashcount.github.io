      101 Years Survived—But Not NYC Streets

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 71: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll on the Street

      A woman, 101 years old, walked with the signal at Brooklyn Avenue and Montgomery Street. A GMC SUV turned left. She was hit. She died twelve days later. The driver did not have a license. Police charged him with aggravated unlicensed operation and failure to exercise due care. He was released with a desk ticket. Her son called it a “tragedy”. Her grandson said she was “extremely independent till her last day”.

      In the last twelve months, one person died and six were seriously hurt on these streets. There were 391 injuries in 681 crashes. The dead are mostly pedestrians. The drivers are often unlicensed, distracted, or turning left. The vehicles are SUVs, trucks, sedans. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      Leadership: Action or Delay

      The police have the tools. They can enforce speed limits. They can ticket drivers who fail to yield. They can target crash hotspots. But the deaths keep coming. The city passed Sammy’s Law. The law lets New York lower speed limits to 20 mph. It is not yet used here. The cameras that catch speeders work, but they need Albany to keep them running. Each delay means another risk, another family broken.

      The Work Left to Do

      Precinct 71 is not safe. The old, the young, the walkers, the cyclists—they pay the price. The police can act. The city can act. The law is there. The question is who will use it.

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand lower speeds. Demand action before another name becomes a number.

      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 71 can enforce speed limits, issue tickets for failure to yield, and target crash hotspots. They can respond to dangerous conditions and crack down on reckless driving. The tools exist. They just need to be used.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Speeding, failure to yield, and unlicensed driving can be stopped with enforcement and safer street design. Each crash is a failure, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and push for more enforcement. They can support speed cameras and demand action from city agencies. Delay costs lives.
