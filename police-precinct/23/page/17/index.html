      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 23?

      Six Dead, Fifteen Broken—Who’s Protecting Our Streets?

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised August 9, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, deduped and cleaned citations.)

      Precinct 23: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Recent Toll

      In Precinct 23, the numbers do not lie. Six people killed. Fifteen left with serious injuries. Over a thousand hurt since 2022. The dead include the old and the young, pedestrians and cyclists, riders and passengers. The wounds are not just numbers. They are crushed bones, broken heads, and families left with empty chairs.

      Just last year, a 59-year-old man was killed at an intersection by a cyclist. A baby died on 3rd Avenue, struck by a sedan. An 86-year-old woman was killed crossing at East 104th by a moped. The details are spare, the pain is not. Every crash is a life changed or ended.

      The Dangers Multiply: Vehicles, Speed, and Lax Enforcement

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm: 172 pedestrian injuries and deaths. Bikes and mopeds killed and maimed too: 1 death and 27 injuries from bikes, 1 death and 10 injuries from mopeds and motorcycles. Trucks and buses added another death and 10 injuries. The street is a gauntlet. The most vulnerable—pedestrians, cyclists, children—pay the highest price.

      The police have the tools. They can enforce speed limits, ticket reckless drivers, and target crash hotspots. But the danger remains. As one witness described after a city worker was slashed by a cyclist, “It’s always something in this city. Bright and early, like 6-something in the morning. It’s crazy.”

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Local leaders have the power to act. The city has started to lower speed limits and expand camera enforcement. But change comes slow. Every day of delay is another day of risk. Community boards have called for real solutions. As one board member demanded, “We want a real solution to the bike and pedestrian safety issue.”

      The police in Precinct 23 can do more. They can crack down on speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving. They can protect the crosswalks and corners where people die. But they need to know the public is watching.

      Call to Action: Demand Relentless Protection

      Do not wait for another name to become a number. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Every crash is preventable. Every life matters.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 23 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 8, assembly district AD 68 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 23?
      It includes the East Harlem (South) and Manhattan CB11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council District District 8, Assembly District AD 68, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 30.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 23?
      Cars and SUVs: 1 death, 172 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 10 injuries. Bikes: 1 death, 27 injuries. All numbers from 2022–2025.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 23 police can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and respond to dangerous conditions. As recent crashes show, every day of delay puts lives at risk.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not fate. They are preventable. Speeding, reckless driving, and unsafe streets can be changed. Every crash is a chance to act before the next one.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, and demand police enforcement. They can listen to residents and act before tragedy strikes again.
