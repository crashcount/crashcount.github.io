      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 43?

      Precinct 43: Night roads, hard deaths

      Written August 16, 2025 (Revised August 16, 2025 – Finisher pass to tighten prose, normalize dates, cite policy, add local analysis and citywide fixes.)

      Precinct 43: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 16, 2025

      Precinct 43 is bleeding. Since 2022 (through 2025-08-16), 23 people have died, 2,474 were injured, and 42 suffered serious injuries (NYC Open Data). The toll keeps rising.

      This week: two riders, no second chances On 2025-08-11 two motorcyclists died on the Bronx River Parkway. Police say a drunk driver tried to pass a Volkswagen, struck it, then hit the motorcycles. Both riders were ejected and later died in hospital. Prosecutors charged the 21‑year‑old driver with vehicular manslaughter and DWI (NY Daily News; Gothamist; ABC7).

      A winter crosswalk death we must remember On 2025-02-03 a driver making a left on White Plains Road struck a 75‑year‑old man crossing with the signal. The crash report lists failure to yield and driver inattention. He did not survive (NYC Open Data).

      Where it happens, when it hurts Two corridors keep repeating: Westchester Avenue and the Cross Bronx Expressway. Nights are deadlier. Deaths spike around midnight and again near 4 a.m., according to precinct crash records. Lighting is poor. Night enforcement is thin.

      What drives the carnage Local data point to the same culprits: alcohol involvement, driver inattention or distraction, unsafe speed, and failure to yield. Cars and SUVs cause most pedestrian deaths and injuries here.

      What Precinct 43 can do now

      • Run sustained night patrols at Westchester Avenue and the Cross Bronx service roads. Focus on late‑night passes and lane weaving.
      • Use daylighting and Leading Pedestrian Intervals (LPIs) at the worst turns. Add targeted lighting at known midnight/early‑morning hotspots.
      • Write the tickets that change behavior: failure‑to‑yield, unsafe speed, DWI. Publish results.

      What leaders must do — local + citywide Council Member Amanda Farías, Assembly Member Emérita Torres, and Senator Jose Serrano must fund fixes here and push citywide change. Lower New York City’s default speed limit. Require intelligent speed‑assistance devices for habitual speeders and drivers who rack up repeated camera or DMV violations. Those two moves — slower streets and tech that limits chronic speeding — cut deaths where they happen and citywide (CrashCount Take Action resources).

      Act This is preventable. Demand night enforcement at precinct hotspots, daylighting and LPIs at dangerous turns, better lighting, a lower default speed citywide, and speed‑limiting tech for repeat offenders. Tell Precinct 43 and City Hall this is not normal. Start here: /take_action/.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 43?
      It includes the Soundview-Bruckner-Bronx River, Soundview-Clason Point, Castle Hill-Unionport, Parkchester, Soundview Park, Westchester Square, and Bronx CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 17 and District 18, Assembly Districts AD 85 and AD 87, and State Senate Districts SD 29, SD 32, and SD 34.
      ▸ Where does Precinct 43 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 18, assembly district AD 85 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just “accidents”?
      They are preventable. Our own record shows patterns: repeated hotspots (Westchester Ave, Cross Bronx, White Plains Rd, Bruckner) and deadly windows at night, with deaths peaking around midnight and 4 a.m.—not random (NYC Open Data). Target the patterns and you cut the harm.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 43?
      • Cars and Trucks: 408 incidents, including 8 deaths and 14 serious injuries (NYC Open Data).
      • Motorcycles and Mopeds: 9 incidents; no deaths; 1 serious injury (NYC Open Data).
      • Bikes: 10 incidents; no deaths; no serious injuries reported (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 43 can save lives by using the tools it already has: sustained speeding enforcement; failure‑to‑yield tickets at turning hotspots like White Plains Rd and Westchester Ave; night and weekend operations on Bruckner and Cross Bronx service roads; and rapid response to refit dangerous corners with daylighting and LPIs. When NYPD acts after deadly crashes—like the Aug. 11 parkway killings that prompted immediate lane closures—streets calm; the job is to do that before people die (NY Daily News).
      ▸ What can local politicians do right now?
      Back a lower default speed citywide, require speed‑limiting tech for repeat speeders, and fund daylighting + LPIs at our worst turns. Demand nighttime enforcement in this precinct and public dashboards on tickets, crashes, and fixes.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
