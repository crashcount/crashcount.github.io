      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      The count does not stop

      Since Jan 1, 2022, this precinct has seen 3,355 crashes, with 7 people killed and 1,666 injured. NYC Open Data

      This year, crashes are up: 683 so far, compared to 616 at this point last year. Injuries rose from 283 to 372. Deaths doubled from 1 to 2. NYC Open Data

      Police list failure to yield, improper passing, and inattention by drivers among recorded factors in recent injuries here. NYC Open Data

      Where it keeps happening

      Queens Boulevard, 108 Street, and the highway edges show heavy harm in this command. Police data mark fatalities and injuries on the Long Island Expressway and Grand Central Parkway, and sustained injuries on Queens Boulevard and 108 Street. NYC Open Data

      People walking are hit mostly by drivers of cars and SUVs. Police count 284 such cases since 2022 in this precinct. Trucks and buses add more. NYC Open Data

      Crashes peak in rush hours and late night here. The 8 AM hour shows the most injuries, with evening and night still high at 5 PM to 9 PM. NYC Open Data

      What leaders can do now

      Slow the streets. New York City has the power to set safer speeds citywide. Advocates are pressing for 20 MPH on local streets and for devices that keep the worst repeat speeders from breaking the limit. /take_action/

      A small group of drivers does outsized harm. Researchers found that about 1.5% of motorists account for 21% of pedestrian deaths, and that vehicles with 16 camera tickets in 12 months are far more likely to kill or seriously injure. Streetsblog

      Precinct 112 can target the known hotspots with failure-to-yield enforcement and hardened turns. Daylight the corners on Queens Boulevard and 108 Street. Focus night operations where injuries spike. NYC Open Data

      Albany and City Hall: names and levers

      This precinct sits with Council Member Robert F. Holden, Assembly Member Sam Berger, and State Senator Leroy Comrie. None are listed here as sponsors of speed‑limiter or speed‑limit bills. What gives? /take_action/

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What area does this cover?
      Police Precinct 112 in Queens, including Forest Hills and Rego Park. It overlaps parts of Council Districts 24, 29, and 30; Assembly Districts 27, 28, 35, and 39; and State Senate Districts 14 and 15.
      ▸ How many crashes and victims are we talking about?
      From Jan 1, 2022 to Sep 18, 2025, police recorded 3,355 crashes in Precinct 112, with 7 people killed and 1,666 injured, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Where are the worst spots?
      Police data show repeated harm on the Long Island Expressway and Grand Central Parkway, and sustained injuries on Queens Boulevard and 108 Street.
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes, Persons, Vehicles). Filters: crash dates 2022-01-01 to 2025-09-18; Police Precinct = 112; all modes. We counted total crashes, deaths, and injuries, and reviewed contributing factors and hourly patterns from the same filters. Data were extracted Sep 17–18, 2025. You can start from the crashes dataset and apply the same filters here.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
