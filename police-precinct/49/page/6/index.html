      Eight Dead, 1,500 Hurt—Precinct 49 Bleeds While Leaders Stall

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 20, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 49: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Slow Grind of Loss

      In Precinct 49, the numbers do not soften. Eight people have died on these streets since 2022. More than 1,500 have been injured. Twenty-two suffered wounds so grave they may never walk the same again. NYC crash data

      A 91-year-old man was killed crossing at Rhinelander and Radcliff. He was working in the roadway. The car kept going straight. The man did not. NYC crash data

      A cyclist, age 64, struck down at Williamsbridge and Pierce. A truck, a sedan, a bike. The crash left him dead. No helmet could save him. NYC crash data

      Pedestrians, cyclists, children—no one is spared. In the last year, 45 people under 18 were hurt. The old are not safe either. Eight people over 75 were injured. The violence is steady, unyielding.

      The Police: Tools in Hand, Action Needed

      The police in Precinct 49 have the power to act. They can enforce speed limits. They can ticket drivers who fail to yield. They can target the corners where blood stains the curb. But action is not a press release. It is a summons, a checkpoint, a patrol car parked where drivers race the light.

      When an ambulance and a box truck collided at Pelham Parkway South and Williamsbridge, three people went to the hospital. The cause is still under investigation. The crash was not an act of God. It was a failure somewhere, by someone. “Three people were hurt: the box truck driver and two ambulance workers.”

      A pedestrian was killed in a hit-and-run. The driver fled. The police are searching. “Police are searching for a driver who allegedly fled after striking and killing a pedestrian in the Bronx early Saturday morning.”

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and the Waiting

      The city has new tools. Sammy’s Law lets New York lower speed limits. Cameras catch speeders day and night. But laws do not save lives unless enforced. Every day of delay is another roll of the dice for a child in a crosswalk.

      The police and local leaders must act. The tools are there. The blood is real. The time is now.

      Call to Action:

      Demand more from Precinct 49. Call your precinct. Call your council member. Tell them to enforce the law, slow the cars, and protect the people who walk and ride. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 49 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx. It overlaps Council Districts District 12, District 13, and District 15, Assembly Districts AD 80 and AD 82, and State Senate Districts SD 33, SD 34, and SD 36.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 49?
      It includes the Pelham Parkway-Van Nest, Morris Park, Pelham Gardens, Allerton, Hutchinson Metro Center, and Bronx CB11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 12, District 13, and District 15, Assembly Districts AD 80 and AD 82, and State Senate Districts SD 33, SD 34, and SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 49?
      Cars and SUVs: 3 deaths, 42 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 1 death, 2 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 5 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 0 deaths, 2 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 49 police can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and issue failure-to-yield tickets. They can target known crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. Every ticket, every patrol, every checkpoint can save a life.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These are not accidents. They are preventable crashes. Speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving can be stopped with enforcement and better street design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can push for lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and demand police enforcement. They can support laws like Sammy’s Law and make sure cameras and traffic calming are used where needed.
