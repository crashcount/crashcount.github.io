      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 120?

      Four Dead, Hundreds Hurt—Staten Island Streets Still Kill

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Precinct 120: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      Death on Bay Street, Blood on Targee

      In the last year, four people died and over 500 were injured on the streets of Precinct 120. The numbers are blunt. The stories are worse.

      On July 5th, Jeremy Claudio, 34, was riding his motorcycle on Bay Street. A Toyota SUV made a K-turn across traffic. Claudio hit the driver’s door. He died at the hospital. There were no arrests. The NYPD Collision Investigation Squad is still working the case. As reported by amNY, “A motorcyclist was killed after colliding with a driver awkwardly turning their car on Staten Island during the Fourth of July holiday weekend.”

      In May, Jose Luis Gomez-Guallazaca, 58, was crossing Targee Street. An e-scooter hit him. The impact threw the rider. Gomez-Guallazaca died from his injuries. No charges. No answers. According to amNY, “Upon impact, the driver was violently ejected off the scooter while Gomez-Guallazaca sustained severe trauma.”

      The dead do not get second chances.

      The Numbers: Relentless and Rising

      In the last 12 months, Precinct 120 saw 4 deaths, 6 serious injuries, and 533 people hurt in 945 crashes. Children, elders, and workers. No one is spared. Cars and SUVs did most of the killing—3 deaths and 160 pedestrian injuries. Trucks and buses killed 2 more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes left their own trail of broken bodies.

      Leaders Act—But Not Fast Enough

      The city has new speed limits and more cameras. But the streets are still deadly. The mayor and NYPD have crushed illegal mopeds and promised more enforcement. As the mayor stated, “New Yorkers have strong feelings about illegal mopeds and scooters, because we hear it all the time, especially when they are driving the wrong way down streets or sidewalks or in the dark without lights.” But the dead keep coming. The police have the tools. They can ticket speeders, crack down on reckless turns, and protect crosswalks. They just need to use them.

      Call to Action: Demand Relentless Enforcement and Real Change

      Every crash is a policy failure. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand speed enforcement, safe crossings, and action at every hotspot. The next life lost could be someone you love. Don’t wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 120 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Staten Island, city council district District 49, assembly district AD 61 and state senate district SD 23.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 120?
      It includes the St. George-New Brighton, Tompkinsville-Stapleton-Clifton-Fox Hills, Rosebank-Shore Acres-Park Hill, West New Brighton-Silver Lake-Grymes Hill, Westerleigh-Castleton Corners, Port Richmond, Snug Harbor, Fort Wadsworth, and Staten Island CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 49 and District 50, Assembly Districts AD 61 and AD 63, and State Senate Districts SD 23 and SD 24.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 120?
      Cars and SUVs: 3 deaths, 160 pedestrian injuries. Trucks and buses: 2 deaths, 21 pedestrian injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 2 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 4 injuries. (2022–2025 data)
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 120 can enforce speed limits, ticket reckless drivers, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue failure-to-yield tickets and respond to dangerous conditions. The NYPD has the tools—speed enforcement, crosswalk protection, and more. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Every crash is a policy failure. Speeding, reckless turns, and unsafe streets can be stopped with enforcement and better design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand camera enforcement, and demand police crack down on dangerous driving. They can listen to residents and act before another life is lost.
