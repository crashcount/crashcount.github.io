      Precinct 120: Three Dead, Hundreds Hurt—Who’s Next?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 120: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      A man steps into the street. A machine meets flesh. In the last twelve months, three people died and 484 were injured in traffic crashes in Precinct 120. Six suffered injuries so grave they may never walk the same. The dead are old and young. The pain is not spread evenly. Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. The numbers do not flinch.

      On May 17, Jose Luis Gomez-Guallazaca, 58, was crossing Targee Street. An e-scooter hit him. The driver was thrown. Jose was left with “severe trauma throughout his body.” He died at Richmond University Medical Center. Police could not confirm the speed or direction, reported amNY. No arrests. The street is quiet again, but the loss does not fade.

      In January, a 68-year-old man was killed in a crosswalk at Forest Avenue and Raymond Place. The SUV driver failed to yield. He was distracted. The man died with a head injury. The crosswalk is still there. The cars do not slow.

      The Police: Tools in Hand, Action Needed

      Precinct 120 holds the power to enforce the law. They can ticket speeders, crack down on reckless drivers, and target the corners where blood stains the curb. They know where the danger lies. They have the tools. They need only to use them.

      When chaos erupts, the police are not immune. In March, two officers crashed their cruiser into a restaurant while swerving to avoid a U-turning car. “Another vehicle attempted to make a U-turn, causing the police cruiser to swerve to avoid a collision,” reported ABC7. The officers survived. The building did not. The street remains unchanged.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Silence

      Local leaders have the power to act. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit stands. The council can demand more enforcement. They can fund redesigns. They can push for speed cameras and keep them running. Every day they wait, the risk remains. The silence is loud.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is failure. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand lower speed limits and real enforcement. Tell Precinct 120 to use every tool. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Take action now.

      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 120 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and issue failure-to-yield tickets. They can target crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. As recent crashes show, sudden U-turns and speeding kill. The precinct has the tools. They must use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or just 'accidents'?
      These are not accidents. They are preventable crashes. Speeding, failure to yield, and distraction kill. Lower speeds, better enforcement, and safer streets save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, and demand more enforcement. They can keep speed cameras running and push for real change. Every delay costs lives.
