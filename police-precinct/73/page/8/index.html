      Brownsville Bleeds While City Sleeps: Demand Action on Deadly Streets

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned up citations.)

      Precinct 73: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll in Brownsville and Ocean Hill

      Three dead. Seven seriously hurt. In the last year, the streets of Precinct 73 have not been quiet. The numbers are blunt: 922 crashes, 570 injuries, 7 left with wounds that will not heal. Three people did not come home. These are not numbers. They are fathers, daughters, neighbors.

      Larry Maxwell, 72, was crossing Sutter Avenue to join his family for a cookout. A driver, fleeing another crash, struck him and kept going. His son, Larnce Vargas, stood on the sidewalk and watched the police and EMS surround his father. “I was just on the phone with him. We just finished watching the Knicks game, actually. Right after the game we were supposed to meet up at the park across the street for a cookout—right across the street from the building he was coming out of. He got struck right in front of the building.” NY Daily News

      Imani Vance, 26, died in the front seat of a Mercedes. The driver, unlicensed, ran a stop sign and slammed into a school bus. He left her behind and ran. Brooklyn DA Eric Gonzalez called it out: “The tragic loss of Imani Vance was allegedly caused by the defendant’s reckless decision to ignore a stop sign and drive at excessive speeds.”

      Leadership: Promises and Gaps

      The city has tools. Speed cameras. Lower speed limits. Laws to hold reckless drivers accountable. But the tools are only as strong as the will to use them. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program has lapsed. Speed cameras need Albany’s blessing to keep running. Sammy’s Law lets the city lower speed limits, but the streets here still wait for action.

      The police in Precinct 73 can do more. They can target speeding. They can ticket drivers who fail to yield. They can stand at the corners where blood stains the curb. They can make it clear: these deaths are not fate.

      What Comes Next

      This is not a storm. It is a slow bleed. Every day without action is another day a family waits for a knock on the door. The numbers will not change themselves.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand lower speed limits. Demand enforcement. Demand that the police in Precinct 73 use every tool they have.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Take action now.

      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 73 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and respond to dangerous conditions. The police have the tools—they just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. The same intersections see repeat injuries. Speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving can be stopped with enforcement and safer street design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can push for lower speed limits, renew life-saving programs like the Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program, and demand full use of speed cameras and enforcement. They can hold city agencies accountable for action—not just promises.
