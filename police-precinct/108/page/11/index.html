      Twelve Dead. Thirty-Four Broken. Who’s Accountable for Queens’ Blood on the Street?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 108: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      A 16-year-old girl, crossing at 47th Avenue and 46th Street, never made it home. A 28-year-old cyclist, struck by a turning truck at Greenpoint and 43rd, died with her helmet still on. An 8-year-old girl, riding in the back seat, left the Brooklyn Queens Expressway by ambulance. In three years, 12 people have died on the streets of Precinct 108. Thirty-four more suffered serious injuries. The youngest killed was a child. The oldest, 75. These are families torn open, futures erased.

      The Pattern: Who Pays the Price

      Cars and trucks did the most harm. SUVs and sedans killed four. Trucks and buses left eight with broken bones or worse. Motorcycles and mopeds, five more. Bikes, too, have left their mark. The streets do not forgive. Pedestrians, cyclists, children—they pay first.

      The Response: What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city talks of Vision Zero. They say, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many” (said Mayor Adams). Speed cameras now run all day and night. The law lets the city lower speed limits to 20 mph. But in Precinct 108, the pace of change is slow. Crashes keep coming. The police have the tools: speeding tickets, failure-to-yield, targeting hotspots. They just need to use them.

      The Warning: Infrastructure Fails, Danger Remains

      Even the sky is not safe. In April, a bolt from the No. 7 train crashed through a windshield at Queens Plaza. “A bolt hit and then right away just the sound of glass exploding and glass all over me. Like on my legs, in my legs, on my chest,” (said Malnick). The MTA promises inspections. The danger hangs above and below.

      The Call: Demand Action—Now

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call the precinct. Demand lower speed limits, real enforcement, and safer crossings. Every day of delay is another day for a child to die. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 108 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens. It overlaps Council District District 26, Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 36, AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 108?
      It includes the Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Woodside, Sunnyside Yards (South), Calvary & Mount Zion Cemeteries, and Queens CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council District District 26, Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 36, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 108?
      Cars and Trucks: 4 deaths, 52 moderate injuries, 3 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 0 deaths, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 108 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue speeding and failure-to-yield tickets, and respond to dangerous conditions. The tools exist. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or just 'accidents'?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speeds, better enforcement, and safer street design save lives. They are not accidents—they are the result of choices and policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, expand automated enforcement, and demand accountability from city agencies and police. They can act now, or answer for more deaths.
