      Red Light, Broken Lives: Queens Kids Hit While Leaders Stall

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 115: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      Children in the Crosswalk, Sirens in the Night

      A four-year-old and her eight-year-old sister stepped into the crosswalk at 37th Avenue and 73rd Street. The light was red. The SUV did not stop. The driver went around another car and hit them both. The girls went to Elmhurst Hospital. The driver fled. The street was quiet again, but the blood stayed. Police said the girls survived. This time.

      The Toll: Lives Shattered, Numbers Rising

      In the last twelve months, Precinct 115 saw 1,178 crashes. Two people died. Eight were left with serious injuries. Seven hundred twenty-seven were hurt. Children, elders, workers on their way to the airport. The dead do not get a second chance. The injured carry it forever.

      SUVs killed more pedestrians here than any other vehicle. Trucks, sedans, bikes, mopeds—they all left bodies behind. The numbers do not lie. Speed, red lights, and failure to yield are not accidents. They are choices.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and the Waiting

      The city says it is fighting. Speed cameras now run all day and night. The law lets the city lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit has not changed. The cameras could go dark if Albany does not act. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program lapsed. The mayor says, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many”. The DA calls out the carnage: “Drunk, drugged and reckless driving are dire threats to everyone on our shared roadways.”

      But the streets do not wait for speeches. Precinct 115 can enforce the law. They can ticket speeders, reckless drivers, and those who fail to yield. They can target the corners where people keep dying.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Demand the police crack down on reckless driving.

      Do not wait for another child in the crosswalk.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 115 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens. It overlaps Council Districts District 21, District 22, and District 25, Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 34, AD 35, and AD 39, and State Senate Districts SD 11 and SD 13.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 115?
      It includes the Jackson Heights, East Elmhurst, North Corona, Laguardia Airport, Queens CB80, and Queens CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 21, District 22, and District 25, Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 34, AD 35, and AD 39, and State Senate Districts SD 11 and SD 13.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 115?
      SUVs and Cars: 3 deaths, 71 moderate injuries, 4 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 1 death, 5 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 5 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 5 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 115 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. The police have the tools—they just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, running red lights, and failing to yield are choices, not fate. Enforcement and safer street design can stop the next tragedy.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, keep speed cameras running, fund safer street designs, and push for stronger enforcement. They can act now, or answer for the next life lost.
