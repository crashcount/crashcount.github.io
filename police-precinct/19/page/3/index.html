      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 19?

      Precinct 19: Crosswalks, Sirens, Silence

      Written August 24, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Precinct 19: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 24, 2025

      Another driver. Same ending.

      • A 71-year-old woman crossed with the signal at East 68th and York. An SUV driver turned right and hit her. She died. Police cited distraction. NYC Open Data lists the case as CrashID 4664179.
      • A 30-year-old woman stood at York and East 87th. Unsafe speed. Multiple cars. She died at the intersection. CrashID 4714580 is on the city database.
      • A 90-year-old woman was crossing near York and East 72nd. A taxi and an SUV going south struck her. She died. CrashID 4790720 sits in the same dataset.
      • On the FDR, a northbound sedan was “demolished.” Unsafe speed. The driver was ejected and killed. CrashID 4790343 is logged on NYC Open Data.

      Twelve dead across this precinct since 2022. Pedestrians lead the toll: six. Cyclists: two. Occupants: four. The city’s own rollup shows SUVs and cars as the main source of harm, with trucks and buses close behind. See the period stats.

      Peak hours show no refuge. Injuries crest from 2 p.m. through the evening rush, with a spike at 5 p.m. Then the night takes its own share. Hourly data mark deaths at 4 a.m., 6 a.m., and again at 10 a.m., 5 p.m., 9 p.m., 10 p.m., 2 a.m.

      Three corners. One fix.

      • The FDR is the top killer here, with three deaths and 336 injured. FDR Drive is the hotspot.
      • Second Avenue follows, with two deaths and 67 hurt. Second Avenue tells the rest.
      • York Avenue keeps taking people at East 68th, 72nd, 83rd, and 87th.

      The causes are not mysteries. “Unsafe speed.” “Driver inattention.” “Disregarded traffic control.” Those are the city’s own words in the case files. The contributing factors list them again and again.

      Block by block, the bodies add up.

      • Pedestrians hit most often by SUVs and cars. The tally shows 112 pedestrian strikes by SUVs, with seven serious injuries and four deaths. Sedans follow close behind. Trucks kill too. Vehicle rollup for pedestrians.
      • Bicyclists: 333 injured, 15 seriously, two dead. Mode split data.

      Officials know what works — do they?

      “Demand for curb space in the city is increasing,” DOT said, as it rolled out paid parking overnight on the West Side. The agency flipped signs in the dark and told drivers to use an app. “They didn’t provide a ton of notice,” a local manager said. Read the West Side Spirit report.

      After a stolen car doing more than 100 mph killed two people at Canal Street and Bowery, the city promised barriers and a redesign. “We are taking immediate steps to fortify this intersection,” the transportation commissioner said. An advocate warned: “the vast majority of the corridor will remain deadly.” Read Gothamist and NY1.

      Here in Precinct 19, the patterns rhyme: speed, turning drivers, heavy vehicles on narrow blocks, a highway that maims.

      Three fixes. Start today.

      • Daylight every corner on York and Second. Harden the turns. Give pedestrians time to step off first with leading intervals. Target the right turns that kill. These address the listed factors: unsafe speed, inattention, failure to yield.
      • Calm the FDR’s feeders. Narrow entries. Raised crossings on the frontage roads. Enforce the lights where people cross to the river.
      • Protect bike routes through the hot blocks. Physical barriers, not paint, where the injuries cluster.

      Citywide, the steps are on the table.

      • Lower the default speed limit. A slower city saves lives. Albany passed Sammy’s Law to let the city act. Our call is simple: make 20 the norm. See our call to act: “Lower our residential speed limit to 20 mph citywide.”
      • Stop the worst repeat speeders. The Stop Super Speeders Act would put speed limiters on the cars that rack up tickets. The bill targets the tiny share of drivers who do the most harm. Our action page has the numbers and the script.

      Names fade on paper. The corners stay the same. The next siren is already coming.

      Act now: tell City Hall and your state reps to slow these streets and rein in the repeat offenders. Start here: Take Action.

      Jun 13 - Cyclists face cars at the 79th Street Rotunda. The city offers only paint, not protection. Officials cite space. Community pleas go unheard. Families feel stress. The hilly detour stays. Danger remains where cars and bikes must mix.

      Streetsblog NYC (2025-06-13) reports that city officials will not build a protected bike lane through the 79th Street Rotunda, despite years of requests from Manhattan Community Board 7. DOT official Colleen Chattergoon said, "With the space that we have, we cannot accommodate a protected bike lane in that area." The city has not acted on a 2019 resolution calling for real protection. Community members and advocates question the safety of the design, especially for children. Officials promised to review the resolution but gave no timeline or details. The plan leaves cyclists exposed to fast-moving traffic, highlighting ongoing policy gaps in street safety.
