      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Nineteen dead since 2022

      Since Jan 1, 2022 in Precinct 102, 19 people have been killed and 3,042 injured in 5,151 crashes. NYC crash data

      This year, crashes stand at 959 versus 1,020 over the same span last year. Deaths are 4 compared with 5. Serious injuries are 9 now; they were 2 at this point a year ago. NYC crash data

      Where it breaks: Woodhaven and the parkway

      Woodhaven Boulevard leads this precinct’s harm ledger. The Jackie Robinson Parkway is not far behind. Police records also show repeat trauma on 101 Avenue and Atlantic Avenue. NYC crash data

      Police attributed injuries here to drivers’ failure to yield, running signals, distraction, alcohol, and improper passing. Each shows up in the reports. NYC crash data

      Nights hurt. Two AM kills.

      Injuries stack up late afternoon into evening, peaking around 5–6 PM and 6–7 PM. Deaths spike at 2 AM. That is the clock the street keeps. NYC crash data

      Make the turns safe. Clear the corners. Slow the traffic.

      Start with the corners on Woodhaven, Atlantic, 101 Avenue: daylight the crosswalks, add leading pedestrian intervals, and harden turns so drivers have to take them slow. Target failure-to-yield and night driving on those corridors. The precinct has the map. The city has the tools. NYC crash data

      Hold speed. Stop repeat harm.

      Lower speeds save lives. The city has the power to set safer limits on local streets, and advocates are pushing to use it. The state’s proposed Stop Super Speeders Act would require habitual speeders to use tech that keeps them within the limit. Both measures are detailed here.

      “Speed cameras have cut speeding by over 60% in locations where installed.” NYS Senate

      Your local officials are Council Member Joann Ariola, Assembly Member David Weprin, and State Senator Leroy Comrie. The bills are on the table. The deaths are on the record. NYC crash data
