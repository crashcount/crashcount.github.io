      Fifteen Dead, Still Waiting: Precinct 102’s Streets Bleed While Leaders Stall

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 29, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 102: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll in Plain Sight

      In Precinct 102, the numbers do not lie. Fifteen people are dead. Nineteen more have suffered serious injuries. Since 2022, there have been 3,743 crashes and over 2,200 injuries. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, parents, children. They are gone.

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and SUVs killed six. Trucks killed two. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll. The street does not care who you are. It only cares how fast you move and how hard you hit.

      Recent Crashes, Fresh Wounds

      The carnage does not pause. In March, two men—one 67, one 31—were killed on Atlantic Avenue. They died in the street, struck by an SUV. The driver lost consciousness. Both pedestrians died of internal injuries. The crash is still under investigation. No arrests. No peace. NYC Open Data

      In April, a 23-year-old woman was killed at the intersection of Woodhaven Boulevard and 83rd. She was crossing. A motorcycle hit her. She bled out on the pavement. NYC Open Data

      A year ago, a 47-year-old man was killed by a pickup truck while crossing with the signal at 101st Avenue and 120th Street. The driver was distracted. The man was left unconscious, never to wake up. NYC Open Data

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have tools. They can act. The city has begun lowering speed limits and expanding speed cameras. But in this precinct, the pace is slow. The police can enforce the law. They can crack down on speeding, reckless driving, and failure to yield. They can target crash hotspots. They just need to know it matters.

      The city says, “One traffic fatality is one too many.” Deputy Mayor Meera Joshi. But the dead keep coming. The message is clear. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      The Call: Demand Action Now

      This is not fate. It is failure. The police in Precinct 102 can enforce speed limits and ticket reckless drivers. Local leaders can push for safer streets and more cameras. Residents can demand action. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand they make traffic violence stop.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 102 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 32, assembly district AD 24 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 102?
      It includes the Kew Gardens, Richmond Hill, South Richmond Hill, Ozone Park (North), Woodhaven, Forest Park, Queens CB9, and Queens CB82 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 29 and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 24, AD 28, and AD 38, and State Senate Districts SD 14 and SD 15.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 102?
      Cars and SUVs: 6 deaths, 246 minor injuries, 57 moderate injuries, 5 serious injuries; Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 14 minor injuries, 1 moderate injury; Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 1 minor injury, 4 moderate injuries; Bikes: No deaths, 5 minor injuries, 1 moderate injury. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 102 police can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue speeding and failure-to-yield tickets, and target known crash hotspots. The tools exist. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Speeding, distraction, and reckless driving can be stopped. Every death is a failure, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign dangerous streets, and demand enforcement. They can push for laws that protect people, not just cars.
