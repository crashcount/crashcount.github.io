      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 66?

      BMW Ran the Light. Two Men Died. Who’s Next?

      Written July 14, 2025

      Precinct 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 14, 2025

      The Week the Street Stopped Two Lives

      Friday morning, July 11th. Third Avenue and 52nd Street. A BMW ran the red. Two men crossing the street—Kex Un Chen, 80, and Faqiu Lin, 59—never made it to the other side. The car did not stop. Both men died in the crosswalk. The driver was caught later, charged with manslaughter and reckless driving. Police said the car was “speeding southbound” and “did not brake before impact” (ABC7).

      Neighbors saw it coming. “Drivers speed on that stretch of roadway,” one told reporters. The numbers back them up. In the last five years, the three blocks around that corner saw 59 crashes and about 80 injuries. The street is a gauntlet.

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      In the past twelve months, six people have died in crashes in Precinct 66. Another 679 were injured. Children, elders, men and women. Two children under 18 are among the dead. The old are not spared: one death each in the 35–44, 45–54, 55–64, and 65–74 age groups. Serious injuries cut across every age.

      The killers are not ghosts. SUVs and cars caused the most harm—eight deaths, 533 injuries. Trucks and buses killed two, injured 48. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes left dozens more hurt. The numbers do not lie. The street is a weapon in the wrong hands.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has tools. Speed cameras. Lower limits. Crackdowns on reckless driving. But the carnage continues. Police have the power to enforce the law, to ticket speeders, to target known hotspots. They know where the bodies fall. They know who is most at risk.

      Local leaders have spoken about Vision Zero. They have passed bills. But the deaths do not stop. The street does not care about promises. It cares about action. Every day without enforcement is another day someone does not come home.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand speed enforcement. Demand real protection for people walking and biking.

      The street will not change itself. Only people can do that. Only you can make them act.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 66 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38, assembly district AD 44 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 66?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, Mapleton-Midwood (West), and Brooklyn CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 66?
      Cars and SUVs: 8 deaths, 533 injuries. Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 48 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 21 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 25 injuries. Most deaths and injuries come from cars, SUVs, trucks, and buses (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 66 police can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and issue tickets for failure to yield. They can target crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. The tools exist—speeding tickets, failure-to-yield enforcement, and focused patrols. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not random. They follow patterns—speeding, red-light running, reckless driving. Enforcement and safer street design can prevent deaths. The recent deaths on Third Avenue were not fate. They were preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can push for lower speed limits, more speed cameras, and safer street designs. They can demand police enforcement at known danger spots. They can listen to residents and act before the next death.
