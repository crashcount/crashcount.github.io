      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 66?

      Brooklyn Bleeds: City Stalls, Kids Die

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Fixed duplicate headline in body, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      Precinct 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Precinct 66, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people killed. Sixteen left with serious injuries. Over 2,100 hurt since 2022. The dead include children, elders, riders, walkers. The living carry scars.

      Just weeks ago, a 17-year-old was killed on Avenue N. His 14-year-old passenger was thrown from the moped and left unconscious, pelvis shattered. The crash involved a sedan, a moped, and a parked SUV. The boy did not come home. See NYC Open Data.

      Last May, a 38-year-old woman died at Ditmas Avenue. The cause: driver inattention. She was behind the wheel. She did not survive. Police reported her death as “Apparent Death.” See NYC Open Data.

      A year ago, a 16-year-old cyclist was crushed by a turning box truck at Coney Island and Ditmas. He was riding. The truck turned. He died.

      The Usual Weapons: Cars, Trucks, Speed

      SUVs and cars did most of the harm: 7 deaths, 5 serious injuries, 524 injured. Trucks and buses killed 2, left one with life-changing wounds. Motorcycles and mopeds, too—one dead, one seriously hurt. Bikes: 25 injured, none killed. The street is a battlefield. The weapons are known.

      Leadership: Promises, Delays, and What’s Missing

      The city has the tools. Sammy’s Law lets New York set safer speed limits. The council can lower the default to 20 mph. They have not. The police can enforce speed, failure to yield, reckless driving. They can target crash hotspots. They know where the bodies fall.

      But the deaths keep coming. The numbers do not change. “A hit-and-run driver struck and killed a 36-year-old man crossing a Brooklyn street,” officials said Friday. The driver sped off. The victim died at Brookdale Hospital.

      “According to the NYPD, the driver was traveling westbound on East New York Avenue when they struck a 36-year-old man walking on the road and kept going,” ABC7 reported.

      What Must Be Done

      Precinct 66 can act. Enforce speed limits. Crack down on reckless drivers. Flood the hotspots. Issue tickets for failure to yield. Demand the council set 20 mph as the law. Do not wait for another child to die.

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand action. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 66 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38, assembly district AD 44 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 66?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, Mapleton-Midwood (West), and Brooklyn CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 66?
      Cars and Trucks: 7 deaths, 5 serious injuries, 524 total injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 1 serious injury, 21 total injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 25 total injuries. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 66 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. The police know where crashes happen. They have the power to act.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The same deadly patterns repeat—speed, turning vehicles, failure to yield. These are preventable deaths, not random acts.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower the speed limit to 20 mph under Sammy’s Law, fund safer street designs, and demand enforcement against reckless drivers. They can act now, or answer for more deaths.
