      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 66?

      No One Safe Until Streets Change

      Written July 9, 2025

      Precinct 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A man steps into the street. A car does not stop. In Precinct 66, this story repeats. Twenty people have died here since 2022. Sixteen more were left with injuries so grave they may never heal. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the empty chair at dinner.

      Just last week, a 70-year-old man was crossing Avenue I. A Smart Car driver plowed into him. The driver stayed. Dov Broyde did not. He died at the hospital. No charges were filed. The street stayed the same.

      A 90-year-old man was left bleeding on the pavement after a masked moped rider hit him and fled. “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open,” said an eyewitness. The rider vanished. The man clings to life.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the damage. Since 2022, they have killed seven people and injured over 500 more in this precinct. Trucks and buses killed two. Mopeds and motorcycles, one. Bikes, none. The street is not a fair fight.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has new tools. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The Council can lower speed limits to 20 mph. The NYPD can crack down on speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving. They can target the corners where blood stains the curb. But the pace is slow. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      The Role of the Precinct

      Precinct 66 has the power to act. They can enforce the law. They can write tickets for speeding, for failing to yield, for reckless turns. They can show up where the crashes happen, again and again. They can make it clear: the street is not a place to kill.

      Act Now—Before the Next Siren

      Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Call the precinct. Demand action. Ask for lower speed limits. Ask for real enforcement. Ask for streets where a walk to the store does not end in blood. The dead cannot speak. You can.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 66 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38, assembly district AD 44 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 66?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, Mapleton-Midwood (West), and Brooklyn CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 66?
      SUVs and Cars: 7 deaths, 373 minor injuries, 140 moderate injuries, 5 serious injuries (total 525 incidents). Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 30 minor injuries, 13 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury (total 46 incidents). Mopeds and Motorcycles: 0 deaths, 14 minor injuries, 6 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury (total 21 incidents). Bikes: 0 deaths, 17 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries (total 25 incidents).
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 66 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue tickets for speeding, failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. The police have the tools—they just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These deaths and injuries are not random. They happen again and again, in the same places, to the same kinds of people. Lower speeds, better enforcement, and safer streets can prevent them.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and demand real enforcement. They can push for policies that protect people, not just cars. They can act now, or answer for the next death.
