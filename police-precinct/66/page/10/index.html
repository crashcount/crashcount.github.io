      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 66?

      Blood on 15th Avenue: How Many More Must Die Before They Act?

      Written July 8, 2025

      Precinct 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 8, 2025

      The Bodies on the Road

      In Precinct 66, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people killed. Sixteen left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. Since 2022, there have been 3,642 crashes. The dead include children, elders, and workers heading home. The living carry scars.

      A 67-year-old man was killed crossing 15th Avenue and 54th Street. The truck kept going straight. He did not get up. The record says only “Apparent Death.” The street remembers more, as shown in NYC Open Data.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars did most of the damage—seven deaths, 373 minor injuries, 140 moderate, five serious. Trucks and buses killed two, left dozens more hurt. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes all took their share. But the pattern is clear. The bigger the machine, the heavier the toll.

      The Police and the Power They Hold

      The police in Precinct 66 have the tools. They can ticket speeders, stop reckless drivers, and target the corners where blood pools. They can act before the next siren. But action is a choice. The record is silent on how often they choose it.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Silence

      Local leaders have the power to change the rules. They can lower speed limits. They can demand safer streets. They can push for enforcement that protects the walker, not the driver. The law is a lever. It only works if someone pulls it.

      The Voices Left Behind

      “He didn’t stop. He just kept going,” said Henry Tziquin after watching his father die in the street. The horror does not end with the crash. “We are there with him, watching and waiting for him to wake up, but he never opened his eyes,” he said. The city moves on. The families do not.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand action. Tell them the numbers. Tell them the names. Tell them you will not wait for another body to hit the pavement. The slow disaster will not end until someone stops it.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 66 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38, assembly district AD 44 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 66?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, Mapleton-Midwood (West), and Brooklyn CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 66?
      SUVs and Cars: 7 deaths, 373 minor injuries, 140 moderate injuries, 5 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 30 minor injuries, 13 moderate, 1 serious. Motorcycles and Mopeds: No deaths, 14 minor injuries, 6 moderate, 1 serious. Bikes: No deaths, 17 minor injuries, 8 moderate, 0 serious. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 66 police can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield. They can respond to dangerous conditions before another person is killed. The tools are there. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. They happen where speed is unchecked, laws go unenforced, and streets are built for cars, not people. The record shows patterns, not chance.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, demand safer street designs, and push for enforcement that protects people walking and cycling. They can hold agencies accountable and make safety the law, not the exception.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
