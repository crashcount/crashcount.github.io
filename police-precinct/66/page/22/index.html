      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 66?

      Blood on Bay Parkway: Precinct 66 Fails to Stop the Killing

      Written July 7, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Fixed link formatting, removed duplicate headline, cleaned citations.)

      Precinct 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Precinct 66, the story is written in blood and broken bodies. Twenty people have died on these streets since 2022. Two were children. Sixteen more suffered injuries so grave they may never heal. In the last year alone, five lives ended, including two children. 657 people were hurt.

      The numbers do not tell you about the silence after the crash. They do not tell you about the shoes left behind, or the families waiting in hospital rooms. But the numbers do not lie. They show a disaster that does not stop.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars are the main killers. They caused seven deaths and over 500 injuries to pedestrians and cyclists. Trucks and buses killed two more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll, but the weight of death rolls on four wheels. The old, the young, the ones just crossing the street—none are spared.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      Just last November, a van changed lanes on Bay Parkway and killed a 54-year-old man. His passenger was left with broken bones. The crash report lists the cause as “unsafe lane changing”—a phrase that means nothing to the dead (NYC Open Data).

      Last summer, a 16-year-old boy was crushed by a truck while riding his bike at Coney Island Avenue and Ditmas. He never made it home. The truck was turning right. The boy was in the way.

      Leadership: Action or Excuses?

      The police in Precinct 66 have the tools. They can ticket speeders. They can crack down on reckless drivers. They can target the corners where people keep dying. But the bodies keep piling up. The law is only as strong as its enforcement.

      Local leaders have the power to demand more. They can push for lower speed limits, more cameras, safer street designs. They can call out delay and demand action. But every day of silence is another day of risk.

      The Words That Remain

      A crash does not end when the sirens fade. “He didn’t stop. He just kept going,” said the son of a man killed by a hit-and-run driver in Brooklyn. The horror does not leave. “I see him on the floor… I’m scared—I don’t know what to do,” he said.

      Call to Action

      This is not fate. This is failure. Call your council member. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Demand that Precinct 66 use every tool to stop the killing. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 66 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38, assembly district AD 44 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 66?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, Mapleton-Midwood (West), and Brooklyn CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 66?
      Cars and Trucks: 7 deaths, 524 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 21 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 25 injuries. Most deaths and injuries come from cars, SUVs, trucks, and buses (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 66 police can enforce speed limits, issue tickets for reckless driving and failure to yield, and focus on crash hotspots. They have the power to act. Every ticket, every crackdown, every patrol at a dangerous corner can save a life.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. They happen again and again at the same places, for the same reasons—speed, reckless driving, unsafe streets. They are preventable with enforcement and better design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can push for lower speed limits, more speed cameras, and safer street designs. They can demand that police enforce the law and that city agencies fix dangerous roads.
