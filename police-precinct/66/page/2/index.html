      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 66?

      Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 29, 2025

      Precinct 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      Five dead. Sixteen seriously hurt. That is the count in Precinct 66 since last summer. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The numbers do not flinch. In the last twelve months alone, two children died and eighty-five more were injured in crashes here. The streets do not forgive mistakes. They do not care about age.

      Last week, a 17-year-old boy rode a moped north on East Eighth Street. He collided with a car making a left turn. The impact threw him and his passenger. He died at the hospital. Police said Jhoan later died at the hospital. No arrests. The investigation drags on.

      A few days before, a drunk, unlicensed driver killed a moped rider in Bay Ridge. The man lived less than half a mile from the crash. Police said Moreno was driving west on 67th Street while Mota was driving south on Third Avenue, and he hit her passenger-side door.

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In three years, they took seven lives and left hundreds more broken. Trucks, buses, mopeds, and bikes all play their part. But the weight of steel falls hardest on the softest bodies.

      Leadership: Action or Delay?

      The police have the tools. They can enforce speed limits. They can ticket drivers who fail to yield. They can target crash hotspots. They can act. But the record is thin. The dead pile up. The tickets do not.

      Local politicians have the power to demand more. They can push for lower speed limits, safer street design, and real accountability. But too often, change comes slow or not at all. The silence is loud.

      The Path Forward: No More Waiting

      Every crash is preventable. These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by leaders, by those who look away. The police in Precinct 66 can crack down on reckless driving today. The council and assembly can push for safer laws. Residents can demand action.

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Do not wait for another child’s name in the news. The slow disaster will not stop until you force it to.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 66 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38, assembly district AD 44 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 66?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, Mapleton-Midwood (West), and Brooklyn CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 66?
      Cars and SUVs: 7 deaths, 372 minor injuries, 138 moderate injuries, 5 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 30 minor injuries, 13 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Motorcycles and Mopeds: No deaths, 14 minor injuries, 6 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Bikes: No deaths, 17 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 66 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue speeding and failure-to-yield tickets, and target known crash hotspots. The tools exist. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. Every crash is preventable. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by leaders, and by those who look away.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop the deaths?
      They can push for lower speed limits, demand safer street design, and hold drivers accountable. They can demand more from police and DOT. They can act now, not after another death.
