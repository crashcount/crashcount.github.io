      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 67?

      No Excuses Left: Slower Streets or More Graves

      Written July 14, 2025

      Precinct 67: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 14, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Human Cost

      In Precinct 67, the numbers do not lie. Nine people killed. Forty left with serious injuries. Over 3,300 hurt since 2022. Each number is a body, a family, a life split open by speed and steel. The dead do not speak. Their absence does.

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. Four deaths, hundreds of injuries. Trucks and buses killed two more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes left their own scars. The street does not care who you are. It only cares if you are in the way.

      Recent Crashes: The Pattern Never Breaks

      The pain is not old news. Just this spring, a woman crossing with the signal at Rutland Road and E 95th Street was killed by a turning sedan. She was 68. The driver was distracted. She had the light. She died anyway. NYC Open Data

      A few months before, a 74-year-old woman was struck and killed at Avenue D and New York Avenue. She was crossing with the signal. The driver failed to yield. The story repeats. The ending does not change.

      Leadership: Promises, Delays, and the Role of Police

      The police have the tools. They can enforce speed limits, ticket reckless drivers, and target crash hotspots. They can act. They must act. The numbers show the need. “Neighbors say drivers speed on that stretch of roadway” said CBS New York. The danger is not hidden.

      Local leaders have the power to demand more. They can push for lower speed limits, more daylighted intersections, and real enforcement. They can fight for laws that protect people, not just cars. But every day of delay is another day of risk. “Speeding ruins lives, and reducing vehicle speeds by even a few miles per hour could be the difference between life or death in a traffic crash,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. The police can crack down on speeding and failure to yield. Local leaders can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. Residents can demand action.

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Every day you wait is another day someone does not come home.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 67 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 41, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 19.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 67?
      It includes the East Flatbush-Erasmus, East Flatbush-Farragut, East Flatbush-Rugby, East Flatbush-Remsen Village, Holy Cross Cemetery, and Brooklyn CB17 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 40, District 41, District 42, and District 45, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 42, AD 43, and AD 58, and State Senate Districts SD 19, SD 20, and SD 21.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 67?
      Cars and SUVs: 4 deaths, 400 minor injuries, 137 moderate injuries, 7 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 26 minor injuries, 10 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: No deaths, 4 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries. Bikes: No deaths, 4 minor injuries, 1 moderate injury.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 67 police can enforce speed limits, issue tickets for reckless driving and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. They can respond to dangerous conditions and act on complaints. As neighbors say drivers speed on that stretch of roadway—the danger is clear. Police have the tools. They must use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These are not accidents. The same deadly patterns repeat: speeding, failure to yield, distracted driving. Each crash could have been prevented with better enforcement, safer street design, and lower speed limits.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can push for lower speed limits, more daylighted intersections, and real enforcement. They can demand the police crack down on reckless driving and support laws that protect people walking and biking.
