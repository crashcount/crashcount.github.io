      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 67?

      Nine Dead. A Thousand Hurt. City Hall Hits the Brakes.

      Written July 16, 2025

      Precinct 67: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      Blood on the Streets: The Human Cost

      In Precinct 67, violence does not come with sirens or headlines. It comes with broken bodies and empty chairs. Nine people have died on these streets since 2022. Forty more have been seriously injured. In the last twelve months alone, four lives were lost and over a thousand people were hurt. These numbers are not just statistics—they are mothers, sons, neighbors. A 68-year-old woman, crossing with the signal at E 95th and Rutland, was killed by a turning sedan. A 74-year-old woman, struck at Avenue D and New York Avenue, never made it home. The dead do not get a second chance. (NYC Open Data)

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs do most of the damage. Four deaths. Hundreds of injuries. Trucks and buses killed two more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll, but the weight of death is carried by the largest machines. The pattern is clear. The risk is not random. It is built into the streets and the rules that govern them.

      Leadership: Words, Action, and Silence

      The city has the tools: speed cameras, lower speed limits, new laws. But in Precinct 67, the pace of change is slow. Police can enforce the law. They can crack down on speeding, reckless driving, and failure to yield. They can target crash hotspots. They just need to act. The dead cannot wait for another study.

      “One traffic fatality is one too many,” said Deputy Mayor Meera Joshi, promising to use “every lever…to ensure that New Yorkers are safe however they choose to get around.”

      But promises do not stop cars. “Speeding ruins lives, and reducing vehicle speeds by even a few miles per hour could be the difference between life or death in a traffic crash,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action at every intersection where someone has died. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 67 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 41, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 19.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 67?
      It includes the East Flatbush-Erasmus, East Flatbush-Farragut, East Flatbush-Rugby, East Flatbush-Remsen Village, Holy Cross Cemetery, and Brooklyn CB17 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 40, District 41, District 42, and District 45, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 42, AD 43, and AD 58, and State Senate Districts SD 19, SD 20, and SD 21.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 67?
      Cars and SUVs: 4 deaths, 400 minor injuries, 137 moderate injuries, 7 serious injuries (cars/SUVs). Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 26 minor injuries, 10 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries (trucks/buses). Motorcycles and Mopeds: No deaths, 4 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries (motorcycles/mopeds). Bikes: No deaths, 4 minor injuries, 1 moderate injury.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 67 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and issue failure-to-yield tickets. They can target crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. The police have the tools—they just need to act.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not random. They follow patterns—speed, failure to yield, dangerous streets. With enforcement and safer design, deaths and injuries can be prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can push for lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand automated enforcement, and demand data-driven policing. They can hold agencies accountable for every death.

      Flatbush Avenue Bus Lanes, Pedestrian Islands Planned
      ▸

      Flatbush Avenue will lose car lanes. Bus lanes and pedestrian islands will take their place. Fifty-five killed or badly hurt since 2019. Buses crawl. Pedestrians dodge traffic. The city moves to fix a deadly, clogged corridor.

      Gothamist reported on June 4, 2025, that New York City's Department of Transportation plans to overhaul Flatbush Avenue between Livingston Street and Grand Army Plaza. The redesign replaces two car lanes with 24/7 bus-only lanes and adds pedestrian islands. DOT officials said, 'almost 70,000 daily bus riders are stuck waiting too long for slow buses, drivers are caught in a mess of traffic and pedestrians are left crossing intersections clogged with vehicles.' Since 2019, 55 people have been killed or severely injured in crashes along this stretch. The plan removes curbside parking and bans cars from bus lanes, aiming to speed up twelve bus routes and protect people on foot. Most residents in the area do not own cars and depend on slow buses. The proposal reflects a shift toward prioritizing vulnerable road users and addressing systemic danger on one of Brooklyn's busiest corridors.
