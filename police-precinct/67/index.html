      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 67?

      Nine Dead, Forty Broken: Precinct 67’s Streets Run Red While Leaders Stand Still

      Written July 12, 2025

      Precinct 67: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      Nine people killed. Forty seriously injured. Three years, one precinct. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about hope or excuses. They only rise. In Precinct 67, the dead are not strangers. They are neighbors, elders, children. They are the man crushed crossing Linden Boulevard, the woman struck turning left on Avenue D, the child bleeding on Schenectady Avenue. The street does not forgive. It remembers every name.

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and the Unprotected

      Cars and SUVs did most of the killing. Four deaths and seven serious injuries came from sedans and SUVs. Trucks and buses took two more lives. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes left bodies broken but did not kill. The sidewalk and the crosswalk offer no shield. The numbers are plain: 548 crashes with cars and SUVs, 40 with trucks and buses, 8 with motorcycles and mopeds, 5 with bikes. The odds are not in your favor if you walk here (NYC Open Data).

      The Human Cost

      A woman, 74, crossing with the light, struck by a turning sedan. A 68-year-old, killed by a distracted driver. A 30-year-old, dead from a car going too fast. The stories repeat. The grief does not fade. “We’re very heartbroken. He was a good person,” said Qin Xiao Chen. The pain is not abstract. It is a hole in a family, a line at a food pantry that never ends.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The police have the tools. They can ticket speeders, crack down on reckless drivers, and target the corners where people die. They can act now. But action is not a press release. Action is a summons in a driver’s hand, a patrol car at the intersection where the last person died. “These two deaths are as horrifying as they were preventable,” said Ben Furnas. The only thing missing is will.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Every day without action is another day the street takes someone else. The numbers will not stop on their own. Only people can do that.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 67?
      Cars and Trucks: 4 deaths, 7 serious injuries from sedans and SUVs; 2 deaths, 2 serious injuries from trucks and buses. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries. Most deaths come from cars and trucks. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 67 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield. They can respond to dangerous conditions and patrol intersections where people have died. The tools are there. Only action is missing.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The same streets see the same kinds of crashes, year after year. Most deaths and injuries here are preventable. As one advocate said, “These two deaths are as horrifying as they were preventable” (Streetsblog NYC).
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can pass laws to lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and demand enforcement. They can push for more speed cameras and hold agencies accountable for inaction. They can listen to families who have lost loved ones and act before the next death.

      NYC DOT Targets Intersection Danger Zones
      ▸

      City crews will block cars from corners. Barriers, granite, planters—hard lines drawn. Sightlines open. Pedestrians and cyclists step forward, visible. Intersections, once blind, now clear. The city moves to cut the toll where most blood spills.

      amNY reported on June 11, 2025, that the NYC Department of Transportation will redesign intersections to protect pedestrians and cyclists. The plan uses 'hardened daylighting'—physical barriers, granite blocks, and planters—to stop vehicles from parking near corners. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety.' Nearly half of city traffic deaths happen at intersections. The new design will first appear at high-crash sites in Brooklyn, including Ocean Avenue at Church Avenue and several others. The move highlights a systemic effort to address visibility failures and reduce deadly conflicts at crossings.

      Flatbush Avenue Bus Lane Plan Advances
      ▸

      City eyes a center bus lane on Flatbush. Concrete islands promise safer crossings. Details remain thin. Cars may still block buses. The street could change. Pedestrians and riders wait. The city holds its breath.

      Streetsblog NYC (2025-06-09) reports the Department of Transportation plans a center-running bus lane on Flatbush Avenue, Brooklyn. The project aims to connect neighborhoods and calm traffic. DOT Assistant Director Dustin Khuu said the goal is a 'high performing transit priority street.' The plan includes concrete bus boarding islands and may reduce car lanes, giving more space to pedestrians and buses. However, the article notes gaps: 'DOT didn't share a block-by-block breakdown,' and curbside parking may remain, risking bus lane obstruction by double-parked vehicles. The city may physically protect the lane, but details are pending. The B41 bus, serving 28,000 daily trips, crawls at 4 mph during rush hour. The proposal highlights the need for clear enforcement and design to keep cars out of bus lanes and protect vulnerable road users.
