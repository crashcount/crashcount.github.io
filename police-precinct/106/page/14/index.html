      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      South Conduit, 131st: a rider down, a truck rolling on

      Precinct 106: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 21, 2025

      Just after midday on Sep 6, at South Conduit Ave and 131 St, a man on a moped was badly hurt in a crash with a truck. Police recorded improper passing and an improper turn in the report (CrashID 4840626).

      This Week

      • Sep 6: At 122 St and 109 Ave, a collision involving two SUVs, a motorcycle, and a person on a bike left a 28-year-old driver unconscious; police noted alcohol and failure to yield (CrashID 4840433).
      • Aug 27: At 97 St and Rockaway Blvd, a driver hit a 10-year-old walking at an intersection; police cited driver inexperience and following too closely (CrashID 4839983).
      • Aug 25: At 109 Ave and 107 St, a driver turning right in an SUV hit a 21-year-old woman crossing with the signal; police recorded failure to yield by the driver (CrashID 4840619).

      The count keeps rising

      Since Jan 1, 2022, Precinct 106 has seen 22 deaths and 3,978 injuries in 5,839 crashes (NYC Open Data). People walking account for 7 deaths and 528 injuries; people biking, 1 death and 149 injuries. Car and SUV drivers inflict most of the harm to people on foot here, with trucks and buses also in the toll (mode and totals from precinct rollup in the same dataset).

      This year, deaths have outpaced last year at this point: 8 so far in 2025 versus 2 last year. Injuries are also higher year to date, 832 vs 759 (year-to-date tallies derived from the same source). The pattern is steady and local.

      Corners we know, losses we can name

      A 73-year-old woman was killed in a left-turning bus crash at 86 St and 107 Ave; police recorded failure to yield by the bus driver (CrashID 4677970). A 23-year-old man was killed on 101 St near 103rd Ave; police recorded unsafe speed on a 2021 sedan (CrashID 4832080). A 63-year-old woman was killed crossing on 84 St; the striking driver was in an SUV (CrashID 4585750).

      Officials also logged a fatal hit-and-run on the Nassau Expressway work zone in South Ozone Park; a 44-year-old woman directing traffic was struck and killed. Police announced an arrest (amNY, NY Daily News).

      North Conduit Ave and the Belt Parkway pop up again and again in the precinct’s crash logs. The Belt accounts for the most injuries and five recorded deaths; North Conduit follows with two deaths and heavy injury counts (hotspot tallies from the precinct’s top-intersections rollup in NYC Open Data).

      Nights are deadly; turns are too

      Deaths cluster after dark. Multiple fatalities land between 8 PM and midnight, with more into the late-night hours (hourly distribution from the precinct rollup). Turning into people is a repeated pattern in reports, often with police recording failure to yield by the driver. Speed shows up in the deadliest cases.

      Do what works, where it’s worst

      Start at the hot spots. Harden turns and add leading pedestrian intervals on Liberty Ave, Lefferts Blvd, and North Conduit’s crossings. Daylight corners and add refuge islands on Rockaway Blvd. Target late-night enforcement at North Conduit and Belt Parkway access, where the deaths and injuries stack up (locations and timing from NYC Open Data precinct rollups).

      Citywide tools can backstop the local fixes. The Stop Super Speeders Act would require drivers with repeat violations to install speed limiters that keep them from speeding, using either 11 DMV points in 18 months or 16 camera tickets in a year as the trigger (details from our briefing).

      Who moves first?

      This is Precinct 106. Your officials are Council Member Joann Ariola, Assembly Member Stacey Pheffer Amato, and State Senator James Sanders. Will they back a precinct-wide push on hardened turns, night enforcement at the Belt and North Conduit, and real consequences for repeat speeders? The bill is named. The corners are known.

      One woman was killed doing her job on the shoulder of the Nassau Expressway. A child was hit crossing Rockaway. The next fix is not abstract. It’s a signal, a curb, a turn that doesn’t sweep.

      Take one action today. Tell your officials to pass the speed limiter bill and lower speeds where you live. Start here.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where is Precinct 106?
      Precinct 106 covers South Ozone Park, Ozone Park, Howard Beach–Lindenwood, and Spring Creek Park in Queens. It overlaps City Council District 32, Assembly District 23, and State Senate District 10.
      ▸ What’s happened here since 2022?
      From Jan 1, 2022 to Sep 21, 2025, Precinct 106 recorded 5,839 crashes, 22 deaths, and 3,978 injuries. People walking account for 7 deaths and 528 injuries; people biking for 1 death and 149 injuries. Source: NYC Open Data (Motor Vehicle Collisions – Crashes).
      ▸ What are the worst trouble spots?
      The Belt Parkway and North Conduit Avenue show the heaviest toll in the precinct’s crash rollups. Liberty Avenue and Lefferts Boulevard also rank high. Targeted fixes should start there. Source: NYC Open Data (top-intersections rollup for Precinct 106).
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4). We filtered for Precinct 106 and dates from 2022-01-01 to 2025-09-21, then tallied crashes, injuries, and deaths. You can run a filtered query here and explore linked Persons and Vehicles tables for injury severity and involved modes. Data last accessed Sep 21, 2025.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
