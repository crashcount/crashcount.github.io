      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 106?

      Twelve Dead, Still No Action: Drop the Speed Limit Now

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations.)

      Precinct 106: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Toll This Year

      Twelve dead. Forty seriously hurt. In the last twelve months, 830 people have been injured on the streets of Precinct 106. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about age or time of day. A mother, a child, a man on his way to work. The road takes them all.

      Just days ago, a BMW tore across the Belt Parkway, struck the divider, and vaulted into oncoming traffic. Six people were left broken, two fighting for their lives. Police said, “The driver then struck a gray 2016 CR-V, as well as a red 2022 Hyundai, which were traveling westbound.” ABC7. The NYPD closed the road and sent in their Collision Investigation Squad. “It was unclear what caused the driver to lose control but police said speed may have been an issue.” New York Post.

      Every week, the list grows. A 25-year-old killed on the Belt Parkway in April. A 51-year-old woman struck and killed while walking near Lefferts Boulevard in December. A moped driver, ejected and dead, on Nassau Expressway last fall. The stories change, but the ending is the same.

      Who Pays the Price

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. They killed three people and injured hundreds more. Trucks and buses followed. Motorcycles and mopeds, too, left bodies in the street. No helmet or crosswalk can stop a speeding car. The dead do not get a second chance.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has new laws. Speed cameras now run day and night. The law allows lower speed limits. But the default is still 25 mph. The city could drop it to 20 mph today. They have not. The police have the power to enforce the law. They can ticket speeders, crack down on reckless drivers, and target the places where people keep getting hurt. They just need to act.

      The Call

      Twelve dead is twelve too many. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell the 106th Precinct to enforce the law. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 106 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 32, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 106?
      It includes the South Ozone Park, Ozone Park, Howard Beach-Lindenwood, Spring Creek Park, and Queens CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 28 and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, and AD 31, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 15, and SD 19.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 106?
      Cars and SUVs: 3 deaths, 291 minor injuries, 55 moderate injuries, 8 serious injuries (total 357 incidents). Trucks and Buses: 1 death, 18 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 4 serious injuries (total 27 incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 3 minor injuries, 2 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries (total 8 incidents). Bikes: 0 deaths, 1 minor injury, 1 moderate injury, 0 serious injuries (total 2 incidents). NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 106 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure-to-yield, and respond to dangerous street conditions. The NYPD’s Collision Investigation Squad is called in after the worst crashes, but the real work is stopping them before they happen.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, reckless driving, and unsafe streets can be changed. Every death is a failure of policy and enforcement, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and demand police enforcement. They can push for a 20 mph default limit and make sure laws protect people, not just cars.
