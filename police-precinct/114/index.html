      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 114?

      Blood on the Crosswalk: Demand Action Before the Next Name Is Yours

      Written June 26, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Precinct 114: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      A cyclist is struck at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She is thrown from her bike, slams into a parked car, and dies. Her name is Amanda Servedio. She was coming home from a cycling event. The driver was fleeing police. He did not stop. A witness said, “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.” NY Daily News

      A few blocks away, a car jumps the curb outside a school. A 7-year-old girl is left with a broken femur and a head wound. A 14-year-old and a 58-year-old man are also hit. The driver did not have a license. New York Post

      In the last twelve months, 3 people have died and 786 have been injured in crashes in Precinct 114. Five were seriously hurt. One was a child. The dead do not get a second chance.

      The Usual Suspects

      Cars and SUVs did most of the harm. In three years, they killed two people and injured hundreds more. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes all played their part. But the numbers are clear: the bigger the vehicle, the greater the damage.

      The streets are not safe for the young or the old. Not for the cyclist, not for the child walking home from school. Not for anyone who steps off the curb.

      Leadership: Words and Silence

      The police have the tools. They can ticket speeders, stop reckless drivers, and watch the corners where people die. They can act. But action is not the same as presence.

      After Amanda Servedio was killed, her father criticized the police chase: “It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened.”

      The District Attorney called it out: “The defendant allegedly led police on a 10-minute chase through the crowded streets of Long Island City and Astoria to avoid responsibility for his actions.”

      The Next Move Is Yours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made upstream. The police can enforce the law. The council can pass bills. The mayor can set the tone. But they move only when pushed.

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand speed enforcement, safe crossings, and real protection for the people who walk and ride.

      Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Half Of NYC Taxis Now Wheelchair Accessible
      ▸

      Half the city’s taxis now take wheelchairs. Ten years of pressure forced change. The fleet stands as the most accessible in the nation. Policy, not luck, drove this. Disabled riders gain ground. Streets shift. Access grows.

      amNY reported on June 5, 2025, that New York City’s Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) reached a milestone: 50% of active taxis are now wheelchair accessible. TLC Commissioner David Do said, “We made that today. It is the most accessible fleet in the entire country.” This achievement follows a decade-long push by disability advocates and a 2013 settlement requiring half the medallion fleet to be accessible. The transition faced delays, including the COVID-19 pandemic and medallion owner financial struggles. Policy changes, such as alternating accessible vehicles and funding support, drove the shift. The article highlights systemic progress, not individual driver actions, and underscores the impact of sustained advocacy and regulatory pressure.

      Queensboro Bridge Splits Paths For Safety
      ▸

      Cyclists and walkers get space at last. Years of crowding end. The bridge’s narrow lane forced conflict. Now, riders and pedestrians move apart. The city takes a lane from cars. The danger shrinks. The span breathes easier.

      amNY reported on May 13, 2025, that New York City will separate cyclists and pedestrians on the Ed Koch-Queensboro Bridge. Starting May 18, the north outer roadway becomes bike-only, while the south outer roadway, once for vehicles, opens to pedestrians. The article notes, “Advocates have fought to open a separate pedestrian path, citing dangerous overcrowding that has led to conflicts among cyclists, pedestrians and micromobility users.” The bridge was the last city-owned East River crossing without split paths. Manhattan Community Board 6 urged the city to act, pressing DOT to open the path despite construction delays. The change doubles space for non-drivers and removes a vehicle lane, addressing years of systemic risk from forced mixing of vulnerable users.
