      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 114?

      Astoria Bleeds While Leaders Look Away

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Precinct 114: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Precinct 114, the street is a wound that never closes. In the last twelve months, three people died in crashes here. 774 were injured. Five suffered injuries so severe they will never be the same. The numbers do not heal. They only grow.

      Just last fall, a cyclist named Amanda Servedio was riding home in Astoria. A pickup, chased by police, ran her down at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She flew thirty feet. She did not scream. “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” a witness told the Daily News. Servedio was 36. She was coming back from a cycling event. She had the right of way. The driver sped off. The police stopped to help her, but it was too late.

      Last week, two people on a moped collided with a BMW in Jamaica. Both were hurt. The NYPD said only, “two people were riding the moped when it collided with a BMW” reported CBS New York. No word on charges. No word on why.

      The Role of Police and Local Leaders

      Precinct 114 has the power to act. Police can enforce speed limits. They can ticket drivers who fail to yield. They can target the corners where blood stains the curb. But they must see the problem first. They must choose to act.

      Local leaders have tools. The city can lower speed limits. The council can demand safer street designs. But action is slow. The dead do not wait.

      The Next Step Is Ours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made somewhere—by a driver, by a city, by a leader who looked away. The numbers are not just numbers. They are names. They are families left with empty rooms.

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets.

      The street remembers every name we forget.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 114 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 34 and state senate district SD 11.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 114?
      It includes the North & South Brother Islands, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 114?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians here were caused by Cars and Trucks (2 deaths, 398 total incidents), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 27 incidents), and Bikes (no deaths, 15 incidents). NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 114 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. Police have the tools—they just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable, or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not random. They happen where streets are dangerous and laws go unenforced. Better design, lower speeds, and real enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, demand safer street designs, and push for stronger enforcement. They can hold agencies accountable for every death and injury on our streets.
