      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 114?

      Blood on Their Hands: NYC Streets Still Kill

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed link formatting, tightened prose, deduped and sorted citations.)

      Precinct 114: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      Fourteen dead. Twenty-one left with injuries so grave they change a life. In just over three years, this is the cost in Precinct 114. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the sound of sirens at 2 a.m., the blood on the crosswalk, the shoes left behind.

      Just last week, a 39-year-old moped rider was killed at 23rd Avenue and 37th Street. He was thrown from his seat, helmet on, but it did not matter. The crash report lists “Traffic Control Disregarded” and “Driver Inattention/Distraction.” The man died at the scene. The street was quiet again by dawn (NYC crash data).

      A year ago, a 17-year-old boy died as a passenger in a sedan on Astoria Boulevard. No helmet, no chance. The car was one of three tangled in a police pursuit. The city moved on. The family did not (NYC crash data).

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars are the main threat. In this precinct, they have killed two, seriously injured four, and left more than 400 hurt. Trucks and buses have injured dozens more. Motorcycles and mopeds, often unlicensed, add to the toll. Bikes injure, but they do not kill. The street is a battlefield, and the dead are almost always on foot, on two wheels, or riding as passengers.

      The Leaders and the Law

      Local leaders have tools. They have laws. They have the power to act. The police can enforce speed limits, ticket reckless drivers, and target crash hotspots. But the work is not done. The city has lowered some speed limits and expanded speed cameras, but the carnage continues. The numbers do not lie.

      After a bus crash in Queens, a shaken passenger recalled, “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us.” Another crash survivor, thrown from a burning BMW, remembered, “They went airborne and into the barrier.”

      Precinct 114 can do more. The police can crack down on speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving. They can make it clear that every death is one too many.

      Call to Action: Demand Action, Demand Life

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Every day of delay is another family broken. The blood is still fresh. Do not let it dry.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 114 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 34 and state senate district SD 11. (See NYC crash data)
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 114?
      It includes the North & South Brother Islands, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59. (See NYC crash data)
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 114?
      Cars and Trucks: 2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 409 total crashes. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 serious injury, 27 total crashes. Bikes: 1 serious injury, 15 total crashes. Cars and trucks are the main killers. (See NYC crash data)
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 114 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and issue failure-to-yield tickets. They can target known crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. The police have the tools—they just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These deaths and injuries are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by policymakers, by those who enforce or fail to enforce the law. Every crash is preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand automated enforcement, and demand the police prioritize traffic safety. They can listen to advocates and act before the next death.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
