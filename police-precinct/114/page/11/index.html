      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 114?

      Astoria Streets Kill: Precinct 114

      Written August 16, 2025 (Revised August 16, 2025 – Finisher pass: tightened prose, normalized dates, checked figures, added local analysis and citywide policy demands.)

      Precinct 114: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 16, 2025

      2025-08-12 — Three people died at 19th Avenue and 42nd Street in Astoria. Two pedestrians and the 84‑year‑old driver were killed when a car crashed into a food cart and nearby people. An eyewitness said, “I have never seen anything like this.” (amNY: https://www.amny.com/new-york/queens/carnage-queens-senior-driver-stroke-crash/)

      2024-10-22 — A cyclist was killed on 34th Avenue at 37th Street at night, according to city crash records. (NYC Open Data: https://data.cityofnewyork.us/Public-Safety/Motor-Vehicle-Collisions-Crashes/h9gi-nx95)

      The count is grim. Since 2022 in Precinct 114 there have been 19 deaths, 2,978 injuries and 21 serious injuries. (NYC Open Data: https://data.cityofnewyork.us/Public-Safety/Motor-Vehicle-Collisions-Crashes/h9gi-nx95)

      Hotspots repeat. Northern Boulevard and 34th Avenue show up in the data again and again. 42nd Street is a recurring danger. Crashes spike around 08:00, in mid‑afternoon, at the 17:00 rush and late at night. That pattern points to daylighting, lighting gaps and night enforcement failures.

      The data show common contributing factors such as failure to yield and inattention/distraction, with unsafe speed noted in many reports. Cars and SUVs cause the bulk of pedestrian injuries here; trucks and buses show up less often but still create life‑threatening crashes.

      Fixes that work and can be done locally: daylight unsighted corners and remove parking that blocks sight lines; install leading pedestrian intervals (LPIs) at turn‑conflict intersections; harden left‑turn geometry with protected turn lanes and centerline separation on 34th Avenue and Northern Boulevard; run sustained, targeted speeding details and night patrols during the deadly hours.

      Queens prosecutors showed what accountability can sound like. “Joseph Lee terrorized other drivers as he purposefully drove the wrong way on a busy Queens highway and crashed into multiple cars,” Queens District Attorney Melinda Katz said after a sentencing this month. (amNY: https://qns.com/2025/08/bayside-man-sentenced-injuring-drivers-clearview-expressway/)

      The 114th Precinct can act today: sustained speeding enforcement, focused failure‑to‑yield enforcement at 34th Avenue, Northern Boulevard and 42nd Street, and directed patrols during the lethal hours. When officers flag hazards, document them and push DOT for fixes.

      Citywide politics matter. Precinct 114’s patterns demand city answers: lower New York City’s default speed limit to 20 mph and require speed‑limiting technology for habitual speeders. Sammy’s Law gives the city power to set safer limits; state action to force intelligent speed assistance for repeat offenders would lock in protections. (Take Action: /take_action/)

      Bold promises won’t stop a truck in a crosswalk. Lower speeds. Enforce turns. Protect people. Do it now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 114 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 34 and state senate district SD 11.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 114?
      It includes the North & South Brother Islands, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 114?
      Cars and Trucks: 5 pedestrian deaths and 484 total pedestrian injuries (data). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths and 31 injuries (data). Bikes: 0 deaths and 16 injuries (data).
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 114 can run sustained speeding details, write failure‑to‑yield summonses at 34th Avenue, Northern Boulevard, and 42nd Street, and deploy during the lethal hours (around 8 a.m., mid‑afternoon, 5 p.m., and late night). Respond fast to flagged hazards and push fixes to DOT. Queens DA Melinda Katz’s words after a wrong‑way case — “terrorized other drivers” — show why focus on dangerous driving must be relentless (amNY).
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just “accidents”?
      They are preventable. The same corridors and hours repeat. Target the hotspots, slow the turns, and enforce the yields. The toll is measurable here: 19 killed, 2,978 injured, 21 seriously hurt (data).
      ▸ What can local politicians do right now?
      Lower the citywide default speed to 20 mph, require speed limiters for repeat speeders, and fund daylighting, LPIs, and hardened turns on Northern Boulevard and 34th Avenue. Then hold monthly progress briefings with the 114th. Start here: Take Action.
