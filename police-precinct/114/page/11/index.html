      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 114?

      No More Excuses: Streets Are Killing Us

      Written June 27, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, tightened prose, and cleaned citations.)

      Precinct 114: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      Three people are dead. Five more have serious injuries. In the last twelve months, 779 people have been hurt in 1,383 crashes across Precinct 114. The dead include a child. The injured are young, old, and everywhere in between. No one is spared.

      Amanda Servedio, 36, was riding home from a cycling event. She never made it. A man fleeing police in a pickup truck ran her down at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She was thrown from her bike into a parked car. A witness said, “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force” (NY Daily News). Her father, Frank Servedio, said, “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened” (NY Daily News).

      SUVs, sedans, trucks, bikes, and mopeds all play their part. But the biggest numbers belong to cars and SUVs: 397 crashes, 2 deaths, and 4 serious injuries. Trucks and buses: 30 crashes, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 27 crashes, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 15 crashes, 1 serious injury. The street is a gauntlet.

      Leadership: Action or Excuses?

      The police have the tools. They can enforce speed limits. They can ticket reckless drivers. They can target the corners where people die. But too often, the carnage continues. The chase that killed Amanda Servedio was not the first. It will not be the last unless something changes.

      Local leaders have the power to demand more. They can push for lower speed limits, more enforcement, and safer street design. They can reward officers who crack down on reckless driving. They can demand that every crash is treated as a preventable tragedy, not a cost of doing business.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Precinct 114 is bleeding. The numbers are not just numbers. They are people. They are families who will never be whole again. The police and local leaders must act. Residents must demand it.

      Call your Council Member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 114 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 34 and state senate district SD 11.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 114?
      It includes the North & South Brother Islands, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 114?
      Cars and SUVs: 397 crashes, 2 deaths, 4 serious injuries. Trucks and buses: 30 crashes, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 27 crashes, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 15 crashes, 1 serious injury. Data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 114 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield. They can respond to dangerous conditions before someone gets hurt. The tools exist. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, reckless driving, and failure to yield can be stopped with enforcement and better street design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can push for lower speed limits, demand more enforcement, and fund safer street designs. They can hold police and city agencies accountable for every death and injury.
