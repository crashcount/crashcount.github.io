      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 114?

      Blood in Astoria: Policy Killed Amanda, Not Just a Driver

      Written June 25, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Precinct 114: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      A cyclist rides home from a community event. A pickup, chased by police, barrels through Astoria. The cyclist is struck, thrown thirty feet, and dies in the street. Her name was Amanda Servedio. She had the right of way. The man behind the wheel was running from the law, his license plate taped over. He did not stop. Police stopped the chase to help her, but it was too late. Her father said, “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened” NY Daily News.

      A few blocks away, an unlicensed driver reverses into a box truck, spins onto the sidewalk, and hits two children and a man outside a school. A seven-year-old girl is left with a broken femur and a head injury. The driver is arrested. The street is quiet again, but the blood on the sidewalk does not wash away New York Post.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      In the last twelve months, Precinct 114 saw 1,384 crashes. Three people died. Five suffered serious injuries. Children were not spared: one killed, 58 injured. Cyclists and pedestrians bear the brunt. Cars and SUVs caused the most harm: 2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 390 more hurt. Motorcycles, mopeds, and trucks add to the toll NYC Open Data.

      The Leaders and the Law

      Local leaders have tools. They can lower speed limits, redesign streets, and enforce the law. They can target the worst drivers. They can crack down on speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving. They can protect children outside schools. But action is slow. “We wanted him to be arrested and we want him to serve time,” said Amanda Servedio’s father NY Daily News. The dead cannot wait for another meeting.

      The Call

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand speed enforcement, safer crossings, and real protection for the most vulnerable. Every day of silence is another day of blood on the street.
