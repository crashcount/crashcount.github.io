      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 114?

      Blood on the Crosswalk: No More Excuses for Deadly Streets

      Written June 25, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 114: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Toll in Plain Sight

      A cyclist lies dead on 37th Street. Amanda Servedio, age 36, hit by a fleeing pickup. She was coming home from a ride. The truck did not stop. She “went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. The police ended their chase to help her. She died at Elmhurst Hospital.

      A few blocks away, a seven-year-old girl is struck outside her school. A car jumps the curb. Her femur snaps. Her head bleeds. The driver is unlicensed. “An out-of-control unlicensed driver rammed into two kids and one adult,” reported the press.

      In the last twelve months, three people have died in Precinct 114. 784 have been injured. Five were hurt so badly they may never walk the same. Children, teens, the old, the young. The street does not care.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. They killed two. They left 281 with minor injuries, 109 with moderate wounds, four with injuries called “serious” by the city. Trucks and buses hurt 30 more. Motorcycles and mopeds, 27. Bikes, 15. The numbers are cold. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Action or Excuse?

      The police have the tools. They can ticket speeders. They can crack down on drivers who fail to yield. They can target the corners where blood stains the curb. But the silence is thick. The father of Amanda Servedio said, “It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened” he told the Daily News.

      Local leaders have the power to demand more. They can push for lower speed limits. They can fight for safer crossings, for cameras, for real enforcement. They can stand with the families who bury their own. Or they can look away.

      Call to Action: Make Them See

      Do not wait for another body on the street. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand speed enforcement. Demand tickets for reckless drivers. Demand action at every crash hotspot. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 114 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 34 and state senate district SD 11.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 114?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 114?
      Cars and SUVs: 2 deaths, 281 minor injuries, 109 moderate injuries, 4 serious injuries (total 396 incidents). Trucks and Buses: 30 incidents, no deaths, 20 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 27 incidents, no deaths, 18 minor injuries, 9 moderate injuries. Bikes: 15 incidents, no deaths, 10 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 114 can enforce speed limits, issue tickets for reckless driving and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. They can respond to dangerous conditions and act before the next crash. The tools are there. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or are they just 'accidents'?
      These are not random acts. Speeding, reckless driving, and failure to yield can be stopped. Enforcement and safer street design save lives. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, push for more enforcement, demand safer crossings, and support speed cameras. They can listen to families and act before another life is lost.
