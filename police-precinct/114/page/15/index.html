      Blood on Astoria Asphalt: Demand Action Before Another Life Is Lost

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 114: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      In Precinct 114, the numbers do not lie. Fourteen people have died since 2022. Twenty-one suffered serious injuries. The wounded number in the thousands. Each number is a life changed or ended. Each crash is a story that does not end well.

      Just last month, a 17-year-old boy died in the front seat of a sedan on Astoria Boulevard. The crash report lists “unsafe speed” and “traffic control disregarded.” The car was being chased by police. The boy never made it home. NYC Open Data

      A 36-year-old woman on a bike was killed at 34th Avenue and 37th Street. A pickup truck going straight. A life ended in the crosswalk. No helmet could save her. No law of physics could stop the force of a truck.

      Buses, Bikes, and the Unforgiving Road

      The violence does not spare the young. A 15-year-old moped rider was killed by a bus on 43rd Street. The record says “crush injuries.” No further detail. The pain is left to the family.

      Buses jump curbs. Eight people were hurt when an MTA bus slammed into a pole in Flushing. One rider said, “I have a baby with me. That would be scary. I’ll be more cautious of my surroundings.” Another said, “It must be very devastating for the people that were on the bus.”

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      The city has lowered speed limits in some places. Cameras catch speeders, but only where they are installed. The police have the power to enforce the law. They can ticket for speeding, failure to yield, reckless driving. They can target the corners where people keep getting hurt. They just need to act.

      Local leaders have tools. They can push for more speed cameras, safer street designs, and real accountability for repeat offenders. But every delay is another name on a report, another family left with empty arms.

      Call to Action: Demand Relentless Enforcement and Real Change

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand they use every tool—speed enforcement, failure-to-yield tickets, and real crackdowns on dangerous drivers. Do not wait for another child’s name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 114 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 34 and state senate district SD 11.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 114?
      It includes the North & South Brother Islands, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 114?
      Cars and Trucks: 2 deaths, 291 minor injuries, 113 moderate injuries, 4 serious injuries (total 410 incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 18 minor injuries, 9 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries (total 27 incidents). Bikes: 0 deaths, 10 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury (total 15 incidents). NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 114 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. Police have the tools—they just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. They are the result of choices—speed, design, enforcement. Every crash that kills or injures is a policy failure, not an act of God.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed and red-light camera programs, redesign dangerous streets, and push for real accountability for repeat offenders. They can demand enforcement and fund safer infrastructure.
