      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 114?

      No More Dead Kids: Slow the Streets, Save a Life

      Written June 24, 2025

      Precinct 114: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 24, 2025

      Blood on the Streets

      A cyclist goes airborne on 34th Avenue. She lands hard against a parked car. The driver does not stop. The police, chasing him through the neighborhood, pull up to help her. She dies at Elmhurst Hospital. Her name was Amanda Servedio. Her father said they were very upset that police didn’t stop the chase. He meant the chase. He meant the street. He meant the loss.

      A few blocks away, a seven-year-old girl is walking near her school. An unlicensed driver reverses, hits a truck, then spins onto the sidewalk. The girl’s femur snaps. Her head hits the pavement. Two others are hurt. The driver is charged with reckless endangerment, reckless driving, and driving without a license. Police charged the driver.

      In the last twelve months, Precinct 114 saw 3 deaths and 791 injuries from crashes. Five people were seriously hurt. Children, cyclists, and pedestrians are the first to bleed.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has new tools. Speed cameras run all day. The law now allows 20 mph limits on local streets. But the work is slow. Streets stay wide. Cars stay fast. Drivers with no license keep driving. The police can enforce the law. They can ticket speeders, reckless drivers, and those who fail to yield. They can target crash hotspots. They just need to act.

      Amanda’s father said it was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase in a residential neighborhood.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Every day, the blood dries and the traffic moves on. The city has the power to slow the cars. The precinct has the power to enforce the law. You have the power to demand it.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call Precinct 114. Tell them: No more waiting. No more empty promises. Slow the cars. Enforce the law. Protect the people who walk and ride.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 114 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens. It overlaps District 22 and District 26 in the City Council, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 114?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 114?
      Cars and Trucks: 2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 390 total pedestrian injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, 27 total pedestrian injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, 15 total pedestrian injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 114 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield. They can target known crash hotspots. Police have the tools—they just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not fate. They happen where streets are wide, cars are fast, and laws go unenforced. Every injury and death can be prevented with slower speeds, better design, and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can push for lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, and demand police enforcement. They can hold agencies accountable and make sure laws protect people, not just cars.
