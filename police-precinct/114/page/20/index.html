      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 114?

      No More Body Bags: Make Queens Streets Safe Now

      Written July 3, 2025

      Precinct 114: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 3, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Precinct 114, the street does not forgive. In the last twelve months, 1 person died and 354 were injured in traffic crashes. Three suffered injuries so grave they may never walk the same. These are not just numbers—they are bodies on the pavement, families waiting in hospital halls.

      Last fall, a cyclist named Amanda Servedio was hit and killed at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She was returning from a ride. The driver was fleeing police. A witness said, “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.” (NY Daily News). Her father added, “It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened.” (NY Daily News).

      Just days ago, two people on a moped collided with a BMW in Jamaica. Both were hurt. The NYPD said, “two people were riding the moped when it collided with a BMW.” (CBS New York).

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Since 2022, 13 people have died and 2,681 have been injured on these streets. Pedestrians and cyclists pay the highest price. Cars and SUVs caused most of the harm, but trucks, motorcycles, and bikes all left their mark. The violence is steady, not sudden. It is a slow bleed.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      Local leaders have the tools. They can demand lower speed limits, daylight corners, and redesign deadly crossings. They can push the precinct to enforce speed and failure-to-yield laws. They can speak up or stay silent. The police can target crash hotspots, ticket reckless drivers, and protect the most vulnerable. Every week without action is another week of risk.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      Contact your council member, your precinct, and the mayor. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Demand action before another name becomes a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 114 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 34 and state senate district SD 11.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 114?
      It includes the North & South Brother Islands, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 114?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians, with 2 deaths, 282 minor injuries, 111 moderate injuries, and 4 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 20 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries, and 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 18 minor injuries, 9 moderate injuries, and 1 serious injury. Bikes caused 10 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, and 1 serious injury. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 114 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue speeding and failure-to-yield tickets, and target known crash hotspots. The police have the tools—they just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving are choices. Safer street design and enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can push for lower speed limits, safer street designs, and stronger enforcement. They can demand the precinct act on crash data and protect people walking and biking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
