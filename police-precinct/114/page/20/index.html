      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 114?

      Thirteen Dead, Thousands Hurt: Precinct 114 Bleeds While Leaders Stall

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Precinct 114: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Numbers Are People

      In Precinct 114, the toll is steady and brutal. Thirteen people killed. Twenty seriously hurt. Over 2,600 injured. That’s since 2022. Each number is a body on the street, a family left waiting. In the last year alone, three more lives ended. One was a child.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      The blood hasn’t dried. Last October, a pickup fleeing police struck and killed Amanda Servedio, a cyclist returning from a ride. She had the right of way. A witness described the impact: “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.” Her father questioned the police pursuit: “It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened.”

      A few months before, a 15-year-old on a moped was killed at 43rd Street and 19th Avenue. A 17-year-old died as a passenger in a speeding car. The list goes on. Each crash is a wound that never closes.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has new tools. Sammy’s Law lets New York lower speed limits. Speed cameras now run 24/7. But in this precinct, the pace of change is slow. Streets remain wide, crossings long, corners blind. Police have the power to enforce speed limits, ticket reckless drivers, and target crash hotspots. They can act. Too often, they don’t.

      What Comes Next

      Precinct 114 is not helpless. The police can crack down on speeding and failure to yield. Local leaders can demand safer street designs, more daylighted corners, and real accountability for repeat offenders. Residents can call, write, and show up until the killing stops.

      Don’t wait for another name on the list. Demand action. Call your council member, your precinct, your mayor. Tell them: slow the cars, fix the streets, save a life.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 114 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 34 and state senate district SD 11.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 114?
      It includes the North & South Brother Islands, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 114?
      Cars and Trucks were involved in 397 pedestrian injuries and 2 deaths. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 27 injuries. Bikes were involved in 15 injuries. Cars and trucks remain the biggest threat to people on foot. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 114 police can enforce speed limits, ticket drivers who fail to yield, and target known crash hotspots. They can respond to complaints about dangerous intersections and reckless driving. The tools are there—speeding tickets, failure-to-yield enforcement, and focused patrols. They just need to act.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street designs, and strong enforcement can stop the deaths. Calling them ‘accidents’ hides the truth: these are failures of policy and action.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can push for lower speed limits, demand street redesigns, and hold police accountable for traffic enforcement. They can fight for more daylighted corners, protected bike lanes, and real penalties for repeat dangerous drivers.
