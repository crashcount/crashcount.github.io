      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 105?

      Blood on the Belt: Speed Kills, Silence Compounds

      Written July 9, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Precinct 105: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      Death on the Parkway, Grief on the Streets

      A BMW hit the divider on the Belt Parkway. It vaulted into oncoming traffic. Flames followed. Two young lives ended—Noah Thompson, 24, and Jewel Perez, 22. Three others were pulled from the wreck. Two more drivers, just in the wrong place, survived. Police said, “No one in the doomed BMW was wearing a seat belt” NY Daily News. The crash closed the highway. The city counted the bodies. The families counted the cost.

      Just days before, a 39-year-old man died on Nashville Boulevard. A left turn, a distraction, a body ejected. A box truck killed a 29-year-old pedestrian on Hillside Avenue. He was crossing with the signal. The truck was making a left. The driver did not see him. He never made it across NYC Open Data.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Eleven dead. Over 2,000 injured. Seven people seriously hurt. In the last year alone, deaths doubled. Injuries rose by a third. The old, the young, the ones just trying to get home—none are spared. Cars and SUVs did most of the harm. Trucks killed. No bikes killed anyone here. The violence is steady, not sudden. It is a slow bleed.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city talks of Vision Zero. Speed limits drop on paper. Cameras blink on. But the carnage does not stop. The police have the tools. They can enforce speed limits. They can ticket reckless drivers. They can target the places where people keep dying. They just need to act.

      After the bus crash in Queens Village, a passenger described the moment: “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us.” The driver did nothing wrong. The street did not forgive.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day leaders wait, another family pays. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to lower the speed limit, enforce the law, and fix the streets. Demand action from Precinct 105. Do not wait for the next siren.

      Half Of NYC Taxis Now Wheelchair Accessible
      ▸

      Half the city’s taxis now take wheelchairs. Ten years of pressure forced change. The fleet stands as the most accessible in the nation. Policy, not luck, drove this. Disabled riders gain ground. Streets shift. Access grows.

      amNY reported on June 5, 2025, that New York City’s Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) reached a milestone: 50% of active taxis are now wheelchair accessible. TLC Commissioner David Do said, “We made that today. It is the most accessible fleet in the entire country.” This achievement follows a decade-long push by disability advocates and a 2013 settlement requiring half the medallion fleet to be accessible. The transition faced delays, including the COVID-19 pandemic and medallion owner financial struggles. Policy changes, such as alternating accessible vehicles and funding support, drove the shift. The article highlights systemic progress, not individual driver actions, and underscores the impact of sustained advocacy and regulatory pressure.
