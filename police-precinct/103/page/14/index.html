      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 103?

      Nine Dead, Thousands Hurt—When Will Queens Leaders Step Up?

      Written July 16, 2025

      Precinct 103: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Precinct 103, the numbers do not lie. Nine dead. Seventeen seriously hurt. More than 2,400 injured since 2022. The bodies are not numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. The pain does not fade. It lingers on the corners and crosswalks of Jamaica, South Jamaica, and Hollis.

      Just months ago, a 19-year-old on a moped was killed at 90th Avenue and 188th Street. A 63-year-old man died behind the wheel at 143rd Street and 90th Avenue. A 56-year-old pedestrian was struck and killed by an SUV on Hillside Avenue. An 18-year-old died, ejected from his scooter, after colliding with a bus on 168th Street. The list is long. The grief is longer.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs do most of the harm. They killed at least one person and injured nearly 500 pedestrians in this precinct alone. Trucks and buses injured dozens more. Motorcycles and mopeds left bodies in the street—one dead, several ejected, many broken. Bikes caused injuries too, but the scale is not the same. The street is a gauntlet, and the most vulnerable pay the price.

      Leadership: Action and Silence

      The city has the tools. Speed cameras now run 24/7. The law allows lower speed limits. But the pace is slow. The dead do not wait for policy. The police can do more. They can enforce the law, crack down on speeding, failure to yield, reckless driving. They can target the corners where blood stains the curb. They just need to act.

      After a recent bus crash in Queens, a passenger said, “It must be very devastating for the people that were on the bus.” Another added, “I have a baby with me. That would be scary. I’ll be more cautious of my surroundings.” The fear is real. The danger is real. The response is not enough.

      The Call to Action

      This is not fate. This is failure. Demand more from your leaders. Call the precinct. Call your council member. Demand speed enforcement, safer crossings, and real protection for those outside the car. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 103 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 24, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 103?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, Hollis, and Queens CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 24, District 27, and District 28, Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 103?
      Cars and Trucks: 2 deaths, 491 injuries (including SUVs, sedans, trucks, buses). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 9 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 9 injuries.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 103 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and respond to dangerous conditions. The tools exist. They just need to act.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better enforcement, and safer street design save lives. Delay costs lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can push for lower speed limits, fund safer crossings, expand automated enforcement, and hold agencies accountable for action—not just words.
