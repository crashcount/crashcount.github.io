      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 103?

      Blood on the Asphalt: Queens Leaders Let the Streets Run Red

      Written July 13, 2025

      Precinct 103: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      In Precinct 103, the numbers do not lie. Nine people dead. Seventeen left with serious injuries. Over 2,400 hurt since 2022. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, parents, children. Some never made it home. Some never stood a chance.

      Last year, a 56-year-old man was killed by an SUV on Hillside Avenue. He was crossing, not at an intersection. The driver kept going straight. The man was left with a concussion, unconscious, and died soon after. No warning. No time to react. NYC Open Data

      In April, a 19-year-old on a motorcycle was struck and killed by an SUV at 90th Avenue and 188th Street. He was ejected, his body crushed. Helmet or not, the outcome was the same. The street did not forgive.

      Buses, Bikes, and the Unforgiving Road

      The violence is not just cars. Buses, mopeds, bikes—they all play a part. In August, an 18-year-old on a moped was killed when he collided with a bus on 89th Avenue and 168th Street. He was ejected, died from severe bleeding. The bus kept going straight. The moped did not.

      Last week, a Q20 bus in Queens jumped the curb, smashed a pole, and injured eight people. The driver, just 25, told investigators he “misjudged the curb.” But after reviewing the video, officials now believe he “had fallen asleep at the wheel.” The MTA pulled him from service. ABC7

      A passenger on the bus said, “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus.” ABC7

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      Local leaders have the tools. The city can lower speed limits. The precinct can enforce them. The police can crack down on reckless drivers, target hotspots, and issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield. But the carnage continues. Every delay is another body in the street.

      The numbers show who pays the price. Cars and SUVs caused the most harm—one death, five serious injuries, and nearly 500 crashes. Trucks and buses followed. Motorcycles and mopeds left a trail of ejections and broken bodies. Bikes were not spared, but their toll was smaller.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Every day you wait is another day someone does not come home.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 103 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 24, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 103?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, Hollis, and Queens CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 24, District 27, and District 28, Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 103?
      Cars and SUVs: 1 death, 5 serious injuries, 491 crashes. Trucks and Buses: 0 deaths, 2 serious injuries, 43 crashes. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, 9 crashes. Bikes: 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, 9 crashes.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 103 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue speeding and failure-to-yield tickets, and target known crash hotspots. The tools are there. The question is whether they will use them.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not fate. They are preventable. Lower speeds, better enforcement, and safer street design can save lives. Every delay means more loss.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, and demand enforcement. They can listen to residents and act before another life is lost.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
