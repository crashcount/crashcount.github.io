      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 14?

      Midtown Bleeds, City Stalls: How Many More Must Die?

      Written July 3, 2025 (Revised July 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      Precinct 14: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 3, 2025

      The Toll in Midtown: Lives Shattered, Streets Unchanged

      In Precinct 14, the numbers do not lie. Six people killed. Thirty-four left with serious injuries. Over 1,100 hurt since 2022. Each number is a body on the street, a family changed forever. The dead do not speak. The living limp home, if they make it at all.

      Just last year, a 23-year-old woman was killed riding her e-bike on West 36th Street. A 34-year-old man died crossing 7th Avenue. A box truck crushed a pedestrian on 9th Avenue this April. The pattern is the same: trucks, SUVs, and cars strike, and people fall. The sidewalk is no refuge. The crosswalk is no shield.

      Crashes Keep Coming, Leaders Talk

      The carnage does not slow. In the last twelve months, two more deaths, nine serious injuries, nearly 400 hurt. Buses collided at Port Authority just days ago, sending fourteen to the hospital. “Fourteen people are being treated for non-life-threatening injuries,” said authorities. The ramp was closed for hours. Commuters waited. The street did not change.

      The police can act. They can ticket speeders. They can crack down on drivers who fail to yield. They can target the corners where people keep getting hit. But action is rare. “Police said at the time no criminality was suspected,” after a woman was killed by a train at Union Square. The system shrugs. The bodies pile up.

      Promises and Pressure: What Will Change?

      City leaders have new tools. Sammy’s Law lets New York lower speed limits. Speed cameras are renewed. But the city moves slow. The council talks. The mayor promises. The dead wait for action.

      Precinct 14 can do more. The police know the hotspots. They know the hours when crashes spike. They have the power to enforce the law. They need to use it.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 14 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, assembly district AD 73 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 14?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, and District 4, Assembly Districts AD 73 and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 14?
      Cars and Trucks: 4 deaths, 173 minor injuries, 88 moderate injuries, 11 serious injuries (total 274 incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds: No deaths, 14 minor injuries, 10 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries (total 26 incidents). Bikes: No deaths, 36 minor injuries, 28 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries (total 66 incidents).
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 14 police can enforce speed limits, issue tickets for reckless driving and failure to yield, and target crash hotspots. They can respond quickly to dangerous conditions and focus on the places and times where crashes keep happening. The tools exist. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. They happen in the same places, for the same reasons—speed, failure to yield, dangerous streets. With enforcement and better design, most crashes can be prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and demand enforcement from police. They can push for more protected crossings, daylighting, and speed cameras. They can act now, or answer for the next death.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
