      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 94?

      Blood on Their Hands: Precinct 94 Fails the Vulnerable, Again

      Written July 19, 2025

      Precinct 94: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 19, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Precinct 94, the numbers do not lie. Nine people killed. Nineteen seriously injured. Over 1,100 hurt since 2022. The dead include a child crushed by a dump truck at an intersection, a man killed by an e-bike, a woman struck crossing with the light. The stories change. The outcome does not.

      Just this March, a 49-year-old man was killed by an e-bike on India Street. He bled out on the pavement. No warning, no time to react. Three weeks before, a child was killed by a truck turning left on Withers Street. The boy was crossing with the signal. The truck kept moving. The child did not.

      “Severe bleeding,” the report reads. “Crush injuries.” No comfort in the words. Only the end result. NYC crash data

      The Role of the Precinct

      The police in Precinct 94 have the power to act. They can enforce speed limits. They can ticket drivers who fail to yield. They can target the corners where people keep dying. They know where the bodies fall. They know who is most at risk.

      But the numbers do not budge. Four deaths in the last year. Eight serious injuries. The same corners, the same mistakes. The same silence.

      What Leaders Have Done—and What They Haven’t

      Local leaders have tools. The city has lowered speed limits in some places. Cameras catch some speeders. But the carnage continues. “As of Thursday, 60 pedestrians had been killed by vehicles across the city this year,” the Daily News reported.

      After a six-month investigation, police charged a driver who killed a 95-year-old woman with failure to yield and failure to exercise due care. “He was charged with two counts of failure to yield in a motor vehicle and failure to exercise due care,” said the NYPD.

      But most drivers walk away. Most families do not get justice. The city moves on. The street stays the same.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Precinct 94 can do more. Enforce the law. Crack down on speeding. Ticket failure to yield. Flood the hotspots. Make it clear: every life matters.

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand action. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Contact your leaders and demand change.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 94 sit politically?
      Precinct 94 belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 34, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 94?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, and Brooklyn CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33 and District 34, Assembly Districts AD 50 and AD 53, and State Senate Districts SD 18 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 94?
      Cars and Trucks: 3 deaths, 83 minor injuries, 31 moderate injuries (cars/SUVs); 3 deaths, 9 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries (trucks/buses). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 6 minor injuries, 2 moderate injuries. Bikes: 2 deaths, 4 minor injuries, 1 moderate injury, 1 serious injury. NYC crash data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 94 police can enforce speed limits, ticket drivers who fail to yield, and target known crash hotspots. They can respond to dangerous conditions and focus on violations that kill—speeding, reckless driving, failure to yield. The tools exist. The question is whether they will use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. People are killed and injured because of speeding, reckless driving, and failure to yield. These are choices, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can push for lower speed limits, more automated enforcement, safer street design, and stronger penalties for dangerous driving. They can demand action from police and city agencies. They can listen to residents and act before the next death.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
