      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 104?

      No More Names: End Traffic Deaths in the 104th Now

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      Precinct 104: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Slow Grind of Loss

      In Precinct 104, the numbers do not sleep. Twenty-one dead. Twenty-nine left with serious injuries. Over 2,500 wounded since 2022. The dead do not get a second chance. The injured carry it home.

      A cyclist, age 47, was crushed on Maurice Avenue. The cause: a truck driver failed to yield, distracted, did not see him. He died from head injuries, thrown from his bike. The record is cold: “Apparent Death” is all it says. The truck kept going straight. The cyclist did not. NYC Open Data

      A 59-year-old on a bike, struck by a turning box truck on Juniper Boulevard North. The record notes “Crush Injuries.” The record notes “Ejected.” The record notes “Apparent Death.” The record does not note the name. Only the loss.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and trucks did most of the harm. They killed and maimed, again and again. SUVs and sedans: 1 death, 4 serious injuries, 326 more hurt. Trucks and buses: 3 dead, 27 injured. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 dead, 12 injured. Bikes: 9 injured. The street is not a fair fight.

      The Leaders and the Law

      The law says slow down. The law says yield. The law says nothing to the dead. Local leaders have the tools. They can lower speed limits. They can redesign streets. They can demand enforcement. They can do more. They must do more.

      The police can act. They can ticket speeders. They can target the corners where people die. They can crack down on failure to yield. They can make the law mean something. But only if they see the problem. Only if they are told, again and again.

      The Words of Witnesses

      After a bus jumped the curb in Flushing, a rider described the chaos: “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus.”

      Another voice spoke to the aftermath: “It must be very devastating for the people that were on the bus.”

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell the 104th Precinct captain you want enforcement, not excuses. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for the next name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 104 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 32, assembly district AD 28 and state senate district SD 12.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 104?
      It includes the Maspeth, Ridgewood, Glendale, Middle Village, Mount Olivet & All Faiths Cemeteries, Middle Village Cemetery, St. John Cemetery, Highland Park-Cypress Hills Cemeteries (North), and Queens CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 30, District 32, and District 34, Assembly Districts AD 28, AD 30, AD 37, AD 38, and AD 39, and State Senate Districts SD 12, SD 15, and SD 18.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 104?
      SUVs and Sedans: 1 death, 4 serious injuries, 326 total harmed. Trucks and Buses: 3 dead, 27 injured. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 dead, 12 harmed. Bikes: 9 harmed. Cars and trucks do most of the killing and maiming. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 104 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield. They can respond to dangerous conditions before another person is killed. The tools are there. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by leaders, by those who design and enforce our streets. Every crash is a policy failure, not an act of God.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets, fund enforcement, and demand accountability from police and city agencies. They can listen to residents and act before the next death.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
