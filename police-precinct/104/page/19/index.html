      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 104?

      Death Is Routine Here—Until You Make It Stop

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 104: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Toll in Precinct 104

      The streets of Precinct 104 do not forgive. Since 2022, 21 people have died and 2,577 have been injured in crashes here. Twenty-nine suffered serious injuries. The dead include cyclists, pedestrians, and drivers. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie.

      In the last twelve months alone, five people died and 729 were injured. Children are not spared. Seventy were hurt. Two people in their thirties and two in their forties did not come home. The violence is steady, not sudden. It is a slow bleed.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      On April 19, a 54-year-old cyclist was killed by a turning box truck on Juniper Blvd North. He was crushed, ejected, and died at the scene. The driver survived. The data says nothing about the weather, the light, or the sound. Only that a man is dead and a truck kept going.NYC Open Data

      On February 22, a 47-year-old cyclist was killed on Maurice Avenue. An SUV and a box truck were involved. The cyclist was ejected and died from head injuries. The record is silent on blame. The loss is not.NYC Open Data

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have tools. They can lower speed limits, redesign streets, and enforce the law. The city has started to act. Speed cameras now run 24/7. The law allows for more red-light cameras. But the pace is slow. The violence is not.

      Police have the power to act. They can target speeding, reckless driving, and failure to yield. They can focus on crash hotspots. They can write tickets. They can save lives. They just need to know it matters.

      The Voices Left Behind

      A survivor of a recent crash described the horror: “Noah, Jewel and [the front passenger] flew out of the car (through the windshield). Jewel was already dead when she flew out the car. Noah was unconscious and [the front passenger] was in the grass not knowing what was going on.”

      After a bus crash, a passenger recalled, “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us.”

      These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by those who set the rules and those who enforce them.

      Call to Action: Make Them Act

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement at crash hotspots. Demand lower speed limits. Demand action, not words.

      Every day of delay is another life at risk. The blood on the street is not washed away by silence.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 104 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 32, assembly district AD 28 and state senate district SD 12.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 104?
      It includes the Maspeth, Ridgewood, Glendale, Middle Village, Mount Olivet & All Faiths Cemeteries, Middle Village Cemetery, St. John Cemetery, Highland Park-Cypress Hills Cemeteries (North), and Queens CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 30, District 32, and District 34, Assembly Districts AD 28, AD 30, AD 37, AD 38, and AD 39, and State Senate Districts SD 12, SD 15, and SD 18.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 104?
      Cars and Trucks: 1 death, 4 serious injuries, 326 other injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 0 serious injuries, 11 other injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 9 other injuries. Most deaths and serious injuries come from cars, SUVs, and trucks, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 104 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and issue failure-to-yield tickets. They can target known crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. Police have the power to act—they just need to know it matters.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speeding, failure to yield, dangerous turns. These are preventable. Every crash is a choice, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets, expand automated enforcement, and fund safety improvements. They can push for more cameras and demand police focus on the most dangerous violations.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
