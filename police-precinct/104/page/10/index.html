      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 104?

      Blood on 104’s Streets, Silence in City Hall

      Written July 14, 2025

      Precinct 104: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 14, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Precinct 104, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-one people killed. Twenty-nine left with serious injuries. In just the last twelve months, five more dead, eleven more lives changed forever. The bodies are not numbers. They are neighbors, children, parents. A cyclist crushed on Maurice Avenue. A motorcyclist dead on Jackie Robinson Parkway. A pedestrian struck and left with nothing but a name on a report. The street does not care who you are.

      The Latest Crashes: No End in Sight

      On April 19, a cyclist was killed by a box truck at Juniper Boulevard North and 80th Street. The data says only this: “Crush Injuries. Apparent Death.” No helmet could save him. No warning was enough. NYC Open Data.

      On June 26, a motorcyclist died on Jackie Robinson Parkway. The report reads: “Head. Severe Bleeding. Ejected. Apparent Death.”

      In February, a 47-year-old cyclist was killed on Maurice Avenue. The record is cold: “Crush Injuries. Apparent Death.”

      The pattern is clear. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city has the power to act. Albany handed New York City the keys with Sammy’s Law. The Council can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. The police can crack down on speeding, reckless turns, and failure to yield. But the streets remain the same. The dead keep coming.

      “It must be very devastating for the people that were on the bus,” said Jacqueline Cox after a recent crash in Queens (CBS New York). The pain is not abstract. It is lived, every day, by survivors and families.

      “I have a baby with me. That would be scary. I’ll be more cautious of my surroundings,” said Samantha Hart, after a bus mounted the curb and smashed a pole (CBS New York). The burden falls on those with the least power.

      The Precinct’s Role: Action or Excuse

      Precinct 104 can enforce the law. They can ticket speeders, reckless drivers, those who fail to yield. They can target crash hotspots. They can respond to dangerous conditions. They have the tools. They just need to use them.

      The disaster is slow, but it is relentless. Every day of delay is another family broken. Every silence from leadership is a choice.

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand action. The dead cannot speak. You can.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 104 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 32, assembly district AD 28 and state senate district SD 12. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 104?
      It includes the Maspeth, Ridgewood, Glendale, Middle Village, Mount Olivet & All Faiths Cemeteries, Middle Village Cemetery, St. John Cemetery, Highland Park-Cypress Hills Cemeteries (North), and Queens CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 30, District 32, and District 34, Assembly Districts AD 28, AD 30, AD 37, AD 38, and AD 39, and State Senate Districts SD 12, SD 15, and SD 18. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 104?
      Cars and Trucks: 1 pedestrian killed, 4 seriously injured, 331 total crashes. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 killed, 0 seriously injured, 12 total crashes. Bikes: 0 killed, 0 seriously injured, 9 total crashes. Cars and trucks are the main source of pedestrian harm in this precinct. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 104 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and issue failure-to-yield tickets. They can target known crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. The precinct has the tools to act—they just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better enforcement, and safer street design can save lives. Every crash is a sign of failure, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and hold agencies accountable for enforcement. They can push for policies that protect people walking and biking, not just drivers.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
