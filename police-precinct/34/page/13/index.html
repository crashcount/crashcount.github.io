      Blood on Their Hands: Demand Action for Safer Streets Now

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 29, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Precinct 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      In Precinct 34, the numbers do not lie. Eight people killed. Over 1,100 injured. Twenty-three left with wounds so deep they are called serious, not just by doctors, but by the city itself. city itself

      The dead are not numbers. A 15-year-old boy, crushed on a moped at West 185th and St. Nicholas. crushed on a moped A 37-year-old cyclist, struck and killed on West 181st by a truck while riding east in the night. struck and killed A 21-year-old woman, burned alive as two cars collided at West 213th and 10th Avenue. burned alive These are the facts. The street does not care who you are. It only takes.

      SUVs and cars do most of the damage. They killed two pedestrians and left dozens more broken. Trucks, buses, mopeds, bikes—they all play their part, but the weight of steel and speed is what shatters bodies here. weight of steel and speed

      The Police: Power and Responsibility

      The police in Precinct 34 have the tools. They can ticket speeders, stop reckless drivers, and watch the corners where blood pools most often. They can act. They must act. The law is not a suggestion. It is a shield, if enforced.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      The city has begun to move. Speed cameras run day and night. The law now lets New York set its own speed limits. But the work is not done. “Speeding ruins lives, and reducing vehicle speeds by even a few miles per hour could be the difference between life or death in a traffic crash,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez

      Yet, too often, the burden falls on the vulnerable. Cyclists and pedestrians are told to watch out, to obey, to yield. But the dead cannot speak. “We need to do something to make sure bicyclists feel safer,” said a community board leader, as the city cut bike lanes from Fifth Avenue. said a community board leader

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a wound that could have been prevented. The police can enforce the law. The city can lower the speed limit. Leaders can build streets that do not kill. But only if you demand it.

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.
