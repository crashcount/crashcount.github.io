      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 75?

      Twelve Dead, Thousands Hurt—Precinct 75 Bleeds While Leaders Look Away

      Written July 13, 2025

      Precinct 75: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      Death on the Streets

      In Precinct 75, the numbers do not lie. Twelve people killed. Fifty-one left with serious injuries. Nearly five thousand hurt since 2022. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. They are the man crossing Pennsylvania Avenue, the woman struck on Broadway, the child whose leg will never heal. The road does not forgive. The cars do not stop.

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. Seven deaths, 515 minor injuries, 134 moderate injuries, nine left with wounds that will not fade. Trucks and buses add to the toll. Bikes and mopeds, too, but the weight of the carnage comes on four wheels, not two. The pattern is old. The pain is fresh.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      Just this year, the blood has not stopped. In January, a woman crossing with the light was killed by an SUV making a left turn. In April, a young man died behind the wheel, speed and distraction listed on the report. In June, a child lost a leg. The stories do not change. The faces do.

      Neighbors know the danger. “Drivers speed on that stretch of roadway,” one said. The police know it too. The records show where the bodies fall, which corners bleed most often. The tools are there: tickets for speed, for failing to yield, for running the light. The question is not what can be done. The question is why it is not done.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city has new laws. The speed limit can drop. Cameras catch the reckless. But in the end, it is the local hands that matter. The precinct can crack down on speeders. They can stand at the corners where people die. They can make the law mean something. “Our message to drivers was clear: follow the rules or face the law,” said the NYPD Commissioner.

      But the bodies keep coming. The numbers keep rising. The silence is loud.

      Act Now: Demand Action

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand redesign. Demand the speed limits that save lives. Do not wait for another name on the list. Do not wait for another family to grieve. The road will not change itself. Only people can do that. Only you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 75 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 42, assembly district AD 54 and state senate district SD 18. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 75?
      It includes the Cypress Hills, East New York (North), East New York-New Lots, Spring Creek-Starrett City, East New York-City Line, Highland Park-Cypress Hills Cemeteries (South), Shirley Chisholm State Park, and Brooklyn CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 37 and District 42, Assembly Districts AD 54 and AD 60, and State Senate Districts SD 18 and SD 19. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 75?
      Cars and Trucks: 7 deaths, 515 minor injuries, 134 moderate injuries, 9 serious injuries (total 665 incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 13 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury (total 18 incidents). Bikes: 0 deaths, 7 minor injuries, 2 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury (total 10 incidents). The vast majority of deaths and injuries come from cars, SUVs, trucks, and buses. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 75 can enforce speed limits, issue tickets for reckless driving and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. They can respond to dangerous conditions and make the law mean something. As the NYPD Commissioner said, “our message to drivers was clear: follow the rules or face the law.”
      ▸ Are crashes preventable, or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. Most deaths and injuries here come from speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving—behaviors that can be stopped with enforcement and safer street design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and push for more enforcement. They can demand the precinct act on crash data and protect the most vulnerable. They can listen to residents and act before another life is lost.
