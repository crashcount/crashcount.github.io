      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Pennsylvania and Hegeman, Sunday morning

      Precinct 75: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 19, 2025

      Just before 10 AM on Sep 14, 2025, at Pennsylvania Avenue and Hegeman Avenue, a driver in a sedan hit a 71‑year‑old man standing at the corner. Police recorded unsafe speed and unsafe lane changing; the driver was unlicensed. The man bled from the head but was conscious. Source.
      “A pedestrian was struck during a Brooklyn police pursuit,” a reporter wrote that day, as debris covered the street. Source.
      ABC7 said it happened “just before 10 a.m.” at the same corner. Source.

      This Week

      • Sep 14 — A driver hit a man on the curb at Pennsylvania and Hegeman; police cited unsafe speed and lane change, and noted the driver was unlicensed. Source
      • Sep 1 — Two people on a motorcycle were killed at Atlantic Avenue and Elton Street after colliding with an SUV turning left. Source

      The toll on these blocks

      Since 2022, in Precinct 75, 19 people have been killed and 6,997 injured in 11,462 crashes. Pedestrians account for 8 of the deaths and 1,047 injuries; people on bikes for 1 death and 316 injuries. Source.

      This year, crashes are down 11.0% compared to last year’s pace, but deaths are up 300.0%. Serious injuries are up 125.0%. Source.

      Evenings are brutal here. Deaths peak around 8 PM, with late afternoon into night stacking the heaviest injuries. Source.

      Where drivers keep hurting people

      Pennsylvania Avenue shows up again and again in crash records. So does Linden Boulevard. A single Sutter Avenue location logged eight serious injuries. Source.

      Named, preventable failures appear in the files: failure to yield, traffic control disregarded, aggressive driving, and unsafe speed. Police wrote those words into the reports. Source.

      On Sep 1 at Atlantic and Elton, police said the SUV driver was turning left as the motorcycle came straight; two people were ejected and died. Source.

      Fix the corners that kill

      Start where the bodies fall: daylight the crosswalks, give leading pedestrian intervals, harden left turns, and slow traffic on Pennsylvania Avenue, Linden Boulevard, and Atlantic Avenue. Focus enforcement where deaths and serious injuries cluster in the evening and at those hotspot blocks. Target failure to yield and red‑light running. The patterns are in the data. Source.

      Citywide levers officials can pull now

      The city can lower speeds. The state can rein in repeat speeders. Our streets department has already said what speed means: “A driver’s speed can mean the difference between life and death in a traffic crash.” Context.

      Two steps match the damage we see here:

      • Lower the default city speed limit to 20 MPH under Sammy’s Law. Details and contacts are here.
      • Pass the Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045C/A2299C) to require speed‑limiters for vehicles with repeated tickets or points. The bill is explained here.

      Precinct 75 sits in Council District 42, Assembly District 54, and State Senate District 18. Council Member Chris Banks, Assembly Member Erik Dilan, and State Senator Julia Salazar: the deaths and injuries above are in your districts. Will you back a 20 MPH default and co‑sponsor the Stop Super Speeders Act? Details.

      One corner, over and over

      The man at Pennsylvania and Hegeman lived. Two riders at Atlantic and Elton did not. Since 2022, this precinct has stacked 19 dead. Slow the cars. Protect the crossings. Start now. Source.

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4). We filtered for Jan 1, 2022–Sep 19, 2025 and geography “Police Precinct 75.” We counted deaths, injuries, serious injuries, modes, locations, hours, and contributing factors directly from those fields. Data were accessed Sep 19, 2025. Start from the Crashes dataset and apply those filters here.
