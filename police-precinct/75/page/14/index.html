      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 75?

      No More Empty Seats: End the Killing on Brooklyn’s Streets

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      Precinct 75: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Twelve dead. Fifty-one seriously hurt. That’s the toll in Precinct 75 since 2022. Nearly five thousand injured. These are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors—the empty seat at dinner, the voice that doesn’t answer.

      Cars and SUVs did most of the killing. Seven deaths, 515 minor injuries, 134 moderate, nine serious. Trucks and buses hurt dozens more. Bikes and mopeds are in the single digits. The street is not a fair fight (NYC Open Data).

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      The carnage does not slow. On January 24, a woman crossing with the signal at Pennsylvania and Blake was killed by an SUV turning left. The driver failed to yield. She died in the street (NYC Open Data).

      On April 29, a 28-year-old man died behind the wheel. The crash report lists driver inattention and unsafe speed. He bled out in his car (NYC Open Data).

      On July 3, a 36-year-old pedestrian was killed at Broadway and East New York Avenue. No signal. No crosswalk. Just a body in the intersection (NYC Open Data).

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has tools. Speed cameras. Lower speed limits. More tickets for reckless drivers. But the deaths keep coming. Police can crack down on speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving. They can target the corners where people keep dying. They just need to act.

      Local voices are not silent. After a hit-and-run killed two men in Brooklyn, a witness described the scene: “I pass through here almost every morning. I see them all lined up… The line is really long. So, they get there early so they can get their stuff early.”

      A food pantry worker responded: “We are deeply saddened by the tragic deaths… We understand they may have been on their way to our Mobile Market simply trying to access food like so many New Yorkers in need.”

      The Call to Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice—by drivers, by leaders, by those who look away. Call your council member. Demand enforcement. Demand lower speed limits. Demand action from Precinct 75. Don’t wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 75 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 42, assembly district AD 54 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 75?
      It includes the Cypress Hills, East New York (North), East New York-New Lots, Spring Creek-Starrett City, East New York-City Line, Highland Park-Cypress Hills Cemeteries (South), Shirley Chisholm State Park, and Brooklyn CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 37 and District 42, Assembly Districts AD 54 and AD 60, and State Senate Districts SD 18 and SD 19.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 75?
      Cars and SUVs: 7 deaths, 515 minor injuries, 134 moderate injuries, 9 serious injuries. Trucks and buses: 0 deaths, 35 minor injuries, 14 moderate, 4 serious. Motorcycles and mopeds: 0 deaths, 13 minor injuries, 4 moderate, 1 serious. Bikes: 0 deaths, 7 minor injuries, 2 moderate, 1 serious. Cars and SUVs are responsible for the vast majority of deaths and injuries. (NYC Open Data)
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 75 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. The police have the tools—they just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. They are preventable. Lower speeds, better enforcement, and safer street design save lives. Every crash is a policy choice.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can push for lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, and demand police enforcement at dangerous intersections. They can listen to residents and act before the next tragedy.
