      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 75?

      Precinct 75: slow death on fast roads

      Written August 28, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Precinct 75: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 28, 2025

      Brooklyn’s 75th Precinct does not rest. Since 2022, 15 people are dead and 5,404 are hurt on its streets, with 66 left seriously injured. Most of the pain lands on those outside the steel shell: pedestrians and cyclists. City crash data shows it.

      Belt Parkway and Pennsylvania: names you learn by loss

      The Belt keeps killing. On Feb 16, 2025, a 41‑year‑old driver died on the eastbound Belt Parkway. The record lists unsafe speed on slippery pavement; the SUV was “demolished.” The dataset records it. The Belt and Pennsylvania Avenue together account for multiple deaths and hundreds of injuries in this precinct’s roll‑up of top locations. See the roll‑up.

      A 28‑year‑old driver died where Seaview meets Pennsylvania on Apr 29, 2025. One vehicle had New York plates, one Pennsylvania plates. One man never made it home. The crash log shows it.

      People on foot don’t walk away

      At Broadway and E New York Ave, a 36‑year‑old man crossing at the intersection was struck and killed just before 10 PM on Jul 3, 2025. The city record lists his death. Two weeks later, around 12:30 AM on Jul 13, 2025, a 21‑year‑old outside the roadway near 395 Gateway Dr was hit by a 2023 sedan. The log cites driver distraction and unsafe speed; he died where he fell. The entry is here.

      Since 2022, SUVs and sedans lead the body count for pedestrians here. The rollup ties pedestrian deaths to SUVs and sedans far more than trucks or buses. The city’s breakdown makes it plain.

      When the clock turns, the sirens rise

      Hurt piles up in the late afternoon and into night. Injuries peak around 3 PM and 4 PM, then again near 6 PM to 9 PM. Deaths stack in the evening hours, with the 6 PM hour carrying the heaviest toll in this period. The hourly counts show the rhythm.

      Here are the corners we already know: Linden Boulevard and Pennsylvania Avenue rack up injuries month after month. The Belt Parkway again. One Sutter Avenue block shows eight serious injuries in this span. Each is listed in the top locations.

      Patterns in the wreckage

      The city’s own labels point to the same causes: “other” leads the harm, but the named culprits are there—failure to yield, aggressive driving, distraction, and unsafe speed. Together they leave a long trail: 1,575 injuries and 16 deaths across these factors in the precinct’s span. The contributing factors table lists them.

      In the last 12 months, deaths in this precinct climbed to eight. This year to date, deaths are already six, up from one at this point last year. Injuries are up, too. The period stats confirm the rise.

      What the precinct can do now

      Target the worst blocks first: Pennsylvania Ave, Linden Blvd, and the Belt Parkway ramps. Use hard turns and daylighting to cut blind spots. Post and hold leading pedestrian intervals at the busy crossings. Post officers at the known evening peaks. Focus on left turns and yielding.

      Go after speed at the hours that kill. Aim enforcement at repeat behavior, not random stops. Center heavy‑vehicle routes off the blocks with serious injuries.

      Citywide moves that stop the bleed

      Lower speeds save lives. New York now has the power to drop limits. A citywide 20 MPH default on local streets would cut death at the source. Sammy’s Law made that possible. We need leaders to use it. Our action guide explains how to push them.

      The worst repeat speeders drive the numbers. One bill would require cars with a streak of tickets or points to use speed limiters that keep them within 5 MPH of the limit. Families are already burying loved ones. We don’t need another case to prove it. Learn how to demand the mandate.
