      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 40?

      Mustang Massacre: Blood on Courtlandt, Silence from City Hall

      Written July 4, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 40: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 4, 2025

      The Street Does Not Forgive

      On July 3, a Ford Mustang turned Courtlandt Avenue into a killing ground. The car struck one person in the crosswalk, then five more, before smashing into scaffolding. The driver and passenger ran. The street was left with screams and blood. “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting,” said Vivian Cole. Six people went to the hospital. The Mustang had no plates. The city had no answers.

      This is not rare. In the last twelve months, five people died and 604 were injured on the streets of Precinct 40. Fourteen suffered injuries so severe they may never walk right again. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. The numbers do not lie. The pain is not abstract.

      The Toll of Delay

      The police know these corners. They know the hotspots. They have the power to enforce speed limits, ticket reckless drivers, and clear sightlines. But the carnage continues. “Once I heard the noise I turned around and it was already on the curb. It immediately backed up and fled,” said Melvin Pemberton. The ambulance came fast. The system did not.

      Cars and SUVs do most of the harm. In three years, they killed four, seriously injured thirteen, and hurt nearly three hundred people on foot. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll. The street is a gauntlet. The city’s response is a whisper.

      Leaders Talk, Streets Stay Deadly

      Local leaders have tools. They can demand more speed enforcement, daylight corners, and redesign deadly crossings. They can push for a citywide 20 mph limit. They can hold the precinct to account for every unheeded hotspot. But action is slow. The dead do not wait.

      Call to Action

      Demand more. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell the 40th Precinct captain you want speeders stopped, corners cleared, and every crash hotspot fixed. Do not wait for another siren.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 40 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 8, assembly district AD 79 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 40?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, and Bronx CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8 and District 17, Assembly Districts AD 79 and AD 84, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 32.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 40?
      Cars and SUVs: 4 deaths, 13 serious injuries, 302 total pedestrian injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 0 serious injuries, 20 total injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 11 total injuries. (Source: NYC Open Data)
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 40 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue speeding and failure-to-yield tickets, respond to dangerous conditions, and clear sightlines at intersections. The tools exist. They just need to be used.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, failure to yield, and poor street design can be fixed. Calling them ‘accidents’ hides the truth.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can demand more enforcement, push for lower speed limits, daylight corners, and redesign deadly crossings. They can hold police and DOT accountable for every crash hotspot.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
