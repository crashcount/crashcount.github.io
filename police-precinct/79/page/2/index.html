      Dragged, Killed, Forgotten: Brooklyn’s Streets Demand Action Now

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 6, 2025 – Fixed duplicate headline in body and cleaned citations list.)

      Precinct 79: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      A woman steps out of her car on Van Buren Street. She is pregnant. She is struck, dragged, and left to die in the street. Her husband says, “I lost my whole family tonight and I don’t think I’ll ever be the same” (NY Daily News).

      In the last twelve months, one person died and five suffered serious injuries on the streets of Precinct 79. There were 390 injuries in 725 crashes. Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians—none are spared. The numbers do not stop. They do not rest.

      Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs killed more pedestrians here than any other vehicle. Trucks, sedans, bikes, and mopeds all left their mark. The dead are not numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. “They dragged her like to the middle of the street, and then they turned wrong up Lafayette and she was just left there,” a witness said.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and the Waiting

      The city has the power to lower speed limits. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can act. The police can enforce. They can ticket speeders, crack down on reckless drivers, and target crash hotspots. They have the tools. They need only to use them.

      But the silence is heavy. Each day without action is another day the street claims someone else. There is no safety in delay. There is no justice in waiting.

      What Comes Next

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call the precinct. Demand action.

      Lower the speed limit. Enforce the law. Protect the living.

      Do not wait for another family to be broken. Do not let the blood dry before you speak. Take action now.

      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 79 police can enforce speed limits, ticket reckless drivers, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue failure-to-yield tickets and respond to dangerous conditions. They have the power to act. They just need to use it.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. Lower speeds, better enforcement, and safer streets prevent deaths. Every crash is a chance to act before the next one.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and demand police enforcement. They can act now or answer for the next death.
