      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 79?

      Eight Dead, Seventeen Broken—Precinct 79 Bleeds While Leaders Sleep

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 24, 2025 – Fixed link attribution for quote, removed duplicate headline, cleaned citations.)

      Precinct 79: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Eight people are dead. Seventeen more are left with serious injuries. In just over three years, Precinct 79 has seen 2,926 crashes. The toll is not abstract. It is bodies on the street, families left with empty chairs. In the last twelve months alone, one person was killed and seven suffered life-altering wounds. Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians—no one is spared.

      The Latest Crashes: No End in Sight

      The blood has not dried. On October 10, a 49-year-old man was killed at Bedford and Lafayette. Last November, a 79-year-old woman crossing with the signal was crushed by two SUVs at Herkimer and New York Avenue. She died at the intersection, her right of way ignored. The numbers keep coming, but the pain is always fresh.

      Who Bears the Brunt?

      SUVs and cars are the main killers. Of the pedestrian injuries and deaths, 270 came from cars and SUVs, 23 from trucks and buses, 13 from bikes, and 4 from mopeds. The machines are heavy, the odds are not fair. The street is not a level field.

      What Has Been Done—and What Hasn’t

      Local leaders have the tools. The police can enforce speed limits, ticket reckless drivers, and target crash hotspots. But the carnage continues. The silence is loud. Every day without action is another day of risk. As one neighbor warned about speeding after a deadly hit-and-run nearby, the message is clear. The response is not.

      The Call: Demand Action Now

      This is not fate. It is policy. The police in Precinct 79 can crack down on speeding and failure to yield. Local politicians can push for safer street design and stronger laws. But nothing changes unless you demand it. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Tell them you want enforcement, not excuses. Tell them you want to live.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 79 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 36, assembly district AD 56 and state senate district SD 25.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 79?
      It includes the Bedford-Stuyvesant (West) and Brooklyn CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33 and District 36, Assembly Districts AD 56 and AD 57, and State Senate District SD 25.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 79?
      Cars and SUVs: 270 injuries and deaths from cars and SUVs. Trucks and Buses: 23. Bikes: 13. Mopeds: 4.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 79 police can enforce speed limits, issue tickets for reckless driving and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. They can respond to dangerous conditions and make streets safer for everyone. As neighbors have said, drivers speed on that stretch of roadway. The precinct has the power to act.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. They are preventable with better enforcement, safer street design, and stronger laws. The numbers show patterns, not random chance.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can push for lower speed limits, safer street designs, and stronger enforcement. They can hold agencies accountable and make sure police target the most dangerous drivers and locations.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
