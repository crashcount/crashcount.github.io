      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 84?

      Precinct 84: Four dead, 1,298 hurt. The streets keep taking.

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Precinct 84: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Bold numbers, quiet streets. In Precinct 84 since 2022: 4 dead, 1,298 injured across 3,000 crashes. Pedestrians hurt 246 times. Cyclists, 197. Buses and trucks still hit people here.

      • Pedestrians were most often struck by sedans and SUVs: 85 by sedans, 55 by SUVs, 26 by trucks, 9 by buses (NYC Open Data).
      • The worst hours stack up in daylight and dusk. Injuries spike at 10 a.m. (67), 11 a.m. (89), 2 p.m. (105), and stay high into the evening. Deaths hit at 8 a.m., 3–4 p.m., 8 p.m., and 11 p.m. (NYC Open Data).

      Unsafe speed and “other” driver actions lead the harm. “Other” accounts for 373 injuries. Distraction is next. Failure to yield keeps showing up. These are choices that break bodies (NYC Open Data).

      Where the street turns on you

      Tillary Street. Fifty-six injured. Four were left with serious wounds. The corner with Jay Street took a 74‑year‑old on an e‑bike when a bus turned right; the rider was killed (crash record).

      Cadman Plaza West. Sixteen injured and one dead. In 2022, a speeding SUV with reported aggressive driving killed a 76‑year‑old passenger near Furman Street (crash record).

      Flatbush Avenue Extension. Sixty-six injured. People keep getting hit where the roadway widens and drivers rush the merge (NYC Open Data).

      Who pays the price

      In the last 12 months: 3 dead, 396 injured. Two deaths this year already, as crashes rose by about 26% year‑to‑date compared to last year’s pace (NYC Open Data).

      • Ages 45–64 bear the brunt of deaths lately. Children were seriously hurt twice this year. Seniors 65–74 also suffered serious injuries (NYC Open Data).
      • Heavy vehicles matter. Trucks and buses account for 43 pedestrian strikes; the bus‑turn death at Tillary and Jay is part of that toll (NYC Open Data).

      “Speed kills” is not a slogan here. It is a record. On Furman and Cadman, the city’s data lists “Unsafe Speed” and “Aggressive Driving/Road Rage” for the 2022 fatal crash that trapped two people and killed one (crash record).

      Fix the corners, slow the cars

      Start at the hotspots. Daylight every corner around Tillary Street and Cadman Plaza West. Harden right turns for buses and trucks at Jay, Navy, and the Flatbush Extension. Give walkers a head start with LPIs. Put concrete where paint fails. Then repeat at the next cluster.

      Target the hours when injuries surge: late mornings through the school rush and evening peaks. Enforce failure‑to‑yield at the crosswalk. Keep trucks to safe routes during the worst windows. Do not wait for another obituary line in the dataset (NYC Open Data).

      Citywide tools that end this

      The city now has the power to set lower speeds. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. A 20 mph default would cut deaths. “A pedestrian hit at 30 mph is five times more likely to die than at 20 mph,” our own advocates note. The law lets City Hall act now (CrashCount Take Action).

      Repeat speeders do outsized harm. Vehicles piling up camera tickets are far more likely to kill. The Stop Super Speeders Act would force the worst drivers to use speed‑limiters after 16 camera tickets in a year or 11 DMV points in 18 months. Pass it, then enforce it (CrashCount Take Action).

      This precinct shows the pattern. The names change. The corners don’t. Slow the cars. Fix the turns. Use the laws we have.
