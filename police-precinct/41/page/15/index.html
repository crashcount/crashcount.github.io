      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 41?

      Precinct 41: two young riders dead on the Bronx River Parkway, and a map full of warnings

      Written August 25, 2025 (Revised August 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Precinct 41: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 25, 2025

      Two men went down on the Bronx River Parkway before dawn. Police say a 21‑year‑old in a Mercedes tried to pass, hit a Volkswagen, then struck two motorcycles. Both riders—Manuel Amarantepenalo, 19, and Enrique Martinez, 21—were thrown and later pronounced dead. The driver was charged with vehicular manslaughter and DWI, then released without bail as the case moves forward, according to prosecutors. “Two people were killed. He was drunk,” the victim’s sister said outside court. Gothamist reported the charges and names. The Daily News carried the family’s words and bail details.

      CBS said the crash involved seven vehicles near East Gun Hill Road. NYPD said, “Two men on motorcycles were killed.” Their early coverage is here. Another segment noted police arrested the Mercedes driver at the scene. That clip is here.

      This is Precinct 41. The map tells the story in numbers as cold as concrete.

      Where it breaks most

      Since 2022, crashes in this precinct cluster on the Bruckner corridors and Westchester Avenue. Two deaths and 52 injuries stack up on Westchester Avenue, and another death and 96 injuries along the Bruckner Expressway. Bruckner Boulevard adds 255 injuries. These are the lines where bodies keep meeting metal. The city’s open dataset confirms the counts for this small area over the period.

      At night the risk climbs. From 9 p.m. to 2 a.m., injuries spike, with a sharp peak at 9 p.m. (143 injuries) and persistent harm through the late night hours. Dawn is no refuge: 5–7 a.m. shows three deaths across those hours. The hourly ledger is blunt about when people get hurt here. The precinct’s crash stats back it up.

      Who gets hit, and by what

      Pedestrians in the 41st face SUVs and sedans most. Of pedestrian cases tracked here, sedans led injuries (85 total) and SUVs caused the most pedestrian deaths (two), with trucks and “unspecified” vehicles each tied to a death. Heavy vehicles are not the bulk of harm, but when trucks hit, people die: one pedestrian death and 20 total pedestrian cases tied to trucks. See the vehicle roll‑up in the dataset.

      Mode by mode, the toll since 2022: pedestrians—four deaths and 242 injuries; bicyclists—one death and 101 injuries; “other motorized” riders—42 injuries; people inside vehicles—five deaths and 1,225 injuries. These are the lives behind police reports. The local roll‑up captures the split.

      What causes repeat harm

      The top coded bucket here is “other,” tied to five deaths and 383 injuries. One death and 40 injuries are logged as “vulnerable road user error.” Distraction shows up with 27 injuries. Failure to yield shows 19. Signals ignored: nine. Labels are imperfect, but the outcomes are not. These contributing factors come from the NYPD crash data.

      Street‑level fixes that fit this map

      • Daylight corners and harden turns on Westchester Avenue and Bruckner Boulevard to cut turning strikes at posted hotspots.
      • Add leading pedestrian intervals and protected crossings at Hunts Point Ave and other listed corridors where injuries pile up.
      • Nighttime focus: targeted speed and DWI enforcement during the 9 p.m.–2 a.m. window the data shows as most dangerous.
      • Truck routing and loading management around Bruckner/Viele/Oak Point, where truck presence and past deaths intersect.

      These are standard tools. They match the places the data highlights.

      Citywide changes that save lives here

      • Lower the default speed limit citywide to 20 mph. The authority exists under Sammy’s Law. Use it. Our reporting has shown how speed kills and how to push City Hall. Read how to act.
      • Stop repeat speeders with mandatory intelligent speed assistance for drivers who rack up violations. The Stop Super Speeders Act would force the worst offenders to slow down. Join the push.

      A sister stood outside a courthouse and asked why the man who hit her brother was walking free. “Two people were killed,” she said. The families wait. The streets don’t.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where did the Bronx River Parkway deaths happen?
      Near East 223rd Street/Gun Hill Road, according to police accounts. Reports say a Mercedes tried to pass, hit a Volkswagen, then struck two motorcycles; both riders later died. See Gothamist and NY Daily News for details.
      ▸ What times are most dangerous in the 41st Precinct?
      From 9 p.m. to 2 a.m., with a spike at 9 p.m. The data also shows deaths around the early morning hours between 5 and 7 a.m., based on the precinct’s hourly distribution.
      ▸ Which streets in Precinct 41 see the most harm?
      Westchester Avenue, Bruckner Expressway, and Bruckner Boulevard top the list for injuries and deaths in this period, per NYC Open Data roll‑ups.
      ▸ What fixes fit these streets?
      Daylighting and hardened turns on Westchester and Bruckner, LPIs and protected crossings on Hunts Point Ave and other corridors, night DWI/speed enforcement 9 p.m.–2 a.m., and tighter truck routing/loading near Bruckner/Viele/Oak Point.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
