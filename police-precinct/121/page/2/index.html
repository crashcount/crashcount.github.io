      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 121?

      Stop the Bloodshed: Hold Precinct 121 Accountable for Every Life Lost

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 3, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Precinct 121: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In Precinct 121, the numbers do not lie. Nine people killed. Sixteen left with serious injuries. Nearly two thousand hurt since 2022. The dead do not get a second chance. The injured carry scars—some seen, some not. In the last year alone, one person died, and 333 were injured. One was a worker at the Amazon warehouse, run down by a box truck in the dark. He had two daughters. He did not go home. “He was 34 years old. He had two daughters. He was killed on the job,” said a coworker. The driver stayed. No charges. The case sits with the Collision Investigation Squad. The family waits.

      The Police: Power and Responsibility

      Precinct 121 has the tools. They can enforce speed limits. They can ticket reckless drivers. They can target the corners where people die. But the grind continues. In the last year, 573 crashes. One death. Six left with life-changing wounds. The city talks about cracking down. The mayor stands by as mopeds are crushed. “New Yorkers have strong feelings about illegal mopeds and scooters, because we hear it all the time, especially when they are driving the wrong way down streets or sidewalks or in the dark without lights,” said Mayor Adams. The city boasts of removing 62,000 illegal vehicles. But the streets are still not safe for the ones on foot, on bikes, or waiting for the bus.

      Leadership: Action or Delay?

      Local leaders have power. They can push for lower speed limits. They can demand more speed cameras. They can fund street redesigns. They can hold the police to account. But too often, they wait. The law lets the city set safer speeds. The city can act now. Every day of delay is another risk, another family changed forever.

      What Next: Demand More

      This is not fate. This is failure. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Demand action before another name becomes a number. Take action now.

      Speed Cameras Coming To MTA Crossings
      ▸

      Cameras will watch for speeding on city bridges and tunnels. Fines start at $50. Lawmakers push the plan to protect workers and travelers. The program runs to 2031. Enforcement aims to slow drivers and cut risk on vital routes.

      Patch reported on May 8, 2025, that New York lawmakers approved an expansion of a speed camera pilot program for MTA bridges and tunnels. The plan allows cameras on seven bridges and two tunnels, ticketing drivers who exceed speed limits. Fines start at $50 for a first offense, rising to $75 and $100 for repeat violations within 18 months. Governor Hochul's office called the program 'one more way the governor is working to improve safety on our roads and bridges for workers and travelers alike.' MTA Bridges and Tunnels President Catherine Sheridan said, 'We want to make sure that our maintenance workers and contractors are safe.' The program, part of the $254 billion state budget, will be reviewed again in 2031. The move targets driver speed, a key factor in crash severity, and signals a shift toward automated enforcement on major city crossings.
