      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 13?

      Six Dead, Thousands Hurt—How Many Bodies Before City Hall Acts?

      Written July 16, 2025

      Precinct 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Slow Grind of Loss

      In Precinct 13, the numbers do not lie. Six people killed. Twenty-three left with serious injuries. Over a thousand hurt since 2022. Each number is a body broken, a life changed, a family left with questions and rage. The dead do not speak, but the record is clear.

      Just last month, a 49-year-old man was crushed by a bus at the corner of East 28th and 3rd Avenue. He died in the street, the cause listed as “driver inattention” (NYC Open Data). In December, a 23-year-old cyclist was killed by a turning sedan on Park Avenue South. The charge: “failure to yield right-of-way.” The blood dries, but the danger remains.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and SUVs are the main weapons—responsible for most deaths and injuries. Buses and trucks add to the toll. Bikes and mopeds are not blameless, but the scale is not the same. The sidewalk is not safe. The crosswalk is not safe. The bike lane is not safe. “We want a real solution to the bike and pedestrian safety issue,” said Community Board 5 Vice Chair Samir Lavingia, as city leaders cut protected bike lanes from the Fifth Avenue redesign. The board warned: without safe space, cyclists will ride the sidewalk, and conflict will follow.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      The city has tools. Speed cameras run 24/7. New laws allow lower speed limits. But the pace is slow. The city sprinkles 20 mph zones, but the default remains higher. The police can enforce speed limits, ticket reckless drivers, and target hotspots. They can act. They must act. “We need to do something to make sure bicyclists feel safer,” said Lavingia. But the city delays, and the bodies pile up.

      Act Now—Or Count the Dead

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph limit. Demand enforcement at every deadly corner. Demand real protection for those who walk and ride. Every day of delay is another life at risk.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 13?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, and Manhattan CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, and District 4, Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 13?
      Cars and SUVs: 2 deaths, 138 minor injuries, 79 moderate injuries, 7 serious injuries (total: 226). Trucks and Buses: 1 death, 14 minor injuries, 5 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries (total: 20). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 10 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries (total: 14). Bikes: 0 deaths, 23 minor injuries, 16 moderate injuries, 3 serious injuries (total: 42).
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 13 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield. They can respond to dangerous conditions and make streets safer for everyone. The tools exist. They just need to act.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by city leaders, by those who design and enforce the rules. Lower speeds, better enforcement, and safer streets can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand automated enforcement, and demand police focus on the most dangerous violations. They can listen to the community and act before another life is lost.
