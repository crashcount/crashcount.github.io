      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 13?

      Another Year, Another Body: Blood on Second Avenue, Silence from City Hall

      Written August 5, 2025 (Revised August 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Precinct 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 5, 2025

      In Precinct 13, the toll does not let up. Seven people killed. Twenty-six left with injuries so serious they may never walk the same. In just the last twelve months, two more lives lost, eight more bodies broken. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old. They do not care if you are careful.

      The Latest Wounds

      Just last week, a 65-year-old e-bike rider was struck on Second Avenue. The driver did not stop. He drove onto the sidewalk, then took the SUV to a car wash. He told police he ran because he had no license. The cyclist was left with a shattered skull. “He now faces charges of leaving the scene of an accident that caused serious injury and driving without a license” (West Side Spirit).

      Days before, eight people were hurt when a car and SUV slammed into scaffolding on Madison Avenue. No one died this time. “Eight people were hurt in the crash. All of the injuries are believed to be non-life-threatening” (ABC7). The cause? Still unknown. No charges filed.

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and Silence

      Cars and SUVs do most of the damage. In three years, they killed three and injured 225 people on foot in this precinct. Buses and trucks took another life. Bikes and mopeds hurt dozens more. The street does not care what hit you. It only matters that you were there.

      Leadership: Promises and Power

      The city has the power to lower speed limits. The council can demand enforcement. The police can target speeders, reckless drivers, and failure-to-yield at known hotspots. But the blood keeps coming. The law is there. The tools are there. The will is not always there.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand action. Tell them to enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and fix the streets where people keep getting hurt. Every day they wait, someone else pays the price.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 13?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, and Manhattan CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, and District 4, Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 13?
      Cars and SUVs: 3 deaths, 225 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 14 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 43 injuries. Buses and trucks: 1 death, 21 injuries. The street does not care what hit you.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 13 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target failure-to-yield at known hotspots. They can issue tickets, respond to dangerous conditions, and focus on streets where people keep getting hurt. The tools are there. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not fate. They happen in the same places, for the same reasons—speed, reckless driving, failure to yield. They can be stopped with enforcement, street design, and political will.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, demand enforcement, and push for safer street design. They can hold the precinct accountable for targeting dangerous drivers and fixing hotspots. Every day they wait, more people get hurt.
