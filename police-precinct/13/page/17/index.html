      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 13?

      Still Bleeding: One Dead, Hundreds Wounded, and No One Stops the Cars

      Written July 28, 2025

      Precinct 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 28, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Latest Toll

      The streets of Precinct 13 do not forgive. In the past year, one person died and 401 were injured in traffic crashes here. Twelve suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again (NYC Open Data). The numbers are not just numbers. They are bodies on Broadway, sirens on Third Avenue, families waiting for news that never gets better.

      Just last month, a bus crushed a man at the corner of East 28th and 3rd. He died at the intersection, his body broken by a vehicle too large for the street (NYC Open Data – CrashID 4820937). A few months before, a cyclist was killed by a turning sedan on Park Avenue South. The city records the age, the time, the type of car. It does not record the silence that follows.

      The Human Cost: Names and Voices

      The pain does not fade. “We lost a true hero this morning,” said NYC Deputy Mayor Kaz Daughtry after firefighter Matthew Goicochea was killed on the FDR. He fell from his motorcycle and was left in the road. The driver who hit him did not stop (West Side Spirit).

      On Canal Street, a vigil was held for a pedestrian and a cyclist killed in a hit-and-run. The crash, reported Alecia Reid, “is raising questions about a bigger safety problem at an intersection of Canal Street” (CBS New York).

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      Local leaders have the power to slow the carnage. Albany gave the city the right to lower speed limits. The council can demand enforcement. The precinct can crack down on speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving. But the blood keeps flowing. The city sprinkles 20 mph zones, but the default remains higher. Cameras catch speeders, but the worst repeat offenders keep driving.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Precinct 13 can act. Police can target crash hotspots, ticket reckless drivers, and enforce the law. Council Members can push for a 20 mph limit and support the Stop Super Speeders Act. Residents can call, write, and demand action. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand safer speeds and real enforcement.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 13?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, and Manhattan CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, and District 4, Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 13?
      Cars and Trucks: 3 deaths, 234 injuries (including 8 serious) from SUVs and sedans. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 14 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 43 injuries (including 3 serious).
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 13 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield. They have the tools—they just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving can be stopped with enforcement and safer street design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, demand enforcement, and support laws like the Stop Super Speeders Act. They can push for safer street design and hold agencies accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
