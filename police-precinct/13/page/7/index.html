      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 13?

      Blood on the Crosswalk: Speed Kills, Leaders Stall

      Written July 8, 2025

      Precinct 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 8, 2025

      The Toll in Plain Sight

      In Precinct 13, the numbers do not lie. Five people killed. Twenty-two left with serious injuries. Over a thousand hurt. These are not distant tragedies. They are neighbors, children, elders. They are the cost of walking, biking, or crossing the street in Midtown, Gramercy, and Murray Hill.

      The dead do not speak. But the record does. A 77-year-old woman, crossing with the signal at Lexington and East 29th, struck and killed by an SUV making a left turn. The driver was unlicensed. She died on the street, her blood on the crosswalk. The crash report lists the cause: “Failure to Yield Right-of-Way” (crash records).

      A cyclist, 23, killed at Park Avenue South and East 27th. The car turned left. The bike went straight. The crash report says, again, “Failure to Yield Right-of-Way” (crash records).

      The pattern is simple. The pain is not.

      The Police: Power and Responsibility

      The police in Precinct 13 have the tools. They can enforce speed limits. They can ticket drivers who fail to yield. They can target the corners where blood stains the curb. They can act before the next name is added to the list.

      But action is not a press release. It is a summons in a driver’s hand. It is a patrol car parked at the intersection where the last person died. It is a message, clear as a siren: slow down, or pay.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      City leaders have made moves. The city now has the power to lower speed limits. Cameras catch speeders day and night. But the work is not done. The deaths keep coming. The injuries pile up. “Speeding ruins lives, and reducing vehicle speeds by even a few miles per hour could be the difference between life or death in a traffic crash,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez (DOT press release).

      “Our message to drivers was clear: follow the rules or face the law,” said NYPD Commissioner Jessica Tisch (NYPD enforcement statement).

      But words are not enough. The numbers do not change on their own.

      The Next Step: Demand Action

      Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Call the precinct. Tell them the blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Demand enforcement. Demand lower speeds. Demand safe crossings. Do not wait for another name.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 28. See crash records.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 13?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, and Manhattan CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, and District 4, Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59. See crash records.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 13?
      Cars and SUVs: 2 deaths, 132 minor injuries, 77 moderate injuries, 7 serious injuries (total 218 incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 10 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries (total 14 incidents). Bikes: 0 deaths, 23 minor injuries, 16 moderate injuries, 3 serious injuries (total 42 incidents). See crash records.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 13 police can enforce speed limits, issue tickets for failure to yield, and target crash hotspots. They can crack down on reckless driving and respond to dangerous conditions before someone is hurt. As NYPD Commissioner Jessica Tisch said, “Our message to drivers was clear: follow the rules or face the law” (NYPD enforcement statement).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Most crashes are preventable. The data shows repeated patterns: failure to yield, speeding, and reckless turns. These are choices, not fate. Enforcement and safer street design can stop them.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and push for stronger enforcement. They can demand the city use its new power to set safer speeds and expand automated enforcement. They can hold agencies accountable for results, not just promises.
