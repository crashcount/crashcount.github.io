      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 13?

      Precinct 13: Broken Bodies, Broken Promises

      Written July 12, 2025

      Precinct 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt

      In Precinct 13, the numbers do not lie. Six people are dead. Twenty-three have suffered serious injuries. Over 1,100 have been hurt since 2022. The toll is steady, merciless, and it does not care who you are. A child struck at East 14th. A cyclist thrown from his bike on 23rd. A pedestrian crushed by a bus at 28th and 3rd. The stories change, but the outcome is the same: bodies broken, families left with silence.

      Just last month, a bus rolled through the intersection at 3rd Avenue and East 28th Street. A 49-year-old man was killed. The record shows only this: “Crush Injuries” and “Apparent Death” according to city crash data. No more words are needed.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs are the main threat. They have killed three, injured 217, and left seven with serious wounds. Trucks and buses have claimed another life and hurt 19 more. Bikes are not blameless—42 injuries, three serious—but the scale is not the same. The street is a battlefield, and the deadliest weapons are still behind the wheel.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      The city has begun to act. Speed cameras now run 24/7. The law allows for lower speed limits. But the pace is slow. The carnage continues. Police have the tools. They can ticket speeders, crack down on failure to yield, and target the corners where blood is most often spilled. They just need to use them.

      When a crash shut down the Lincoln Tunnel, the city watched the chaos. “Over an hour delay stuck in the Lincoln tunnel while a car was on fire,” a commuter wrote. When two buses collided at Port Authority, “It required some extrication, meaning they had to use some equipment to free the patient,” said FDNY Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. The system is brittle. The cost is paid in flesh.

      What Next: Demand Action Now

      Precinct 13 does not need more studies. It needs enforcement. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand speed enforcement, failure-to-yield crackdowns, and real protection for people outside the car. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 13?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, and Manhattan CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, and District 4, Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 13?
      Cars and SUVs: 3 deaths, 217 injuries, 7 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 1 death, 19 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 14 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 42 injuries, 3 serious injuries. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 13 police can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. The tools exist. They just need to be used.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. Most are preventable with lower speeds, better street design, and real enforcement. Every crash is a policy failure, not just bad luck.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand automated enforcement, and hold agencies accountable for protecting people outside cars. They can demand police focus on the violations that kill.
