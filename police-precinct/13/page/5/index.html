      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 13?

      Five Dead, Hundreds Broken—Where’s the Will to Stop the Killing?

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Precinct 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      Five dead. Twenty-two left with serious injuries. In just over three years, the streets of Precinct 13 have not let up. Cyclists, pedestrians, elders, children—no one is spared. In the last twelve months alone, 327 people were hurt in 590 crashes. Eleven were left with injuries that change a life forever. No one died this year. Not yet. But the numbers do not rest.

      A cyclist, age 23, killed at East 21st and 3rd. A 77-year-old woman, crossing with the signal at Lexington and 29th, struck down by an unlicensed driver making a left turn. A pedestrian, age 48, crushed on Broadway. The stories pile up. The street does not care who you are.

      The Precinct’s Role: Tools Unused

      The police have the power. They can ticket speeders. They can crack down on drivers who run red lights or fail to yield. They can target corners where the blood dries slow. But too often, the work falls to the families left behind.

      After a hit-and-run at 5th and 13th, the victim, Myung Jin Chung, woke up in an ambulance. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” he said. The driver had blown through a red light and fled. The arrest came only after public outcry and video evidence. The system reacts, but it does not prevent.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      Local leaders talk of Vision Zero. They tout new speed cameras, lower limits, and intersection redesigns. But the pace is slow. Cameras need Albany’s blessing to keep running. The city can lower speed limits now, but has not. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program lapsed. The streets wait.

      The family of Abdulhekim Esiyok, killed by a drunk driver on Third Avenue, is left with nothing but grief. “The family is devastated. They’re still in disbelief,” said their lawyer.

      What Next: Demand Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand 20 mph limits. Demand speed cameras that never sleep. Demand the police use every tool they have. Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.
