      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 13?

      Five Dead, Hundreds Broken—Where’s the Will to Stop the Killing?

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Precinct 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      Five dead. Twenty-two left with serious injuries. In just over three years, the streets of Precinct 13 have not let up. Cyclists, pedestrians, elders, children—no one is spared. In the last twelve months alone, 327 people were hurt in 590 crashes. Eleven were left with injuries that change a life forever. No one died this year. Not yet. But the numbers do not rest.

      A cyclist, age 23, killed at East 21st and 3rd. A 77-year-old woman, crossing with the signal at Lexington and 29th, struck down by an unlicensed driver making a left turn. A pedestrian, age 48, crushed on Broadway. The stories pile up. The street does not care who you are.

      The Precinct’s Role: Tools Unused

      The police have the power. They can ticket speeders. They can crack down on drivers who run red lights or fail to yield. They can target corners where the blood dries slow. But too often, the work falls to the families left behind.

      After a hit-and-run at 5th and 13th, the victim, Myung Jin Chung, woke up in an ambulance. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” he said. The driver had blown through a red light and fled. The arrest came only after public outcry and video evidence. The system reacts, but it does not prevent.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      Local leaders talk of Vision Zero. They tout new speed cameras, lower limits, and intersection redesigns. But the pace is slow. Cameras need Albany’s blessing to keep running. The city can lower speed limits now, but has not. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program lapsed. The streets wait.

      The family of Abdulhekim Esiyok, killed by a drunk driver on Third Avenue, is left with nothing but grief. “The family is devastated. They’re still in disbelief,” said their lawyer.

      What Next: Demand Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand 20 mph limits. Demand speed cameras that never sleep. Demand the police use every tool they have. Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Congestion Pricing Tolls Face Legal Showdown
      ▸

      Tolls still hang over Third Avenue. Deadlines pass. Courts hold the answer. Fewer cars enter Manhattan. Millions flow to transit. The fight is bureaucratic, not on the street. Riders wait. The city’s pulse slows, but the outcome is uncertain.

      West Side Spirit reported on May 23, 2025, that New York’s congestion pricing tolls remain in place despite three missed federal deadlines to remove them. The article details a standoff between the MTA, New York State, and U.S. DOT Secretary Sean Duffy, who demands the tolls be taken down, threatening to withhold highway funds. The MTA argues the legality of the tolls and seeks a court injunction, stating, 'congestion pricing is legal and proper.' The system, operational since January 2025, has reduced daily vehicle entries into Manhattan’s core by 76,000 in April and raised $159 million in the first quarter. The dispute highlights tensions over funding priorities and the impact on lower-income drivers, but the courts, not drivers or pedestrians, will decide the fate of the tolls.

      Queensboro Bridge Splits Paths For Safety
      ▸

      Cyclists and walkers get space at last. Years of crowding end. The bridge’s narrow lane forced conflict. Now, riders and pedestrians move apart. The city takes a lane from cars. The danger shrinks. The span breathes easier.

      amNY reported on May 13, 2025, that New York City will separate cyclists and pedestrians on the Ed Koch-Queensboro Bridge. Starting May 18, the north outer roadway becomes bike-only, while the south outer roadway, once for vehicles, opens to pedestrians. The article notes, “Advocates have fought to open a separate pedestrian path, citing dangerous overcrowding that has led to conflicts among cyclists, pedestrians and micromobility users.” The bridge was the last city-owned East River crossing without split paths. Manhattan Community Board 6 urged the city to act, pressing DOT to open the path despite construction delays. The change doubles space for non-drivers and removes a vehicle lane, addressing years of systemic risk from forced mixing of vulnerable users.

      Helicopter Breaks Apart Over Hudson River
      ▸

      Six died when a sightseeing helicopter shattered midair over the Hudson. The fuselage, rotor, and tail tore loose. Loud bangs echoed. The craft plunged. No black box. No warning. Only fragments and silence left behind.

      NY Daily News reported on May 7, 2025, that federal officials released images showing a sightseeing helicopter breaking apart in midair before crashing into the Hudson River, killing six. The National Transportation Safety Board’s preliminary report details how the Bell 206L-4 split into three pieces: 'the fuselage, the main rotor system, and the tail boom.' Witnesses heard 'several loud 'bangs'' before the breakup. The helicopter had flown eight tours that day, all with the same pilot, who had less than 50 hours in this model. The aircraft had a prior maintenance issue with its transmission assembly and lacked flight data recorders. The NTSB noted the pilot wore video-capable sunglasses, but they remain missing. The crash highlights gaps in oversight and the risks of repeated tour flights without robust recording or inspection requirements.
