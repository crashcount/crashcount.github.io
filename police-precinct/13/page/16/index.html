      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 13?

      Five Dead, Thousands Hurt—How Many Bodies Until City Hall Wakes Up?

      Written July 4, 2025

      Precinct 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 4, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Precinct 13, the numbers do not lie. Five people have died on these streets since 2022. Over 1,100 have been injured. Twenty-two suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again. The dead include the old and the young. A 77-year-old woman, crossing with the light, was killed by an SUV making a left turn. The driver was unlicensed. She never made it across Lexington Avenue.

      A cyclist, age 23, was killed at Park Avenue South and East 27th. The car turned left. The cyclist bled out on the street. The city moved on.

      Just last month, a child was hit crossing Avenue C by a distracted SUV driver. The child survived. Many do not.

      The System Responds—Slowly

      The police have the tools. They can write tickets for speeding, for failure to yield, for reckless driving. They can target the corners where people keep dying. But the carnage continues. The numbers do not change.

      After a crash, the police write their report. Sometimes they say, “no criminality was suspected” as they did after a woman was killed by a train at Union Square. The families bury their dead. The city waits for the next one.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Local leaders have spoken. Council Member Erik Bottcher said, “The number of lives lost to traffic violence is simply unacceptable… 127 deaths in this calendar year alone is 127 too many” said Council Member Bottcher. The city has started to lower speed limits in some places. Cameras catch speeders, but the worst repeat offenders keep driving. The police and politicians have the power to act. They need to use it.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit, real enforcement, and street redesigns that put people first. Every day of delay is another life at risk.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 28. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 13?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, and Manhattan CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, and District 4, Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 13?
      Cars and Trucks: 2 deaths, 132 minor injuries, 77 moderate injuries, 7 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 10 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 23 minor injuries, 16 moderate injuries, 3 serious injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 13 police can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and issue tickets for failure to yield. They can target known crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. The tools exist. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, failure to yield, and distracted driving are choices. Better enforcement and safer street design can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and push for real enforcement. They can demand action from city agencies and hold the NYPD accountable for protecting people, not just cars.
