      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 13?

      Five Dead, Hundreds Broken: How Many More Before City Hall Acts?

      Written July 7, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, deduped and reordered citations, fixed FAQ link formatting.)

      Precinct 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      In Precinct 13, violence comes slow, steady, and without warning. Since 2022, five people have died in crashes. Over 1,100 have been hurt. Twenty-two suffered injuries so grave they may never walk the same. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, parents, children. A 77-year-old woman, crossing with the signal at Lexington and 29th, was killed by a left-turning SUV. The driver was unlicensed. She never made it home. NYC Open Data

      Recent Crashes, Unanswered Wounds

      The pain does not fade. In the last year alone, 325 people were injured in 579 crashes. Eleven were left with serious wounds. Cyclists and pedestrians take the brunt. Cars and SUVs are the main weapons—over 200 crashes, seven serious injuries, two deaths. Bikes and mopeds are not blameless, but the numbers are clear: the larger the vehicle, the greater the harm.

      The news keeps coming. Just last week, three NJ Transit buses collided at Port Authority, sending a dozen to the hospital. “One with moderate injuries that required extrication, 10 patients with minor injuries all transported to local area hospitals,” said FDNY Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. The ramp was blocked for hours. The city moved on. The injured did not.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      The city has tools. Speed cameras. Lower limits. More enforcement. But the pace is slow. The police can crack down on speeding, reckless turns, failure to yield. They can target the corners where blood stains the crosswalk. They can write tickets that matter. They just need to act.

      The message from City Hall is clear: “The 32 percent drop in traffic fatalities that we have seen this year is historic… further proof that our Vision Zero efforts are working,” said Mayor Adams. But for the families left behind, the drop is not enough. One death is too many. The work is not done.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand action. Tell them the numbers. Tell them the names. Tell them you want speed limits enforced, reckless drivers off the street, and every crash treated as a preventable tragedy. The slow grind of traffic violence will not stop until we force it to stop.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 13?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, and Manhattan CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, and District 4, Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 13?
      Cars and SUVs: 2 deaths, 7 serious injuries, 218 total crashes. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 14 total crashes. Bikes: 0 deaths, 3 serious injuries, 42 total crashes. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 13 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. The tools exist. They just need to act.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, failure to yield, and reckless driving can be stopped with enforcement and better street design. Every crash is a chance to act before the next one.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, expand automated enforcement, and demand police focus on the most dangerous violations. They can listen to residents and act before tragedy strikes again.
