      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 13?

      Six Dead, a Thousand Hurt—City Hall Hits the Brakes on Safety

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Slow Grind of Loss

      In Precinct 13, the numbers do not lie. Six people killed. Twenty-three left with serious injuries. Over a thousand hurt. The dead do not speak. The injured carry scars the rest of us cannot see. In the last twelve months alone, one person lost their life and twelve more were seriously injured. The disaster moves slow, but it does not stop. NYC crash data

      A bus struck a man at 3rd Avenue and East 28th. He died in the street. The record says “crush injuries.” No more. A cyclist, age 23, was killed at Park Avenue South and East 27th. The record says “severe bleeding.” The city moves on. The families do not. NY Daily News report

      The Machines That Harm

      Cars and SUVs did the most damage. They killed two, seriously injured seven, and hurt over two hundred more. Trucks and buses killed one and hurt twenty. Motorcycles and mopeds left fourteen injured. Bikes caused forty-two injuries, three of them serious. The street is not safe for anyone who walks or rides. NYC crash data

      What Leaders Have Done—And Not Done

      The city has new tools. Speed limits can be lowered. Cameras can catch the reckless. But change comes slow. The police have the power to act. They can ticket speeders. They can crack down on drivers who fail to yield. They can target the corners where people keep getting hurt. They just need to know it matters.

      “One bus made contact with the rear of the other bus,” said Deputy Chief Jason Saffon after a crash at Port Authority left fourteen injured. The words are flat. The pain is not. After another crash, the FDNY explained, “It required some extrication, meaning they had to use some equipment to free the patient.” The tools exist. The will is what’s missing.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 13?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, and Manhattan CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, and District 4, Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 13?
      Cars and SUVs: 2 deaths, 7 serious injuries, 225 total injuries. Trucks and Buses: 1 death, 0 serious injuries, 20 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 14 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 3 serious injuries, 42 injuries.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 13 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. After a recent crash, FDNY explained, “It required some extrication, meaning they had to use some equipment to free the patient.” The tools exist. The will is what matters.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. The same corners see the same pain. Speeding, failure to yield, and unsafe streets can be changed. Policy and enforcement save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, and demand enforcement against reckless drivers. They can push for more speed cameras and hold city agencies accountable for action and results.
