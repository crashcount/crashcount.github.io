      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 13?

      Dead on Arrival: City Hall’s Silence Is Killing Us

      Written July 6, 2025

      Precinct 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Toll in Plain Sight

      In Precinct 13, the numbers do not lie. Five people killed. Twenty-two left with serious injuries. Over a thousand hurt. These are not distant tragedies. They are neighbors, workers, children. In the last year alone, 326 people were injured on these streets. Eleven suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. No one was spared—not the young, not the old, not the ones just crossing with the light.

      A 77-year-old woman was killed crossing Lexington Avenue with the signal. The driver turned left and never saw her. She died on the street. The driver was unlicensed. The record shows only this: “Failure to Yield Right-of-Way” and “Apparent Death” (NYC Open Data).

      Recent Crashes, Unanswered Cries

      The carnage does not pause. In April, a taxi turned left and struck four people on Fifth Avenue. In May, a cyclist was thrown in a collision on East 23rd. The list goes on. Each crash is a wound that never heals. Each one could have been stopped.

      The police have the tools. They can enforce speed limits. They can ticket drivers who fail to yield. They can focus on the corners where people keep getting hit. But the silence is louder than the sirens.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have spoken. Council Member Erik Bottcher said, “The number of lives lost to traffic violence is simply unacceptable… 127 deaths in this calendar year alone is 127 too many. New York City is, and will always be, a city for pedestrians. To ensure the safety of all New Yorkers, we need to implement congestion pricing and continue investing in pedestrian infrastructure citywide.”

      But words are not enough. The city has started to lower speed limits in some places. Cameras catch speeders, but only after the fact. The blood dries before the next press release. Elizabeth Adams of Transportation Alternatives said, “Our leaders are failing New Yorkers. City Hall is maintaining a deadly status quo by falling behind on mandated street safety upgrades.”

      The Call: No More Waiting

      Precinct 13 can act. Police can crack down on speeding and failure to yield. Leaders can demand a 20 mph speed limit, more daylighted corners, and real protection for people walking and biking. Every day of delay is another life at risk.

      Call your council member. Call the precinct. Demand action. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 13 sit politically?
      Precinct 13 is part of borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, assembly district AD 66, and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 13?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, and Manhattan CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, and District 4, Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 13?
      Cars and Trucks were involved in 218 pedestrian injuries and deaths, including 2 deaths and 7 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds were involved in 14 cases. Bikes were involved in 42 pedestrian injuries, including 3 serious injuries. These numbers show that cars and trucks do the most harm, but all modes can injure vulnerable people. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 13 police can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and issue failure-to-yield tickets. They can target crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. Every ticket for speeding or failure to yield is a chance to prevent the next tragedy.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—left turns, failure to yield, speeding. These are preventable. Each crash is a result of choices and conditions that can be changed.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and demand enforcement at dangerous intersections. They can push for more daylighted corners and protected crossings. They can hold city agencies accountable for slow progress.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
