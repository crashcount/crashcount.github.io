      Ocean Parkway: Reckless Drivers, Dead Families, Absent Leaders

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 70: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      A mother and her daughters cross Ocean Parkway. They do not make it home. A driver with 93 violations and a suspended license barrels through, flips her car, and leaves three dead, a family broken, and another family scarred. The boy who survived now lives with pain and fear. His mother sinks into depression. His sister’s head aches. Shakhzod described ongoing back pain and fears of another accident.

      In the last twelve months, one person died and 664 were injured in Precinct 70. Ten suffered serious injuries. Children are not spared. Fifty-two under 18 were hurt, four left with wounds that will not heal. The old are not spared. A 73-year-old woman was killed crossing with the light. A 58-year-old woman was crushed by a truck while walking with the signal. The numbers do not flinch: twelve dead, 2,245 injured, 26 seriously hurt since 2022. NYC Open Data

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and trucks do most of the damage. Four killed by SUVs, one by a sedan, one by a truck. Buses and bikes take their share. The street is a gauntlet. The curb is no refuge. A school bus jumps the sidewalk at Bedford and Farragut, smashing a fence, sending an eight-year-old boy and his mother to the hospital. The bus was waiting for a pedestrian. Then it leapt forward. “The bus was waiting because a pedestrian was in front of the school bus and all of a sudden we heard a boom. The bus jumped over the pavement and crashed.”

      Leadership: Words, Not Enough

      Councilmember Farrah Louis stands at the scene. She says, “We do know we need more traffic safety in this area.” The words are true. The action is slow. The police have the tools. They can ticket speeders, crack down on reckless turns, target the corners where people die. They can make the numbers fall. But the numbers do not fall fast enough.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day without action is another day of blood on the street. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand enforcement. Demand slower speeds. Demand streets where a child can cross and live. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 70 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn. It overlaps Council Districts District 40, District 44, District 45, and District 48, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 42, AD 44, AD 45, and AD 48, and State Senate Districts SD 20, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 70?
      It includes the Flatbush, Flatbush (West)-Ditmas Park-Parkville, Midwood, and Brooklyn CB14 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 40, District 44, District 45, and District 48, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 42, AD 44, AD 45, and AD 48, and State Senate Districts SD 20, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 70?
      Cars and Trucks: 5 deaths, 128 moderate injuries, 10 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 3 deaths, 11 moderate injuries, 3 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 3 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 3 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 70 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue speeding and failure-to-yield tickets, and target known crash hotspots. Councilmember Farrah Louis said, “We do know we need more traffic safety in this area.”
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. The same streets see the same kinds of crashes year after year. Policy and enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can push for lower speed limits, demand better enforcement, fund safer street designs, and hold agencies accountable for action—not just words.
