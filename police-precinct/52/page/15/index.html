      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 52?

      Blood on 52nd’s Streets—How Many Must Die Before City Hall Acts?

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 52: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      In Precinct 52, traffic violence does not pause. Since 2022, 11 people have died and 1,923 have been injured on these streets. Twenty-seven were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. In the last twelve months alone, two lives were lost and 661 people were hurt. The numbers do not flinch.

      Just days ago, a 71-year-old woman was killed when a 79-year-old driver crashed into two cars and a light pole. Seven others were injured. “A 79-year-old driver crashed into two cars in the Bronx before hitting a light pole, killing his passenger and injuring himself and six others, police said” (NY Daily News). On the sidewalk, a witness saw the aftermath: “I saw one lady was out on the ground. They was giving her medical attention, checking her body. She was laid out” (ABC7).

      Who Pays the Price?

      The most vulnerable pay first. In the last year, 69 children were injured. People over 65 were hit 41 times; two did not survive. Cars and SUVs did the most harm, but trucks, vans, and even an ambulance took lives. A van killed a 65-year-old man in a crosswalk. A moped crash killed a 15-year-old girl. The dead do not get a second chance.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Local leaders have tools. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. Speed cameras are renewed. But the work is not finished. Police in Precinct 52 can enforce speed limits, ticket reckless drivers, and target crash hotspots. They can act now. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      The Call

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph limit. Demand real enforcement. Demand that Precinct 52 stops counting bodies and starts saving lives.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 52 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 11, assembly district AD 78 and state senate district SD 31.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 52?
      It includes the University Heights (North)-Fordham, Bedford Park, Norwood, Bronx Park, Bronx CB7, and Bronx CB27 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 11, District 14, and District 15, Assembly Districts AD 78, AD 80, AD 81, AD 86, and AD 87, and State Senate Districts SD 31 and SD 33.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 52?
      Cars and SUVs: 1 death, 218 minor injuries, 75 moderate injuries, 9 serious injuries (total 303 incidents). Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 13 minor injuries, 3 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries (total 18 incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 9 minor injuries, 5 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries (total 14 incidents). Bikes: 0 deaths, 4 minor injuries, 5 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries (total 9 incidents).
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 52 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and respond to dangerous conditions. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and target known crash hotspots. Police have the tools—they just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. They are the result of choices—speed, failure to yield, unsafe streets. Policies and enforcement can prevent them. Every crash is a chance to act before the next one.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and demand enforcement. They can push for a 20 mph limit and real accountability. Their choices save lives or cost them.
