      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Precinct 52?

      Sidewalks Are Not Safe Until City Hall Wakes Up

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 52: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      Six on the Sidewalk, Six in the Hospital

      July 3rd. Courtlandt Avenue and East 149th Street. A Ford Mustang turned, hit a woman in the crosswalk, then mounted the curb and plowed into five more people under the scaffolding. The driver and passenger ran. The car, front smashed, plates gone, left behind. Six neighbors went to the hospital. One witness described the chaos: “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting.” Another said, “We thought it was a bomb or something, because we are all panicking around here.”

      This is not rare. In the last twelve months, Precinct 52 saw 2 deaths, 5 serious injuries, and 665 people hurt in 1,003 crashes. Children, elders, workers. The sidewalk is not safe. The crosswalk is not safe. The numbers pile up. The pain does not fade.

      The Police and the Power to Act

      Precinct 52 has the tools. Speeding tickets. Failure-to-yield tickets. Crackdowns on reckless driving. The power to target crash hotspots. The power to make drivers slow down, or pay. But the danger remains. In the last year, cars and SUVs caused the most harm: 1 death, 9 serious injuries, 293 other injuries to pedestrians. Trucks and buses killed two more. Bikes and mopeds, far less. The threat is heavy and fast.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      City leaders talk about Vision Zero. They pass laws. They promise action. But the blood on the sidewalk says not enough has changed. The city has new speed limits and more cameras. But the crashes keep coming. The Mustang driver ran. The people on the ground could not. The silence from leaders is loud. The police can act. The council can act. The mayor can act. But only if you force them.

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. The numbers are not just numbers. They are neighbors. They are you.

      Call to Action:

      Demand more from Precinct 52. Demand more from your council member, your assembly member, your mayor. Call for speed enforcement. Call for real protection at every crosswalk. Call until the killing stops.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Precinct 52 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 11, assembly district AD 78 and state senate district SD 31.
      ▸ Which areas are in Precinct 52?
      It includes the University Heights (North)-Fordham, Bedford Park, Norwood, Bronx Park, Bronx CB7, and Bronx CB27 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 11, District 14, and District 15, Assembly Districts AD 78, AD 80, AD 81, AD 86, and AD 87, and State Senate Districts SD 31 and SD 33.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 52?
      Cars and SUVs: 1 death, 9 serious injuries, 293 other pedestrian injuries. Trucks and buses: 2 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 13 other injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 9 injuries. Mopeds and motorcycles: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 14 injuries. Cars and trucks are the main threat to people on foot. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 52 can enforce speed limits, issue failure-to-yield tickets, and crack down on reckless driving. They can target known crash hotspots and respond to dangerous conditions. Every ticket, every crackdown, every patrol can save a life. The tools are there. They just need to use them.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Every crash is a failure of policy, enforcement, or design. The numbers show patterns, not random chance. Action saves lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      Local politicians can push for lower speed limits, more automated enforcement, safer street designs, and real accountability for dangerous drivers. They can demand action from the NYPD and DOT. They can listen to residents and act before another person is killed.

      Commissioner Clears Officer In Bronx Killing
      ▸

      An officer shot an unarmed man during a Bronx traffic stop. The NYPD judge called for firing. The commissioner overruled. The man’s family called it outrage. The system shielded the shooter.

      Gothamist (2025-07-03) reports NYPD Commissioner Jessica Tisch overruled a departmental judge and cleared Lt. Jonathan Rivera, who fatally shot Allan Feliz, unarmed, during a 2019 Bronx traffic stop. Judge Maldonado found Rivera's use of deadly force unjustified, writing it 'requires more than the mere possibility of danger.' Tisch disagreed, citing a 'split-second decision' and risk to another officer. The stop began over a seatbelt. Feliz tried to flee; Rivera fired once, killing him. Tisch's decision echoes the state attorney general, who declined charges. The case highlights the commissioner's power over discipline and ongoing debate about police accountability.
