      Northern Boulevard: Blood on Their Hands

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Precinct 109: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Bone

      In Precinct 109, the numbers are blunt. Twenty-two dead. Seventy-three left with serious injuries. Nearly 3,000 hurt since 2022. The dead are not numbers. They are elders crossing Northern Boulevard. They are children struck on their way home. They are cyclists, pinned and ejected, their bikes twisted in the gutter.

      A 78-year-old woman tried to cross Northern Boulevard. A dark minivan hit her and kept going. She died at the hospital. Police reported “A 78-year-old woman was fatally struck by a hit-and-run driver as she crossed a Queens street.” No arrest. No justice. Just another name lost.

      On 32nd Avenue, a car pinned a man beneath its weight. A child, eight or ten, was hit too. The man was critical. The child went to the hospital. Police responded and found an adult man pinned under a vehicle.

      Most of the killing is done by cars and SUVs. Sedans and SUVs alone are behind 10 of the deaths and 24 of the most serious injuries to pedestrians here. Trucks, buses, bikes, mopeds—they all play their part, but the weight of the harm is steel and speed.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      The city has tools. Sammy’s Law lets leaders lower speed limits to 20 mph. Speed cameras can run all day, all night. But change is slow. The council and mayor have the power to act. They have not used it yet. Every day of delay is another risk, another family waiting for a call that will break them.

      The police in Precinct 109 have the means. They can ticket speeders. They can crack down on failure to yield. They can target the corners where blood stains the crosswalk. They just need to act like it matters.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by someone, or by a city that refuses to act. If you live here, call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand lower speed limits. Demand enforcement. Demand that the police treat every death as a crime, not a cost of doing business.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      It belongs to borough Queens. It overlaps District 19 and District 20 in the City Council, Assembly Districts AD 25, AD 26, AD 27, AD 40, and State Senate Districts SD 11 and SD 16.

It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19 and District 20, Assembly Districts AD 25, AD 26, AD 27, and AD 40, and State Senate Districts SD 11 and SD 16.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 109?
      Cars and SUVs: 10 deaths, 24 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 0 deaths, 3 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries. Most harm comes from cars and SUVs.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 109 police can enforce speed limits, issue tickets for failure to yield, and target crash hotspots. They can respond to dangerous conditions and use their authority to crack down on reckless driving. Every ticket, every patrol, every crackdown on a known hotspot can save a life.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. They are not acts of nature. They happen because of choices—by drivers, by city leaders, by those who set policy and enforce it.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, fund safer street designs, and demand police accountability. They can act now, not after another death.
