      How Many Dead Before You Act?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and ensured citation order and deduplication.)

      Precinct 24: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll on the Street

      A woman steps into the crosswalk at Amsterdam and 96th. An ambulance turns left. She does not make it to the other side. Miriam Reinharth, 69, dies from her wounds. The officer tells her family, “The police officer said the accident was not Miriam’s fault at all.”

      A girl, 13, is struck by an SUV at Cathedral Parkway and Manhattan Avenue. She dies at the intersection. A baby boy is killed on the Henry Hudson Parkway. The numbers pile up. In the last twelve months, 2 people have died and 3 suffered serious injuries in Precinct 24. 106 more were hurt.

      Who Bears the Weight

      The dead are not numbers. They are children, elders, neighbors. In three years, 5 people have died on these streets. 434 have been injured. The most vulnerable—pedestrians and cyclists—take the brunt. SUVs, sedans, trucks, bikes: all have left blood on the asphalt. NYC Open Data

      Leadership: Action or Delay?

      The city has tools. Speed cameras. Lower speed limits. Laws that let police ticket reckless drivers. But change crawls. The ambulance driver who killed Miriam was charged months later—failure to yield, failure to exercise due care. The street where she died is a double-wide truck route. It stays dangerous.

      Local leaders talk of Vision Zero. They promise safer streets. But the carnage continues. Precinct 24 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless turns, target crash hotspots. They can write tickets for failure to yield. They can make it clear: these deaths are not fate.

      What Comes Next

      Every day of delay is another risk. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand enforcement. Demand safer streets. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Take action now.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Precinct 24?
      Cars and Trucks: 3 deaths, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries.
      ▸ What can police do to protect vulnerable road users here?
      Precinct 24 can enforce speed limits, crack down on reckless driving, and target known crash hotspots. They can issue tickets for speeding and failure to yield, and respond quickly to dangerous conditions. The tools exist. They just need to be used.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. They are preventable. Enforcement, safer street design, and lower speeds save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand automated enforcement, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can push for action, not just words.
