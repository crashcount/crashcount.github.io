      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn Blood on Their Hands: Cars Kill, Leaders Stall

      Written July 16, 2025

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Brooklyn, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, 207 people have died in crashes. Over 44,000 have been injured. More than 500 suffered life-changing injuries. The dead include children, elders, and people just trying to cross the street. The city calls them accidents. The families know better.

      Just days ago, two men were killed in Sunset Park. They were crossing 3rd Avenue at 52nd Street when a BMW ran the red light and hit them. The driver fled. Both men died at the scene. They were on their way to a food pantry. “We are deeply saddened by the tragic deaths of two individuals in Sunset Park this morning,” said City Harvest. “They may have been on their way to our Mobile Market simply trying to access food like so many New Yorkers in need.”

      A week before, an eight-year-old boy was killed at Eastern Parkway and Albany Avenue. A sedan struck a 70-year-old man at Avenue I and East 5th Street. A moped rider died on East 35th Street. The list does not end.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      Brooklyn’s leaders have taken some steps. The city has lowered speed limits in some zones and expanded speed cameras. The NYPD has cracked down on ghost cars and reckless drivers. But the carnage continues. In the last 12 months, 61 people died and 175 were seriously injured in Brooklyn crashes.

      Too often, the focus shifts to blaming cyclists or pedestrians. But the numbers are clear. Cars and trucks are the main killers. The city’s own data shows that sedans and SUVs caused the most deaths and injuries to pedestrians. Enforcement must target the real threat.

      The Call for Action

      The time for half-measures is over. Every day, another family is broken. “The line is really long. So, they get there early so they can get their stuff early,” said Mike Gonzalez, describing the food pantry where two men died. No one should die trying to feed their family.

      Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand enforcement against reckless drivers, not the people on foot or bike. Join groups like Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 38, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 17. See ABC7 coverage.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Brooklyn CB8, and District 38 neighborhoods. See ABC7 coverage.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Cars and trucks (sedans, SUVs, trucks, buses, vans, taxis) caused the most deaths and injuries to pedestrians. Motorcycles and mopeds caused fewer but still deadly crashes. Bikes were involved in some injuries and a few deaths. The city’s own data shows cars and trucks are the main killers.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement against reckless drivers save lives. Every crash is a policy failure, not just bad luck.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits citywide, expand automated enforcement, redesign streets for safety, and focus enforcement on reckless drivers—not people walking or biking. They can pass and fund laws that protect the most vulnerable.

      ▸

      City will rip out a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. The lane calmed a deadly stretch. Drivers still parked illegally. Children darted into traffic. Now, cyclists and pedestrians face more danger. Policy shifts, safety slips. Streets stay lethal.

The decision highlights persistent driver violations and policy gaps that leave vulnerable road users exposed.

      NYC DOT Targets Intersection Danger Zones
      ▸

      City crews will block cars from corners. Barriers, granite, planters—hard lines drawn. Sightlines open. Pedestrians and cyclists step forward, visible. Intersections, once blind, now clear. The city moves to cut the toll where most blood spills.

      amNY reported on June 11, 2025, that the NYC Department of Transportation will redesign intersections to protect pedestrians and cyclists. The plan uses 'hardened daylighting'—physical barriers, granite blocks, and planters—to stop vehicles from parking near corners. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety.' Nearly half of city traffic deaths happen at intersections. The new design will first appear at high-crash sites in Brooklyn, including Ocean Avenue at Church Avenue and several others. The move highlights a systemic effort to address visibility failures and reduce deadly conflicts at crossings.
