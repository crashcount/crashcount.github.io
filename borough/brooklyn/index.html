      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn Bleeds: Lives Lost, Leaders Silent

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, and cleaned citations.)

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Brooklyn, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, 207 people have died on the borough’s streets. Over 44,000 have been injured. 513 suffered wounds so grave they changed lives forever.

      The dead are not numbers. They are children, elders, neighbors. On June 28, an eight-year-old boy was killed crossing Eastern Parkway. The driver kept going straight. The boy did not get up. NYC Open Data.

      Just days later, two men—one 80, one 59—were struck and killed by a BMW that ran a red light on Third Avenue. The driver sped away. Police found him later. The charges: manslaughter, criminally negligent homicide, reckless driving. “EMS pronounced both men dead at the scene. Their identities are being withheld pending family notification,” reported amNY.

      Reckless Acts, Broken Families

      The violence is not always anonymous. On July 13, a young man did donuts in a mall parking lot. He lost control. His girlfriend, 21, was sitting on the curb. He struck her. She died at the hospital. “He lost control when he was doing donuts with the vehicle,” according to NY Daily News.

      A moped rider, 34, was killed on E 35th Street. A 36-year-old man was beaten with a metal object after a crash in Cypress Hills. The violence does not end with the collision. It spills out into the street, into families, into memory.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Brooklyn’s leaders have tools. The city now has the power to lower speed limits. Speed cameras run 24/7. The NYPD has cracked down on ghost cars and reckless drivers. But the carnage continues. Every week brings new deaths. Every week, another family waits for a call that never comes.

      Some leaders call for more enforcement. Some call for better street design. Some say nothing at all. The silence is loud. The streets stay dangerous.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat offenders. Join Families for Safe Streets. Stand with the living. Remember the dead. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 38, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Brooklyn CB8, and District 38 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 37, District 38, District 39, District 42, District 44, District 45, District 46, District 47, and District 48, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 46, AD 51, AD 59, and AD 60, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 18, SD 19, SD 20, SD 21, SD 22, SD 23, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Cars and SUVs: 72 deaths, 6,935 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 4 deaths, 239 injuries. Bikes: 3 deaths, 285 injuries. Cars and SUVs do the most harm by far. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      They are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement against reckless driving save lives. The same deadly patterns repeat because policy allows them.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets, expand automated enforcement, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can listen to victims and act before the next death.

      Adams Removes Bedford Avenue Bike Lane
      ▸

      City will rip out a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. The lane calmed a deadly stretch. Drivers still parked illegally. Children darted into traffic. Now, cyclists and pedestrians face more danger. Policy shifts, safety slips. Streets stay lethal.

      Streetsblog NYC reported on June 13, 2025, that Mayor Adams will remove the protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue, a corridor known for high crash rates. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'before this bike lane from 2018 to 2022 this is the area that had the second largest numbers of pedestrian fatalities and crashes in the city.' The lane faced opposition from some Hasidic leaders, citing children running into the lane from illegally parked cars and buses. Despite tweaks—school bus zones, daylighted corners, no standing areas—drivers kept parking illegally, blocking sightlines and endangering children. The city rarely removes protected lanes, but Adams acted after political pressure. Moving the lane to Classon Avenue, as some demand, would force cyclists onto a chaotic BQE ramp. The decision highlights persistent driver violations and policy gaps that leave vulnerable road users exposed.
