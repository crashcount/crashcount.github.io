      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn Bleeds: Speed Kills, Leaders Stall

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Brooklyn, the bodies do not stop. In the last twelve months, 61 people have died in crashes. More than 13,600 have been hurt. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. Just two weeks ago, an 8-year-old boy was killed crossing Eastern Parkway. His head struck by an SUV. He never made it home. NYC Open Data

      Last week, a 90-year-old man tried to cross Avenue U. A blue moped hit him. The driver wore a mask. He left the old man bleeding in the street. “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open,” said a witness. The man died at the hospital. The driver is still gone.

      The Numbers Are Names

      The violence is relentless. In the past year, Brooklyn saw 178 people seriously hurt. The dead are old and young: 5 children, 3 elders over 75, and dozens in between. Cars and SUVs kill most. Trucks, bikes, mopeds—they all take their share. For every death, hundreds more are left with broken bodies.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has moved, but not fast enough. Speed cameras now run 24/7. The law lets New York set its own speed limits. But the default is still not 20 mph. The city sprinkles slow zones, but most streets stay fast. After the latest hit-and-run, Assemblyman Novakhov called for all fast motorized vehicles to be registered and insured: “It would be much easier to find” the killer.

      But the killers keep driving. The dead keep coming. “They just don’t care,” said a neighbor. The city cracks down on cyclists, but the cars and mopeds still run red.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit on every street. Demand real enforcement for the drivers who kill and flee. Join the fight. Do not wait for another child, another elder, another neighbor to die. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 38, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Brooklyn CB8, and District 38 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 37, District 38, District 39, District 42, District 44, District 45, District 46, District 47, and District 48, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 46, AD 51, AD 59, and AD 60, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 18, SD 19, SD 20, SD 21, SD 22, SD 23, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Most deaths and injuries come from Cars and SUVs (72 deaths, 6,934 injuries), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (1 death, 238 injuries), and Bikes (3 deaths, 285 injuries) since 2022. Trucks and buses caused 22 deaths and 601 injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These deaths and injuries are not fate. Speed limits, street design, and enforcement can prevent them. The numbers drop where real action is taken.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can set a 20 mph speed limit, expand automated enforcement, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws that target the most dangerous drivers. They can choose to act now.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      NYC DOT Targets Intersection Danger Zones
      ▸

      City will block cars from corners. Barriers, granite, planters will clear sightlines. DOT acts after half of deaths hit at intersections. Brooklyn gets first fixes. Cyclists and walkers may see drivers before impact. Steel and stone replace painted lines.

      The Brooklyn Paper reported on June 11, 2025, that NYC DOT will redesign intersections to protect cyclists and pedestrians. The plan uses 'hardened daylighting'—physical barriers, granite blocks, and planters—to stop cars from parking near corners. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety.' Nearly half of city traffic deaths happen at intersections, often due to blocked sightlines and turning vehicles. The redesign targets high-crash Brooklyn intersections first, including Ocean Avenue at Church Avenue. The move highlights the city's shift from painted lines to physical infrastructure, aiming to reduce systemic risk for vulnerable road users.
