      Brooklyn’s Streets Are Killing Us—Who Will Stop the Bloodshed?

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      In Brooklyn, the slow bleed continues. Since January 2022, 196 people have died on Brooklyn streets. Over 42,000 have been injured. 478 suffered serious injuries—the kind that change a life, or end it. In the last 12 months alone, 53 people died and 160 were seriously hurt. Children are not spared: 4 killed, 18 seriously injured in a year. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. A mother crossing Marcus Garvey. A passenger ejected on Avenue U. A baby in a stroller on Kings Highway.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      The carnage does not pause. On May 24, a 32-year-old woman was killed by an SUV on Van Buren Street. On May 28, a 38-year-old woman died behind the wheel on Ditmas Avenue. On June 12, a 42-year-old man was killed in a motorcycle crash on Avenue P. The violence is relentless, the stories the same.

      Even the police are not immune. On June 15, a drunk driver in a Chevy Suburban slammed into a Volvo, then into a marked NYPD cruiser at Coney Island Avenue and Avenue U. Two officers and two passengers went to the hospital. One officer suffered a broken pelvis and arm, another a broken hip. A passenger was ejected and left in critical condition. Police charged the driver with DWI and reckless endangerment. One police officer suffered a broken pelvis and arm, as well as head trauma, while another suffered a broken hip, according to police reported by NY Daily News.

      Leadership: Action, Delay, and Retreat

      Local leaders talk of safety, but action is slow. The city has added protected bike lanes and lowered some speed limits. But when push comes to shove, safety is too often the first thing cut. On June 13, Mayor Adams and DOT removed three blocks of the Bedford Avenue protected bike lane after local pushback. Council Member Lincoln Restler called it “a purely political decision to rip out a bike lane with no alternative.” For months, the Mayor has repeatedly ignored our recommendations for ensuring the safety of children on Bedford Avenue, Restler said.

      What Next: The Cost of Waiting

      Speed kills. Delay kills. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has the power to protect bike lanes and expand camera enforcement. Every day of delay is another day of blood on the street. Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand action. Take action now.
