      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn Bleeds While City Stalls: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written July 21, 2025

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 21, 2025

      The Toll This Month

      In Brooklyn, the street never rests. Just days ago, an 11-year-old boy was hit by a car near a playground in Sheepshead Bay. He survived. Many do not. Police are still investigating.

      A week before, a cyclist was struck on McGuinness Boulevard. The driver turned across an unprotected bike lane. The city had promised safety. The city did not deliver. “The fact is that this wouldn’t be a problem if Mayor Adams had just done what he had promised to do, which was, make all of McGuinness safe,” said Kevin LaCherra. The cyclist lived. Others have not.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the past 12 months, Brooklyn saw 74 people killed and over 15,000 injured in crashes. Children are not spared: 7 killed, 1,316 injured. The old are not spared: 13 dead, 779 hurt, just in the 65–74 age group. Serious injuries cut across every age. The city counts the bodies. The city counts the broken.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      The city passed new laws. Some streets are being rebuilt. But the work is slow. Promises pile up. Asphalt stays the same. “The road diet works where it’s been installed and it’s needed for the entire corridor before this happens again,” LaCherra said. The mayor cut the plan short. The unprotected lane stayed. The crash came.

      What Comes Next

      Every crash is preventable. Every delay is a choice. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand slower speeds. Demand action before the next child, the next elder, the next neighbor is lost.

      Do not wait for another headline. Make them act.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 38, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Brooklyn CB8, and District 38 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 37, District 38, District 39, District 42, District 44, District 45, District 46, District 47, and District 48, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 46, AD 51, AD 59, and AD 60, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 18, SD 19, SD 20, SD 21, SD 22, SD 23, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Most injuries and deaths come from SUVs and Cars (77 deaths, 7,223 injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (25 deaths, 632 injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (4 deaths, 246 injuries), and Bikes (3 deaths, 289 injuries).
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Every crash is preventable. The data shows patterns—speed, lack of protection, and failure to yield. These are not random acts. They are the result of choices and policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for slower speeds, build protected bike lanes, redesign dangerous streets, and hold reckless drivers accountable. Delay is deadly.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
