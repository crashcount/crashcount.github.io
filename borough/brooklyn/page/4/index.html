      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn’s Streets Run Red—How Many More Must Die?

      Written July 13, 2025

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Toll in Brooklyn

      Brooklyn bleeds every week. In the last twelve months, 61 people died on its streets. Over 13,500 were hurt. One hundred seventy-five suffered injuries so severe they changed a life forever. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared.

      Just days ago, on July 11, two men tried to cross 3rd Avenue at 52nd Street. A BMW ran the red. The driver did not stop. Kex Un Chen, 80, and Faqiu Lin, 59, died where they fell. The man behind the wheel left car parts in the street and bodies behind. He told police, “I had a six pack of Modelos and two drinks. I was driving the car by myself last night. It’s my fault. After I hit them, I came home.”

      Neighbors know the truth: “Drivers speed on that stretch of roadway.”

      The Pattern

      This is not rare. In the last year, Brooklyn saw 21,604 crashes. Five children died. Eleven elders aged 65 to 74. The dead do not get a second chance. The living carry scars.

      Most deaths come at the hands of cars and SUVs—72 killed, 6,935 hurt. Trucks and buses took 22 more. Motorcycles and mopeds killed one, bikes three. The numbers do not lie. The danger is not spread evenly. It falls hardest on those walking, riding, or too young to drive.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Some steps forward: The city has lowered speed limits in select zones. Speed cameras now run 24/7. More intersections are cleared for sightlines. But the carnage continues. Laws target the reckless, but too many drivers slip through. Enforcement is uneven.

      When the city cracks down, it is often on the wrong people. Cyclists get court summonses for running lights, while drivers with a record of tickets keep their keys. Community boards call this out: “It is unjust to hold cyclists to a far higher standard than motor vehicle drivers.”

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. Every death is preventable. The city has the power to set a 20 mph speed limit. It can take the worst repeat offenders off the road. It can build streets that forgive mistakes. But only if you demand it.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. Do not wait for another body in the crosswalk. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 38, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Brooklyn CB8, and District 38 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 37, District 38, District 39, District 42, District 44, District 45, District 46, District 47, and District 48, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 46, AD 51, AD 59, and AD 60, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 18, SD 19, SD 20, SD 21, SD 22, SD 23, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Cars and Trucks: 72 deaths, 6,935 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 239 injuries. Bikes: 3 deaths, 285 injuries. Most deaths and injuries come from cars, SUVs, trucks, and buses. Source: NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. Every death and serious injury is preventable. Speed, reckless driving, and unsafe streets are choices, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets, expand automated enforcement, and remove repeat dangerous drivers. They can focus enforcement on the most dangerous violations, not on cyclists and pedestrians.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How many people have been killed or seriously hurt in Brooklyn recently?
      In the last twelve months, 61 people died and 175 suffered serious injuries in Brooklyn traffic crashes. Source: NYC Open Data
      ▸ How can I take action?
      Call your council member and the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit, safer street design, and real accountability for repeat dangerous drivers. Take action now.
