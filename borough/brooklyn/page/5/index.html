      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn Bleeds—City Stalls. Demand Action Before Another Child Dies.

      Written July 24, 2025

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 24, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Blood

      In Brooklyn, the street is a gauntlet. Since January 2022, 225 people have died in crashes. 542 suffered serious injuries. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about age. Fourteen children are dead. Fifty-four more are maimed. The old are not spared. Thirty-six people over 65 have died. The city keeps moving. The bodies keep coming.

      Just two weeks ago, two men were killed crossing Third Avenue. The driver ran the red and kept going. The city put up a sign: “Be careful.” State Senator Andrew Gounardes did not mince words: “We should all be offended. Angry. Insulted. That the response from our city to two deaths blocks away from here is a sign that says: ‘be careful.’”

      On Bedford Avenue, an 11-year-old boy was hit near a playground. He survived. This time. The police are still investigating. There are no answers. Only the sound of sirens and the hush that follows. ABC7 reported the boy is in stable condition.

      Promises, Pauses, and Political Drift

      The city has plans. They stall. Third Avenue was supposed to be rebuilt. The plan is on hold. Assemblymember Marcela Mitaynes asked, “We started talking about a plan in 2014 and it’s now 2025. What is going on?” There is no answer. Only more funerals.

      Some leaders speak up. Councilmember Alexa Avilés called for real investment: “We want the city to actually commit real capital to make sure that it can do treatments that truly address the safety concerns while balancing the fact that we have an industrial business zone here.”

      But the street stays the same. The cars keep coming. The dead keep piling up.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by someone in power. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns, not more signs. Do not wait for another child’s name in the news.

      Act now. The blood on the street is not washed away by words.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn sit politically?
      Brooklyn belongs to city council district District 38, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn?
      Brooklyn includes Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Brooklyn CB8, and District 38. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 37, District 38, District 39, District 42, District 44, District 45, District 46, District 47, and District 48, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 46, AD 51, AD 59, and AD 60, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 18, SD 19, SD 20, SD 21, SD 22, SD 23, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Most pedestrian injuries and deaths in Brooklyn came from cars and SUVs (81 deaths, 7,299 injuries), followed by trucks and buses (25 deaths, 634 injuries), motorcycles and mopeds (2 deaths, 248 injuries), and bikes (3 deaths, 291 injuries).
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. They are the result of choices—by drivers and by leaders who set policy. Every death is preventable with safer streets, lower speeds, and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, fund protected bike lanes and crossings, and hold reckless drivers accountable. They can act now, or answer for the next death.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
