      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn Bleeds. City Sleeps. Who Will Wake Up Before More Die?

      Written June 23, 2025 (Revised June 22, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 23, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Blood

      A woman steps out of her car in Bedford-Stuyvesant. She is pregnant. She is struck, dragged, and left to die. Her name is Tiffany Cifuni. Her family is left with silence and rage. “She’s a coward. She’s a monster,” said James Cifuni. In another corner of Brooklyn, a moped rider named Joel Mota is killed by a drunk, unlicensed driver. His brother remembers, “He never stopped working.” Francisco Mota said.

      In the last 12 months, 52 people have died on Brooklyn streets. Over 12,600 have been injured. 160 suffered injuries so severe they will not walk the same again. The dead include children, elders, and workers heading home. The numbers do not stop. The pain does not fade.

      Who Pays, Who Acts

      Cars and SUVs killed 67. Trucks and buses killed 22. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 4. Bikes killed 3. The city counts the bodies. The city counts the broken. The city moves on.

      Local leaders speak of Vision Zero. They pass laws. They praise speed cameras and lower speed limits. But the carnage continues. Some leaders fight for safer streets. Some fight to remove bike lanes. Some say nothing at all. The silence is loud. The delays are deadly.

      The Fight for Safety

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure of will. The city has the power to lower speed limits. The city can build more protected space for people who walk and bike. The city can keep cameras running and hold reckless drivers to account. But power unused is no power at all.

      Call your council member. Demand action. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand that every child, every elder, every worker gets home alive. Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn?
      Brooklyn covers a wide stretch of neighborhoods, including Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Brooklyn CB8, and District 38.       ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Cars and SUVs: 67 deaths, 6,696 injuries. Trucks and buses: 22 deaths, 581 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 4 deaths, 226 injuries. Bikes: 3 deaths, 276 injuries. Cars and SUVs are the main killers.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. Every crash is preventable. Speed, reckless driving, and unsafe streets can be changed. These are not accidents. They are failures to protect people.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, build protected bike lanes, keep speed cameras running, and hold reckless drivers accountable. They can act now or let more people die.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in Brooklyn recently?
      In the last 12 months, 52 people died and 160 suffered serious injuries on Brooklyn streets. Over 12,600 were injured.

      Speeding Sedan Slams Dump Truck, Passenger Killed
      ▸

      A sedan tore down Morgan Avenue. It smashed into a dump truck’s rear. Metal screamed. The front passenger, 22, died from a head wound. Another passenger, 21, broke his arm. The car was wrecked. Speed and ignored signals led to blood and silence.

      A sedan traveling north on Morgan Avenue near Division Place in Brooklyn struck the rear of a dump truck. According to the police report, the sedan was moving at unsafe speed and disregarded traffic control. The front passenger, age 22, suffered a fatal head injury and died at the scene. Another passenger, age 21, sustained a fractured arm. The sedan was demolished. The police report lists 'Unsafe Speed' and 'Traffic Control Disregarded' as contributing factors. The front passenger’s airbag deployed during the crash. No driver or passenger actions beyond these errors are cited in the report.
