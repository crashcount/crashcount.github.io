      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn Bleeds While Politicians Stall

      Written August 4, 2025

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 4, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Brooklyn, the bodies keep piling up. Fifty-four people killed on the borough’s streets this year alone. Over 9,500 injured. One hundred twenty-nine left with wounds so deep they will not heal. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the sound of sirens at 3 a.m. and the hush that follows.

      Just weeks ago, two men tried to cross Third Avenue in Sunset Park. The driver ran the red. He did not stop. Both men died. Since 2018, drivers have killed or seriously injured 80 people on this two-mile stretch. State Senator Andrew Gounardes called out the city’s inaction: “We should all be offended. Angry. Insulted. That the response from our city to two deaths blocks away from here is a sign that says: ‘be careful.’” The city’s answer is a sign. The street stays the same.

      A week later, a 47-year-old man tried to cross Broadway at Suydam. A driver hit him and kept going. Police found the man dead in the street. Police reported: “A driver struck and killed a 47-year-old pedestrian crossing a Brooklyn street, then left the scene.” The driver is still out there.

      Promises and Pauses

      Local leaders talk. The streets do not change. Councilmember Alexa Avilés called for real investment: “We want the city to actually commit real capital to make sure that it can do treatments that truly address the safety concerns while balancing the fact that we have an industrial business zone here.” Plans for a safer Third Avenue have been stalled since 2014. Assemblymember Marcela Mitaynes asked: “We started talking about a plan in 2014 and it’s now 2025. What is going on?”

      The Cost of Delay

      Every week brings new names. The dead are old and young. A 90-year-old man killed by a moped on Avenue U. A 21-year-old woman crushed by a sedan on Gateway Drive. Children, elders, workers. No one is spared.

      The city removes protected bike lanes. Cyclists are left exposed. The city posts warnings. The drivers keep driving.

      Act Now: Demand Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand the city finish what it started. Every day of delay is another body in the road.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 38, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Brooklyn CB8, and District 38 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 37, District 38, District 39, District 42, District 44, District 45, District 46, District 47, and District 48, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 46, AD 51, AD 59, and AD 60, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 18, SD 19, SD 20, SD 21, SD 22, SD 23, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Cars and Trucks: SUVs and cars caused 81 deaths and over 7,300 injuries. Trucks and buses caused 25 deaths and over 600 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: Motorcycles and mopeds caused 2 deaths and over 250 injuries. Bikes: Bicycles caused 3 deaths and nearly 300 injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. The same streets see deaths and injuries year after year. Delayed redesigns and lack of enforcement let it happen again.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, redesign dangerous roads, and push for enforcement against reckless drivers. They can stop stalling and start building safer streets.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Brooklyn

      Adams Removes Bedford Avenue Bike Lane
      ▸

      City will rip out a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. The lane calmed a deadly stretch. Drivers still parked illegally. Children darted into traffic. Now, cyclists and pedestrians face more danger. Policy shifts, safety slips. Streets stay lethal.

      Streetsblog NYC reported on June 13, 2025, that Mayor Adams will remove the protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue, a corridor known for high crash rates. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'before this bike lane from 2018 to 2022 this is the area that had the second largest numbers of pedestrian fatalities and crashes in the city.' The lane faced opposition from some Hasidic leaders, citing children running into the lane from illegally parked cars and buses. Despite tweaks—school bus zones, daylighted corners, no standing areas—drivers kept parking illegally, blocking sightlines and endangering children. The city rarely removes protected lanes, but Adams acted after political pressure. Moving the lane to Classon Avenue, as some demand, would force cyclists onto a chaotic BQE ramp. The decision highlights persistent driver violations and policy gaps that leave vulnerable road users exposed.
