      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn Streets Still Kill—Leaders Still Stall

      Written July 14, 2025

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 14, 2025

      The Toll in Brooklyn: Lives Lost, Streets Unchanged

      Brooklyn bleeds in the crosswalks and at the curb. In the last twelve months, 61 people died on Brooklyn streets. More than 13,500 were hurt. One hundred seventy-five suffered injuries so severe they will never be the same. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. An eight-year-old boy was killed at Eastern Parkway and Albany Avenue just weeks ago. A sedan, a crosswalk, a child’s life ended.

      Just days later, two men were struck and killed at Third Avenue and 52nd Street. The car ran a red light. The driver did not stop. Both victims died where they fell. The suspect was charged with manslaughter, criminally negligent homicide, and reckless driving. “The vehicle was traveling south on Third Avenue when it struck the pedestrians and sped away,” police said. The crash site has seen 59 collisions and 80 injuries in five years. Hunger brought the men to the food pantry. A speeding car sent them home in body bags.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Brooklyn’s leaders have the tools. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can now set its own speed limits. The law sits unused. DOT has started to lower speeds near schools, but the default remains too high. Cameras catch speeders, but the dead stay dead.

      The city touts a drop in fatalities, but the bodies still pile up. “There have been at least 98 traffic deaths citywide this year, down from 135 by the same point last year,” said the NYPD. The numbers shrink, but the pain does not. Enforcement ramps up, but the most dangerous drivers keep killing.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another family broken. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It can redesign streets, protect crossings, and end the reign of repeat speeders. But it will not happen unless you demand it.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them: no more waiting, no more excuses. The dead cannot speak. You must speak for them.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 38, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Brooklyn CB8, and District 38 neighborhoods.       ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Cars and Trucks: Cars and SUVs were involved in 72 pedestrian deaths and 6,935 injuries. Trucks and buses caused 22 deaths and 601 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: These vehicles caused 1 death and 239 injuries. Bikes: Bicycles were involved in 3 deaths and 285 injuries. Source
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Speeding, red-light running, and unsafe street design are choices. Policies like lower speed limits and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws to stop repeat speeders. They can act now, or answer for every life lost to delay.
