      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn: A Night, A Body, A Pattern

      Written August 29, 2025 (Revised August 29, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 29, 2025

      Just after 1 AM on Aug 9, 2025, at Ocean Parkway and Avenue C, an SUV hit a 45‑year‑old woman. She died there in the road (NYC Open Data).

      They were one of 234 people killed on Brooklyn streets since Jan 1, 2022, through Aug 29, 2025, with 48,719 injured and 583 seriously hurt in that span (NYC Open Data). The toll does not pause. So far this year, Brooklyn has seen 62 deaths, up from 38 at this point last year, with crashes up 21% and serious injuries up 74.7% (NYC Open Data).

      “Police are investigating three separate car crashes that left two people dead and critically injured several others,” reported Gothamist. One of those was the Pitkin Avenue crash where a car hit a moped and the rider died; police said charges were pending (Gothamist).

      Ocean Parkway, then Morgan Avenue

      Four days earlier, just before 8 AM on Aug 6, a box truck going straight on Morgan Avenue struck a 46‑year‑old man outside an intersection. He died of crush injuries (NYC Open Data). The truck was registered in Tennessee. The man never made it across Maspeth Avenue.

      That same morning, at 18th Avenue and 86th Street, a 76‑year‑old woman in the crosswalk was killed. The data lists “Traffic Control Disregarded” as a factor (NYC Open Data).

      Pitkin Avenue before dawn

      On Aug 5, just before 4 AM, a 29‑year‑old motorcyclist on Pitkin Avenue was ejected and killed. The record cites unsafe speed and improper lane use. He wore a helmet. He still died (NYC Open Data).

      “Police were looking … for the driver of a car that struck and dragged a 47‑year‑old man” on Broadway at Suydam around 3:45 AM, said Gothamist. He was dragged more than 50 feet. The driver fled. NYPD sources told the Daily News the vehicle might have been a garbage truck.

      The numbers don’t blink

      In Brooklyn, cars and SUVs have injured over 7,000 pedestrians in this period, with at least 83 pedestrian deaths tied to those vehicle types in the dataset’s rollup; trucks add 20 pedestrian deaths (NYC Open Data). The year‑to‑date ledger shows 16,846 crashes, 62 deaths, 10,828 injuries, and 159 serious injuries as of Aug 29, 2025, compared to 13,912 crashes, 38 deaths, 8,170 injuries, and 91 serious injuries at this point last year (NYC Open Data).

      Who moves now

      New York has tools. Albany renewed 24/7 school‑zone speed cameras through 2030, and advocates are pressing for the citywide 20 MPH default and devices that cap speeds for repeat offenders. A tiny pool of drivers causes outsized harm; repeat camera tickets flag the worst risk (CrashCount Take Action).

      Brooklyn’s electeds for this area include Council Member Alexa Avilés, Assembly Member Kalman Yeger (AD 41), and State Senator Steve Chan (SD 17). The record provided here does not list their sponsorship on a speed‑limiter bill. The deaths above sit inside their districts. What gives?

      Lower the default speed. Stop the super speeders. Start with one call. Start today. Here’s how.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets: Crashes (h9gi-nx95), Persons (f55k-p6yu), and Vehicles (bm4k-52h4). We filtered for dates 2022-01-01 to 2025-08-29 and borough = Brooklyn. We counted deaths, injuries, serious injuries, and total crashes from the Persons table and used the year-to-date comparison provided in the context. Data was accessed Aug 29, 2025. You can start from the Persons table here and apply the same filters.
      ▸ Why focus on Ocean Parkway, Morgan Avenue, and Pitkin Avenue?
      They are specific fatal cases in August 2025 documented in NYC Open Data: Ocean Parkway at Avenue C (CrashID 4833650), Morgan Ave at Maspeth Ave (CrashID 4833207), and Pitkin Ave at Strauss St (CrashID 4833031).
      ▸ What does “serious injury” mean here?
      We use the dataset’s “serious injury” classification from the Persons table. It reflects the NYPD/EMS coding in the open data. We did not reinterpret injury severity.
      ▸ Which officials represent this area?
      According to the context, Council Member Alexa Avilés (District 38), Assembly Member Kalman Yeger (AD 41), and State Senator Steve Chan (SD 17) are listed for this geography. The context we have does not include their sponsorship positions on a speed‑limiter bill.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
