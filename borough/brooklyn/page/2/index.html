      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Blood in the Crosswalk, Silence in City Hall

      Written August 6, 2025 (Revised August 6, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 6, 2025

      The Blood on Broadway

      Just days ago, a man was struck and dragged more than fifty feet at the corner of Broadway and Suydam in Bushwick. He died in the street. The driver kept going. Police are still looking for the killer. Police were looking for the driver who struck and dragged a 47-year-old man on a Brooklyn street over the weekend, killing him. The victim’s name has not been released. The city moves on. The blood dries. The driver is gone.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      In the last twelve months, 81 people died on Brooklyn’s streets. More than 15,900 were hurt. Over 200 suffered injuries so severe they changed lives forever. Children, elders, workers, strangers. The dead are not numbers. They are fathers, daughters, friends. Each one is a hole in a family.

      Leaders Talk. Streets Stay Deadly.

      After two pedestrians were killed in a hit-and-run in Sunset Park, the calls for change grew louder. “We wait until someone dies. We wait until a tragedy. We wait to say, ‘oh my gosh, how could this possibly have happened?’ We let this happen time and time again,” said State Senator Andrew Gounardes. Plans to fix Third Avenue have been stalled for more than a decade. “We started talking about a plan in 2014 and it’s now 2025. What is going on?” asked Assemblymember Marcela Mitaynes. The city promises, then pauses. The street stays the same. The bodies keep coming.

      What Now? No More Waiting.

      Every death is preventable. Lower the speed limit. Build real protection for people on foot and bike. End the delays. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. The next victim is already walking.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 38, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Brooklyn CB8, and District 38 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 37, District 38, District 39, District 42, District 44, District 45, District 46, District 47, and District 48, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 46, AD 51, AD 59, and AD 60, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 18, SD 19, SD 20, SD 21, SD 22, SD 23, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Cars and SUVs: Caused 82 deaths and 7,364 injuries. Trucks and buses: 25 deaths and 639 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 2 deaths and 255 injuries. Bikes: 3 deaths and 292 injuries. (Source: NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Every death is preventable. Streets can be designed to slow cars, protect people, and save lives. Delay is a choice.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, and push for enforcement that targets dangerous drivers—not people walking or biking. They can stop delaying and start building streets that protect the most vulnerable.

      NYC DOT Targets Intersection Danger Zones
      ▸

      City crews will block cars from corners. Barriers, granite, planters—hard lines drawn. Sightlines open. Pedestrians and cyclists step forward, visible. Intersections, once blind, now clear. The city moves to cut the toll where most blood spills.

      amNY reported on June 11, 2025, that the NYC Department of Transportation will redesign intersections to protect pedestrians and cyclists. The plan uses 'hardened daylighting'—physical barriers, granite blocks, and planters—to stop vehicles from parking near corners. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety.' Nearly half of city traffic deaths happen at intersections. The new design will first appear at high-crash sites in Brooklyn, including Ocean Avenue at Church Avenue and several others. The move highlights a systemic effort to address visibility failures and reduce deadly conflicts at crossings.
