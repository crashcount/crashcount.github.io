      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn’s red lights and white sheets

      Written September 3, 2025 (Revised September 3, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 3, 2025

      Just after 12 AM on Jul 13, 2025, at 395 Gateway Dr, a 21-year-old woman was struck and killed. The data says she was not in the roadway; the crash listed driver inattention and unsafe speed. Source (CrashID 4827641).

      She was one of 236 people killed on Brooklyn streets since Jan 1, 2022, alongside 48,941 injured in 84,758 crashes. Source.

      —

      The count won’t stop

      Year to date, Brooklyn has logged 17,160 crashes, 11,050 injuries, and 64 deaths. That’s up from 14,270 crashes, 8,389 injuries, and 40 deaths at this point last year — increases of 20.3%, 31.7%, and 60.0%. Source.

      SUVs and sedans keep doing the most harm to people on foot. The period data shows thousands of pedestrian injuries tied to those vehicles. Source.

      Speed sits in the wreckage. In the Gateway Dr death, the crash record flags unsafe speed. Source (CrashID 4827641).

      —

      Corners with sirens, mornings with bodies

      About 2 AM on Aug 6, 2025, a 76-year-old woman was killed in the crosswalk at 86 St and 18 Ave. Source (CrashID 4833274).

      Just before 8 AM on Aug 6, a man died near Morgan Ave and Maspeth Ave, hit by a box truck. Source (CrashID 4833207).

      On Aug 31, an NYPD sergeant directing traffic at Schenectady and Church Avenues was hit from behind by a black sedan that fled. “Police are looking for the hit-and-run driver,” the paper said. Source.

      —

      What works is not a mystery

      “Speed cameras have cut speeding by over 60% in locations where installed.” Source.

      New York City now has the power to set safer default speeds. Advocates are pressing for a 20 MPH residential default and tools to stop repeat speeders with speed limiters. The bills and steps are laid out here.

      —

      Who will move first?

      Brooklyn’s representatives here are Council Member Alexa Avilés (District 38), Assembly Member Kalman Yeger (AD 41), and State Senator Steve Chan (SD 17). Will they back a citywide 20 MPH default and the Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045C/A2299C)? The plan is on the table. The deaths are on the street. Details.

      —

      Lower the default speed. Fit repeat offenders’ cars with speed limiters. Then count the lives we do not have to bury. Start here: Take action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets for crashes (h9gi-nx95), persons (f55k-p6yu), and vehicles (bm4k-52h4), filtered to borough = Brooklyn and dates 2022-01-01 through 2025-09-03, extracted Sep 3, 2025. We counted total crashes from the Crashes table, and injuries and deaths from the Persons table. You can open a reproducible filtered query for crashes here.
      ▸ Who are the local officials for this area?
      Council Member Alexa Avilés (District 38), Assembly Member Kalman Yeger (AD 41), and State Senator Steve Chan (SD 17).
      ▸ What can I do right now?
      Ask city leaders to lower the default speed to 20 MPH and back speed‑limiters for repeat speeders. Start with our guidance and contacts here.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
