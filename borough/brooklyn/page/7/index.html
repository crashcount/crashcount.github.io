      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn Bleeds—City Stalls

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised July 1, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      Blood on the Parkway

      Brooklyn’s streets keep taking. In the last twelve months, 33 people died in crashes here. Over 6,300 were injured. Seventy-two suffered wounds so grave they may never heal. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The living carry scars.

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy tried to cross Eastern Parkway with his sister. A black Honda Pilot hit him. “I saw the kid being dragged from underneath the car by a woman,” a witness said. “I just saw a lot of blood gushing out of his ears, his mouth.” NY Daily News. The driver stayed. The boy did not.

      Just days before, a 17-year-old on a moped was thrown and killed in Midwood. His passenger was left broken. “Jhoan later died at the hospital,” police told reporters.

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. But the pain is spread by every kind of wheel. No corner is safe.

      Promises and Delays

      Local leaders talk of Vision Zero. They pass laws. They hold press conferences. But the bodies keep coming. In 2024, the city got new power to lower speed limits. Some streets now see 20 mph signs. But most do not. The pace is slow. The risk is fast.

      Red-light cameras have been expanded. Speed cameras now run all night. These are steps. But not enough. Every day of delay is another family changed forever.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by planners, by politicians. “Yeah, this street is dangerous,” a neighbor said after the boy died.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph limit on every street. Demand real protection for people walking and biking.

      Do not wait for another child’s blood to stain the road.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Most deaths and injuries come from SUVs and Cars (67 deaths, 6,724 injuries), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (4 deaths, 228 injuries), and Bikes (3 deaths, 276 injuries). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These crashes are not random. They happen because of choices—by drivers, by city planners, by politicians. Streets can be made safer. Lower speeds, better design, and enforcement save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, expand camera enforcement, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking. They can act faster and refuse to accept delay.
