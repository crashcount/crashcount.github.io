      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn Bleeds While City Hall Waits

      Written July 28, 2025

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 28, 2025

      The Toll in Brooklyn: Lives Lost, Promises Broken

      Brooklyn’s streets do not forgive. In the last year, 53 people died here. Over 9,000 were hurt. One hundred twenty-two suffered injuries so grave they will not walk the same again. These are not numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. The city counts them. Their families count the days since.

      Just weeks ago, a driver in a BMW ran a red light at Third Avenue and 52nd Street. Two men, Kex Un Chen and Faqui Lin, were crossing with the signal. The car hit them. The driver fled. Both men died. The city put up a sign: “Be careful.” State Senator Andrew Gounardes said, “We should all be offended. Angry. Insulted. That the response from our city to two deaths blocks away from here is a sign that says: ‘be careful.’”

      A Corridor of Death and Delay

      Eighty people have been killed or seriously hurt on this two-mile stretch since 2018. The city promised a redesign. The plan stalled. Assemblymember Marcela Mitaynes put it plain: “We started talking about a plan in 2014 and it’s now 2025. What is going on?” There are no answers. There are more funerals.

      Elsewhere, a cyclist was struck on McGuinness Boulevard. The city had promised protection. The paint on the road did nothing. The driver was not charged. The cyclist was left broken. The city moved on.

      Leadership: Words, Not Action

      Brooklyn’s leaders know the danger. They hold press conferences. They call for studies. They wait. “We wait until someone dies. We wait until a tragedy. We wait to say, ‘oh my gosh, how could this possibly have happened?’ We let this happen time and time again,” said State Senator Gounardes.

      The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has the power to redesign streets. It has the power to act. It does not use it.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings. Demand action before the next name is added to the list. The city will not move until you make it move.

      Brooklyn belongs to city council district District 38, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 17.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn were caused by Sedans and SUVs (7,313 incidents), followed by Trucks and Buses (636 incidents), Motorcycles and Mopeds (249 incidents), and Bikes (292 incidents). Source
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random accidents. They happen again and again on the same streets, at the same dangerous intersections. Delayed redesigns and ignored safety plans mean these deaths and injuries could have been prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, fund and build protected bike lanes and crossings, and push for street redesigns that put people before cars. They can act now, or they can wait for the next tragedy.
      ▸ How many people have been killed or seriously injured in Brooklyn recently?
      In the last year, 53 people were killed and 122 suffered serious injuries in Brooklyn traffic crashes.
