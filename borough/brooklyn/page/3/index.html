      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn Streets Run Red—Lower the Limit, Save a Life

      Written July 19, 2025

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 19, 2025

      The Toll This Month

      In Brooklyn, the street does not forgive. Just last Friday, an 11-year-old boy was hit by a car near a playground on Bedford Avenue. He survived. Others have not. On June 28, an 8-year-old boy was killed at the corner of Eastern Parkway and Albany Avenue, struck by an SUV while crossing at the intersection. A week before, a 70-year-old man died crossing Avenue I at East 5th Street. The list grows. The names fade. The pain stays.

      In the past twelve months, 61 people have died in Brooklyn traffic. More than 13,500 have been injured. One hundred seventy-five were seriously hurt—lives changed in a heartbeat. Children, elders, workers. No one is spared.

      The Human Cost

      A 95-year-old woman survived the Nazis and Chernobyl. She was killed crossing Cropsey Avenue in January. After six months, police charged the driver with failure to yield and failure to exercise due care. “Police have arrested a man they say struck and killed a beloved 95-year-old Brooklyn woman with a cargo van as she crossed the street near her home,” reported Gothamist.

      In East New York, a young man doing donuts in a parking lot lost control. His girlfriend, 21, was sitting on the curb. She died. “He lost control when he was doing donuts with the vehicle,” he told police.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      Brooklyn leaders have seen the blood on the street. Some have acted. The city has started to lower speed limits, daylight intersections, and expand speed cameras. But the pace is slow. Charges come months after deaths. Enforcement cracks down on cyclists and delivery workers, not just the drivers who kill. The city can set a 20 mph limit now. It has not. Every day of delay is another family broken.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat offenders. Join Families for Safe Streets and Transportation Alternatives. Do not wait for another child’s name to be added to the list. The street will not wait. Neither should you.

      It belongs to city council district District 38, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 17.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn were caused by cars and trucks (72 deaths, 6,935 injuries), followed by motorcycles and mopeds (1 death, 239 injuries), and bikes (3 deaths, 285 injuries). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement focused on dangerous driving can save lives. Delay costs lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can set a 20 mph speed limit, expand daylighting, install more speed cameras, and pass laws targeting repeat dangerous drivers. They can act now, or answer for every preventable death.

      A Honda sedan, moving south on Kings Highway, struck a man in the crosswalk. The impact shattered his skull. He died on the pavement, headlights burning above, metal cooling in the Brooklyn night.

      A man was killed when a southbound Honda sedan struck him head-on at the intersection of Kings Highway and Church Avenue in Brooklyn. According to the police report, the pedestrian was crossing against the signal when the collision occurred. The report identifies 'Unsafe Speed' as a contributing factor on the part of the driver. The impact was severe, with the victim suffering fatal head injuries and dying at the scene. The vehicle's point of impact was the center front end, and damage was noted on the left front bumper. The police report also lists 'Unspecified' as an additional contributing factor. While the pedestrian's action of crossing against the signal is noted, the primary focus remains on the driver's unsafe speed as a critical factor in this fatal crash.
