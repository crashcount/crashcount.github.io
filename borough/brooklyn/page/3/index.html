      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn Bleeds—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written August 12, 2025 (Revised August 12, 2025 – Fixed duplicate headline in body and cleaned up citations list.)

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 12, 2025

      The Toll in Brooklyn

      In Brooklyn, the street is a wound that never closes. Since 2022, 232 people have died in crashes. Nearly 48,000 have been hurt. In the last twelve months alone, 85 people died. Over 15,000 were injured. The numbers do not slow. The bodies do not stop coming.

      Just last week, a 47-year-old man was killed crossing Broadway at Suydam Street. The driver dragged him more than 50 feet, then fled. Police have not named the victim. They are still looking for the driver. Police were looking on Monday for the driver of a car that struck and dragged a 47-year-old man on a Brooklyn street over the weekend, killing him. The street was quiet. The man died before help could reach him. A driver struck and killed a 47-year-old pedestrian crossing a Brooklyn street, then left the scene, police said Sunday.

      On Morgan Avenue, a box truck killed another man crossing mid-block. No crosswalk. No protection. It is the third death on that stretch in three years. The city has not acted. The dead pile up. The living wait.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay with their lives. Trucks and cars do the killing. In Brooklyn, cars and SUVs have killed 82, trucks and buses 26, motorcycles and mopeds 2, and bikes 3 since 2022. The old, the young, the ones on foot—none are spared. Children, elders, workers. The street does not care.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have made noise. They have passed laws. They have promised change. But the blood on the street says otherwise. The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has not done so. The city can redesign deadly roads. It has not done so on Morgan Avenue. The city can crack down on repeat offenders. The worst drivers keep driving.

      Some leaders have called for more. Some have stayed silent. The dead do not care who speaks. Only action matters.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by someone in power. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand safe crossings. Demand action. The street will not change itself. Only you can force their hand.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn?
      Since 2022, Cars and Trucks (cars/SUVs: 82 deaths, trucks/buses: 26 deaths) have caused the most harm to pedestrians. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 2 deaths. Bikes caused 3 deaths. Most injuries and deaths come from cars, SUVs, trucks, and buses.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not just accidents. They are preventable. Lower speed limits, safer street design, and stronger enforcement can save lives. Every death is a policy failure.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous roads, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can fund and demand real safety improvements, not just studies or promises.
