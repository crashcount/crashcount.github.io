      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn?

      Brooklyn’s deadly August: hit-and-run on Broadway, Belt Parkway rollover, two more gone

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Brooklyn, August did not spare you. A man lay on Broadway before sunrise. A Lexus flipped on the Belt. A motorcyclist never made it home.

      Broadway and Suydam: dragged and left

      Police found a 47-year-old man near Broadway and Suydam just before 3:45 a.m. He had been hit by a northbound vehicle and dragged more than 50 feet. The driver fled. Detectives asked for tips. It was not yet clear if he was in the crosswalk. That uncertainty did not change the ending. Police said they were seeking the car and the person behind the wheel. Early reports pointed to a garbage truck, but no arrest yet. “A driver struck and killed a 47-year-old pedestrian… then left the scene,” police said.

      Belt Parkway: speed, a roll, and a body on the road

      Just after midnight on August 5, a 2019 Lexus headed west on the Belt Parkway. The car overturned. The 22-year-old driver was ejected and died. The crash log lists two causes: unsafe speed and driver distraction. No seat restraint noted. Three others rode in the car. One moment upright, the next upside down. The city’s dataset records the death.

      Pitkin and Strauss: bike vs. turn

      Hours later, also August 5, a Ford sedan turned left off Mother Gaston’s grid at Pitkin and Strauss. A 29-year-old on a Suzuki motorcycle was going straight. He was ejected and died. The car was making the turn. The rider wore a helmet. It did not save him. The crash record lists him as killed.

      18th Avenue at 86th Street: a crossing that ended

      On August 6, a 76-year-old woman was in a marked crosswalk at 18th Avenue and 86th Street. The database says the signal was not in her favor. The contributing factor reads “Traffic Control Disregarded.” She died there. A standing vehicle is listed, but the person is dead, and the paperwork is cold. The city log documents the fatality.

      The drumbeat in the numbers

      Brooklyn keeps the highest toll on foot. Through June, reporters counted 228 injured pedestrians and two killed. The month after, the bodies kept coming. Across the city since 2022, sedans and SUVs are behind most pedestrian injuries and deaths. Trucks follow. The city’s rollup shows sedans linked to 3,104 pedestrian injuries and 26 deaths; SUVs to 2,998 injuries and 44 deaths; trucks to 410 injuries and 20 deaths. Those figures are in the open data.

      Year to date, the city counts 16,629 crashes and 62 deaths, up sharply from last year’s pace. Serious injuries are up too. The ledger does not flinch. The dataset shows the rise.

      Slow it down or keep digging graves

      Speed shows up again and again in the wreckage. The city has tools. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The council can lower speeds. The state renewed school‑zone speed cameras. Advocates say the worst repeat offenders do the most harm. One analysis found that 1.5% of motorists cause 21% of pedestrian deaths, and drivers with 16 camera tickets in 12 months are twice as likely to kill or seriously injure; 30+ tickets multiplies the risk fifty‑fold. Read the reporting. A Brooklyn case this spring put names to it. A driver with 29 camera tickets killed a mother and her two girls. That story is here.

      Lower the default speed. Fit chronic speeders’ cars with limiters. End the rerun.

      Take one step now. Tell City Hall and your council member to act. Start here: take action.

The 44-year-old man driving the moped suffered fatal crush injuries and died at the scene. The report lists 'Aggressive Driving/Road Rage' as a contributing factor, highlighting dangerous driver behavior. The narrative states, 'Aggression and failure to yield cleared the path.' The moped rider was not wearing a helmet, as noted in the report, but the primary cause remains the aggressive actions behind the wheel. No other injuries were specified.
