      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens?

      Queens Bleeds, City Waits—Demand 20 MPH Now

      Written July 2, 2025

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 2, 2025

      The Toll in Queens

      The streets of Queens do not forgive. In the last twelve months, 29 people have died and 68 have been left with serious injuries in crashes here. The numbers do not flinch. Behind them are lives cut short, families broken, shoes left on the asphalt.

      Just days ago, a 39-year-old man on a scooter was killed on 116th Avenue. A van turned left. He was thrown from the scooter. Paramedics could not save him. Police said, “As the van attempted to turn left onto Nashville Boulevard, it collided with the scooter, immediately propelling Lagredelle off the device.” No arrests. The case is still open.

      On June 29, two people riding a moped collided with a BMW. The NYPD said only, “two people were riding the moped when it collided with a BMW.” No word on charges. The street is silent again.

      The Pattern: No End in Sight

      The dead are not numbers. They are sons, daughters, neighbors. In Queens, cars and SUVs have killed 52 pedestrians and cyclists since 2022. Trucks and buses killed 13 more. Motorcycles and mopeds, 4. Bikes, 1. The rest are lost in the noise of the city.

      The violence is not random. It is the same story, told over and over. A left turn. A red light. A driver in a hurry. A body in the road. The city counts the dead. The city moves on.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      Local leaders have made promises. They have passed laws. Speed cameras now run 24/7. The city can lower speed limits under Sammy’s Law. But the pace is slow. The city has started to drop some streets to 20 mph, but most remain unchanged. The blood dries before the paint does.

      What Now: Demand More

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by someone, somewhere. The city can act. The council can act. The mayor can act. Call them. Demand a 20 mph limit on every street. Demand real protection for the people who walk and ride.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 19, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Queens CB10, and District 25 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19, District 23, District 24, District 26, District 27, District 28, District 30, District 31, and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, AD 25, AD 26, AD 27, AD 29, AD 31, AD 33, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, SD 12, SD 14, SD 15, and SD 16.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      Since 2022, Cars and SUVs caused 52 deaths to pedestrians and cyclists in Queens. Trucks and buses caused 13 more. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 4 deaths. Bikes caused 1 death. Cars and SUVs are the main killers.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. They follow patterns—speed, turns, failure to yield. They can be prevented with lower speed limits, better street design, and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, expand protected bike lanes, redesign dangerous intersections, and enforce laws against reckless driving. They can act now, or wait for more deaths.
