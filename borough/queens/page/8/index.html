      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens?

      Queens Streets Kill—City Hall Lets It Happen

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      Blood on the Boulevard

      In Queens, the numbers do not lie. Fifty-one dead in the last year. Over 11,000 injured. One hundred forty-one left with wounds that will never heal. The toll is steady, merciless, and it does not care about age. Children, elders, workers—all caught in the path.

      Just days ago, two people riding a moped collided with a BMW in Jamaica. The NYPD said, “two people were riding the moped when it collided with a BMW” (CBS New York). No word on charges. No word on why. Only the sound of sirens and the scrape of metal.

      Last fall, a cyclist named Amanda Servedio was killed in Astoria. She was returning from a ride. A man fleeing police ran her down. “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. Her father blamed the high-speed chase: “It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood.”

      The Numbers That Haunt

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. In the last three years, cars and SUVs took 52 lives and left thousands hurt. Trucks and buses killed 13. Motorcycles and mopeds, 4. Bikes, 1. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors. They are the old man crossing Linden Boulevard. The young woman on her bike. The child on the way to school.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have moved, but slow. The city has started to lower speed limits in some places. Cameras catch speeders, but only after the fact. The council passed laws to decriminalize jaywalking, shifting blame away from those on foot. But the streets remain the same. The danger remains.

      Some speak out. Some stay silent. “The number of lives lost to traffic violence is simply unacceptable… 127 deaths in this calendar year alone is 127 too many,” said Council Member Erik Bottcher. But words do not slow cars. Promises do not heal broken bodies.

      The Call

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand street redesigns that put people first. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The blood is on the street. It does not wash away.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 19, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Queens CB10, and District 25 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19, District 23, District 24, District 26, District 27, District 28, District 30, District 31, and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, AD 25, AD 26, AD 27, AD 29, AD 31, AD 33, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, SD 12, SD 14, SD 15, and SD 16.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm: 52 deaths from cars/SUVs, 13 from trucks/buses. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 4 deaths. Bikes caused 1 death. The majority of pedestrian injuries and deaths come from cars and SUVs. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These are not random accidents. The steady toll of deaths and injuries in Queens is the result of policy choices—speed limits, street design, and enforcement. Every crash is preventable with the right action.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking. They can act now, or let the toll keep rising.
