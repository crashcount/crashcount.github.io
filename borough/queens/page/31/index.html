      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Queens keeps the body count rising. Who will slow the cars?

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 5, 2025

      Around 1 PM on Aug 31, at 30 St and 39 Ave, a right‑turning SUV struck a 38‑year‑old woman in a marked crosswalk. She died at the scene, police listed the cause as failure to yield. NYC Open Data

      This week:

      • Aug 23: A sedan going straight killed a male pedestrian near the U.S. Open site. NYC Open Data
      • Aug 12 (night): A 61‑year‑old man crossing outside an intersection was killed at Union Tpke and 189 St. NYC Open Data
      • Aug 12 (morning): Two pedestrians and an 84‑year‑old driver were killed at 42 St and 19 Ave. NYC Open Data

      They were four of 227 people killed on Queens streets since Jan 1, 2022. The borough logged 71,287 crashes, 41,485 injuries, and 472 serious injuries in that span. NYC Open Data

      The toll isn’t easing

      Through Sep 5 this year, Queens recorded 52 traffic deaths, up from 34 at this point last year. Injuries are 9,861 this year, compared to 7,494 over the same span last year. NYC Open Data

      SUVs and sedans are the face of this harm. Pedestrians struck by SUVs account for at least 36 deaths in the period; sedans account for 22. NYC Open Data

      “Speed cameras have cut speeding by over 60% in locations where installed.” NYS Senate

      Where leaders stand when streets go dark

      This is Queens. Your local officials are Council Member Vickie Paladino, Assembly Member Stacey Pheffer Amato, and State Senator James Sanders. Our record here does not show whether they have sponsored or co‑sponsored a repeat‑speeder bill or pushed a lower default speed limit.

      The tools exist. The city can lower speeds on local streets. Albany can force the worst repeat speeders to slow down with intelligent speed assistance. See the details and bill numbers on our Take Action page.

      What stops the next right turn into a body?

      • Lower the default speed limit on city streets. Fewer dead at 20 mph than 30. Take Action
      • Pass a strong repeat‑speeder law that requires speed limiters for drivers with long camera‑ticket records. The “Stop Super Speeders Act” (S4045C/A2299C) is one path. Take Action

      The woman on 39 Ave is gone. The corner is the same. Call it what it is and change it: slow the cars. Start here: Take Action.
