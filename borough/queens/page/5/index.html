      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens?

      Queens Bleeds, Leaders Sleep: End the Killing on Our Streets

      Written August 9, 2025 (Revised August 10, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 9, 2025

      The Toll in Queens: No End in Sight

      The streets of Queens do not rest. Since January 2022, 219 people have died in crashes here. Over 40,000 have been hurt. The numbers do not slow. They do not care about the weather, the hour, or the name of the street.

      Just last week, a 55-year-old woman was killed riding her e-bike on Hollis Court Boulevard. The driver stayed at the scene. Police are still investigating. The news is always the same: another life gone, another family left to wait for answers. The driver stayed on the scene, and police are still investigating the crash.

      On the same day, a 23-year-old man was run down on 101st Street. The district attorney filed no charges. The car moved on. The street was quiet again. The Queens District Attorney’s Office opted not to charge the driver because he feared for his life when he struck Sonalall.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs do most of the harm. They killed 58 people and hurt thousands more. Trucks and buses took 14 lives. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 4. Even bikes, meant for freedom, took one. The numbers are plain. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city passed new laws. Speed cameras now run day and night. The council can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. But the limit stands. The cameras catch the same cars, again and again. The city waits. The dead do not.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by someone in power. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to lower the speed limit. Tell them to act before another name is added to the list. The street will not change itself.

      ▸ Where does Queens sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 19, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Queens CB10, and District 25 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19, District 23, District 24, District 26, District 27, District 28, District 30, District 31, and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, AD 25, AD 26, AD 27, AD 29, AD 31, AD 33, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, SD 12, SD 14, SD 15, and SD 16.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      Cars and SUVs killed 58 people and caused thousands of injuries. Trucks and buses killed 14. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 4. Bikes killed 1. The numbers show who does the harm. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. These deaths and injuries are not random. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by city planners, by lawmakers. They can be prevented with safer streets, lower speed limits, and better enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower the speed limit, redesign dangerous streets, and make sure repeat dangerous drivers are kept off the road. They can act now, or wait for the next death.
