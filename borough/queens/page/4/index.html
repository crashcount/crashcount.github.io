      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens?

      Queens Bleeds While City Sleeps—How Many More Must Die?

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Toll in Queens: No End in Sight

      A woman steps into a pizzeria bathroom. An e-bike battery explodes outside the door. The fire moves like a blowtorch. She cannot get out. Her son finds her burned body. “For me to find my mom burnt like a roast pig, almost 100 percent of her whole body, is a memory I cannot forget.”

      On the Belt Parkway, a BMW vaults the divider. Passengers are thrown from the car. The car burns. Two die. Three more are hurt. The road is littered with glass and blood. “They went airborne and into the barrier,” a survivor says.

      In Queens, the disaster is not sudden. It is slow. It is every day. In the last twelve months, 59 people died on these streets. 155 were seriously hurt. More than 12,000 were injured. The numbers do not stop. They only grow.

      Who Pays the Price?

      The old. The young. The ones who walk. The ones who ride. In Queens, SUVs and cars do the most harm—54 pedestrian deaths and over 5,200 injuries since 2022. Trucks and buses killed 13. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 4. Bikes killed 1. See the data.

      No one is spared. A 76-year-old woman. A 22-year-old passenger. A 15-year-old boy falls from a train. The city calls these accidents. They are not accidents. They are the cost of inaction.

      What Leaders Have Done—And What They Haven’t

      The city has lowered speed limits in some zones. Cameras catch speeders day and night. The mayor calls the drop in deaths “historic.” But the carnage goes on. The NYPD cracks down on cyclists, not the drivers who kill. Laws target e-bike batteries, not the cars that crush. The city redesigns some streets, but leaves others deadly.

      What Comes Next: Demand More

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them: Lower the speed limit to 20 mph. Build real protection for people who walk and bike. End the crackdown on the vulnerable. Hold drivers who kill accountable. Act now.

      ▸ Where does Queens sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 19, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Queens CB10, and District 25 neighborhoods.       ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      SUVs and Cars: 54 deaths, 5,241 injuries. Trucks and Buses: 13 deaths, 358 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 4 deaths, 160 injuries. Bikes: 1 death, 143 injuries. SUVs and cars do the most harm. See the data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. The numbers show a pattern. These deaths and injuries are preventable. They are the result of policy, street design, and enforcement choices.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, expand automated enforcement, and focus on the drivers who do the most harm. They can stop blaming the vulnerable and start protecting them.
