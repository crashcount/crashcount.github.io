      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens?

      Queens: three bodies in two weeks, and the meter keeps running

      Written August 31, 2025 (Revised August 31, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 31, 2025

      A man lay on United Nations Avenue South in Flushing Meadows Corona Park. “A man died after a police officer in an NYPD car ran over him,” the department said.

      —

      Park road, patrol car, one man dead

      It was just before 5 PM near the Queens Theatre. NYPD says the car was moving about 10 mph when it struck him. He was taken to NewYork‑Presbyterian Queens and pronounced dead, according to Gothamist.

      His death sits with others from this month. On Aug 26, a 24‑year‑old passenger died in a BMW on the Cross Island Parkway; the crash was speed‑related, per NYPD’s crash record (CrashID 4837610) in NYC Open Data. On Jul 31, a driver going straight hit and killed a 23‑year‑old man in Ozone Park; the record flags unsafe speed and a slick roadway (CrashID 4832080), also in NYC Open Data.

      —

      The toll in Queens keeps climbing

      From Jan 1, 2022 through Aug 31, 2025, Queens logged 70,976 crashes, 41,273 injuries, and 226 deaths, by our analysis of city collision data.

      This year is worse than last year at this point. Year‑to‑date, crashes are up 28.5%, injuries up 31.8%, and deaths up 59.4% compared with the same stretch in 2024, based on the same datasets as of Aug 31, 2025 (see methodology and links below from NYC Open Data).

      —

      Speed shows up in the bodies

      The Cross Island death is coded “Unsafe Speed.” So is the Ozone Park killing. Those two records—CrashID 4837610 and 4832080—name speed as a factor in a young man’s death and another man’s death. Both appear in the city’s official crash files (Crashes, Persons, Vehicles).

      —

      Officials named. Will they act?

      Queens residents here are represented by Council Member Vickie Paladino, Assembly Member Stacey Pheffer Amato (AD 23), and State Senator James Sanders (SD 10). The policy tools are on the table.

      City leaders can use Sammy’s Law to lower speeds on local streets. Albany has the Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045C/A2299C), which targets repeat camera violators with speed‑limiting tech. Both steps are laid out in our site’s action brief here.

      The man in the park. The young passenger on the parkway. The pedestrian in Ozone Park. Different roads. Same result.

      —

      Take one step now. Tell City Hall and Albany to act on lower speeds and repeat speeders: take action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4), filtered to the borough of Queens and the period 2022-01-01 through 2025-08-31. We aggregated crashes, injuries, and deaths from those records. Data was accessed Aug 31, 2025. You can start from the Crashes dataset here and apply the same filters.
      ▸ Which recent Queens crashes are cited here?
      Three cases: the NYPD patrol car striking a man on United Nations Ave S in Flushing Meadows Corona Park reported by Gothamist on Aug 25, 2025; CrashID 4837610 (Aug 26, 2025) on the Cross Island Parkway, speed-related; and CrashID 4832080 (Jul 31, 2025) near 101 St in Ozone Park, also flagged for unsafe speed.
      ▸ Who are the local officials for this area?
      Council Member Vickie Paladino (District 19), Assembly Member Stacey Pheffer Amato (AD 23), and State Senator James Sanders (SD 10). Contact links and details are on their district pages: District 19, AD 23, and SD 10.
      ▸ What policies could reduce these deaths?
      NYC can lower speed limits under Sammy’s Law, and Albany can pass the Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045C/A2299C) to rein in repeat speeders with speed limiters. See our action guide here.
