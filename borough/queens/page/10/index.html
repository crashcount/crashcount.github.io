      Queens Bleeds: Speed Kills, Leaders Stall, Families Grieve

      Written June 24, 2025

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 24, 2025

      The Toll in Queens: Lives Lost, Families Shattered

      A cyclist goes airborne. A father waits for justice. A man lies unresponsive in the road, the driver gone. In Queens, the violence comes in waves—fast, silent, and without warning. In the last twelve months, 52 people died on Queens streets. 138 suffered serious injuries. The numbers are blunt. The pain is sharp.

      Amanda Servedio, 36, was riding her bike when a man fleeing police hit her. She “went flying off her bicycle and slammed into a parked BMW while the driver sped off.” A witness said, “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.” Her father wants justice: “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood.”

      On 101st Avenue, a man in his fifties was left fighting for his life after a black SUV struck him and kept going. Police found him unresponsive. The driver did not stop. The story repeats: a body in the street, a car speeding away.

      Who Bears the Brunt?

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the worst of it. In the last year, Queens saw over 11,000 injuries and more than 18,000 crashes. SUVs and cars did most of the harm—52 pedestrian deaths from cars and SUVs alone. Trucks and buses killed 13. Motorcycles and mopeds, 4. Bikes, 1. See the data.

      What Are Leaders Doing?

      Some progress, more delay. The city has the power to lower speed limits under Sammy’s Law. But most streets in Queens are still set at 25 mph or higher. Speed cameras are up, but only in some places. The worst repeat offenders still drive. The city has not made 20 mph the default. The carnage continues.

      What Comes Next?

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day leaders wait, another family pays. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat speeders. Join Transportation Alternatives or Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name to join the list.

      Lower speeds. Save lives. Act now.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      Cars and SUVs caused 52 pedestrian deaths, trucks and buses killed 13, motorcycles and mopeds caused 4 deaths, and bikes caused 1 death in the last year in Queens. See the NYC Open Data crash stats for details.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The numbers and stories show a pattern. Speed, reckless driving, and policy choices make these deaths preventable. Every crash is a policy failure, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph under Sammy’s Law, expand speed cameras, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can redesign streets to protect people, not just cars. Delay costs lives.
      ▸ How many people were killed or seriously injured in Queens recently?
      In the last twelve months, 52 people died and 138 suffered serious injuries in Queens traffic crashes.

      ▸ How can I help make Queens streets safer?
      Call your council member and demand a 20 mph speed limit. Join advocacy groups like Transportation Alternatives or Families for Safe Streets. Speak up at community board meetings. Every voice matters.
