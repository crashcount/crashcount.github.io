      Queens Bleeds: Speed Kills, Leaders Stall

      Written July 18, 2025

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll in Queens: Lives Lost, Families Shaken

      The streets of Queens do not rest. Since 2022, 208 people have died and 414 have been seriously injured in crashes here (NYC Open Data). The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about hope or habit. They only climb.

      Just days ago, a BMW tore across the Belt Parkway. It struck the median, flew, and burned. Two dead. Six hurt. The NYPD said the driver “lost control of the car and collided with the median divider” (amny reported). The fire left nothing but metal and smoke.

      On July 11, an MTA bus in Flushing “jumped the curb” and slammed into a pole. Eight people were injured. A passenger said, “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus” (ABC7 reported). The driver had fallen asleep. The MTA pulled him from service. The investigation drags on.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Cars and SUVs do most of the killing. Since 2022, these vehicles have caused 54 deaths and over 5,200 injuries to pedestrians and cyclists in Queens (NYC Open Data). Trucks and buses killed 13. Motorcycles and mopeds, 4. Bikes, 1. The numbers are not fair. They are not random. They follow the weight of steel.

      The dead are not numbers. A 22-year-old woman, ejected from a car. A 76-year-old, killed as a passenger. A 39-year-old, struck on a moped. The stories do not end. The pain does not fade.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      Local leaders have acted. The city has lowered speed limits in some zones. Speed cameras now run 24/7. The NYPD cracks down on “ghost cars” and reckless drivers. But the carnage continues. The city can set a 20 mph limit everywhere. It has not. The law sits idle. The streets do not wait.

      “One traffic fatality is one too many,” said Deputy Mayor Meera Joshi. She promised to use “every lever…to ensure that New Yorkers are safe however they choose to get around” (NYC.gov). The levers move slow. The bodies pile up fast.

      The Call: Demand Action Now

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit on every street. Demand streets built for people, not for speed. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 54 deaths and over 5,200 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 4 deaths and 160 injuries. Bikes caused 1 death and 143 injuries. The weight of steel does most of the killing. [NYC Open Data]
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The numbers show a pattern, not chance. Speed, distraction, and unsafe streets make these deaths preventable. Policy and design can stop the next one.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets, and enforce laws that protect people walking and cycling. They can pass a 20 mph limit citywide and build streets that put people first.
