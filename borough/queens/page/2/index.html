      Queens Bleeds: City Stalls, People Die

      Written August 14, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 14, 2025

      The Toll in Queens

      Queens bleeds on the street. In the last twelve months, 69 people died in crashes here. Another 180 were left with injuries so severe they changed lives. The count does not stop. In just the past week, two men were killed while buying food from a truck. A car, moving too fast, slammed into them. The street was left with bodies and broken glass. One witness said, “I have never seen anything like this,” his head in his hands.

      On the same day, a 52-year-old man was crossing near JFK. A driver hit him and kept going. Police said, “The driver sped off without stopping. No arrests have been made.” The man died at the hospital. His name became a number in the city’s tally.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the worst of it. In three years, 219 people have died in Queens traffic. Over 40,000 have been hurt. SUVs and cars do most of the killing—58 deaths, 5,662 injuries. Trucks and buses, 14 deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds, 4 deaths. Bikes, 1 death. The numbers do not care about age. Children, elders, workers, all are at risk.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has the power to slow the bloodshed. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The Council can set a 20 mph speed limit. They have not. Speed cameras are renewed, but the most dangerous drivers still roam. A handful of drivers rack up dozens of tickets and keep driving. The city talks of redesigns and new laws, but the pace is slow. Each delay means another family broken.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every day leaders wait, more people die. Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join the fight. Do not wait for another body in the road.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 19, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Queens CB10, and District 25 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19, District 23, District 24, District 26, District 27, District 28, District 30, District 31, and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, AD 25, AD 26, AD 27, AD 29, AD 31, AD 33, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, SD 12, SD 14, SD 15, and SD 16.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      Most deaths and injuries come from SUVs and cars (58 deaths, 5,662 injuries), followed by trucks and buses (14 deaths, 393 injuries), motorcycles and mopeds (4 deaths, 178 injuries), and bikes (1 death, 154 injuries).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The numbers show a pattern. Speed, repeat offenders, and lack of safe street design make these deaths preventable. Policy can change the outcome.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can set a 20 mph speed limit, crack down on repeat speeders, and redesign streets for safety. Every delay costs lives.
