      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens?

      Queens Streets Bleed—How Many More Must Die Before City Hall Acts?

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Toll in Queens

      The bodies keep coming. Since 2022, 203 people have died on Queens streets. More than 36,000 have been hurt. Four hundred have suffered injuries so grave they will not forget them. The dead are not numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the man crushed by a bus on Woodhaven Boulevard, the woman burned alive by a battery in a Flushing pizzeria, the young mother thrown from a BMW on the Belt Parkway. The pain does not fade.

      Just last week, a BMW hit the divider on the Belt Parkway. The car vaulted the median and caught fire. No one inside wore a seatbelt. Two died. A survivor described the crash: “They went airborne and into the barrier.” The fire left nothing but metal and ash. The children left behind will remember.

      A day later, an e-bike battery exploded in a Queens pizzeria. The flames moved fast. The victim, trapped in the bathroom, had no way out. FDNY Commissioner Robert Tucker warned, “These fires are treacherous. They move very quickly. They are very hot. And you know, you don’t have many feet to move before you’re overtaken by the fumes and the smoke and the flames.”

      Who Bears the Brunt

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay the price. Cars and trucks are the main killers. SUVs and sedans alone have taken 52 lives and left more than 5,000 people hurt. Trucks and buses killed 13. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 4. Bikes killed 1. The numbers are cold. The stories are not.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has lowered speed limits in some zones. Cameras catch speeders in school areas. The DOT has built more bike lanes and daylighted intersections. But the pace is slow. The carnage does not wait. Every week brings new names to the roll of the dead.

      Some leaders have called for more enforcement. Some have blamed the vulnerable. The streets remain the same. The dead remain dead.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people, not just promises. Every day of delay is another day for the next family to grieve. The disaster is not fate. It is policy. Change it.
