      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens?

      Queens Bleeds While City Hall Waits

      Written July 6, 2025

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Blood Price of Speed

      The summer began with sirens. On July 5, a white BMW tore east on the Belt Parkway. The driver lost control, hit the divider, and vaulted into oncoming traffic. Six people were hurt. Two are fighting for their lives. Police closed the westbound lanes. The NYPD’s Collision Investigation Squad is still counting the cost. Police reported that “the driver then struck a gray 2016 CR-V, as well as a red 2022 Hyundai.”

      The day before, a 15-year-old boy died under the wheels of a 7 train at Queensboro Plaza. He fell from the roof. His body was carried out before dawn. Police confirmed that “a 15-year-old boy was killed subway surfing in Queens early Friday.”

      In the last twelve months, Queens has seen 29 deaths and 72 serious injuries from crashes. The numbers do not stop. They only climb.

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      The dead are young and old. Children, elders, workers. In Queens, SUVs and cars lead the body count: 52 deaths and 96 serious injuries for pedestrians. Trucks and buses killed 13 more. Motorcycles and mopeds, four. One cyclist dead. The street does not forgive. The city’s own data shows the pattern.

      Leaders Move, But Not Fast Enough

      After years of waiting, Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can now set its own speed limits. Some 20 mph zones have appeared, but the default is still 25. Cameras catch speeders, but the worst repeat offenders keep driving. The city council and mayor talk of safety, but the blood dries before the paint does. The NYPD investigates, but families bury their dead.

      What Comes Next

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. The city has the tools. The law is on their side. The only thing missing is the will to act.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit for every street. Demand real protection for people walking and biking. Do not wait for another siren.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 19, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Queens CB10, and District 25 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19, District 23, District 24, District 26, District 27, District 28, District 30, District 31, and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, AD 25, AD 26, AD 27, AD 29, AD 31, AD 33, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, SD 12, SD 14, SD 15, and SD 16.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      Most deaths and serious injuries to pedestrians came from SUVs and cars (52 deaths, 96 serious injuries), followed by trucks and buses (13 deaths, 14 serious injuries), motorcycles and mopeds (4 deaths, 5 serious injuries), and bikes (1 death, 3 serious injuries). This is confirmed by NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Every crash is preventable. The same patterns repeat: speed, loss of control, dangerous roads. These are failures of policy and design, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, redesign streets for safety, and enforce laws against reckless driving. They can act now, or answer for every life lost.
