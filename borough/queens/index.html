      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll in Queens: Lives Lost, Families Shaken

      Queens bleeds every week. Since 2022, 208 people have died on its streets. Over 37,000 have been hurt. In the past year alone, 59 people were killed, 152 suffered serious injuries, and more than 12,000 were wounded. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old. One child, one mother, one grandfather—gone, and the city moves on.

      Just last week, a BMW lost control on the Belt Parkway. The car struck the median, went airborne, and caught fire. Two people died. Three more were hurt. The NYPD said, “Thompson lost control of the car and collided with the median divider.” No arrests. No answers. Only the sound of sirens and the smell of burning rubber. Chain-Reaction Crash Kills Two On Belt Parkway

      On July 11, an MTA bus in Flushing jumped the curb and slammed into a pole. Eight people were injured. A passenger said, “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus.” The driver had fallen asleep. The MTA pulled him from service. The investigation drags on. Bus Jumps Curb, Eight Injured In Flushing

      Who Pays the Price?

      The dead do not get a second chance. In Queens, the old and the young fall first. In the last twelve months, 1,029 children were hurt. Seven people over 75 died. The city counts the bodies. It does not count the empty chairs at dinner. Motor Vehicle Collisions – CrashID 4823380

      Cars and SUVs do most of the killing. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, mopeds—they all take their share. Pedestrians and cyclists are left to dodge and pray.

      What Has Been Done—And What Hasn’t

      Leaders talk. Streets stay the same. The city has lowered some speed limits. Cameras catch some speeders. The mayor calls the drop in deaths “historic” and vows to “continue using all tools available to drive down traffic violence.”

      But the carnage continues. Enforcement comes down hard on cyclists and delivery workers, while the worst drivers rack up tickets and keep driving. Community boards demand fairness. “It is unjust to hold cyclists to a far higher standard than motor vehicle drivers,” said Manhattan’s Community Board 7. Motor Vehicle Collisions – CrashID 4823380

      Act Now: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand the city stop blaming the vulnerable and start protecting them. Every day of delay is another life lost.

      Do not wait for another siren. Act now.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      Most injuries and deaths come from SUVs and Cars (54 deaths, 5,243 total incidents), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (4 deaths, 160 incidents), and Bikes (1 death, 143 incidents). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These are not random accidents. The same patterns repeat: speeding, loss of control, vehicles mounting sidewalks. Policies and street design can prevent them.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets, expand automated enforcement, and focus on the drivers who do the most harm. They can stop blaming the vulnerable and start protecting them.
