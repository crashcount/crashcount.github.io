      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens?

      Queens Is Bleeding—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Toll in Queens

      The streets of Queens do not rest. Since 2022, 208 people have died in crashes here. Over 37,000 have been hurt. Four hundred fourteen suffered wounds so deep they will not heal. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors, children.

      Last week, a bus in Flushing jumped the curb. Eight people were hurt. One rider described the chaos: “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus.” The driver, just 25, had fallen asleep. The MTA pulled him from service. The investigation goes on. The street is the same.

      On the Belt Parkway, a BMW lost control, hit the divider, and caught fire. Two died. Three more were hurt. The NYPD reported that “Thompson lost control of the car and collided with the median divider.” The crash is under investigation. The road is still open.

      Who Bears the Brunt

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In Queens, they took 54 lives and left over 5,000 people hurt. Trucks and buses killed 13. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 4. Bikes killed 1. The dead are not just numbers. They are the cost of speed, weight, and inattention.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has started to act. Speed limits are dropping in some places. Cameras catch more reckless drivers. But the pace is slow. The carnage is not. Every week brings new names to the list.

      Some leaders speak. Some stay silent. After the bus crash, the MTA promised to investigate. After the parkway deaths, police said the same. But the streets do not change themselves.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It can redesign streets. It can take the most dangerous drivers off the road. But only if you demand it.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand safer streets. The dead cannot speak. You can.

      ▸ Where does Queens sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 19, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Queens CB10, and District 25 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19, District 23, District 24, District 26, District 27, District 28, District 30, District 31, and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, AD 25, AD 26, AD 27, AD 29, AD 31, AD 33, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, SD 12, SD 14, SD 15, and SD 16.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      Cars and SUVs: 54 deaths, 5,243 injuries. Trucks and buses: 13 deaths, 358 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 4 deaths, 160 injuries. Bikes: 1 death, 143 injuries. Most deaths and injuries come from cars, SUVs, trucks, and buses. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. The pattern is clear. Speed, heavy vehicles, and unsafe streets cause deaths. These are preventable with better policy and design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets, expand automated enforcement, and take repeat dangerous drivers off the road. They can act now, or wait for more deaths.
