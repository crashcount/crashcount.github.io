      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens?

      Queens Streets: Four Killed in One Deadly Week

      Written August 16, 2025 (Revised August 16, 2025 – Finisher pass: tightened prose, fixed figures, added concrete local fixes and citywide policy demands.)

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 16, 2025

      This Week: Four Killed, One Driver Fled

      On Tuesday morning in Astoria, an 84-year-old driver lost control, plowed into a food cart and died at the scene. Two men at the cart were killed. “I have never seen anything like this,” an eyewitness told amNY (2025-08-13).

      The next night, a car struck a 52-year-old man crossing 155th Street at South Conduit Avenue near JFK and fled the scene. “The operator of the vehicle fled the scene,” police told ABC7 (2025-08-13).

      Queens courts did act in another case. After a 2023 wrong-way rampage on the Clearview Expressway, Queens DA Melinda Katz said: “Joseph Lee terrorized other drivers as he purposefully drove the wrong way on a busy Queens highway and crashed into multiple cars” (amNY, 2025-08-15). Two motorists remain seriously injured.

      This is not random. It is a pattern.

      Since 2022, Queens has recorded 222 traffic deaths, 460 serious injuries and 40,642 total injuries, per NYC Open Data (updated 2025-08-16). Cars and SUVs, plus trucks and buses, cause the bulk of pedestrian harm — a combined 74 pedestrian deaths and 6,066 total pedestrian injuries in the dataset (cars/SUVs: deaths 60, total 5,673; trucks/buses: deaths 14, total 393) (NYC Open Data).

      Contributing factors are clear in these files and in reporting: unsafe speed, medical incapacitation behind the wheel, and hit-and-run. Heavy vehicles and SUVs dominate pedestrian deaths.

      Who fixes this? Streets are a civic design problem. Prosecutors can lock up one driver. They cannot rebuild the crossings or slow the lanes where people die. The city can.

      What must happen now (local, concrete):

      • Install leading pedestrian intervals (LPIs) and daylight key crossings so walkers get a head start.
      • Harden dangerous turns and add curb extensions (shorten crossings).
      • Targeted enforcement and improved nighttime lighting and crash-investigation resources where late-night strikes and hit-and-runs cluster.

      Political solutions (citywide):

      • Lower NYC’s default speed limit to 20 mph to cut the deadliest crashes. Sammy’s Law gives the city that authority; the city should use it now.
      • Require intelligent speed-assistance devices for habitual speeders — a practical tool to stop repeat offenders.

      Act: Call your Council Member and the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph default and speed limiters for repeat offenders. Take Action: /take_action/.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 19, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Queens CB10, and District 25 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19, District 23, District 24, District 26, District 27, District 28, District 30, District 31, and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, AD 25, AD 26, AD 27, AD 29, AD 31, AD 33, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, SD 12, SD 14, SD 15, and SD 16.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      From 2022 through Aug. 16, 2025, cars and SUVs, plus trucks and buses, account for 74 pedestrian deaths and 6,066 total pedestrian injuries combined (cars/SUVs: deaths 60, total 5,673; trucks/buses: deaths 14, total 393) NYC Open Data. Motorcycles and mopeds account for 4 deaths and 178 injuries. Bikes account for 1 death and 154 injuries NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these just “accidents”?
      Look at the pattern. A wrong-way driver “terrorized other drivers” on the Clearview, leaving people badly hurt said DA Katz. Days later, a driver hit a man near JFK and fled police said. This is preventable violence on public roads, repeated again and again.
      ▸ What can local politicians do right now?
      Set a citywide 20 mph default. Require speed limiters for repeat speeders. Fund quick-build street fixes that narrow lanes and shorten crossings. Back up NYPD crash investigations so hit-and-run drivers are found. Hold agencies to measurable timelines.
      ▸ How can I help today?
      Call your Council Member and the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph default and speed limiters for repeat offenders. Show up at your community board and ask for concrete safety changes at the corners you cross.
