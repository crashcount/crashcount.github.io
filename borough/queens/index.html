      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens?

      Queens Blood on the Asphalt—When Will City Hall Hit the Brakes?

      Written August 13, 2025

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 13, 2025

      The Toll in Queens

      Three dead at a food truck. It happened just yesterday. A Toyota sped through a stop sign at 19th Avenue and 42nd Street in Astoria. The car hit two men waiting for coffee. Both died. The car spun and hit another vehicle. The driver, 84, died too. The street was left with blood and wreckage. A witness described the crash: “Must have been going at least 60+ miles an hour just right through the stop sign and within seconds I heard the crash and screams and just dropped everything and ran over there, it was instant.”

      This is not rare. In the last 12 months, Queens saw 44 killed and 8,917 injured in crashes. 115 were left with serious injuries. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and workers. The numbers do not slow. They grind on, year after year.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the hit. Cars and SUVs killed 58. Trucks and buses killed 14. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 4. Bikes killed 1. The street is not safe for the ones on foot or on two wheels. The numbers are not just numbers. They are fathers, mothers, brothers, sisters. One man’s brother was killed at the truck. “He’s the best brother. The best person you can find in the world.”

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has tools. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The Council can lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not done it. Speed cameras are renewed, but the worst repeat offenders still drive. The city has started to redesign some streets. But the pace is slow. The dead pile up faster than the fixes.

      What You Can Do

      Call your Council Member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join Families for Safe Streets or Transportation Alternatives. Do not wait for another crash. The next name could be yours.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 19, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Queens CB10, and District 25 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19, District 23, District 24, District 26, District 27, District 28, District 30, District 31, and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, AD 25, AD 26, AD 27, AD 29, AD 31, AD 33, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, SD 12, SD 14, SD 15, and SD 16.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      Most deaths and injuries to pedestrians in Queens come from Cars and SUVs (58 killed), followed by Trucks and Buses (14 killed), Motorcycles and Mopeds (4 killed), and Bikes (1 killed). SUVs and cars are responsible for the majority of pedestrian deaths and injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speed limits, and enforcement against reckless driving. The same deadly patterns repeat year after year.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower the speed limit to 20 mph, redesign streets for safety, and crack down on repeat speeders. They can act now or keep letting people die.
