      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens?

      Queens Bleeds While Leaders Stall

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Toll in Queens

      The numbers do not flinch. Since 2022, Queens has seen 208 people killed and 414 seriously injured in crashes. The dead do not speak. The wounded carry scars. In the last twelve months alone, 59 people died on Queens streets. 152 were seriously hurt. Every week, another family gets the call.

      Recent Wounds, Unanswered

      Just last week, a BMW lost control on the Belt Parkway. The car hit the median, went airborne, and caught fire. Two young people died. Three more were thrown from the car. Police said, “Thompson lost control of the car and collided with the median divider” (amny). The investigation is not over. The pain is.

      On July 11, an MTA bus in Flushing jumped the curb. Eight people were hurt. One rider said, “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb… I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus” (ABC7). The driver may have fallen asleep. The MTA pulled him from service. The street stayed the same.

      Leadership: Steps and Stalls

      Queens leaders have tools. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. The speed camera program is renewed. More red-light cameras are coming. But the default speed limit is still not 20 mph. Cameras catch speeders, but the worst repeat offenders keep driving. The city redesigns some streets, but the work is slow. Enforcement falls hardest on cyclists and delivery workers, not the drivers who kill.

      The Call

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every delay means another mother, another child, another name on a slab. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph default. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join the fight. Do not wait for the next siren.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 19, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Queens CB10, and District 25 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19, District 23, District 24, District 26, District 27, District 28, District 30, District 31, and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, AD 25, AD 26, AD 27, AD 29, AD 31, AD 33, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, SD 12, SD 14, SD 15, and SD 16.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      Most deaths and injuries to pedestrians in Queens came from SUVs and Cars (54 deaths, 5,243 injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (13 deaths, 358 injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (4 deaths, 160 injuries), and Bikes (1 death, 143 injuries).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. The pattern is clear. Speed, street design, and repeat dangerous drivers make these deaths preventable. Policy can change the outcome.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, expand automated enforcement, redesign streets for safety, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can act now or answer for more deaths.
