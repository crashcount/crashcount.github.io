      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens?

      Queens Bleeds While Leaders Stall—How Many More Must Die?

      Written July 7, 2025

      Queens: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Toll in Queens

      The bodies keep coming. Since 2022, Queens has seen 201 people killed and over 36,000 injured on its streets. In the past year alone, 52 dead, 142 seriously hurt. These are not numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the price of delay.

      Just days ago, a BMW tore across the Belt Parkway. It hit the divider, vaulted into oncoming traffic, and slammed into two cars. Six people were hurt, three left fighting for their lives. Police said, “The driver then struck a gray 2016 CR-V, as well as a red 2022 Hyundai.” The road closed. The city waited for the next siren.

      A week before, a 15-year-old boy died under the wheels of a 7 train. He fell from the roof at Queensboro Plaza. “EMS crews rushed him to the hospital where he was pronounced dead.” The trains kept running. The platform stayed slick.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Queens politicians have tools. In 2024, Albany gave the city power to lower speed limits. The city started to drop limits to 20 mph near schools and in some zones. But the default speed on most streets stays high. Cameras catch speeders, but the worst repeat offenders keep driving.

      The city has built more bike lanes and daylighted intersections. Police crack down on “ghost cars” and reckless drivers. But too often, enforcement falls on cyclists and delivery workers instead of the drivers who kill.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. Every crash is a policy failure. Every delay is a choice. The city can set a 20 mph limit everywhere. The state can pass the Stop Super Speeders Act and force the most dangerous drivers off the road.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand safer speeds, real enforcement, and streets built for people, not just cars.

      The blood on the pavement is not an accident. It is a warning. Act now, or count the bodies.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens?
      SUVs and Cars: 52 deaths and 96 serious injuries from SUVs and cars; Trucks and Buses: 13 deaths and 14 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 4 deaths and 5 serious injuries. Bikes: 1 death and 3 serious injuries. These numbers show the greatest danger comes from SUVs, cars, trucks, and buses. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not random. They follow patterns—speed, street design, and enforcement. Policies can prevent them. Every crash is a failure to act.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws like the Stop Super Speeders Act. They can focus enforcement on the drivers who do the most harm.
