      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Staten Island?

      Staten Island: 45 dead since 2022. The year is getting worse.

      Written August 30, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Staten Island: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 30, 2025

      At Forest Avenue and Raymond Place, a 68‑year‑old man in the crosswalk was struck and killed by a left‑turning SUV. The police record lists failure to yield and distraction. NYC Open Data CrashID 4784160.

      They are one of 45 people killed on Staten Island streets since Jan 1, 2022, according to city crash data for this borough through Aug 30, 2025 (NYC Open Data).

      This year is worse

      Year to date, Staten Island has recorded 2,938 crashes, 1,737 injuries, and 10 deaths, up from 6 at this point last year. That’s a 26.0% rise in crashes and a 66.7% rise in deaths compared to the same period in 2024 (NYC Open Data).

      The toll does not come from one kind of street. A box truck backing at a Gulf Avenue warehouse killed a 34‑year‑old man (NYC Open Data CrashID 4804882). An 80‑year‑old man was killed at Hylan Boulevard and Benton Avenue (NYC Open Data CrashID 4797079). A teen on a moped hit an MTA bus just after 1 AM on Castleton Avenue and was left in critical condition; as amNY reported, “No arrests have been made, and the investigation is ongoing by the NYPD’s Highway District Collision Investigation Squad” (amNY). In May, police said a 58‑year‑old pedestrian died after an e‑scooter struck him on Targee Street (amNY).

      The pattern on paper: who gets hit, and by what

      Across Staten Island since 2022, pedestrians are most often hurt or killed by cars and SUVs. City data attributes 486 pedestrian casualties to sedans and 369 to SUVs, with trucks and buses involved in 103 more. These counts include fatal and non‑fatal injuries reported in the crash database through Aug 30, 2025 (NYC Open Data).

      The names behind the numbers thread through the borough. Forest Avenue. Hylan. Gulf. College Avenue. Each record is short. Age. Body part. “Apparent Death.”

      Officials on the hook

      This area is represented by Council Member Joseph C. Borelli, Assembly Member Charles Fall (AD 61), and State Senator Jessica Scarcella‑Spanton (SD 23). The city and state have tools at hand.

      • Lower speeds: New York City can set safer limits under Sammy’s Law. A citywide 20 MPH default on residential streets is a concrete step. Details and contacts are here.
      • Stop repeat speeders: The Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045C/A2299C) targets drivers with heavy point or camera‑ticket histories by requiring speed‑limiting tech. See the bill summary and how to press your legislators here.

      The deaths keep coming. The levers exist.

      Do the simple, life‑saving things

      Slow the cars. Hold the worst drivers to account. If you live here, tell your council member, your senator, your assembly member: use the tools now. Start here.
