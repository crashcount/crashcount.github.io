      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx?

      Red Lights, Dead Bodies: Bronx Bleeds While City Stalls

      Written June 29, 2025

      Bronx: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      Blood on the Sidewalks

      Three men lay on the sidewalk at Hunts Point Avenue and Bruckner Boulevard. It was Friday night, June 27. A driver ran a red light, crashed into another car, then plowed into the men. One is still fighting for his life. The driver ran. Police caught him. He faces charges for vehicular assault and fleeing the scene. “Jenkins slammed on the gas and sped off east on Hunts Point Ave., blowing through a red light,” cops said.

      Five people went to the hospital. The street was left littered with broken glass and blood. The city calls these accidents. The families know better.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      In the last 12 months, 34 people have died on Bronx streets. Over 6,200 have been injured. Ninety-five were left with injuries so serious they may never walk the same. Children are not spared: 584 injured, two killed. The old are not spared: 127 injured, two killed. NYC Open Data

      Cars and SUVs do most of the killing. Trucks, buses, and motorcycles add to the toll. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about excuses.

      Leadership: Words, Delays, and the Cost of Waiting

      Local leaders talk about Vision Zero and safer streets. But the blood keeps coming. Speed cameras work, but only where they are installed. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so. The Bronx waits. “The unlicensed 28-year-old driver is facing multiple counts of vehicular assault following the 9:45 p.m. crash,” police said.

      Every delay is another family broken. Every unclaimed promise is another name on a slab. The city can act. The council can act. The mayor can act. They have not acted fast enough.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras on every deadly stretch. Demand action, not words.

      If you do nothing, nothing will change.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Bronx sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 11, assembly district AD 78 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in Bronx?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Bronx CB6, and District 11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 11, District 12, District 13, District 15, District 16, District 17, and District 18, Assembly Districts AD 78, AD 80, AD 81, AD 82, AD 83, AD 84, AD 85, and AD 87, and State Senate Districts SD 29, SD 31, SD 32, SD 33, SD 34, and SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Bronx?
      Most injuries and deaths come from Cars and SUVs (38 deaths, 71 serious injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (8 deaths, 18 serious injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (1 death, 4 serious injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 3 serious injuries). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. The numbers show patterns. Red lights run, speed ignored, laws unenforced. These deaths are preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, expand speed cameras, redesign dangerous streets, and act faster when lives are at stake.
