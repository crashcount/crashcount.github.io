      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx?

      The Bronx Bleeds: End Traffic Violence Now

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed citation formatting, deduped citations.)

      Bronx: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Bronx Crashes

      The Bronx does not sleep. Neither does the violence on its streets. In the last twelve months, 36 people have died and 94 have been seriously injured in traffic crashes here (NYC Open Data). The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the sound of sirens at midnight, the hush after a crash.

      Just days ago, a driver in a Ford Mustang failed a left turn at East 149th and Courtlandt. He mounted the sidewalk and struck six people. The driver ran. The victims went to Lincoln Hospital. Their injuries were called minor, but the fear will last longer. Police are still looking for the man who left them behind. The Daily News reported that “Cops are looking for a man who rammed into six people after botching a turn at an intersection in the Bronx.”

      A week before, a 79-year-old driver crashed into two cars and a pole. His passenger, Stella Nyarko-Dei, died. Seven others were hurt. A witness said, “I saw one lady was out on the ground. They was giving her medical attention.”

      The Numbers: Relentless and Rising

      Over 10,000 crashes in the past year. 6,909 injured. (NYC Open Data) The Bronx is a slow-motion disaster. Most victims are not behind the wheel. They are walking, riding, waiting for a light to change. Cars and SUVs do the most harm: 40 deaths and 74 serious injuries to pedestrians and cyclists since 2022 (NYC Open Data). Trucks and buses killed 8, motorcycles and mopeds 1. Bikes caused none.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      City leaders have moved—slowly. The city now has the power to lower speed limits. Some streets are down to 20 mph. Speed cameras run day and night. But the default speed is still too high. Enforcement falls hardest on the vulnerable. Cyclists get tickets. Drivers get warnings. The dead stay dead.

      What Comes Next: Demand Action

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to lower the speed limit to 20 mph everywhere. Tell them to target the drivers who do the most harm. Tell them to stop blaming the people in the crosswalk. The Bronx bleeds. It does not have to.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Bronx sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 11, assembly district AD 78 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in Bronx?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Bronx CB6, and District 11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 11, District 12, District 13, District 15, District 16, District 17, and District 18, Assembly Districts AD 78, AD 80, AD 81, AD 82, AD 83, AD 84, AD 85, and AD 87, and State Senate Districts SD 29, SD 31, SD 32, SD 33, SD 34, and SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Bronx?
      Cars and SUVs: 40 deaths, 74 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 8 deaths, 19 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 4 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 3 serious injuries. Cars and SUVs do the most harm. (NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. These crashes are preventable. They happen because of choices—speed, street design, enforcement priorities—not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower the speed limit to 20 mph citywide, expand automated enforcement, redesign dangerous streets, and focus enforcement on the drivers who do the most harm.
