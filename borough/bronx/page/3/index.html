      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx?

      Bronx Streets Still Bleed—How Many More Before City Hall Acts?

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, deduped and reordered citations, and ensured all quotes have inline links.)

      Bronx: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      Blood on the Boulevard

      In the Bronx, the street keeps taking. Thirteen people killed since January. 3,218 injured. Forty-three left with wounds so deep they may never heal. These are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the sound of sirens at night, the hush after impact.

      Just days ago, a man ran a red light at Hunts Point and Bruckner. He hit three people on the sidewalk. One is fighting for his life. The driver ran, but police caught him. He faces charges for vehicular assault and reckless endangerment. Police said, “Jenkins slammed on the gas and sped off east on Hunts Point Ave., blowing through a red light” (NY Daily News).

      On Pelham Parkway, a 31-year-old motorcyclist died when a Chevy Equinox turned left into him. The driver stayed. The rider did not make it. “Both she and Martinez were transported by EMS to Jacob Medical Center, where Martinez died from severe internal injuries” (NY Daily News).

      The Toll Grows, The Clock Ticks

      Since 2022, 129 dead. 21,493 injured. Most killed are on foot or on bikes. Cars and SUVs do most of the damage—38 deaths, 2,315 minor injuries, 840 moderate, 72 serious. Trucks and buses crush eight more. Motorcycles and mopeds, one. Bikes, none dead, but 83 hurt. The street is not safe for the softest bodies.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      City leaders have passed new laws. Speed cameras run 24/7. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. Some streets have changed. But the pace is slow. The blood keeps coming. “The number of lives lost to traffic violence is simply unacceptable… 127 deaths in this calendar year alone is 127 too many,” said Council Member Erik Bottcher (transalt.org).

      What Now? Demand More

      Every day of delay is another family broken. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph limit on every street. Demand real protection for those who walk and ride. The Bronx cannot wait. The dead cannot speak. You must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Bronx sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 11, assembly district AD 78 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in Bronx?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Bronx CB6, and District 11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 11, District 12, District 13, District 15, District 16, District 17, and District 18, Assembly Districts AD 78, AD 80, AD 81, AD 82, AD 83, AD 84, AD 85, and AD 87, and State Senate Districts SD 29, SD 31, SD 32, SD 33, SD 34, and SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Bronx?
      Cars and SUVs: 38 deaths, 2,315 minor injuries, 840 moderate injuries, 72 serious injuries. Trucks and buses: 8 deaths, 172 minor injuries, 63 moderate injuries, 18 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 99 minor injuries, 53 moderate injuries, 4 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 57 minor injuries, 23 moderate injuries, 3 serious injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes can be prevented. Speeding, red-light running, and reckless driving are choices. Safer street design and lower speed limits save lives. The Bronx keeps losing people because action is too slow.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, expand protected bike lanes, daylight intersections, and enforce laws against reckless driving. They can push for faster street redesigns and hold city agencies accountable.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
