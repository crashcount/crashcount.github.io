      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx?

      Blood on the Bronx Sidewalks—Who Will Stop the Next Hit?

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Bronx: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Bone

      Six people on a Bronx sidewalk. A Mustang mounts the curb, hits them, keeps going, smashes into scaffolding. The driver and passenger run. The street is left with blood and twisted metal. “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting,” said Vivian Cole. The youngest victim is 30. The oldest is 79. All are sent to Lincoln Hospital. The car is left behind, its plates gone.

      Days later, a truck overturns and floods a Bronx road with liquid asphalt. No one dies, but the street is closed, the air thick with the smell of tar. The city moves on. Liquid asphalt poured onto a road in the Bronx after a truck overturned Wednesday.

      The Numbers That Don’t Stop

      In the last year, 36 people died on Bronx streets. Ninety-six suffered serious injuries. Over 6,900 were hurt. Most were walking, riding, or just trying to cross. Cars and SUVs did most of the killing—40 deaths and 74 serious injuries. Trucks and buses added eight more deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds, one. Bikes, none. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors, gone.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      The city has started to act. Lower speed limits. More speed cameras. Some streets redesigned. But the work is slow, and the blood comes fast. The Mustang that hit six people had no plates. The driver ran. Cameras can’t catch what isn’t there. Laws can’t save those already down.

      Local leaders have the power. They can lower speed limits to 20 mph. They can push for the Stop Super Speeders Act. They can demand streets that put people first. Some have spoken. Many have not. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join the fight.

      No one else will do it for you. The street will not forgive your silence.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Bronx sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 11, assembly district AD 78 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in Bronx?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Bronx CB6, and District 11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 11, District 12, District 13, District 15, District 16, District 17, and District 18, Assembly Districts AD 78, AD 80, AD 81, AD 82, AD 83, AD 84, AD 85, and AD 87, and State Senate Districts SD 29, SD 31, SD 32, SD 33, SD 34, and SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Bronx?
      Cars and SUVs: 40 deaths, 74 serious injuries. Trucks and buses: 8 deaths, 19 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 death, 4 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 3 serious injuries. Cars and SUVs are the main killers.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speed limits, and enforcement focused on dangerous driving. They are not random. They are the result of choices.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, expand speed cameras, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws to stop repeat dangerous drivers. They can act now, or answer for the next death.
