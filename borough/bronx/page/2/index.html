      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx?

      Bronx Streets Are Killing Fields—Lower the Speed, Save a Life

      Written August 13, 2025 (Revised August 13, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, tightened prose, ensured all citations are deduped and properly linked.)

      Bronx: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 13, 2025

      The Death Count Keeps Climbing

      In the Bronx, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, 139 people have died and 353 have been seriously injured on Bronx streets (NYC Open Data). In the last twelve months alone, 38 lives ended and 119 more were shattered by injuries that do not heal. The bodies are not numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. They are the man thrown from his moped, the woman crossing White Plains Road, the two young riders who never made it home.

      Just this week, two men on motorcycles were killed on the Bronx River Parkway. Police say a 21-year-old driver tried to pass, slammed into a car, then struck the riders. Both were thrown onto the road. Both died at the hospital. The driver was charged with DWI and manslaughter. The families ask why he walks free. “Two people were killed. He was drunk. Think about how he took two lives. That’s not fair,” said the victim’s sister. “How could they let him go? How could they do that?” asked the mother.

      The Pattern: Speed, Distraction, and Policy Failure

      The Bronx is a killing field for the vulnerable. SUVs and cars are the main weapons—41 deaths, 83 serious injuries since 2022. Trucks and buses killed 11 more. Motorcycles and mopeds: another death toll. Pedestrians and cyclists are left broken. The cause is not fate. It is speed, distraction, and a system that lets repeat offenders back behind the wheel.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      Local leaders have made noise. Albany renewed speed cameras. The city can now lower speed limits. But the default is still 25 mph. Cameras catch speeders, but the worst repeat offenders keep driving. The city has the power to set a 20 mph limit. It has not used it. The Stop Super Speeders Act sits in Albany. The dead keep coming.

      What You Can Do

      This is not an accident. It is a choice. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join groups like Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Bronx sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 11, assembly district AD 78 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in Bronx?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Bronx CB6, and District 11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 11, District 12, District 13, District 15, District 16, District 17, and District 18, Assembly Districts AD 78, AD 80, AD 81, AD 82, AD 83, AD 84, AD 85, and AD 87, and State Senate Districts SD 29, SD 31, SD 32, SD 33, SD 34, and SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Bronx?
      SUVs and cars (including sedans) caused 41 deaths and 83 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 11 more. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 1 death and 4 serious injuries. Bikes caused no deaths but 4 serious injuries. The majority of deaths come from cars and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These are not accidents. The main causes are speed, distraction, and repeat dangerous driving. Policies like lower speed limits and action against repeat offenders can prevent these deaths.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the speed limit to 20 mph, pass and enforce laws against repeat speeders, and redesign streets for safety. They can also support and expand speed camera programs.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
