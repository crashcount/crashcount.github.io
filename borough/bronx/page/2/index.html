      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx?

      Steel Wins. Bodies Break. Bronx Streets Need Justice Now.

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations for accuracy and order.)

      Bronx: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Blood

      In the Bronx, the street is a gauntlet. Since 2022, 130 people have died and over 21,000 have been injured in crashes. 316 suffered serious injuries—the kind that do not heal. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the sound of sirens at dawn, the hush after impact.

      Just days ago, on July 3rd, a Ford Mustang mounted the curb at East 149th and Courtlandt. Six people were struck. The driver and passenger ran. Vivian Cole heard the aftermath: “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting.” The street was chaos. Christina Sieh thought it was a bomb: “We thought it was a bomb or something, because we are all panicking around here.”

      This is not rare. In the last year, 14 people have died and 44 have been seriously injured in the Bronx. The dead include children and elders. The wounded are left to rebuild their lives, if they can.

      Who Bears the Brunt

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. Since 2022, they have killed 38 and seriously injured 73 pedestrians. Trucks and buses killed 8, motorcycles and mopeds 1. Bikes killed none. The street is not neutral. It favors steel over flesh.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has passed laws. Speed cameras now run 24/7. Speed limits can be lowered. But the carnage continues. Every delay is another body on the pavement. Every unprotected crosswalk is a dare.

      Local leaders have spoken. But words do not stop cars. Policies that shift blame to the walker or the cyclist are a lie. The Bronx needs action—street redesigns, enforcement against reckless drivers, and a citywide 20 mph speed limit.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand safer streets. Do not wait for the next siren. The Bronx cannot afford another day of silence.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Bronx sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 11, assembly district AD 78 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in Bronx?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Bronx CB6, and District 11 neighborhoods.       ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Bronx?
      Cars and SUVs: 38 deaths, 73 serious injuries. Trucks and buses: 8 deaths, 18 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 death, 4 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 3 serious injuries. Cars and SUVs are the main killers.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—speed, street design, lack of enforcement. With better policies and action, most could be prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, enforce laws against reckless driving, and fund infrastructure that protects people walking and biking. They can act now, not after another death.
