      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx?

      Bronx Streets Run Red While Leaders Stall

      Written July 24, 2025 (Revised July 24, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Bronx: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 24, 2025

      The Bronx Bleeds—One Crash at a Time

      The Bronx does not get a break. In the last twelve months, 40 people died and 7,846 were injured on its streets. That’s more than 21 people hurt every single day. The dead do not get a second chance. The injured carry scars the rest of us never see.

      Just two weeks ago, a driver in a Ford Mustang failed a left turn at East 149th and Courtlandt. He mounted the sidewalk and hit six people. The driver ran. The victims went to Lincoln Hospital. The police are still looking. “Cops are looking for a man who rammed into six people after botching a turn at an intersection in the Bronx,” the report read.

      A few days before that, a 79-year-old driver crashed into two cars and a pole in Co-op City. His 71-year-old passenger died. Seven others were hurt. “I saw one lady was out on the ground. They was giving her medical attention, checking her body. She was laid out,” said Samuel Cherry.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Since 2022, 138 people have died and 23,477 have been injured in Bronx traffic crashes. Of those, 341 suffered serious injuries—lives changed in a heartbeat. The old, the young, the ones just trying to cross the street. No one is spared. SUVs and sedans lead the body count.

      What Leaders Have Done—And Not Done

      Local leaders have talked about Vision Zero. They have promised safer streets. But the blood keeps flowing. Speed cameras are in place, but the citywide speed limit is still 25 mph. The power to lower it to 20 mph is there. It sits unused. The Bronx needs more than words. It needs action.

      What You Can Do—And Must Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand streets that put people first. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Every day of delay is another day of loss.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Bronx sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 11, assembly district AD 78 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in Bronx?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Bronx CB6, and District 11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 11, District 12, District 13, District 15, District 16, District 17, and District 18, Assembly Districts AD 78, AD 80, AD 81, AD 82, AD 83, AD 84, AD 85, and AD 87, and State Senate Districts SD 29, SD 31, SD 32, SD 33, SD 34, and SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Bronx?
      Most injuries and deaths come from SUVs and Sedans (cars), which together account for the majority of pedestrian harm in the Bronx. Trucks and Buses also cause significant injuries and deaths. Motorcycles and Mopeds are involved in fewer cases, but still cause deaths and serious injuries. Bikes are involved in a small number of incidents, with no deaths reported. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speed, driver inattention, and unsafe street design are common factors. Safer policies and enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower the speed limit to 20 mph, redesign dangerous streets, expand automated enforcement, and fund safe infrastructure. They can act now, or answer for more deaths.
