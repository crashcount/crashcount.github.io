      Bronx Streets Run Red—Hold Leaders to Account

      Written June 19, 2025

      Bronx: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 19, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Bone

      In the Bronx, the street takes its due. In the past year, 35 people have died in crashes. More than 6,300 have been injured. Ninety-four were hurt so badly their lives will never be the same. The dead include children, elders, men, and women. The living carry scars.

      A 76-year-old woman was killed by an SUV on White Plains Road. The cause: driver inattention. She was not at an intersection. She was just there, and then she was gone. NYC Open Data

      A 43-year-old man died at an intersection on Webster Avenue. The car was speeding. The car did not stop. The man did not get up. NYC Open Data

      The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the sound of sirens in the night.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders talk of safety. The city has added protected bike lanes, redesigned intersections, and lowered speed limits in some places. But the work is not finished. The Bronx still bleeds.

      Speed cameras have cut speeding by 63% where installed. Injuries drop 14%. But the law that keeps these cameras running is always at risk. Each year, Albany must act or the cameras go dark.

      Mayor Adams said, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” But the city has not yet used its new power to lower the speed limit to 20 mph everywhere. The law is there. The will is not.

      The Work Ahead

      This is not fate. These deaths are not the weather. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by those who design and police the streets.

      If you want change, you must demand it. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to lower the speed limit, keep the cameras on, and redesign the streets for people, not cars. Take action now.

      The street does not care. But you can.
