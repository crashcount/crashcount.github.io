      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx?

      Bronx Streets Bleed While City Sleeps

      Written June 20, 2025 (Revised June 20, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      Bronx: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Steel

      In the Bronx, the street is a gauntlet. In the last twelve months, 35 people died in crashes. 94 were seriously injured. The wounded fill hospital beds. The dead leave shoes in the crosswalk, blood on the curb. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared.

      A 75-year-old man walked home from mosque. He tried to wave off a turning BMW SUV. The driver did not stop. Four months later, police charged him. “The accident is so terrible,” a neighbor said. “I’ve never seen anything like this in my life.”

      A 14-year-old boy rode the top of a subway car. He fell. His friends ran. He went to the hospital in critical condition. “Six people, most of them teens, died subway surfing in the city last year. The youngest was just 11,” police told the NY Daily News.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      In the last year:

      • 9,417 crashes in the Bronx.
      • 6,309 injured.
      • 35 dead.
      • 94 left with injuries that change a life forever.

      Most victims are young. Under 24s suffered 8 deaths and 914 injuries. Elders are not safe. Ages 55 and up: 15 deaths, 1,003 injuries. The street does not care how old you are.

      Leadership: Action and Silence

      The city passed Sammy’s Law. Now, New York can set its own speed limits. But the limit is not yet lowered. The law sits. The blood does not wait. Speed cameras work, but Albany lets their future hang by a thread. The Bronx needs more than promises. It needs action.

      What You Can Do

      Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras stay on.

      Every day of delay is another family broken. The city knows what works. The numbers are not a mystery. The only question is how many more must die before leaders act.

      Take action now.

      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not just accidents. The numbers show patterns—speed, failure to yield, and dangerous turns. Lowering speed limits and enforcing the law can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits to 20 mph, keep speed cameras running, and redesign streets for safety. They can pass laws that protect people, not just cars. Delay costs lives.
