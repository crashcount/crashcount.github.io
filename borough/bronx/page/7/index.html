      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx?

      Bronx Streets Bleed While City Hall Sleeps

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations.)

      Bronx: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Blood on Bronx Streets

      Sixteen dead. Forty-eight left with wounds that will not heal. In the Bronx this year, these numbers are not just statistics. They are mothers, sons, neighbors—the cost of walking, riding, or simply being in the wrong place when a driver loses control.

      Just days ago, a 71-year-old woman died when a 79-year-old driver crashed into two cars and a light pole. “I saw one lady was out on the ground. They was giving her medical attention, checking her body. She was laid out,” said Samuel Cherry. Seven others were hurt. The street was quiet, except for the sound of sirens and the scrape of metal.

      A week before, liquid asphalt poured across a Bronx road after a truck overturned. No one died, but the street was left slick and dangerous. The risk is always there, waiting. “Liquid asphalt poured onto a road in the Bronx after a truck overturned Wednesday,” reported CBS New York.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Pedestrians and passengers pay most. In the last twelve months, 36 people died in Bronx crashes. Ninety-four suffered serious injuries. The young, the old, the ones just trying to cross the street. Cars and SUVs did most of the killing—forty deaths. Trucks and buses took eight more. Motorcycles and mopeds, one. Not one person died from a bicycle.

      What Has Been Done—And What Hasn’t

      Local leaders have talked about safety. Some have acted. The city has lowered speed limits in some zones, added speed cameras, and started to redesign streets. But the pace is slow. The dead keep coming. The city has the power to set a 20 mph limit everywhere. It has not used it. The Bronx waits.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      Every day of delay is another family broken. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to use the laws they have. Lower the speed. Build real protection. End the carnage. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Bronx sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 11, assembly district AD 78 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in Bronx?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Bronx CB6, and District 11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 11, District 12, District 13, District 15, District 16, District 17, and District 18, Assembly Districts AD 78, AD 80, AD 81, AD 82, AD 83, AD 84, AD 85, and AD 87, and State Senate Districts SD 29, SD 31, SD 32, SD 33, SD 34, and SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Bronx?
      Cars and Trucks: Cars and SUVs were involved in 40 deaths and 74 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 8 deaths and 19 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death and 4 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 3 serious injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. The same deadly patterns repeat. They are not random.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, expand automated enforcement, and focus on drivers who repeatedly break the law. They can act now, not just talk.
      ▸ How many people have been killed or seriously hurt in Bronx traffic crashes recently?
      In the past year, 36 people died and 94 suffered serious injuries in Bronx crashes. NYC Open Data
