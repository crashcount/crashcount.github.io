      Manhattan Bleeds: City Watches, Drivers Speed, Victims Fall

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      Manhattan: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 18, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Bone

      A man steps into the crosswalk. An SUV keeps going. He does not get up. In the last twelve months, 39 people died on Manhattan’s streets. Over 5,500 were hurt. 125 were seriously injured—bodies broken, lives changed. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old. Two children died. Seven people over 75. The city keeps moving. The dead stay still.

      The Faces Behind the Numbers

      A cyclist in the West Village is hit. He wakes up in an ambulance, blood soaking his sweater. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” said Myung Jin Chung. He does not remember the crash. The driver ran a red light, then ran away. The city found him later. The pain stays.

      A woman climbs down to the subway tracks at Union Square. She tries to get back up. She cannot. The train does not stop. “When a train entered the station, the woman scrambled in vain to get back onto the platform but was struck by the train and died at the scene,” reported NY Daily News.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Speed kills. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can now set its own speed limits. But the limit is not lower yet. The law sits on a desk. Cameras that catch speeders are at risk of going dark unless Albany acts again. The city built new bike lanes, redesigned intersections, and added pedestrian space. But the deaths keep coming. The city says, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many”—but words do not slow cars.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. Every crash is a choice, a failure to act. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. Albany can keep speed cameras on. You can call. You can demand more. The dead cannot.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand safer speeds and streets. Take action now.
