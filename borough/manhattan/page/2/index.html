      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan?

      Manhattan’s Streets Run Red—Speed Kills, Silence Lets It

      Written June 22, 2025 (Revised June 21, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations.)

      Manhattan: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 22, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Bone

      A man rides an e-bike in Central Park. He swerves to avoid a pedestrian in the crosswalk. He flies from the saddle, strikes his head on the curb, and dies at Mount Sinai Morningside. The pedestrian walks away with a cut hand. The city marks another death. A 43-year-old Bronx resident died on June 18 after flying from an e-bike and striking his head on the curb.

      In the last twelve months, 39 people have died on Manhattan’s streets. 124 suffered serious injuries. The dead include children, the old, and the nameless. The living carry scars. In three years, 112 have died. Over 18,000 have been hurt. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      Who Pays the Price

      SUVs and cars kill most often. Trucks, buses, bikes, and mopeds all take their share. Of the pedestrians struck, 38 died under the wheels of cars or SUVs. Trucks and buses killed 15. Bikes killed 2. The city counts the bodies. The city moves on.

      A 74-year-old cyclist dies after a crash with a bus on West End Avenue. A 57-year-old man is crushed by an SUV on Broadway. A child is killed crossing Manhattan Avenue. The stories do not end. They only pause for breath.

      Leadership: Words and Waiting

      City leaders say the right things. They promise zero deaths. They pass laws. They install cameras. But the pace is slow. “In order to make New York City the best place to raise a family, we need to be safer at every level — including on our streets,” said Mayor Eric Adams. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so. Cameras that catch speeders need Albany’s blessing to keep running. Each day of delay is another risk, another family left with a hole.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      Call your council member. Demand the city lower speed limits now. Tell Albany to keep the speed cameras running. Join Transportation Alternatives or Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      The disaster is slow, but it is not silent. Make them hear you.

      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or can they be prevented?
      These are not random accidents. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement prevent deaths. Every delay in action means more lives lost.
