      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan?

      Blood on Broadway: Lower the Speed or Count More Dead

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, and cleaned citations.)

      Manhattan: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Steel

      Manhattan bleeds, slow and steady. In the past year, 35 people died on its streets. Five thousand four hundred twenty-one were hurt. One hundred twenty-one suffered injuries so grave they changed a life. The dead do not speak. The wounded limp, or do not walk at all.

      Just last month, a 74-year-old man on a bike was killed by a bus on West End Avenue. A 44-year-old cyclist died after being thrown from his bike on Centre Street. A pedestrian was crushed by an SUV at Broadway and 86th. The numbers pile up. The names fade.

      The Faces Behind the Numbers

      On January 26, Willow Hall tried to cross Harlem River Drive. He never made it. An unlicensed driver hit him. Six months passed before police made an arrest. The paper reported “Donald Pinnock was charged Sunday with aggravated unlicensed operation of a motor vehicle.”

      In Central Park, Salvador Nico-Garcia rode his e-bike. He collided with a pedestrian in a crosswalk. He was thrown from his bike and struck his head on the curb. As the NY Daily News described, “Nico Garcia was thrown from his electric bike by the impact and slammed his head on the curb.”

      Each crash is a wound. Each wound is a warning.

      Leaders Move, But the Blood Still Flows

      The city has lowered speed limits in some places. It has added red-light cameras. It has decriminalized jaywalking. But the deaths keep coming. Council Member Erik Bottcher said, “The number of lives lost to traffic violence is simply unacceptable… 127 deaths in this calendar year alone is 127 too many.”

      The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has the power. It has not used it.

      Call to Action: Make Them Feel the Heat

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to use the power they have. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand streets that put people first. Every day they wait, someone else does not come home.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan?
      Cars and Trucks: 38 deaths, 2206 minor injuries, 1102 moderate injuries, 77 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 123 minor injuries, 81 moderate injuries, 14 serious injuries. Bikes: 2 deaths, 257 minor injuries, 188 moderate injuries, 19 serious injuries. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement save lives. The city has the power to act but too often waits until after the next death.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the speed limit to 20 mph citywide, redesign streets for safety, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can act now or answer for every life lost while they wait.
