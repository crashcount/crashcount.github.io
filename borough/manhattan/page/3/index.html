      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan?

      Manhattan’s Blood Price: 117 Dead, Thousands Broken—Who Will Stop the Next Siren?

      Written July 19, 2025

      Manhattan: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 19, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Steel

      In Manhattan, the numbers do not sleep. Since January 2022, 117 people have died on these streets. Nearly 20,000 have been hurt. 376 were left with injuries so grave they will not forget them.

      The dead are not numbers. They are the old man crushed by an SUV on West 77th. The cyclist killed on Broome Street. The child who never made it across Manhattan Avenue. Just last month, a city worker fixing a sign was slashed by an e-bike rider after a near miss. The DOT called it an “abhorrent assault”. The worker bled on the sidewalk. The rider fled.

      In Central Park, a 72-year-old woman was hit twice by cyclists. She suffered kidney damage. The police never followed up. “Ninety percent of their crashes have no police report and in 90 percent of these crashes over 90 percent the rider flees the scene,” she said. “It is so much worse than the DOT stats show.”

      Who Bears the Weight

      Cars and SUVs do the killing. They are behind 39 pedestrian deaths and over 3,400 injuries. Trucks and buses add 16 more dead. Bikes and mopeds, too, leave their mark—2 killed by bikes, 1 by mopeds. But the scale is not the same. The street is a field of unequal force.

      The city has moved. Speed limits are dropping. Cameras catch speeders day and night. Intersections are cleared for sightlines. But the work is not done. The city can set a 20 mph limit everywhere. It has not. The most dangerous drivers still roam. The laws to stop them wait in Albany.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Every day of delay is another family broken. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Do not wait for the next siren.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan?
      Cars and SUVs: 39 pedestrian deaths and over 3,400 injuries; Trucks and Buses: 16 deaths and over 350 injuries; Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death and over 200 injuries; Bikes: 2 deaths and nearly 500 injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      The numbers show a pattern, not chance. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement prevent deaths. Every crash is a policy choice.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the speed limit to 20 mph citywide, expand automated enforcement, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws to stop repeat dangerous drivers.
