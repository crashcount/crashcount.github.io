      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan?

      Lower the Limit, Save a Life—No More Blood on Manhattan Streets

      Written July 2, 2025

      Manhattan: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 2, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Bone

      In Manhattan, the killing does not stop. In the last twelve months, 35 people died. Over 5,400 were hurt. One hundred twenty-four were left with wounds that will not heal. These are not just numbers. They are bodies on the street, families waiting for a call that never comes.

      Just last month, a 43-year-old man on an e-bike struck a pedestrian in a Central Park crosswalk. He flew from his bike, hit his head on the curb, and died at the hospital. The pedestrian walked away with a cut hand. Police said, “Nico-Garcia was then flung from his bike and struck his head on the curb” according to West Side Spirit.

      Six months after a crash in East Harlem, police arrested a 74-year-old unlicensed SUV driver for killing a pedestrian. The man was charged with aggravated unlicensed operation. The victim was 53. He died crossing Harlem River Drive. The driver stayed at the scene. The charge came half a year later. “Donald Pinnock was charged Sunday with aggravated unlicensed operation of a motor vehicle” reported NY Daily News.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the harm. In three years, cars and SUVs killed 38 pedestrians, left 79 with serious injuries, and hurt thousands more. Trucks and buses killed 15. Motorcycles and mopeds killed one. Bikes killed two. The streets are not safe for anyone who walks or rides.

      What Leaders Do—And What They Don’t

      After years of blood, Albany passed Sammy’s Law. Now the city can set its own speed limits. The city started to lower some streets to 20 mph. But the default is still 25. The council and the mayor could drop it citywide. They have not. “The number of lives lost to traffic violence is simply unacceptable… 127 deaths in this calendar year alone is 127 too many,” said Council Member Erik Bottcher on transalt.org.

      Red-light cameras have been expanded. Speed cameras run all day. But the city drags its feet on redesigning streets, daylighting corners, and building real protection for people outside cars. The dead keep coming.

      The Call

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit everywhere. Demand streets built for people, not machines. Do not wait for another name to be added to the count.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan?
      Cars and SUVs: Killed 38, seriously injured 79, and hurt thousands. Trucks and buses: Killed 15, seriously injured 16. Motorcycles and mopeds: Killed 1, seriously injured 14. Bikes: Killed 2, seriously injured 19. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The patterns are clear. Most deaths and injuries come from cars, SUVs, and trucks. These are preventable with lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement. Policy, not fate, shapes the toll.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower the citywide speed limit to 20 mph, redesign streets for safety, expand daylighting, and enforce against reckless driving. They can act now, or let the deaths continue.
