      Manhattan’s Streets Are Killing Us—Lower the Speed, Save a Life

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, deduped and reordered citations.)

      Manhattan: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll: Death and Injury on Manhattan Streets

      A man steps off the curb. A cyclist pedals through Soho. A child waits at the crosswalk. The city does not stop for them. In the last twelve months, 38 people died on Manhattan’s streets. 128 were seriously injured. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old. Two children under 18 are dead. Seven people over 75, gone. The city keeps moving.

      The Latest Crashes: No One Is Safe

      A 44-year-old cyclist was killed at Broome and Centre. A truck, a bike, and an SUV. The cyclist was ejected and died at the scene, according to NYC Open Data.

      On West 86th, a 57-year-old man was crushed by an SUV while crossing. On West End Avenue, a 74-year-old cyclist died after colliding with a bus. The city’s most vulnerable—pedestrians, cyclists, the old—pay the price.

      Leadership: Promises, Delays, and the Fight for Change

      The city says it is acting. Speed cameras now run day and night. The law is called Sammy’s Law. It lets the city lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit has not changed yet. The law sits, waiting for leaders to act.

      The NYPD cracks down on cyclists and delivery workers. Hundreds of tickets for minor bike violations. Ben Furnas of Transportation Alternatives called it a “really inappropriate escalation”. The crackdown lands hardest on those who cannot fight back.

      Commissioner Jessica Tisch says, “When it comes to traffic safety, compliance is not optional.” But the city’s own numbers show who is dying. It is not the drivers getting tickets. It is the people outside the cars.

      What Comes Next: The Cost of Waiting

      Every day the city waits, another family risks losing someone. The law is there. The cameras are there. The deaths keep coming. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Tell them to protect the vulnerable, not punish them. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Take action now.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan come from cars and trucks (37 deaths, 1,090 moderate injuries, 76 serious injuries), followed by bikes (2 deaths, 183 moderate injuries, 18 serious injuries), and motorcycles and mopeds (1 death, 81 moderate injuries, 14 serious injuries).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns: speeding, failure to yield, and dangerous street design. These are preventable. Every crash is a choice made by a driver or a city that could have acted.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, expand protected bike lanes, redesign intersections, and enforce laws that protect people outside cars. They can stop blaming cyclists and pedestrians and start acting on the laws they pass.
