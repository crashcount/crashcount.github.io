      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan?

      City Sleeps, Streets Bleed—Demand Safety Now

      Written July 16, 2025

      Manhattan: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A man steps off the curb. A bus crushes him at the intersection of 28th and 3rd. He is forty-nine. He dies there, early morning, June 16th. The cause: driver inattention. The record says “crush injuries.” No one else dies that day, but the street keeps its silence. See NYC crash data.

      Just days later, a cyclist is killed in Central Park. Head trauma. Ejected from his bike. The crash log lists “unspecified” as the cause. No answers, only another name erased. The city moves on.

      In the last twelve months, Manhattan saw 35 deaths and 130 serious injuries from traffic crashes. Seven children under 18 were killed or seriously hurt. The old are not spared: fourteen people over 65 are dead. The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do.

      Leaders Act—But Not Fast Enough

      The city claims progress. Mayor Adams calls the 32% drop in fatalities “historic” and vows to “continue using all tools available to drive down traffic violence”.

      But on Fifth Avenue, the city cut the bike lane and bus lane from the redesign. Two lanes for cars remain. Sidewalks are wider, but the most vulnerable are left exposed. “We want a real solution to the bike and pedestrian safety issue,” says Community Board 5 Vice Chair Samir Lavingia. “We need to do something to make sure bicyclists feel safer” (Fifth Avenue redesign coverage).

      The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so. Cameras catch speeders, but the worst repeat offenders keep driving. The city delays. The bodies pile up.

      What You Can Do

      Every day of delay is another risk. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, not promises. Demand that the city use every tool it has. The dead cannot speak. You must.

      Take action now.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan?
      Cars and Trucks: 39 deaths, 2293 minor injuries, 1154 moderate injuries, 84 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 125 minor injuries, 83 moderate injuries, 14 serious injuries. Bikes: 2 deaths, 272 minor injuries, 194 moderate injuries, 21 serious injuries. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      The data shows patterns: driver inattention, speeding, lack of safe infrastructure. These are not random. They can be prevented with lower speed limits, protected lanes, and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the speed limit to 20 mph, build protected bike lanes, and target repeat dangerous drivers. They can act now, not later.
