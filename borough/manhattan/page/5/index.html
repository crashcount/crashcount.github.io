      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan?

      Six Months, Two Deaths, Zero Urgency: Manhattan Streets Still Bleed

      Written June 24, 2025

      Manhattan: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 24, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Bone

      A man steps onto Harlem River Drive at dawn. A black SUV comes down the road. The man is struck. His name is Willow Hall. He dies there, at 53. The driver, Donald Pinnock, is unlicensed. He stays at the scene. Police wait six months to charge him. Donald Pinnock was charged Sunday with aggravated unlicensed operation of a motor vehicle.

      In Central Park, a cyclist named Salvador Nico-Garcia rides an e-bike. He collides with a pedestrian in a crosswalk. He is thrown from his bike. His head hits the curb. He dies. The pedestrian walks away with a scraped hand. A 43 year-old Bronx resident…died on June 18 after flying from an e-bike and striking his head on the curb.

      In the last 12 months, 38 people have died on Manhattan streets. Five thousand four hundred ninety-four have been hurt. One hundred twenty-five were left with injuries so grave they may never walk right again.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In the last three years, they took 38 lives and left 76 with serious wounds. Trucks and buses killed 15. Motorcycles and mopeds, one. Bikes, two. The numbers do not care about blame. They only count the bodies.

      What Leaders Do—And What They Don’t

      The city has built new crosswalks. It has hardened bike lanes. It has added speed cameras and lowered some speed limits. But the pace is slow. The carnage is not. The city can set a 20 mph limit on every street. It has not. The law is there. The will is not.

      The Call

      This is not fate. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to use Sammy’s Law. Lower the speed. Build the lanes. Protect the people. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not fate. The city can lower speed limits, redesign streets, and enforce the law. Every death is a policy choice, not an accident.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can set a 20 mph speed limit on every street using Sammy’s Law, build more protected bike lanes, and expand automated enforcement. They can act now, or answer for every preventable death.
      ▸ What has the city done lately to address traffic violence?
      The city has added speed cameras, built new crosswalks, and hardened bike lanes. But the default speed limit is still above 20 mph on most streets, and progress is slow.
