      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan?

      Manhattan Streets Still Kill. City Hall Still Sleeps.

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations.)

      Manhattan: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Blood

      In Manhattan, the street is a hard place. Since 2022, 117 people have died and 376 have been seriously injured in crashes. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about age or luck. In the last twelve months alone, 35 people died. Six were over 75. One was a child. The city counts the bodies. The families count the empty chairs.

      Just last week, a DOT worker was slashed by an e-bike rider at Broadway and Cedar. The worker was fixing a sign. The cyclist nearly hit the truck. Words were exchanged. Then a blade. The DOT called it an “abhorrent assault of a NYC DOT employee who performs critical work to keep our city moving”. The worker survived. The wound will heal. The fear will not.

      On July 15, a van crashed in Midtown. Police found 76 propane tanks and 75 gallons of gasoline inside. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment. A woman and a child sat in a parked car, struck by a loose food cart. They went to the hospital. The city was lucky this time. Firefighters forced entry into the van, removing 76 20-pound propane cylinders and 15 five-gallon fuel containers.

      Who Bears the Brunt

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay the highest price. SUVs and cars killed 39. Trucks and buses killed 16. Bikes killed 2. The old and the young are not spared. The street does not care if you are careful. It does not care if you have the light. The street remembers only the dead.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      The city has moved. Speed limits are dropping. Cameras now watch day and night. The city touts a historic drop in deaths, but the work is not done. Every day, drivers speed. Every day, someone does not come home. The law lets the city set a 20 mph limit. The city has not done it. The council has not forced the issue. The mayor has not signed the order. The blood dries. The street waits.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit for every street. Demand action against repeat speeders. Do not wait for another name on the list. The street will not wait. The city will not save itself. Only you can force their hand.

      ▸ Where does Manhattan sit politically?
      It belongs to city council district District 3, assembly district AD 65 and state senate district SD 27.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan?
      Cars and SUVs: 39 deaths, 84 serious injuries. Trucks and buses: 16 deaths, 16 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 death, 14 serious injuries. Bikes: 2 deaths, 21 serious injuries. Cars and trucks are the biggest killers by far.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns: speeding, reckless driving, and unsafe streets. These deaths and injuries are preventable with better laws, enforcement, and street design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can set a 20 mph speed limit citywide, expand automated enforcement, redesign dangerous streets, and crack down on repeat speeders. They can act now, or answer for every preventable death.
      ▸ How many people have been killed or seriously injured in Manhattan traffic since 2022?
      Since 2022, 117 people have died and 376 have been seriously injured in Manhattan traffic crashes.

      ▸

      Six died when a sightseeing helicopter shattered midair over the Hudson. The fuselage, rotor, and tail tore loose. Loud bangs echoed. The craft plunged. No black box. No warning. Only fragments and silence left behind.

      NY Daily News reported on May 7, 2025, that federal officials released images showing a sightseeing helicopter breaking apart in midair before crashing into the Hudson River, killing six. Witnesses heard 'several loud 'bangs'' before the breakup. The helicopter had flown eight tours that day, all with the same pilot, who had less than 50 hours in this model. The aircraft had a prior maintenance issue with its transmission assembly and lacked flight data recorders. The NTSB noted the pilot wore video-capable sunglasses, but they remain missing. The crash highlights gaps in oversight and the risks of repeated tour flights without robust recording or inspection requirements.
