      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan?

      Forty Dead, Thousands Broken—Who’s Still Waiting to Act?

      Written August 7, 2025

      Manhattan: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 7, 2025

      The Toll This Summer

      The city keeps count. The bodies do not. In the last twelve months, 40 people died on Manhattan streets. Seven thousand more were injured. One hundred forty-six suffered injuries so grave they may never walk right again.

      Just weeks ago, a stolen car tore through Chinatown at 100 miles per hour. It struck a woman on a bench and a man on his bike. Both died where they landed. The driver tried to run. The police found guns and a bottle in the car. The court record called the injuries “devastating”.

      On the Upper West Side, a cyclist was hit and left in the street. Neighbors said, “No one stops at these stop signs. We see people go through these red lights all the time.”

      The pattern is old. The pain is fresh.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      City Hall talks about Vision Zero. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not. Speed cameras now work around the clock, but the worst drivers keep driving. One and a half percent of motorists cause a fifth of pedestrian deaths.

      The city has started to rebuild Fifth Avenue for people, not cars. But the pace is slow. The blood dries faster than the paint.

      What Comes Next

      Every crash is preventable. The dead do not care about excuses. The living should not have to wait.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to use the power they have. Lower the speed limit. Fix the streets. Stop the carnage.

      Do not wait for another name on the list.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan come from cars and trucks (42 deaths, 3,806 total injuries), followed by motorcycles and mopeds (1 death, 235 total injuries), and bikes (2 deaths, 524 total injuries). See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. Every crash is preventable. Speed, reckless driving, and unsafe streets can be changed. The dead do not care about excuses.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets, and enforce laws that protect people walking and biking. They can act now, not just talk.
