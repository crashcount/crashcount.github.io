      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan?

      Speed Kills. City Stalls. Lives Shatter.

      Written July 31, 2025

      Manhattan: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 31, 2025

      The Toll on Manhattan’s Streets

      Every week, more lives are broken. In the last twelve months, Manhattan saw 39 deaths and 140 serious injuries from traffic crashes. These are not just numbers. They are people—cyclists, seniors, children—struck down on their way home, to work, or simply living their lives.

      Just this week, a 65-year-old man on an e-bike was hit on Second Avenue. The driver fled. The cyclist was left with severe head trauma, “unconscious when paramedics arrived” and rushed to Bellevue Hospital in critical condition (Streetsblog NYC). The unlicensed driver was arrested two hours later (West Side Spirit).

      On July 19th, a speeding car flew off the Manhattan Bridge, killing Kevin Cruickshank, a cyclist, and May Kwok, who was sitting on a bench. Guns and alcohol were found in the car. The intersection was “known to some to be very dangerous.” Kevin’s sister said, “It is time to make this known to all and time for the city to take action” (West Side Spirit).

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Local leaders have moved, but not fast enough. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not used it. Speed cameras are renewed, but the worst repeat offenders still drive. Council Member Christopher Marte called out the Department of Transportation for “ongoing neglect” after the bridge crash (West Side Spirit).

      Bills sit in Albany that could force the most dangerous drivers off the road. The Stop Super Speeders Act would require repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit. It is not law yet.

      The Numbers Behind the Blood

      The violence is relentless. In the last year, Manhattan saw 6,942 injuries and 140 serious injuries. The dead are mostly the old, the young, the ones on foot or bike. Cars and SUVs do most of the killing. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, and bikes all play their part. The city counts the bodies. The rest of us count the days until the next siren.

      What Now: Demand Action

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real penalties for repeat speeders. Demand streets built for people, not cars. Every day of delay is another family broken.

      Take action now.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan come from Cars and SUVs (42 deaths, 3,791 injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (16 deaths, 406 injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (1 death, 233 injuries), and Bikes (2 deaths, 521 injuries).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. These crashes are preventable. Speeding, reckless driving, and unsafe street design are choices. Every death and injury is a failure to act.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, pass laws to keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road, and ensure enforcement targets the most dangerous behavior—not the most vulnerable people.

      DOT Plans Car-Free 34th Street Busway
      ▸

      City aims to clear cars from 34th Street. Buses crawl now. Riders wait. DOT wants faster trips, safer crossings. Community boards back the plan. Painted lanes, fewer cars, more eyes on the street. Change comes for riders and walkers.

      West Side Spirit reported on May 20, 2025, that the Department of Transportation (DOT) has proposed turning 34th Street into a mostly car-free busway, modeled after the 14th Street busway. The move targets slow bus speeds—now just 3 mph—and aims to reduce systemic danger for bus riders and people on foot. No driver errors are cited, but the plan addresses longstanding risks from car traffic.
