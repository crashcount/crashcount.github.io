      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan?

      Manhattan’s Blood Price: Two More Dead, City Still Stalls on Safer Streets

      Written July 21, 2025

      Manhattan: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 21, 2025

      The Toll This Year

      The streets of Manhattan do not forgive. Since January 2022, 122 people have died in crashes. 385 more were seriously injured. In the last twelve months alone, 40 killed, 136 left with life-altering wounds. These are not numbers. They are mornings that never ended, plans that never happened, families that will never be whole again.

      Just this weekend, a stolen car jumped the curb at Canal and Bowery. Kevin Cruickshank was riding his bike. May Kwok was sitting on a bench. Both are dead. Police say the driver lost control and struck them before crashing into a police van. “It appears to be excessive speed. Two people are in custody, unfortunate incident. No one should lose their life in that manner,” said Mayor Eric Adams. Drugs and alcohol were found in the car. The driver had a prior arrest for unlicensed driving after a Brooklyn crash in April. She was released without bail. Three months later, two more are dead. “The out-of-control driver… had been freed without bail in April after she was arrested for leaving the scene of a crash that badly injured a pedestrian,” reported the Daily News.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. In the last year, children, the elderly, and working people have been killed or maimed. SUVs and sedans do most of the harm, but trucks, buses, and bikes all leave their mark. The city’s own data shows that cars and SUVs alone were involved in over 3,700 pedestrian injuries and deaths since 2022 (NYC Open Data). The violence is steady. It does not let up.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      City Hall has moved, but not fast enough. The city legalized jaywalking, ending a law that punished Black and Latino New Yorkers for crossing the street. Fifth Avenue is being rebuilt for people, not cars. Police chases are now limited to violent crimes, after too many ended in blood. But the speed limit remains too high. The city has the power to lower it to 20 mph. It has not. Each day of delay is another day of risk.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat dangerous drivers. Do not wait for the next name.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan?
      Cars and trucks (including SUVs) were involved in over 3,700 pedestrian injuries and deaths since 2022. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 231. Bikes were involved in 511. Cars and trucks do the most harm, but every mode leaves a mark. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The pattern is clear and relentless. Crashes happen where policy allows speed, repeat offenders, and dangerous streets. These deaths are preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower the speed limit to 20 mph, redesign streets for safety, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can act now, or answer for every life lost.
