      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 27?

      Speed Kills on Canal—Lower the Limit, Stop the Bloodshed

      Written July 21, 2025

      SD 27: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 21, 2025

      Blood on the Bowery

      Just days ago, a stolen car tore through Chinatown. The driver, 23, sped off the Manhattan Bridge, lost control at Canal and Bowery, and killed two people. Kevin Cruickshank was riding his bike. May Kwok was sitting on a bench. Both are gone. The car crashed into a police van. Drugs and alcohol were found inside. The driver had a record—arrested for leaving the scene of a crash that badly hurt a pedestrian in April, but released without bail. The city failed to stop her.

      A witness said, “the driver was speeding when tragedy struck” CBS New York. The mayor called it “excessive speed” and said, “No one should lose their life in that manner.” ABC7.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the last 12 months, SD 27 saw 4 killed and 25 seriously injured in traffic crashes. Over 1,200 were hurt. In three and a half years, 27 people have died. Nearly 60 suffered life-changing injuries. Most were walking or riding. Cars and SUVs did the most harm—killing 10, seriously injuring 15. Trucks and buses killed 7. Bikes killed 1. No one is safe. The street is a gauntlet.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Senator Brian Kavanagh has voted for safety. He backed the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit Open States. He voted to renew school speed zones. He supported truck weight sensors on the BQE and plans to reclaim the FDR for people, not cars. But the carnage continues. Laws are slow. Streets stay deadly. The city has the power to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not done it. The dead keep coming.

      Call to Action: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action on repeat speeders. Join the fight. Every day of delay is another life lost. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, votes on budgets, and oversees state policy, including traffic safety.
      ▸ Where does SD 27 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 1 and assembly district AD 65.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 27?
      It includes the Financial District-Battery Park City, Tribeca-Civic Center, Soho-Little Italy-Hudson Square, Greenwich Village, West Village, Chinatown-Two Bridges, Lower East Side, East Village, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 1, District 2, and District 3, and Assembly Districts AD 61, AD 65, AD 66, and AD 74.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 27?
      Cars and SUVs killed 10 and seriously injured 15. Trucks and buses killed 7 and seriously injured 2. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 3 serious injuries. Bikes killed 1 and seriously injured 3. Source: NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, reckless driving, and unsafe streets can be changed.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, pass laws to stop repeat speeders, fund safer street designs, and hold reckless drivers accountable.

      Kavanagh Supports Safety Boosting FDR Drive Removal Plan
      ▸

      Manhattan Borough President Mark Levine wants to tear down FDR Drive south of the Brooklyn Bridge. He calls for federal funds to turn the highway into a bike and pedestrian haven. The plan faces legal and political hurdles but aims to reclaim space for people.

      On September 20, 2023, Manhattan Borough President Mark Levine revived a proposal to remove the FDR Drive viaduct south of the Brooklyn Bridge. The plan, not yet in committee or assigned a bill number, seeks federal support through the Reconnecting Communities and Neighborhoods grant. Levine urges Mayor Adams to apply for funding, stating, 'We’re a perfect candidate for Reconnecting Communities.' State Senator Brian Kavanagh voiced support, saying, 'We will work with the borough president.' The proposal would transform the underused highway into a waterfront space for cyclists and pedestrians, echoing successful removals in cities like Seattle and Seoul. The project faces major legal, environmental, and jurisdictional obstacles, as the FDR is state-owned and exempt from congestion pricing. The city previously considered this removal for coastal resiliency, but dropped it from the final plan. No formal safety analysis was provided, but the plan centers vulnerable road users by reclaiming space from cars.
