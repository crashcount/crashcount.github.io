      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 27?

      Three Dead, Hundreds Broken—Kavanagh’s District Runs Red

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 27: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Blood on the Asphalt

      Three dead. Twenty-five left with life-altering wounds. In the last year alone, Senate District 27 has seen 1,326 crashes. Cyclists crushed. Pedestrians broken. These are not just numbers—they are bodies on Bowery, sirens on Broome, blood on the FDR.

      On May 1, George Smaragdis, a 44-year-old cyclist, was doored on Broome Street. The door flung open. He hit it, fell, and a truck ran him over. He died at Bellevue. The van driver said, “It happened so fast. I didn’t even see the guy.”

      Just days later, a hit-and-run driver in the West Village blew through a red, struck a cyclist, then reversed, nearly running him over again. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” said the victim, Myung Jin Chung. He woke up in an ambulance. The driver fled.

      Who Pays the Price

      The dead are not the only victims. In the past twelve months, 647 people have been injured in SD 27. Twenty-five suffered serious injuries. Most were walking or riding. Most never saw it coming.

      The killers are cars, trucks, SUVs. In this district, cars and trucks caused 669 pedestrian injuries and deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds, 44. Bikes, 101. The street is a gauntlet. The odds are against you if you are not behind the wheel.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      State Senator Brian Kavanagh has voted for bills to curb repeat speeders and create safety zones. He backed the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force the worst offenders to install speed-limiting devices. He voted to extend school speed zones. He supported truck weight sensors on the BQE. These are steps. But the blood keeps coming.

      No law has yet stopped the next door from swinging open, the next driver from running the light.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people who walk and ride. Every day you wait is another day someone does not come home. The street will not get safer until you force it to.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing statewide policy. Senators represent districts and vote on bills that affect all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 27 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 1 and assembly district AD 65. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 27?
      It includes the Financial District-Battery Park City, Tribeca-Civic Center, Soho-Little Italy-Hudson Square, Greenwich Village, West Village, Chinatown-Two Bridges, Lower East Side, East Village, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 1, District 2, and District 3, and Assembly Districts AD 61, AD 65, AD 66, and AD 74. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 27?
      Cars and trucks caused 669 injuries and deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 44. Bikes caused 101. The majority of harm comes from cars and trucks. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. They follow patterns and can be prevented with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, support automated enforcement, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws that target repeat dangerous drivers.
