      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 10?

      Belt Parkway Bleeds—How Many Lives Until We Change?

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 21, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 10: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Blood on the Parkway

      Bodies fly from cars. Metal burns. On July 5th, a BMW lost control on the Belt Parkway near Howard Beach. The car hit the divider, went airborne, and slammed into oncoming traffic. Two young lives ended. Five more people were left broken or burned. Police said, “No one in the doomed BMW was wearing a seat belt” (NY Daily News). The fire left nothing but wreckage and grief.

      This is not rare. In the last twelve months, 14 people died and 1,979 were injured in crashes across SD 10. Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians are all at risk (NYC Open Data).

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      The carnage is steady. Thirty-six dead. 6,198 injured. Sixty left with wounds that will never heal. Most are struck by cars and SUVs. Some are thrown from motorcycles. A few are hit while walking or riding a bike. The numbers do not care about age. Children under 18: two dead, 598 hurt. Elders over 75: three dead, 119 hurt (NYC Open Data).

      The Belt Parkway is a killing field. Speed and loss of control are common. In April, a Mazda SUV changed lanes too fast. The driver died. In February, a Toyota sedan sped down the Parkway. The driver was ejected and killed. The pattern repeats. The pain does not fade.

      What Has Senator Sanders Done?

      Senator James Sanders has voted for bills to curb repeat speeders and expand safety zones. In June, he voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force the worst repeat offenders to install speed-limiting devices (Open States). He also voted to extend school speed zones, aiming to protect children on their way to class. These are steps, not solutions.

      But the blood keeps flowing. The streets are not safe. The laws are not enough. Every day of delay means another family shattered.

      “Jewel was a loving, energetic mother who dreamed of entering the medical field so she could help others the way she always helped others” (NY Daily News).

      Call to Action: Demand More

      Call Senator Sanders. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat speeders. Demand streets built for people, not just cars.

      Do not wait for another crash. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York. [amny.com]
      ▸ Where does SD 10 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 32 and assembly district AD 23. [amny.com]
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 10?
      It includes the South Ozone Park, Howard Beach-Lindenwood, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, Springfield Gardens (South)-Brookville, Rosedale, Far Rockaway-Bayswater, Rockaway Beach-Arverne-Edgemere, Breezy Point-Belle Harbor-Rockaway Park-Broad Channel, and Queens CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 28, District 31, and District 32, and Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 31, and AD 32. [amny.com]
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 10?
      Most injuries and deaths were caused by Cars and Trucks (11 deaths, 468 minor injuries, 142 moderate injuries, 10 serious injuries), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 6 minor injuries, 2 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury), and Bikes (no deaths, 4 minor injuries, 2 moderate injuries, no serious injuries). [NYC Open Data]
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not inevitable. Patterns show that speed, repeat dangerous driving, and unsafe road design can be changed to prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, pass and enforce laws against repeat dangerous drivers, fund safer street designs, and push for real accountability for those who harm others on the road.
