      Belt Parkway Bleeds—How Many More Must Die Before We Act?

      Written August 6, 2025 (Revised August 6, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, removed headline/body duplication, cleaned citations, and ensured proper inline links.)

      SD 10: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 6, 2025

      The Blood on the Asphalt

      Just last month, two lives ended on the Belt Parkway. A BMW vaulted the divider. Metal tore. Two people were ejected and killed. Four more were left broken. The cause: speed. The record shows it again and again—speed, the weight of steel, and the silence after. In the last twelve months, 16 people died on these streets. Over 2,350 were injured. Thirty-one suffered injuries so severe they will never be the same. The youngest lost was just 22. The oldest, 78. No one is spared. NYC crash data

      The System Fails, Again and Again

      The carnage is not random. It is policy. It is the absence of action. Just days ago, two NYPD cruisers collided in Edgemere, Queens, racing to a call. Four officers went to the hospital. The intersection is a tangle of roads under the A train. The news reported, “Two police vehicles were badly damaged from the collision.” The cause is under review. The risk is always there. The next time, it could be a child in the crosswalk.

      A year ago, a 14-year-old girl named Fortune Williams died when an unlicensed teen, driving over 100 mph, lost control and crashed. Her mother said, “I wish they would never have given him that car… because if they didn’t give him that car, my daughter would still be here right now.”

      What Has Senator Sanders Done?

      Senator James Sanders has voted for bills to create safety zones and expand ignition interlock monitoring. He voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit legislation. He backed the extension of school speed zones. He has supported studies for new rail lines to connect transit deserts. But when congestion pricing was paused—cutting funds for safer streets—he did not stand up for the most vulnerable. The deaths keep coming.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call Senator Sanders. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat speeders. Demand streets built for people, not cars.

      Every day of delay is another life lost. The numbers are not just numbers. They are names. They are families. They are us.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for making laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect all residents. See more about new traffic laws in New York.
      ▸ Where does SD 10 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 32 and assembly district AD 23. Details on new traffic laws in New York.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 10?
      It includes the South Ozone Park, Howard Beach-Lindenwood, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, Springfield Gardens (South)-Brookville, Rosedale, Far Rockaway-Bayswater, Rockaway Beach-Arverne-Edgemere, Breezy Point-Belle Harbor-Rockaway Park-Broad Channel, and Queens CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 28, District 31, and District 32, and Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 31, and AD 32. See new traffic laws in New York.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 10?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for 11 deaths and 10 serious injuries; motorcycles and mopeds caused 1 serious injury; bikes caused no deaths or serious injuries. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. The data shows patterns—speed, repeat offenders, dangerous roads—that can be changed by policy and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, enforce laws against repeat dangerous drivers, and support transit and infrastructure that protect people walking and cycling.

      Sanders Supports Safety Boosting Traffic Laws and Lower Speeds
      ▸

      New York rolls out sweeping traffic laws for 2025. Charter bus seatbelts, lower speed limits, and congestion pricing hit the streets. Senator Sanders and Governor Hochul push for safer roads. School zones, bridges, and bus riders see new protections. Change comes fast.

      On December 31, 2024, New York announced new transportation laws for 2025. Senator James Sanders sponsored a ban on car lease turn-in fees, stating, "The legislation prohibits charging a turn-in fee at the expiration of the term of a vehicle lease, the basis of which is solely for administrative, handling or clerical charges." Governor Kathy Hochul signed this and other safety bills into law. Charter bus passengers ages 8 to 16 must now wear seatbelts, a response to a fatal crash in Orange County. Sammy's Law, enacted in 2024, lets the city lower speed limits to 20 mph, and even 10 mph on redesigned streets. The Department of Transportation will target 250 locations, focusing on school zones. The MTA launches congestion pricing in Manhattan, with funds set for transit upgrades. Hochul said, "These enhancements to our roads and bridges will improve mobility for motorists, pedestrians and cyclists alike."
