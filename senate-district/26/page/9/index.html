      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 26?

      No More Names on Asphalt: Demand Safer Streets Now

      Written July 9, 2025

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Toll: Death and Injury on Our Streets

      In Senate District 26, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, 31 people have died and 5,353 have been injured in 10,585 crashes. Seventy-four of those injuries were serious. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. Each number is a name, a family, a life cut short. In the last twelve months alone, seven more are gone.

      Just weeks ago, a 22-year-old moped rider was killed at Third Avenue and 67th Street. His brother remembered him simply: “He never stopped working.” The driver who killed him was drunk and unlicensed. The passenger survived with broken bones. The street remains the same.

      In February, a woman stepped out of a taxi on Flatbush Avenue. She was struck and killed. Both drivers stayed. The city moved on. No arrests have been made.

      Who Bears the Brunt?

      Cars and SUVs killed five pedestrians here. Trucks and buses killed two more. Bikes and mopeds left dozens injured. The most vulnerable—those on foot, on bikes, on small wheels—pay the price for every policy delay, every loophole, every unlicensed driver who slips through.

      What Has Senator Gounardes Done?

      Senator Andrew Gounardes has backed speed cameras and pushed for their renewal. He called the program “an extra boost” for safety and praised its record. He sponsored a bill to let cameras catch drivers who hide or deface their plates, closing loopholes for reckless drivers. He voted yes on redesigning streets for all users, not just cars. But the work is not done. Repeat offenders still drive. Streets still kill.

      What Next? Demand More

      Every day of delay is another risk. Call Senator Gounardes. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real consequences for repeat offenders. Join Families for Safe Streets. Stand up before another name becomes a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees state policy alongside the Assembly.
      ▸ Where does SD 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38 and assembly district AD 51.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 26?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Park Slope, Sunset Park (West), Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, Dyker Beach Park, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 38, District 39, District 47, and District 50, and Assembly Districts AD 46, AD 51, AD 52, and AD 64.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 26?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians in SD 26, with 5 deaths and 18 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 2 deaths and 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no pedestrian deaths but left 3 seriously injured. Bikes caused no deaths but 3 serious injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and holding repeat offenders accountable can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, close loopholes for reckless drivers, and ensure real consequences for repeat offenders.
