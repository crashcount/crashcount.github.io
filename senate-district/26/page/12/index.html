      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 26?

      Seven Dead. Hundreds Broken. Who Will Make Our Streets Safe?

      Written June 24, 2025

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 24, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 26, the numbers do not lie. Seven people killed. Thirteen left with serious injuries. Over 1,600 hurt in crashes in the last year alone (NYC Open Data). Crashes come steady as rain. A 22-year-old moped rider, Joel Mota, died on Third Avenue in Bay Ridge. He lived half a mile from the spot where his life ended. His brother described him as “a hardworking man” who “had a lot of love for his family”.

      A 74-year-old man was killed crossing Bay Ridge Avenue. A cyclist, age 43, died on the Belt Parkway. An elderly man on an e-bike was crushed by a turning bus at Tillary and Jay. The list runs long. The pain does not fade.

      Who Pays the Price

      The old, the young, the ones on foot or two wheels. In this district, cars and SUVs caused the most harm: five deaths, 17 serious injuries to pedestrians. Trucks and buses killed two more. Bikes and mopeds, too, are not spared—three killed, six seriously hurt (NYC Open Data).

      The streets do not forgive. The numbers are not just numbers. Each is a name, a family, a future cut short.

      What Gounardes Has Done—and Not Done

      Senator Andrew Gounardes has pushed for speed cameras. He called their renewal “possibly the most important street safety law on the books,” and said, “speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone” (Streetsblog NYC). He voted yes on bills for safer street design. He sponsored a bill to let cameras catch drivers who hide their plates. But when it came to the law that would force the worst repeat speeders to slow down, the Senate watered it down. The threshold was raised. The teeth were pulled. The most dangerous drivers still roam free (Streetsblog NYC).

      The Next Step Is Ours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Senator Gounardes. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real action against repeat offenders. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York.
      ▸ Where does SD 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38 and assembly district AD 51.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 26?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Sunset Park (West), Bay Ridge, Dyker Beach Park, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, Brooklyn CB10, and Brooklyn CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 38, District 39, District 47, and District 50, and Assembly Districts AD 46, AD 51, AD 52, and AD 64.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 26?
      Cars and SUVs caused 5 pedestrian deaths and 17 serious injuries in SD 26. Trucks and buses killed 2 more. Bikes and mopeds were involved in additional deaths and serious injuries, but did not cause pedestrian deaths (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are not random accidents. Policies like lower speed limits, street redesign, and enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to reduce traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand speed and red-light camera programs, redesign streets for safety, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable.

      Gounardes Supports Safety Boosting 24/7 Traffic Camera Enforcement
      ▸

      Mayor Adams stood at a deadly Brooklyn intersection. He blamed Albany for tying the city’s hands. He called out the NYPD for weak enforcement. Twenty-seven pedestrians dead this year. Cameras go dark at night. Speeders win. Vulnerable lives lost.

      On March 25, 2022, Mayor Adams held a press conference on street safety and traffic enforcement policy. The event spotlighted the city’s struggle to control its own traffic laws. Adams slammed state rules that force red light and speed cameras off between 10 p.m. and 6 a.m., calling it 'unbelievable.' He said, 'Who are we protecting? We’re protecting the speeders. How about protecting Isaiah?' State Senator Andrew Gounardes, district 26, echoed Adams: 'It makes no sense that the city cannot control its own destiny on these streets.' Adams admitted NYPD traffic enforcement lags, with summonses down by more than half since 2019. Twenty-seven pedestrians have died so far this year—three more than last year. The mayor pressed for 24/7 camera operation and renewed NYPD focus. The council and advocates demand Albany act before more lives are lost.

      SUV Turns, Strikes Pedestrian With Head-On Impact
      ▸

      A Ford SUV turned right at 4th Avenue and 32nd Street. The driver’s view was blocked. The bumper hit a 61-year-old woman crossing with the light. She fell. Blood pooled. The street bore witness to another wound.

      A 61-year-old woman was struck by a Ford SUV while crossing 4th Avenue at 32nd Street in Brooklyn. According to the police report, the SUV turned right as the woman crossed with the signal. The vehicle’s bumper hit her head, causing severe bleeding. The report states, “The driver’s view was blocked. He saw too late.” The listed contributing factor is 'View Obstructed/Limited.' The driver’s action—turning with limited visibility—led to the crash. The pedestrian was injured at the intersection while following the signal. No other contributing factors were cited in the report.
