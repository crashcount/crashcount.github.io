      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 26?

      Third Avenue’s Killing Field: Two Dead, No More Excuses

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 20, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      Blood on Third Avenue

      Just last week, two men tried to cross Third Avenue at 52nd Street. A BMW ran the red light. The car did not brake. Both men, ages 59 and 80, died in the street. The driver left them behind and sped away. Police found him later. He told them, “I had a six pack of Modelos and two drinks. I was driving the car by myself last night. It’s my fault. After I hit them, I came home.”

      The men may have been on their way to a food pantry. City Harvest said, “We understand they may have been on their way to our Mobile Market simply trying to access food like so many New Yorkers in need.”

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the last twelve months, SD 26 saw 7 deaths and 1,711 injuries from 3,126 crashes. Fourteen people suffered serious injuries. Most of the dead were older. Most of the injured were young. Cars and SUVs did the most harm, but trucks, bikes, and mopeds all left bodies behind. The street is not safe for anyone.

      Leadership: Progress and the Long Road Ahead

      Senator Andrew Gounardes has pushed for change. He sponsored and voted for the law that keeps speed cameras running in school zones through 2030. He called it clear: “Speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone… At the end of the day, this is about keeping New Yorkers safe. I’m proud to have fought for this program from the beginning, and to renew it now for five years.”

      He also sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed-limiting devices. The bill passed committee but has not yet become law. The carnage continues.

      The Call

      Every day of delay means another family shattered. Call Senator Gounardes. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat offenders. Do not wait for another body in the crosswalk.

      Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 26.
      ▸ Where does SD 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38 and assembly district AD 51.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 26?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Park Slope, Sunset Park (West), Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, Dyker Beach Park, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 38, District 39, District 47, and District 50, and Assembly Districts AD 46, AD 51, AD 52, and AD 64.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 26?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm, with 5 deaths and 18 serious injuries to pedestrians. Trucks and buses caused 2 deaths and 2 serious injuries. Bikes caused no deaths but 3 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no deaths and no serious injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and strong enforcement. Calling them ‘accidents’ hides the fact that policy and choices shape every outcome.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, redesign streets for safety, and target repeat dangerous drivers. They can also listen to advocates and act without delay.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
