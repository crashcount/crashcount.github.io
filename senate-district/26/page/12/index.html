      Third Avenue: Promises Broken, Bodies Counted

      Written July 24, 2025

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 24, 2025

      Blood on Third Avenue

      Just two weeks ago, a BMW ran a red light at Third Avenue and 52nd Street. The car hit two men in the crosswalk. Kex Un Chen was 80. Faqiu Lin was 59. Both died where they fell. The driver fled. Police found him later. The charge: manslaughter. The street stayed the same. Police said the car “sped through a red light” and never braked. The men were trying to reach a food pantry. They never made it.

      Since 2018, drivers have killed or seriously injured 80 people on this two-mile stretch. The city promised a redesign. It never came. Assemblymember Marcela Mitaynes said, “We started talking about a plan in 2014 and it’s now 2025. What is going on? There’s been no conversation, no updates.”

      The Toll in Numbers

      In the last 12 months, 11 people died and 1,944 were injured in SD 26. Eighteen suffered serious injuries. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. Cars and SUVs did the most harm, but trucks, bikes, and mopeds all left bodies broken. The disaster is slow, but it never stops.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      State Senator Andrew Gounardes has fought for speed cameras and for laws to stop repeat speeders. He sponsored the bill to force the worst drivers to install speed limiters. He voted yes. He praised the renewal of the city’s speed camera program: “Speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone… At the end of the day, this is about keeping New Yorkers safe.”

      But the city’s promises to redesign deadly streets like Third Avenue have stalled. “We wait until someone dies. We wait until a tragedy. We let this happen time and time again,” said Gounardes at the scene. The dead keep coming. The waiting goes on.

      What Now?

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city finish the job. Tell Senator Gounardes to keep pushing for speed limiters and real street redesigns. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. It is time to choose life.

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 26 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does SD 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38 and assembly district AD 51.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 26?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Park Slope, Sunset Park (West), Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, Dyker Beach Park, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 38, District 39, District 47, and District 50, and Assembly Districts AD 46, AD 51, AD 52, and AD 64.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 26?
      Most harm came from Cars and SUVs (7 deaths, 19 serious injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (3 deaths, 2 serious injuries), Bikes (0 deaths, 3 serious injuries), and Motorcycles and Mopeds (0 deaths, 0 serious injuries). Data source
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Delayed redesigns and weak enforcement let the same streets kill again and again.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, install speed cameras, and redesign streets to protect people walking and cycling.
