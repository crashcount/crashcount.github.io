      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 26?

      Blood on Gounardes’ Watch: Seven Dead, Hundreds Broken, Still No Slowdown

      Written June 23, 2025 (Revised June 22, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned and deduped citations, and ensured correct inline citation formatting.)

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 23, 2025

      The Toll: Death on Familiar Streets

      A man steps out before dawn. A moped hums down Third Avenue. A sedan waits at a light. In Senate District 26, these moments end in blood and sirens. In the last year, seven people died and 1,624 were injured on these streets. Thirteen suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same.

      Joel Mota, 22, died after his moped struck a car door in Bay Ridge. His brother said, “He was a hardworking man. He had a lot of love for his family. He was always passionate about the things that he did.” NY Daily News. The driver was drunk and unlicensed. The passenger on the moped left the hospital with broken bones. Mota lived less than half a mile from where he died. Gothamist.

      The dead are old and young. In the last twelve months, three people over 65 died. One was under 25. The rest were in the middle, caught in the crosswalk or behind the wheel. The numbers do not care about age.

      Who Pays the Price

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm. In three years, they killed five, seriously injured 17, and left 614 others hurt. Trucks and buses killed two. Motorcycles and mopeds, none. Bikes, none. The street is not fair. The bigger machine wins.

      What Gounardes Has Done—and Not Done

      Senator Andrew Gounardes has pushed for speed cameras and supported bills to make streets safer. He called the speed camera program “the most important street safety law on the books” and said, “speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone.” Streetsblog NYC. He voted yes on a bill to redesign streets for all users, not just drivers. Open States.

      But the fight is slow. The “Super Speeder” bill was gutted. The threshold for action was raised, letting thousands of reckless drivers off the hook. The city can lower speed limits, but has not done so everywhere. Each delay means another family waits for a call that never should come.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call Senator Gounardes. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand more protected crossings. Demand the city use every tool it has.

      The dead cannot speak. The living must. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect all residents.
      ▸ Where does SD 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38 and assembly district AD 51. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 26?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Sunset Park (West), Bay Ridge, Dyker Beach Park, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, Brooklyn CB10, and Brooklyn CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 38, District 39, District 47, and District 50, and Assembly Districts AD 46, AD 51, AD 52, and AD 64. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 26?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 5 deaths and 17 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no deaths or serious injuries to pedestrians. Bikes caused no deaths and 3 serious injuries. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are not random accidents. Data shows that lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign streets for safety, and close loopholes that let reckless drivers avoid consequences.

      SUV Turns, Strikes Pedestrian With Head-On Impact
      ▸

      A Ford SUV turned right at 4th Avenue and 32nd Street. The driver’s view was blocked. The bumper hit a 61-year-old woman crossing with the light. She fell. Blood pooled. The street bore witness to another wound.

      A 61-year-old woman was struck by a Ford SUV while crossing 4th Avenue at 32nd Street in Brooklyn. According to the police report, the SUV turned right as the woman crossed with the signal. The vehicle’s bumper hit her head, causing severe bleeding. The report states, “The driver’s view was blocked. He saw too late.” The listed contributing factor is 'View Obstructed/Limited.' The driver’s action—turning with limited visibility—led to the crash. The pedestrian was injured at the intersection while following the signal. No other contributing factors were cited in the report.
