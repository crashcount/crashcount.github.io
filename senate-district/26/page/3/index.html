      Third Avenue: Another Night, Another Body

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, and deduped citations.)

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      Blood on Third Avenue

      On July 11, two men tried to cross 3rd Avenue at 52nd Street. A BMW ran the red light. It did not slow. It struck Kex Un Chen, 80, and Faqiu Lin, 59. Both died in the street. The driver fled. Police found him later. He told them, “I had a six pack of Modelos and two drinks. I was driving the car by myself last night. It’s my fault. After I hit them, I came home.” told police

      The men were near a food pantry. They were not alone. “We understand they may have been on their way to our Mobile Market simply trying to access food like so many New Yorkers in need,” said City Harvest.

      This is not rare. In the last year, SD 26 saw 3 deaths and 9 serious injuries from crashes. 996 people were hurt. NYC Open Data

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Since 2022, 31 people have died on these streets. 5,488 have been hurt. Seventy-five suffered serious injuries. Cars and SUVs did most of the killing—5 deaths, 18 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 2, left 2 more with life-changing wounds. Bikes, mopeds, motorcycles: 3 serious injuries, no deaths. NYC Open Data

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Senator Andrew Gounardes has pushed for speed cameras and for laws to stop repeat speeders. “The verdict came in a long time ago: speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone… At the end of the day, this is about keeping New Yorkers safe. I’m proud to have fought for this program from the beginning, and to renew it now for five years.” said Gounardes

      He sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force the worst drivers to install speed limiters. He voted yes. But the carnage continues. Cameras catch speeders, but they do not stop the car. Laws pass, but people still die in the crosswalk.

      Call to Action: No More Waiting

Join Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 26 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does SD 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38 and assembly district AD 51.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 26?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Park Slope, Sunset Park (West), Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, Dyker Beach Park, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 38, District 39, District 47, and District 50, and Assembly Districts AD 46, AD 51, AD 52, and AD 64.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 26?
      Cars and SUVs were responsible for 5 deaths and 18 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 2 and caused 2 serious injuries. Bikes, mopeds, and motorcycles caused 3 serious injuries, no deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speeds, and real consequences for reckless driving. They are not just accidents—they are the result of choices and policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws that force repeat dangerous drivers off the road. They can also fund safer street redesigns and hold agencies accountable.

      Gounardes Urges Immediate Safety Boosting Congestion Pricing Implementation
      ▸

      Governor Hochul slashes NYC’s congestion toll to $9. The move aims to beat a federal block but guts traffic reduction. Streets will see less relief. The plan leaves vulnerable road users exposed. The city trades speed and safety for political timing.

      ""It s more important than ever that we start congestion pricing immediately, before Trump can block it," said state Senator Andrew Gounardes (D-Brooklyn). "The time to commit to better public transit, less traffic and cleaner air is now."" -- Andrew Gounardes

      On November 8, 2024, Governor Hochul proposed lowering New York City’s congestion pricing toll from $15 to $9. The plan, a policy proposal to adjust congestion pricing, comes as officials rush to implement it before a new presidential administration can intervene. The original $15 toll, crafted by the Traffic Mobility Review Board and approved by the MTA Board, promised strong traffic reduction and included credits and caps. The $9 version, previously reviewed in environmental assessments, may lack those protections. Economist Charles Komanoff warns, 'You lose other benefits. Most noticeably, you don’t get the immediate traffic speed gain that a $15 toll would give.' State Senator Andrew Gounardes urges swift action, saying, 'The time to commit to better public transit, less traffic and cleaner air is now.' The lower toll is projected to improve traffic speeds by only 6.4 percent, far less than the 17 percent expected from the original plan. With less traffic reduction, streets remain dangerous for pedestrians and cyclists.
