      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 26?

      Two Dead, City Stalls—Third Avenue Bleeds Again

      Written July 12, 2025

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      Blood on Third Avenue

      Two men are dead. On July 11, a BMW tore through the crosswalk at Third Avenue and 52nd Street in Sunset Park. Kex Un Chen, 80, and Faqiu Lin, 59, were walking to a food pantry. The car did not brake. The bodies landed hard. The driver fled. Police found him later and charged him with manslaughter and leaving the scene. Councilmember Alexa Avilés said she was “saddened and angered at the tragic and senseless killing of two individuals earlier this morning. Instances like this remind us of why Third Avenue needs serious investments in safety enhancements from both the state and the city.”

      This was not a surprise. Since City Hall paused a safety redesign for Third Avenue, 96 crashes have injured 80 people. The city knew this stretch was deadly. The community knew. Julio Peña said, “It was only a matter of time before somebody else died on this corridor and here we are. Every death is preventable, and once again we are in a place of frustration where we have this incredibly dangerous corridor and the city just refusin…”

      The Slow Disaster

      In the last year, SD 26 saw 3 killed, 9 seriously hurt, and 996 injured in 1,844 crashes. Most victims were walking or cycling. Cars and SUVs did the most harm, but trucks, bikes, and mopeds all left bodies behind. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors. They are gone.

      What Has Senator Gounardes Done?

      Senator Andrew Gounardes has pushed for safety. He sponsored the bill to expand speed camera enforcement and close loopholes for drivers who hide their plates. He said, “The verdict came in a long time ago: speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone… At the end of the day, this is about keeping New Yorkers safe. I’m proud to have fought for this program from the beginning, and to renew it now for five years.” He voted yes on complete street design. He has backed bills to cut driving and force repeat speeders to slow down. But the carnage continues. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so.

      What Next? Demand More

      Every crash is preventable. Call Senator Gounardes. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real redesigns for deadly corridors. Demand action before another neighbor dies.

      State Sen. Andrew Gounardes introduced a bill requiring New York to reduce vehicle miles traveled by 20 percent by 2050. The bill, modeled after Minnesota law, is not yet passed or assigned to a committee. It would amend the state’s climate law, forcing transportation officials to align highway projects with the goal of fewer car trips. Gounardes warns, 'If we’re just going to use that money to double down on the transportation of the past, on just purely automobile usage, we’re making a great mistake.'
