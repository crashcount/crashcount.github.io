      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 26?

      Seven Dead, Silence Wins: Hold Gounardes to the Fire

      Written June 27, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Death Toll Grows

      In Senate District 26, the numbers do not lie. Seven people killed. Fourteen left with serious injuries. Over 1,600 hurt in the last year alone. The dead include the young and the old. A 22-year-old moped rider, Joel Mota, died at Third Avenue and 67th Street. His brother said, “He was a hardworking man. He had a lot of love for his family” (NY Daily News).

      The violence does not stop. In the same crash, a passenger suffered broken bones. The driver who killed Joel was drunk and unlicensed. She was arrested and released without bail. The street remains the same.

      The Usual Weapons: Cars, Trucks, and Silence

      Cars and SUVs killed five. Trucks and buses killed two. Bikes and mopeds left dozens more injured. The streets are not safe for anyone on foot or on two wheels. The pain is not spread evenly. Children, elders, and workers pay the price.

      What Has Senator Gounardes Done?

      Senator Andrew Gounardes has pushed for speed cameras and safer street design. He called speed cameras “lifesaving” and fought for their renewal: “speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone” (Streetsblog NYC). He voted yes on a bill to redesign streets for all users, not just drivers. But the biggest bills—those that would force repeat speeders to slow down—died in Albany. The senator promised to keep fighting. The streets wait.

      The Road Ahead: No More Waiting

      Every delay means another family broken. The city has the power to lower speed limits now. The state can pass laws to stop repeat killers behind the wheel. But action is slow. The dead do not wait.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call Senator Gounardes. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real penalties for repeat offenders. Do not let another name become a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees state policy for all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38 and assembly district AD 51.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 26?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Park Slope, Sunset Park (West), Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, Dyker Beach Park, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 38, District 39, District 47, and District 50, and Assembly Districts AD 46, AD 51, AD 52, and AD 64.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 26?
      Cars and trucks caused 5 deaths and 538 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 35 injuries. Bikes caused 68 injuries. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and real enforcement save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws to stop repeat dangerous drivers.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      State Senator Andrew Gounardes introduced a bill in 2022 to reward citizens who report illegal license plates. The bill remains in committee. The measure would give whistleblowers 25% of the ticket, up to $75. The matter targets drivers who obscure or remove plates to dodge automated enforcement. As city and state agencies rely more on cameras to catch speeders and red-light runners, more drivers hide their plates.
