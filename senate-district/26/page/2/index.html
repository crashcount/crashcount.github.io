      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 26?

      Two Dead, No Justice: Third Avenue Bleeds While Lawmakers Stall

      Written July 13, 2025

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Toll on Third Avenue

      Two men lay dead in the crosswalk. Friday morning, July 11th, a BMW ran the red at 3rd Avenue and 52nd Street. Kex Un Chen, 80, and Faqiu Lin, 59, were crossing. The car did not slow. The driver fled. Both men died where they fell. Police found the car later. The man behind the wheel, 23, told them, “I had a six pack of Modelos and two drinks. I was driving the car by myself last night. It’s my fault. After I hit them, I came home.” the NY Daily News reported.

      This is not rare. In the last 12 months, SD 26 saw 7 deaths and 1,721 injuries in 3,099 crashes. Fourteen people were seriously hurt. Most never make the news. Most are not counted by name. NYC Open Data tracks every crash.

      Who Pays the Price

      The old. The young. The ones on foot. In the last year, three people over 65 died. Children were hurt 131 times. Cars and SUVs did most of the killing—five deaths, 440 minor injuries, 193 moderate, 18 serious. Trucks and buses killed two more. Bikes, mopeds, motorcycles: none killed, but dozens injured. The street does not care who you are.

      The site of Friday’s crash is no stranger to blood. In five years, a three-block stretch around 52nd and 3rd saw 59 crashes and 80 injuries, according to amNY.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Senator Andrew Gounardes has fought for speed cameras and tougher laws. He called the verdict clear: “Speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone… At the end of the day, this is about keeping New Yorkers safe. I’m proud to have fought for this program from the beginning, and to renew it now for five years,” he told Streetsblog NYC.

      He voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force the worst repeat offenders to install speed limiters, according to Open States.

      But the deaths keep coming. Cameras catch speeders, but the city’s default speed limit is still above 20 mph. The worst drivers keep their keys. The street is still a gauntlet for the old, the young, the poor, the hungry.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Senator Gounardes. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph default speed limit. Demand real consequences for repeat offenders. Demand streets where crossing for food is not a death sentence.

      Do not wait for another body in the crosswalk.
