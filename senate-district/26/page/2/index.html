      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 26?

      Third Avenue’s toll: two men dead, years of delay

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Third Avenue, 52nd Street. Two men down. A BMW ran the light and kept going. Police named the victims: Kex Un Chen, 80, and Faqiu Lin, 59. Both died in the crosswalk before sunrise, July 11. The driver was arrested hours later and charged with manslaughter and more, police and reporters said (Gothamist, amNY, ABC7).

      ABC7 reported the men “may have been on their way” to a City Harvest food market. City Harvest said it was “deeply saddened.” “Simply trying to access food,” the group said (ABC7).

      Police data list both victims as pedestrians crossing with the signal. The car “disregarded” the light at unsafe speed, investigators wrote (NYPD crash data).

      A corridor that keeps taking

      Since 2018, drivers on this two‑mile stretch have killed or seriously hurt at least 80 people, reporters found. Children cross up to nine lanes here to reach school. A plan to add protection was approved, then shelved (Gothamist).

      The city put up a warning sign. The bodies were still warm. “We should all be offended. Angry. Insulted,” State Sen. Andrew Gounardes said at the site. “This is not meant to be a highway. This is where people walk. This is where people live. This is where kids go to school” (Gothamist, Brooklyn Paper).

      Assemblymember Marcela Mitaynes said the fixes have been “paused” since last fall. “We started talking about a plan in 2014 and it’s now 2025. What is going on?” (CBS New York).

      City and state knew the danger

      The driver ran the red and fled, police said. Hours later, detectives found him using video and debris. Charges include manslaughter, criminally negligent homicide, reckless driving, and leaving the scene (amNY, Gothamist).

      Residents said cars “speed off the ramp” like it’s a race. Poor lighting. Long crossings. People keep getting hit (CBS New York).

      Officials have stood here before. “We wait until someone dies,” Gounardes said. “We let this happen time and time again” (CBS New York).

      The larger pattern

      In SD 26, deaths keep coming. Eleven killed in the last 12 months. More than two thousand injured. Crashes keep rising compared to last year-to-date (NYC Open Data).

      The state renewed New York City’s school‑zone speed cameras through 2030. “Speed cameras save lives,” the governor said. The renewal was sponsored in part by Gounardes (Streetsblog NYC, AMNY).

      Lawmakers also pushed the Stop Super Speeders Act to force repeat violators to use speed‑limiting tech. It moved in committee this session (NYS Senate bill S4045).

      What will end this

      Lower speed everywhere. Cameras that don’t blink. Cars that can’t break the limit. The tools exist: a renewed camera program and a bill to choke off the worst repeat speeders. Use them. Make Third Avenue a street, not a chute.

      Take one step now: push City Hall and Albany to act. Start here: take action.

      ▸

      Albany keeps speed cameras rolling. Lawmakers extend the city’s program to 2030. Cameras catch speeders near schools. Streets get safer for walkers and riders. Fewer crashes, fewer deaths. A hard-won victory for the vulnerable. The fight against traffic violence continues.

      ""The verdict came in a long time ago: speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone... At the end of the day, this is about keeping New Yorkers safe. I'm proud to have fought for this program from the beginning, and to renew it now for five years."" -- Andrew Gounardes

      On June 18, 2025, the New York State Legislature reauthorized the city’s speed camera program for five more years, extending it until July 1, 2030. The bill passed both the Senate and Assembly and awaits Governor Hochul’s signature. The measure, described as 'possibly the most important street safety law on the books,' covers 750 school zones. Sen. Andrew Gounardes praised the program, saying, 'speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone.' DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez credited advocates and lawmakers for the renewal. Amy Sohn supported the move. Safety analysts note that speed cameras reduce vehicle speeds and crashes, improving safety for pedestrians and cyclists without burdening them. The reauthorization marks a rare, clear win for vulnerable road users in the city’s ongoing battle against traffic violence.

      S 4045
      Gounardes votes yes in committee, boosting street safety by curbing repeat speeders.
      ▸

      Senate backs S 4045. Repeat speeders face forced installation of speed assistance tech. Eleven points or six camera tickets triggers action. Law targets reckless drivers. Streets may get safer for those outside the car.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on June 11 and June 12, 2025. The bill, titled 'Relates to requiring the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices for repeated violation of maximum speed limits,' mandates these devices for drivers who rack up eleven or more points in 24 months, or six speed or red light camera tickets in a year. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving. Senators including Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, and others voted yes. The bill targets drivers with a pattern of violations, seeking to cut risk for pedestrians and cyclists by limiting repeat speeding.

Assembly Member Michaell Novakhov (R-Midwood) argued the threshold was too low. The amendment means only 17,000 drivers, not 150,000, will face restrictions. According to safety analysts, this move undermines deterrence, increases risk to pedestrians and cyclists, and signals that dangerous driving is tolerated. Vulnerable road users remain at risk. The bill’s status now sets a weaker standard for accountability.
