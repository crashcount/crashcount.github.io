      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 26?

      Bay Ridge Bleeds: How Many More Must Die Before We Act?

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, tightened prose, and cleaned citations.)

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 26, the numbers do not lie. Thirty-one people killed. Seventy-four seriously injured. Over 5,300 hurt. These are not just numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. They are the man who never made it home, the woman who stepped out of a taxi and never saw the next sunrise.

      Just weeks ago, on June 21, Joel Mota, 22, died on Third Avenue in Bay Ridge. His brother remembered him as “a hardworking man. He had a lot of love for his family. He was always passionate about the things that he did” NY Daily News. Mota’s passenger left with broken bones. The driver who struck them was drunk and unlicensed. She was arrested, then released. The street remains the same.

      In February, a woman was killed exiting a taxi on Flatbush Avenue. Both drivers stayed. The city moved on. “First responders transported the victim to New York-Presbyterian Brooklyn Methodist Hospital, where she was pronounced dead” ABC7.

      Who Pays the Price

      The most vulnerable pay most. In the last year, children under 18 suffered 123 injuries. People over 65, 106 injuries and 3 deaths. Cars and SUVs did most of the harm—five deaths, 429 minor injuries, 190 moderate, 18 serious. Trucks and buses killed two. Bikes and mopeds left dozens more hurt. The street is a gauntlet.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Senator Andrew Gounardes has pushed for speed cameras and tougher enforcement. He sponsored a bill to let the city use cameras to catch drivers who hide their plates and speed near schools sponsored a bill. He voted yes on a bill to redesign streets for all users voted yes. He backed the renewal of the city’s speed camera program, which has cut speeds and injuries where installed backed the renewal. But the pace is slow. The deaths keep coming.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Senator Gounardes. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns, not just promises. Every day of delay is another family broken.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 26.
      ▸ Where does SD 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38 and assembly district AD 51.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 26?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Park Slope, Sunset Park (West), Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, Dyker Beach Park, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 38, District 39, District 47, and District 50, and Assembly Districts AD 46, AD 51, AD 52, and AD 64.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 26?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 5 deaths and 637 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 35 crashes. Bikes were involved in 68 crashes, leaving dozens injured. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and strong enforcement. They are not random—they follow patterns that policy can change.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, expand automated enforcement, and close loopholes that let reckless drivers avoid consequences.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
