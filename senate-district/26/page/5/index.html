      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 26?

      Seven Dead, 1,600 Hurt—Still No Safe Streets

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised July 1, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Senate District 26, the bodies do not stop. In the last twelve months, seven people died in crashes. Six were men and women over 45. One was just 18. Over 1,600 were injured. Fourteen suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again. These are not numbers. They are fathers, daughters, neighbors. They are the man who never made it home from work. The woman who never finished her walk.

      On June 21, a moped rider, Joel Mota, was killed at Third Avenue and 67th Street. Police said the driver who hit him was drunk and unlicensed. Mota’s brother remembered him simply: “He was a hardworking man. He had a lot of love for his family.” NY Daily News.

      The Usual Weapons: Cars, Trucks, and Speed

      Cars and SUVs killed five. Trucks killed two. Bikes and mopeds left dozens more hurt. The numbers are blunt: Cars and trucks caused 641 pedestrian injuries and 5 deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds, 35 injuries. Bikes, 68 injuries. NYC Open Data.

      The violence is not random. It is routine. It happens on wide avenues, at corners with broken sightlines, in the dark before dawn. The drivers who kill are often unlicensed, drunk, or speeding. The victims are almost always the ones on foot, on bikes, or riding home late.

      What Has Been Done—and What Hasn’t

      Senator Andrew Gounardes has pushed for more speed cameras and tougher laws. He sponsored a bill to let the city use cameras to catch drivers who hide or deface their plates, and to extend school-zone speed enforcement Open States. He voted yes on redesigning streets for safety Open States. He called speed cameras “possibly the most important street safety law on the books” Streetsblog NYC.

      But the deaths keep coming. Cameras catch speeders, but the city still drags its feet on redesigning deadly streets. Laws pass, but loopholes remain. “Our elected officials in Albany have failed the livable streets movement again,” wrote one advocate after another session ended with little change Streetsblog NYC.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Senator Gounardes. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns, not just more cameras. Every day of delay is another family broken.

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York. Open States
      ▸ Where does SD 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38 and assembly district AD 51. Open States

      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Park Slope, Sunset Park (West), Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, Dyker Beach Park, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 38, District 39, District 47, and District 50, and Assembly Districts AD 46, AD 51, AD 52, and AD 64. Open States
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 26?
      Cars and trucks caused 641 injuries and 5 deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 35 injuries. Bikes caused 68 injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speed limits, and strong enforcement. They are not random—they are the result of choices and policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass laws for lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand automated enforcement, and close loopholes that let dangerous drivers avoid consequences.
