      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 26?

      Blood on the Crosswalks, Blame in Albany

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, ensured all quotes have inline links, and cleaned citations.)

      SD 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      In Senate District 26, violence doesn’t always come with sirens or headlines. It comes in the dark and in daylight, at corners and crosswalks, on bikes and on foot. Since January 2022, 31 people have died and 5,484 have been injured in crashes here. Seventy-five suffered serious injuries. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians—neighbors who never made it home.

      Just weeks ago, on June 21, a 22-year-old moped rider was killed at 67th Street and 3rd Avenue. Police said the driver who hit him was drunk and unlicensed. His passenger left with broken bones. The driver was arrested at the scene. His brother described him as “a hardworking man” who “had a lot of love for his family.” The loss is sharp and final.

      In February, a woman stepped out of a taxi on Flatbush Avenue and was struck and killed by a passing car. Both drivers stayed. No arrests. The street swallowed another life. First responders transported the victim to New York-Presbyterian Brooklyn Methodist Hospital, where she was pronounced dead.

      Who Pays the Price

      Most victims are not behind the wheel. In these years, cars and trucks caused 656 injuries or deaths to pedestrians. Motorcycles and mopeds added 35 more. Bikes, 68. These numbers are bodies. The bodies are neighbors.

      What Has Been Done—And What Hasn’t

      State Senator Andrew Gounardes has pushed for change. He sponsored the bill that renewed New York City’s speed camera program through 2030. Gounardes said, “Speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone… At the end of the day, this is about keeping New Yorkers safe. I’m proud to have fought for this program from the beginning, and to renew it now for five years.”

      But cameras alone are not enough. Unlicensed, reckless drivers keep killing. The law still lets repeat offenders back behind the wheel. The city’s default speed limit is still above what’s proven to save lives. Each delay is another risk. Each loophole is another funeral.

      What You Can Do Now

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call Senator Gounardes. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand real penalties for repeat speeders and unlicensed drivers. Join groups like Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another family to lose someone.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees state policy for all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38 and assembly district AD 51.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 26?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Park Slope, Sunset Park (West), Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, Dyker Beach Park, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 38, District 39, District 47, and District 50, and Assembly Districts AD 46, AD 51, AD 52, and AD 64.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 26?
      Cars and trucks caused 656 injuries or deaths, motorcycles and mopeds caused 35, and bikes caused 68.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speed limits, and stronger enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, close loopholes for repeat offenders, fund safer street designs, and push for stronger enforcement against reckless drivers.
