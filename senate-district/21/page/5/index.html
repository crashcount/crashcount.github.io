      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 21?

      Brooklyn Bleeds: How Many More Must Die Before We Act?

      Written July 18, 2025

      SD 21: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Senate District 21, the street is a gauntlet. Since 2022, 18 people have died. 71 have been seriously injured. 5 deaths already this year. These are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. A 16-year-old on a bike, crushed by a truck. A 74-year-old woman, killed crossing with the signal. A 90-year-old man, struck by a moped and left to die. The road does not forgive.

      Last week, two men were killed crossing a Brooklyn street. The car did not stop. Neighbors said drivers speed on that stretch of roadway. The bodies were still warm when the next crash came. Fourteen people were hurt when a van slammed into a tree. The city moves on. The blood dries. The danger stays.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay the highest price. In this district, cars and trucks are the main killers—8 deaths and 17 serious injuries from cars and SUVs, 4 deaths and 4 serious injuries from trucks and buses. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll. The young are not spared. Two children have died since 2022. The old are not spared. The city counts the bodies. The street stays the same.

      What Has Senator Parker Done?

      State Senator Kevin Parker has voted for safety bills. He voted yes to require speed-limiting devices for repeat speeders, a move to curb repeat dangerous driving. He voted to extend school speed zones. These are steps. But the crisis is not over. The crashes keep coming. The dead keep coming. “Two people died when a car drove through a Brooklyn intersection, hitting and killing them as they crossed the street,” reported CBS New York.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Senator Parker. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking. The street will not change itself. Act now. Or count the bodies.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 21. For example, Senator Parker represents SD 21 and votes on street safety bills. Open States
      ▸ Where does SD 21 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 45 and assembly district AD 41. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 21?
      It includes the Kensington, Flatbush, Flatbush (West)-Ditmas Park-Parkville, East Flatbush-Erasmus, East Flatbush-Farragut, East Flatbush-Rugby, Holy Cross Cemetery, Flatlands, Marine Park-Mill Basin-Bergen Beach, and Brooklyn CB17 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 40, District 45, and District 46, and Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 42, AD 44, AD 58, and AD 59. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 21?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 8 deaths and 17 serious injuries from cars/SUVs, 4 deaths and 4 serious injuries from trucks/buses. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no deaths but 18 injuries; bikes were involved in 10 injuries but no deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Lower speeds, better street design, and stronger enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand protected bike lanes, and support laws that keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road.

      On June 7, 2022, Councilman Lincoln Restler (District 33) announced legislation to revoke tens of thousands of personal vehicle parking placards, with exemptions for people with disabilities and those covered by collective bargaining. The editorial, titled 'Free perking: Pull back run amok NYC parking placards,' calls out rampant placard abuse: 'End this scam.'
