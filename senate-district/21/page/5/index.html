      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 21?

      No More Names on the Asphalt: Demand Action Now

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 21: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A 90-year-old man stepped onto a Brooklyn street. A moped hit him and kept going. He died at the hospital. The driver did not stop. The street did not forgive. CBS New York reported the facts: “New security video shows the moment the man was struck.” There is no comfort in the footage. Only loss.

      Just weeks before, a van slammed into a tree. Fourteen people hurt. No warning, no time to react. CBS New York again: “Fourteen people were hurt after a van slammed into a tree in Brooklyn on Tuesday morning.” The numbers pile up. In the last twelve months, SD 21 saw 6 deaths and 1,456 injuries in crashes. Twenty people were seriously injured.

      Who Pays the Price

      The dead are not numbers. They are children, elders, mothers. In the past year, a child died. So did a man over 75. The pain is not spread evenly. Cars and SUVs killed 8. Trucks and buses killed 4. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes also caused injuries, but at much lower rates. NYC Open Data

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      State Senator Kevin Parker has taken some steps. He voted yes on a bill to force safer street design for all users, not just drivers. The bill passed. It calls for “complete street design principles” to protect people on foot and bike. Senate Bill S 9718 is a start. Parker also co-sponsored a bill to require safer design on projects using public money. But the blood on the street says more is needed. The city and state have the power to lower speed limits, redesign crossings, and end the reign of repeat dangerous drivers. The question is not what can be done. It is why it is not done faster.

      The Call

      Every day of delay is another day of loss. Call Senator Parker. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesign. Demand an end to repeat offenders. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.
