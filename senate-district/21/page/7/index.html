      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Flatbush at midnight. A body on the pavement. The car kept going.

      SD 21: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      Just after midnight on Sep 12, a 43‑year‑old woman walking on Flatbush Avenue was hit by a driver in a 2012 Toyota sedan. Police recorded failure to yield and improper passing; she was semiconscious and bleeding badly (city data).

      This Week

      • Sep 12: A driver passed and hit a woman walking mid‑block on Flatbush Avenue; police recorded failure to yield. She suffered severe bleeding (city data).
      • Sep 8: At Flatbush Ave and E 35 St, a 25‑year‑old driver was ejected and died; speed was recorded and the Honda sedan was demolished (city data).
      • Sep 6: At Bedford Ave and Farragut Rd, a driver making a left turn hit a 23‑year‑old man walking; he was incoherent with severe lacerations (city data).
      • Aug 18: At Flatlands Ave, a driver turning left failed to yield and hit a 28‑year‑old woman walking in the intersection (city data).

      The count does not stop

      Since Jan 1, 2022, in Senate District 21: 25 people killed and 7,333 injured in 10,932 crashes. Police recorded 102 serious injuries in that span (NYC Open Data).

      In the past 12 months: 2,542 crashes, 1,890 injured, 34 seriously hurt, 8 killed in this district (NYC Open Data).

      This year: 1,795 crashes, 7 killed, 1,339 injured, 27 serious injuries. Last year by this point: 2,091 crashes, 4 killed, 1,466 injured, 19 serious injuries. Deaths are up 75%, even as crashes fell (district stats derived from NYC Open Data).

      Streets built for speed give you this

      A small group does outsized harm. Vehicles that rack up repeated camera tickets are far more likely to kill or maim, as documented by advocates and recent cases citywide (our analysis and sources). The state has a bill to force change. The Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045) would require intelligent speed‑assistance devices for repeat violators (bill file).

      In June, State Senator Kevin Parker voted yes twice in committee on S4045 (June 11 vote, June 12 vote). Assembly Member Kalman Yeger is the local vote in Albany. Will he back the companion bill? Council Member Farah Louis holds power over local speed policy.

      Police chases add another layer of risk. “The evidence is clear: police vehicle pursuits and high‑speed car chases can be dangerous and even fatal, and it is time for a change,” said Attorney General Letitia James, calling to limit pursuits to protect “everyone on the road” (Times Union).

      Slow the cars. Stop the worst offenders.

      • Lower speeds save lives. Advocates are pushing the city to set a lower default limit on neighborhood streets. The first step is a clear, citywide move to 20 MPH on local roads (details and actions).
      • Habitual speeders need hard stops. Mandate speed‑limiters for repeat violators by passing S4045 in full and its Assembly companion (bill file).

      The woman on Flatbush is still a number in a file. The file does not heal. The street can. Take one step today: add your voice at Take Action.
