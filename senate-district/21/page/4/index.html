      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 21?

      Blood on Flatbush: The Speed Limit That Never Came

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, clarified FAQ citation, deduped and reordered citations.)

      SD 21: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      Just last week, two men died crossing a Brooklyn street. A car tore through the intersection. The driver kept going. Neighbors were not surprised. “Drivers speed on that stretch of roadway,” one said. The bodies were left behind. The street stayed the same.

      The week before, a 90-year-old man was killed by a moped. The driver fled. The old man died in the hospital. No new laws, no new barriers, just another name for the list. CBS New York reported the moment he was struck. The city moved on.

      In the last year, SD 21 saw 6 deaths and 1,566 injuries in 2,161 crashes. Twenty-two people were seriously hurt. Children, elders, cyclists, pedestrians. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      Who Pays the Price

      The killers are cars, trucks, mopeds, vans. SUVs and sedans caused most of the pain—over 895 injuries and 8 deaths since 2022. Trucks and buses killed 4. Motorcycles and mopeds injured 18. Bikes, too, left their mark. The street does not care who you are. It only matters who is heavier, who is faster, who is left standing.

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      State Senator Kevin Parker voted yes on bills to curb repeat speeders and extend school speed zones. He backed the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force the worst offenders to slow down. He voted to create more safety zones. These are steps, not leaps. The blood on the street says more is needed.

      But the city still waits for a 20 mph speed limit. The cameras catch some, but not all. The law cracks down on the reckless, but the dead stay dead. The survivors limp home.

      Call to Action: Make Them Hear You

      Call Senator Parker. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement. Demand streets where children can cross and elders can walk.

      Every day you wait, another family waits for a call that will break them. The time for patience is over.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect every resident.
      ▸ Where does SD 21 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 45 and assembly district AD 41.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 21?
      It includes the Kensington, Flatbush, Flatbush (West)-Ditmas Park-Parkville, East Flatbush-Erasmus, East Flatbush-Farragut, East Flatbush-Rugby, Holy Cross Cemetery, Flatlands, Marine Park-Mill Basin-Bergen Beach, and Brooklyn CB17 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 40, District 45, and District 46, and Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 42, AD 44, AD 58, and AD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 21?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm to pedestrians in SD 21, with 8 deaths and 895 injuries since 2022. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 18 injuries. Bikes were involved in 10 injuries and no deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and stronger enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass laws to lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, redesign dangerous streets, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
