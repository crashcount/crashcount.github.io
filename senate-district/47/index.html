      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 47?

      Twenty Dead, No Answers: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written July 6, 2025

      SD 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 47, the street does not forgive. Twenty people are dead since 2022. Sixty people have been left with serious injuries. In the last twelve months alone, five more lives ended in the road, with seventeen others marked by wounds that will not heal. These are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. A 69-year-old woman, crossing with the light at Amsterdam and 96th, killed by a turning SUV. A 74-year-old man, helmet on, struck dead while riding his bike near West End Avenue and 70th. A 39-year-old man crushed by a truck on 9th Avenue. The city moves on. Their families do not. NYC Open Data

      Recent Crashes, Unanswered Pain

      The violence does not slow. On July 2, three NJ Transit buses collided at Port Authority. Fourteen people were hurt. One driver was trapped and had to be cut free. The crash shut the terminal for hours. “One bus made contact with the rear of the other bus,” said Deputy Chief Jason Saffon of FDNY EMS. “It required some extrication, meaning they had to use some equipment to free the patient.”

      In Central Park, a man on an electric unicycle was left in critical condition after a hit-and-run with a cyclist. The cyclist fled. Police found her later. The victim was rushed to the hospital. The story ends with a charge: “She was arrested Wednesday morning and charged with leaving the scene of an accident that caused a serious injury,” police said.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Senator Brad Hoylman-Sigal has voted for safer streets. He backed the law to let New York City lower its speed limits. He supported the expansion of 24/7 speed cameras. He co-sponsored the new moped registration law, shifting accountability to retailers and away from delivery workers. He voted yes on the complete streets bill, demanding safer design for all. But the blood on the street says it is not enough. The city can set a 20 mph limit now. It has not. The dead keep coming.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another family broken. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call Senator Hoylman-Sigal. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand the Stop Super Speeders Act. Demand streets that do not kill. The city will not change unless you force it.

      The street will not wait. Why should you?

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the State Legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York.
      ▸ Where does SD 47 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 6 and assembly district AD 67.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 47?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Upper West Side-Lincoln Square, Upper West Side (Central), Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Central Park, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB7, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 6, and District 7, and Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, AD 69, and AD 75.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 47?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 6 deaths and 14 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 3 serious injuries but no deaths. Bikes caused 4 serious injuries and no deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. Calling them ‘accidents’ hides the fact that policy choices shape who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, redesign dangerous streets, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable. They can also push for laws that protect people walking and biking instead of blaming them.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
