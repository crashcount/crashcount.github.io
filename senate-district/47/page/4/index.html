      No More Bodies in the Crosswalk: Make 20 MPH the Law Now

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed citation formatting, deduped citations.)

      SD 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Senate District 47, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-one people killed. Sixty-three left with serious injuries. Nearly 6,500 crashes since 2022. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. A 69-year-old woman, crossing with the light at Amsterdam and 96th, was struck and killed by a turning SUV. She had the signal. She did not make it home. NYC crash data

      Just last month, a food cart broke loose from a van on 42nd Street. It slammed into a parked car with a woman and a ten-year-old boy inside. Firefighters found the van packed with 76 propane tanks and 75 gallons of gasoline. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment. The woman and child survived, but the street was one spark away from disaster. Firefighters removed 76 propane cylinders and 15 fuel containers.

      On July 2nd, two buses collided inside the Port Authority Terminal. Fourteen people were hurt. Emergency crews had to drive the wrong way up a ramp to reach the injured. “One bus made contact with the rear of the other bus,” said Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. “It required some extrication, meaning they had to use some equipment to free the patient.”

      The Policy Response: Progress and Gaps

      Senator Brad Hoylman-Sigal has moved. He co-sponsored and voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed-limiting devices. He backed the extension of school speed zones. He sponsored bills for automated bike and bus lane enforcement. He pushed for moped registration and safer e-bike batteries. He fought for the law that lets New York City drop the speed limit to 20 mph. “This is about trying to eliminate all unnecessary deaths, and one of the ways you do that is by making the speed limit lower,” said Senator Hoylman-Sigal.

      But the streets are not safe yet. SUVs and trucks still kill. Buses still crush. Repeat speeders still drive. The city has the power to lower the speed limit. It has not done so citywide. The carnage continues.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Every death is preventable. Every injury is a failure of policy, not fate. The time for half-measures is over. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to use the laws Senator Hoylman-Sigal helped pass. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand enforcement that targets the real killers—speed, red lights, blocked lanes. Do not let another family become a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, including those that affect street safety in New York City.
      ▸ Where does SD 47 sit politically?
      It belongs to Manhattan, city council district 6, and assembly district 67. See crash data
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 47?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’s Kitchen, Upper West Side-Lincoln Square, Upper West Side (Central), Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Central Park, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB7, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts 3, 6, and 7, and Assembly Districts 66, 67, 69, and 75. See crash data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 47?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 6 deaths, 16 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths, 3 serious injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths, 5 serious injuries. See crash data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and enforcement focused on dangerous driving can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws that target repeat dangerous drivers.

The crackdown leaves low-paid, mostly immigrant workers exposed to fines, lost income, and road danger, while app companies escape responsibility.
