      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 47?

      Lower the Limit, Spare a Life: 20 MPH Now

      Written July 7, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list for accuracy and deduplication.)

      SD 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      A man steps off the curb. A truck crushes him on 9th Avenue. He does not get up. A cyclist, 74, collides with a bus on West End Avenue. He dies at the scene. An elderly woman, 69, is killed crossing with the signal at Amsterdam and 96th. The SUV that hits her is making a left turn. The numbers do not soften: 20 dead, 60 seriously injured, 2,808 hurt in SD 47 since 2022. The old, the young, the ones just trying to get home.

      Just last week, three NJ Transit buses crashed at Port Authority. Fourteen people were hurt. The ramp was closed for hours. “One with moderate injuries that required extrication, 10 patients with minor injuries all transported to local area hospitals,” said FDNY Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. The city rerouted traffic. The pain stayed put.

      Who Pays the Price

      The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors. The old man on a bike. The woman in the crosswalk. The man dragged by a truck. SUVs and cars killed six. Trucks and buses killed two. Bikes killed none. The pattern is clear. The largest vehicles do the most harm. The city records the bodies, the broken bones, the lives cut short. The street does not care who had the light. Only who is left standing.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      State Senator Brad Hoylman-Sigal has voted for safer street design, for more cameras, for lower speed limits. He backed the law that lets NYC set its own speed. He sponsored new rules to register mopeds and collect crash data. “By requiring the registration of mopeds at point of sale and the collection of e-bike collision data, we are making our streets safer and increasing accountability,” said Assemblymember Alex Bores. Hoylman-Sigal has called for more. But the deaths keep coming. The city has the power to lower speeds to 20 mph. It has not done so everywhere. The law is not enough. The street is not safe.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit on every street. Demand action, not delay. The dead cannot speak. You can.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 47 in Albany. For more, see the official NY State Senate website.
      ▸ Where does SD 47 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 6 and assembly district AD 67. Source
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 47?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Upper West Side-Lincoln Square, Upper West Side (Central), Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Central Park, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB7, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 6, and District 7, and Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, AD 69, and AD 75. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 47?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 6 deaths and 14 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no deaths and 3 serious injuries. Bikes caused no deaths and 4 serious injuries. Source
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries. Source
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws that protect people walking and cycling. Source

      ▸

      Resolution 0750-2023 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on September 14, 2023, and closed at session's end on December 31, 2023. The measure urges the New York State Legislature to pass, and the Governor to sign, A.1679/S.561, which would 'increase the penalty for leaving the scene of an accident involving an e-scooter and further calls upon the New York State Legislature to include e-bikes in such legislation.' The council argues this could keep more crash-involved riders at the scene, helping protect pedestrians and cyclists.
