      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 47?

      Six Dead, Hundreds Broken: Your Move, New York

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      SD 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      Six dead. Twenty seriously hurt. In the last twelve months, SD 47 saw 880 people injured in 1,740 crashes. The dead are old and young: a 69-year-old woman crossing with the light on Amsterdam, a 74-year-old cyclist struck by a bus on West End, a 57-year-old man crushed by an SUV on Broadway. The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do.

      Just weeks ago, three New Jersey Transit buses collided at Port Authority. Fourteen people were hurt. One driver was trapped and had to be cut free. The crash shut the terminal for hours. “One bus made contact with the rear of the other bus,” said FDNY Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. No one died that day. Not everyone is so lucky.

      A man on an electric unicycle lies in a hospital bed, critical, after a hit-and-run in Central Park. The cyclist who struck him fled. Police found her later. “She was arrested… and charged with leaving the scene of an accident that caused a serious injury,” police told the Daily News.

      Who Pays the Price

      SUVs and cars do the most harm. In three years, they killed six, seriously injured sixteen, and left hundreds more battered. Trucks and buses killed two. Bikes left five with serious injuries. The old, the young, the ones on foot or two wheels—they pay the price for someone else’s speed, someone else’s inattention.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Senator Brad Hoylman-Sigal has voted for safer street design, 24/7 speed cameras, and a 20 mph citywide limit. He backed the law that lets New York City lower its speed limits. He supported bills for bike lane enforcement and moped registration. But the carnage continues. Laws are passed. Streets stay deadly. The work is not finished.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit on every street. Demand real enforcement against repeat speeders. Join Families for Safe Streets. Join Transportation Alternatives. The numbers will not stop until you do.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 47. For more information, see NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Where does SD 47 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 6 and assembly district AD 67. Source
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 47?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Upper West Side-Lincoln Square, Upper West Side (Central), Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Central Park, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB7, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 6, and District 7, and Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, AD 69, and AD 75. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 47?
      Cars and trucks killed 6 and seriously injured 16. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 3 serious injuries. Bikes caused 5 serious injuries. Most deaths came from cars, SUVs, and trucks. Source
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents. Most are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws that protect people walking and cycling.

      Hoylman-Sigal Supports Moped Registration at Point of Sale
      ▸

      Albany lawmakers move to force moped registration before sale. The bill aims to block illegal, unregistered mopeds from city streets. Dealers, not riders, would handle paperwork. Advocates say this targets bad actors and protects pedestrians from rogue machines.

      On October 23, 2023, State Sen. Brad Hoylman-Sigal (D-Manhattan) introduced a bill in Albany to require moped dealers to register vehicles with the DMV before buyers leave the shop. The bill, supported by Assembly Member Alex Bores (D-Upper East Side), seeks to close a loophole that lets illegal mopeds flood city streets. Ligia Guallpa of the Worker's Justice Project backs the move, stressing manufacturer responsibility. Attorney Daniel Flanzig says the law could clear streets of unregistered, dangerous vehicles. The bill is not yet law, but enforcement actions against illegal mopeds are already underway.

      Resolution 0750-2023 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on September 14, 2023, and closed at session's end on December 31, 2023. The council argues this could keep more crash-involved riders at the scene, helping protect pedestrians and cyclists.
