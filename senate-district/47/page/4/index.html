      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 47?

      Another Pedestrian Down—How Many Bodies Will It Take?

      Written July 14, 2025

      SD 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 14, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      A man steps off the curb. A truck keeps moving. On April 4, a 39-year-old pedestrian was crushed by a box truck on West 40th and 9th. He died in the street. No second chances. Just nineteen days later, a 57-year-old man was killed by an SUV while crossing Broadway at West 86th. The numbers do not stop. In the last twelve months, SD 47 saw 6 deaths, 20 serious injuries, and 879 people hurt in 1,738 crashes (NYC Open Data).

      The old, the young, the ones just trying to get home.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs, trucks, sedans—they do most of the damage. SUVs alone killed 4 pedestrians and injured 185 more in this district since 2022 (NYC Open Data). Buses and bikes hurt too, but the weight of steel and speed is what breaks bodies. The city’s own data shows the pattern: the bigger the vehicle, the greater the harm (NYC Open Data).

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      State Senator Brad Hoylman-Sigal has taken steps. He voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed-limiting tech. He sponsored a bill for automated bike lane enforcement, aiming to keep lanes clear for cyclists. He co-sponsored bus lane camera enforcement. He backed Sammy’s Law, which lets NYC lower speed limits. But the blood keeps flowing. “One bus made contact with the rear of the other bus,” (ABC7) said FDNY Deputy Chief Jason Saffon after a crash at Port Authority that left 14 injured. “It required some extrication, meaning they had to use some equipment to free the patient.” (ABC7)

      The Work Left to Do

      The crisis is not over. Laws that sound tough often land hardest on the vulnerable. Crackdowns on cyclists and e-bike riders do not stop SUVs from killing. Cameras in bike lanes help, but only if the lanes are protected and clear. Speed limits save lives, but only if enforced. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Call your council member. Call Senator Hoylman-Sigal. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking.

      Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts and vote on bills that affect all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 47 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 6 and assembly district AD 67 (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 47?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’s Kitchen, Upper West Side-Lincoln Square, Upper West Side (Central), Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Central Park, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB7, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 6, and District 7, and Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, AD 69, and AD 75 (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 47?
      Cars and trucks (including SUVs and sedans) caused the most harm, with 6 deaths and hundreds injured. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 35 crashes, while bikes were involved in 93 crashes, but the greatest number of deaths came from larger vehicles (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. Calling them ‘accidents’ hides the fact that policy and infrastructure choices decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, keep crosswalks clear, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable. They can also push for laws that protect people walking and biking, not just drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
