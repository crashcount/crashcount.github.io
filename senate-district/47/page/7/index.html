      No More Names on Asphalt: Demand Safe Streets Now

      SD 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 47, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-one people killed. Sixty-three left with serious injuries. Nearly 2,900 hurt since 2022. Each number is a name, a life, a family changed. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The violence does not stop.

      Just this spring, a 74-year-old cyclist was killed by a bus at West 70th and West End. A 57-year-old man was crushed by an SUV at Broadway and 86th. A 69-year-old woman was struck and killed by a turning SUV while crossing with the signal at Amsterdam and 96th. The street does not forgive. The city moves on.

      The System Responds—Slowly

      Most deaths come from cars and trucks. SUVs alone killed six, left sixteen with serious injuries, and hurt hundreds more. Trucks and buses killed two, with dozens of injuries. Bikes and mopeds are not blameless, but the carnage is driven by larger vehicles.

      The news does not stop. On July 2, three NJ Transit buses collided at Port Authority, injuring a dozen. “One with moderate injuries that required extrication, 10 patients with minor injuries all transported to local area hospitals,” said FDNY Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. The tunnel closed. Commuters waited. The city shrugged.

      What Has Senator Hoylman-Sigal Done?

      Senator Brad Hoylman-Sigal has moved. He co-sponsored and voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed-limiting devices. He backed the law giving NYC the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. He supported new rules for moped and e-bike safety, shifting accountability to retailers and away from workers. But the deaths keep coming. The pace of change is slow. The streets are not safe yet.

      The Call

      Every day of delay is another risk. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call Senator Hoylman-Sigal. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Demand streets that do not kill. The dead cannot call. You can.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents districts like SD 47. See the Stop Super Speeders Act for an example of recent legislation.
      ▸ Where does SD 47 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 6 and assembly district AD 67. Crash data for SD 47 is available from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 47?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Upper West Side-Lincoln Square, Upper West Side (Central), Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Central Park, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB7, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 6, and District 7, and Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, AD 69, and AD 75. See crash data for SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 47?
      Cars and trucks caused most deaths and injuries: 6 killed, 16 seriously hurt, hundreds injured. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no deaths but several serious injuries. Bikes caused no deaths but 5 serious injuries. See NYC Open Data for details.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and stronger enforcement. They are not random acts—they follow patterns that can be changed.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, target repeat dangerous drivers, fund safer street redesigns, and hold city agencies accountable for delays.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Hoylman Opposes Misguided Idling Law Weakening Plan
      ▸

      City wants to loosen idling rules. Community Board 4 says no. Advocates warn of dirtier air, sicker kids. DEP claims clarity, but enforcement already weak. Spectrum wants a break. No one supports it. Drivers idle near playgrounds, hospitals. Danger grows.

      On March 4, 2022, the Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) held a public hearing on a proposed rule change to New York City's vehicle idling law. The change would broaden the definition of 'processing device,' letting more vehicles idle legally. Manhattan Community Board 4 voted unanimously to reject the change, warning, 'These exemptions... would create an argument that just about any activity that requires power in a vehicle is an idling defense.' State Sen. Brad Hoylman testified, 'Drivers most often idle in front of highly trafficked pedestrian areas... those that live near large roadways face serious health repercussions.' Advocates, residents, and Dr. Patrick Schnell all opposed the proposal, citing health and environmental harm. Spectrum requested a variance, but all testimony opposed it. The DEP claims the change clarifies the law, but enforcement is already weak and large companies are frequent violators. No safety analyst assessment was provided.

      Hoylman-Sigal Supports Safety-Boosting Elimination of Parking Minimums
      ▸

      State lawmakers push to end mandatory parking in new buildings. The bill targets car dominance, aims to cut congestion, and free space for homes. Sen. Hoylman leads the charge. The move could shift city streets, making room for people, not just cars.

      Senate Bill, introduced by Sen. Brad Hoylman (D-Manhattan), would ban cities from forcing developers to build off-street parking in new projects. The bill, considered as of January 31, 2022, is under review by the state legislature. The measure, described as aiming to 'encourage public transit use, reduce car ownership, and address climate change,' would give New York City 180 days to update zoning if passed. Sen. Hoylman, the sponsor, calls parking minimums 'backwards' and a barrier to affordable housing. Sen. Alessandra Biaggi (D-Bronx) has shown support for similar efforts. The Department of City Planning and the governor's office will review the legislation if it advances. The bill does not ban parking outright, but removes the mandate, letting developers decide. Advocates say this could lower costs and open space for housing or community needs.
