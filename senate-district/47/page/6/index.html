      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 47?

      Another Body, Same Street: How Many Dead Before They Act?

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A man steps off the curb. A truck crushes him on 9th Avenue. He is 39. Dead at the scene. A 69-year-old woman crosses with the light at Amsterdam and 96th. An SUV turns left. She never makes it to the other side. Five people have died on these streets in the last year. Nearly 800 more are injured. Seventeen are left with wounds that will never heal. The numbers do not flinch: 20 killed, 2,812 injured, 60 seriously hurt in SD 47 since 2022, according to NYC Open Data.

      The Pattern Is Relentless

      The machines do not care. SUVs, sedans, trucks, and buses are the main weapons. SUVs alone have killed six and injured hundreds. Bikes and mopeds hurt too, but the carnage comes on four wheels. The old and the young are not spared. A 74-year-old cyclist dies after colliding with a bus on West End Avenue. A 57-year-old man is killed crossing Broadway. The street does not forgive. The city counts the bodies. The families count the empty chairs.

      Leadership: Progress and Pressure

      Senator Brad Hoylman-Sigal has voted for safer street design and supported the push for a 20 mph citywide speed limit. He backed the expansion of 24/7 speed cameras and called for more protected bike lanes. He has spoken out for laws that target reckless drivers, not just vulnerable road users. But the work is not done. The city can lower the speed limit now. The state can pass the Stop Super Speeders Act. The council can demand more daylighted intersections and camera enforcement for blocked bike lanes. The senator has moved, but the crisis moves faster.

      The Call

      Every day of delay means another name on the list. Call your council member. Call the senator. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Do not wait for another empty chair.

      Hoylman Opposes Misguided Idling Law Weakening Plan
      ▸

      City wants to loosen idling rules. Community Board 4 says no. Advocates warn of dirtier air, sicker kids. DEP claims clarity, but enforcement already weak. Spectrum wants a break. No one supports it. Drivers idle near playgrounds, hospitals. Danger grows.

      On March 4, 2022, the Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) held a public hearing on a proposed rule change to New York City's vehicle idling law. The change would broaden the definition of 'processing device,' letting more vehicles idle legally. Manhattan Community Board 4 voted unanimously to reject the change, warning, 'These exemptions... would create an argument that just about any activity that requires power in a vehicle is an idling defense.' State Sen. Brad Hoylman testified, 'Drivers most often idle in front of highly trafficked pedestrian areas... those that live near large roadways face serious health repercussions.' Advocates, residents, and Dr. Patrick Schnell all opposed the proposal, citing health and environmental harm. Spectrum requested a variance, but all testimony opposed it. The DEP claims the change clarifies the law, but enforcement is already weak and large companies are frequent violators. No safety analyst assessment was provided.
