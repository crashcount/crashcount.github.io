      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 47?

      Blood on the Asphalt: Lower the Limit or Count More Graves

      Written July 9, 2025

      SD 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A man steps off the curb. A truck crushes him on 9th Avenue. He is 39. He dies in the street. A 74-year-old cyclist collides with a bus on West End Avenue. He does not get up. A 69-year-old woman, walking with the light at Amsterdam and 96th, is struck by an SUV turning left. She dies at the intersection. These are not rare stories. They are the drumbeat of life and death in Senate District 47.

      In the last 12 months, five people have died in crashes here. Seventeen more suffered serious injuries. In total, 793 people were hurt. The old, the young, the ones just trying to cross the street. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie. NYC crash data

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      Most deaths come from cars and trucks. SUVs killed four. Trucks killed two. Bikes and mopeds hurt, but they do not kill like steel and speed. The city’s streets are not built for the softest bodies. They are built for the biggest vehicles. The cost is measured in funerals and empty chairs.

      Last week, a car fire in the Lincoln Tunnel trapped commuters for over an hour. One was hospitalized. Five more were treated at the scene. The fire was put out, but the fear lingers. “One commuter was taken to Mount Sinai West to be treated for injuries. The extent of the victim’s injuries was unclear,” said the FDNY. The tunnel reopened, but the city did not change.

      Leadership: Progress and Pressure

      Senator Brad Hoylman-Sigal has voted for safer street design. He backed the bill to require “complete street design principles”—a step toward roads that protect everyone, not just drivers. He has supported speed cameras, lower speed limits, and better enforcement. But the deaths keep coming. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so. The law is on the books. The blood is on the asphalt.

      “One bus made contact with the rear of the other bus,” said Deputy Chief Jason Saffon, after a crash at Port Authority left 14 injured. The system is brittle. The bodies are real.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another day of risk. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand enforcement against the worst repeat offenders. Join Families for Safe Streets. Stand with the living before you join the dead. The city will not save you unless you force its hand.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for making and passing state laws. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect the entire state.
      ▸ Where does SD 47 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 6 and assembly district AD 67. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 47?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Upper West Side-Lincoln Square, Upper West Side (Central), Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Central Park, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB7, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 6, and District 7, and Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, AD 69, and AD 75. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 47?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 6 deaths and 14 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths and 3 serious injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths and 4 serious injuries. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws that protect people walking and cycling.
