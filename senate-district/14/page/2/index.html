      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 14?

      Nine Dead, Thousands Hurt—Demand Comrie Stop the Killing Speed Now

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      SD 14: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Blood Price of the Road

      A man steps off the curb. A woman crosses at dusk. In Senate District 14, the story repeats. Nine dead in the last year. Eighteen left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. Over 2,000 hurt. These are not numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors.

      Just weeks ago, a 39-year-old man on a scooter was struck and killed at 116th Avenue and Nashville Boulevard. The van turned left. He never got up. Police said, “As the van attempted to turn left onto Nashville Boulevard, it collided with the scooter, immediately propelling Lagredelle off the device.” [amny, 2025-06-30]

      On July 11, an MTA bus slammed into a pole in Queens. Eight people hurt. Metal and glass. The bus did not stop for anyone.

      In the last twelve months, cars and SUVs have done most of the damage: eight dead, over 800 injured. Trucks and buses, motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes all add to the toll. No street is safe. No hour is sacred.

      Leadership: Progress and the Long Road Ahead

      State Senator Leroy Comrie has moved. He co-sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act and voted yes in committee. The bill would force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit. He also backed the extension of school speed zones, a small shield for children on foot.

      But the work is not done. Comrie has opposed the “Idaho stop” bill, which would let cyclists treat stop signs as yields. He cited concerns for seniors. The debate drags on while the street claims more.

      The Next Fight: Demand Relentless Action

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. The city has the power to lower speed limits. The state can rein in repeat offenders. But power unused is as deadly as a car left in drive.

      Call Senator Comrie. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand action on the Stop Super Speeders Act. The cost of waiting is counted in bodies.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York.
      ▸ Where does SD 14 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27 and assembly district AD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 14?
      It includes the Forest Hills, Kew Gardens Hills, Jamaica Hills-Briarwood, Kew Gardens, Jamaica, South Jamaica, St. Albans, Hollis, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, and Queens CB13 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 24, District 27, District 29, and District 31, and Assembly Districts AD 27, AD 28, AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 14?
      Cars and SUVs caused 8 deaths and over 800 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 13 crashes with injuries, but no deaths. Bikes were involved in 12 crashes, with no deaths and one serious injury.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Every crash is preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and holding repeat offenders accountable can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, pass and enforce laws against repeat dangerous drivers, and support infrastructure that protects people walking and cycling.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
