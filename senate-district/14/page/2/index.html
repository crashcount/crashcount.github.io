      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 14?

      No More Graves on Linden: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, deduped citations.)

      SD 14: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      Blood on Linden Boulevard

      A man with a cane tried to cross Linden Boulevard. He did not make it home. On April 24, 2025, Christopher Gayton, 62, was struck by a driver who fled, then run over by a second car. His family said, “Today is a sad day. We lost a brother, father, son, uncle, and cousin. [He] has been snuffed from us by a hit and runner driver.” (NY Daily News). The police are still searching for the first driver. No arrests. No justice. (Patch).

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the last 12 months, SD 14 saw 6 deaths, 16 serious injuries, and 1,874 people hurt in 2,808 crashes. Pedestrians and cyclists are the most exposed. Cars and SUVs did the most harm: 6 deaths, 10 serious injuries, and 798 injuries. Trucks and buses killed 1, injured 47. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes left more bodies broken. (NYC Open Data).

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      State Senator Leroy Comrie has moved on some fronts. He voted yes on a bill to require complete street design—roads built for people, not just cars (Senate Bill S 9718). He co-sponsored a bill for a yellow alert system to help catch hit-and-run drivers (S 4946). He also backed a bill to rate cars for pedestrian safety (S 1675).

      But when it came to the “Idaho stop” bill, which would let cyclists treat stop signs as yields, Comrie said no. He cited concerns for seniors, but the law would have made streets safer for people on bikes (Streetsblog NYC).

      The Work Is Not Done

      Every delay means more blood on the street. The dead cannot speak. The living must. Call Senator Comrie. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns. Demand action before another family sets a table for one less.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees state policy.
      ▸ Where does SD 14 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27 and assembly district AD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 14?
      It includes the Forest Hills, Kew Gardens Hills, Jamaica Hills-Briarwood, Kew Gardens, Jamaica, South Jamaica, St. Albans, Hollis, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, and Queens CB13 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 24, District 27, District 29, and District 31, and Assembly Districts AD 27, AD 28, AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 14?
      Cars and SUVs caused 6 deaths, 10 serious injuries, and 798 injuries to pedestrians in SD 14. Trucks and buses caused 1 death and 47 injuries. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes caused 1 serious injury and 11 injuries combined. (NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Most could be prevented with safer street design, lower speeds, and stronger enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, and support bills that protect people walking and biking.

      S 9718
      Comrie votes yes, boosting street safety with complete street design.
      ▸

      Senate Bill S 9718 passed. It pushes for complete street design. The aim: safer roads for everyone. Pedestrians, cyclists, and passengers stand to gain. The vote split. Some senators said no. But the bill moved forward. Streets may change.

      Senate Bill S 9718, titled "Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles," advanced through the Senate with committee votes on May 28 and June 3, 2024. The bill, sponsored by Sean Ryan (District 61) and co-sponsored by Liz Krueger, Monica Martinez, Jack M. Martins, Anthony H. Palumbo, and Julia Salazar, passed both votes despite opposition. The measure calls for redesigning streets to protect all users, not just drivers. The vote was not unanimous—several senators voted no, but the majority carried it through. The bill’s focus is clear: safer streets for people on foot, on bikes, and in vehicles. No safety analyst note was provided, but the intent is to cut danger at the curb and crosswalk.

      The MTA’s Queens Bus Network Redesign, announced December 12, 2023, proposes $30 million in service upgrades and expands local routes from 83 to 91. Council Transportation Chair Selvena Brooks-Powers voiced 'serious concerns' about disadvantages for her district, especially with congestion pricing. No safety analyst has assessed the impact on vulnerable road users.
