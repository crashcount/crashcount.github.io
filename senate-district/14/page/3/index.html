      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 14?

      Still Bleeding: Queens Streets Claim More Lives While Politicians Stall

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 14: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A woman steps into the crosswalk. A car keeps going. On December 25, a 45-year-old woman was killed at 158th Street and 111th Avenue. She was crossing at the intersection. The driver did not see her. She died in the street. NYC crash data.

      A week later, a 21-year-old man died on Laurelton Parkway. The cause: unsafe speed. He did not make it home for the holiday. The car was demolished. The road was quiet again. NYC crash data.

      In June, a scooter rider named Shaun Lagredelle was thrown from his scooter when a van turned left into him. He died at the hospital. Police said, “As the van attempted to turn left onto Nashville Boulevard, it collided with the scooter, immediately propelling Lagredelle off the device.”

      In the last 12 months, 8 people have died and 16 have been seriously injured in SD 14. Over 1,800 have been hurt. Most never make the news.

      The Shape of the Disaster

      The numbers are blunt. Since 2022, there have been 22 deaths and 40 serious injuries in this district. Cars and SUVs did most of the killing. Trucks, buses, and motorcycles added to the toll. Pedestrians and cyclists are the ones who pay. The stories repeat: unsafe speed, inattention, a turn not yielded.

      A Queens driver dragged a man three blocks after a hit-and-run. The District Attorney called it “a complete disregard for human life.”

      What Has Senator Comrie Done?

      Senator Leroy Comrie has voted for safer street design. He backed a bill to require complete streets for all users, not just drivers. He also co-sponsored a bill to create a pedestrian safety rating system for cars. He sponsored a “yellow alert” bill to help catch hit-and-run drivers. See his record.

      But Comrie opposed the “Idaho stop” bill, which would have let cyclists treat stop signs as yields. He said it was out of concern for seniors. Comrie’s statement.

      The work is not done. The dead keep coming. The streets are not safe. The laws are not enough.

      Call to Action

      Call Senator Comrie. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking.

      Every day of delay means another family broken. Do not wait for the next name. Act now.

      Comrie Supports Busway Hour Reductions Against Permanent Busways
      ▸

      City made Jamaica and Archer Avenue busways permanent. Jamaica Avenue hours slashed. Archer stays 24/7. Northern Boulevard bus lanes started after months of delay. Local politicians fought restrictions. Bus riders still face slow trips. City bows to business pressure.

      On November 15, 2022, the Adams administration made busways on Jamaica and Archer avenues permanent after a one-year pilot. The Department of Transportation cut Jamaica Avenue’s busway hours from 24/7 to 6 a.m. to 8 p.m. daily, while Archer Avenue remains round-the-clock. The Northern Boulevard bus lane project, stalled for months after Council Member Francisco Moya lobbied against it, finally began installation. Council Members Nantasha Williams and Selvena Brooks-Powers opposed the busways, calling for their elimination and citing business concerns. Queens Borough President Donovan Richards and other local officials pushed for even shorter hours. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez defended the busways, saying, 'strong bus infrastructure is a necessity.' Despite opposition, the city kept some busway hours intact, but caved to business interests, reducing protection for bus riders and vulnerable road users.
