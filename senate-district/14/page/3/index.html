      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 14?

      Comrie’s District: Seven Dead, No Justice, Streets Still Bleed

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, tightened prose, ensured all inline links match citations, and deduped citations.)

      SD 14: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Latest Toll

      Seven dead. Seventeen seriously hurt. In the past year, Senate District 14 has seen 2,801 crashes. These are not just numbers—they are bodies on Linden Boulevard, 116th Avenue, and Nashville Boulevard. On June 26, Shaun Lagredelle, 39, rode his scooter west on 116th. A van turned left. Lagredelle was thrown to the ground and died at the hospital. Police said, “As the van attempted to turn left onto Nashville Boulevard, it collided with the scooter, immediately propelling Lagredelle off the device” (police said).

      A few days later, two moped riders collided with a BMW in Jamaica. Both were injured. No charges. No answers. The street stays the same (moped riders collided).

      Pedestrians are not spared. In April, Christopher Gayton, 62, was crossing Linden Boulevard. A black car hit him and fled. Another car ran him over. He died the next day. Police are still searching for the first driver (police are still searching).

      The System Responds—Slowly

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. Of the deaths and injuries to pedestrians here, cars and SUVs are responsible for the vast majority. Trucks, bikes, and mopeds trail far behind. The pattern is clear: the danger is not random.

      Senator Leroy Comrie has taken steps. He voted yes on a bill to require safer street designs for all users (voted yes on a bill). He co-sponsors bills for pedestrian safety ratings and a yellow alert system to catch hit-and-run drivers. But when it comes to the “Idaho stop”—a law that would let cyclists treat stop signs as yield—he stood in the way, citing concerns for seniors (stood in the way). The streets remain dangerous for those on foot and on wheels.

      The Call: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another family shattered. “This was a horrific death for the victim,” said Queens District Attorney Melinda Katz after a driver dragged a pedestrian three blocks before fleeing (said Queens District Attorney Melinda Katz). The system is slow. The blood dries. The danger stays.

      Call Senator Comrie. Demand faster action. Tell him: safer street design, lower speed limits, and real protection for people outside cars. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The time is now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 14 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does SD 14 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27 and assembly district AD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 14?
      It includes the Forest Hills, Kew Gardens Hills, Jamaica Hills-Briarwood, Kew Gardens, Jamaica, South Jamaica, St. Albans, Hollis, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, and Queens CB13 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 24, District 27, District 29, and District 31, and Assembly Districts AD 27, AD 28, AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 14?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians in SD 14, with 6 deaths and 10 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 1 death and 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes accounted for 1 serious injury each, but no deaths.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. The same streets see the same tragedies year after year. Better street design, lower speeds, and enforcement can prevent deaths.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for safer street design, lower speed limits, and real accountability for dangerous drivers. They can also support bills that protect people walking and biking.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
