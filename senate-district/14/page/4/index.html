      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 14?

      Nine Dead. Hundreds Broken. Hold Comrie to Account.

      Written July 13, 2025

      SD 14: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Recent Toll

      In Senate District 14, the numbers do not lie. Nine people killed. Eighteen left with serious injuries. Over two thousand hurt. In the last twelve months alone, the streets have not spared the old or the young. A 39-year-old man on a scooter, thrown to the ground and killed at an intersection. A 76-year-old woman, dead in the back seat after a lane change on the Cross Island Parkway. A pedestrian, 62, crushed crossing Linden Boulevard. The list goes on. Each number is a name, a family, a life cut short.

      Just weeks ago, two riders on a moped collided with a BMW in Jamaica. The NYPD said, “two people were riding the moped when it collided with a BMW” (CBS New York). Days before, a bus slammed into a pole, injuring eight. The city calls these minor injuries. For those on board, it was a morning that did not end as planned (CBS New York).

      Who Pays the Price?

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. In three years, they killed seven, seriously injured ten, and left hundreds more with broken bodies. Trucks and buses add to the toll. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes are not blameless, but the deadliest threat rolls on four wheels. The victims are not just numbers. They are neighbors, elders, children.

      Senator Comrie: Progress and Gaps

      Senator Leroy Comrie has taken steps. He voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed-limiting tech (Open States). He co-sponsored a bill to create a public safety rating for vehicles, shining a light on which cars are most deadly to walkers. But when it came to the “Idaho Stop”—a measure to let cyclists treat stop signs as yields—he stood in the way, citing concerns for seniors (Streetsblog NYC). The city still waits for faster, broader action. Every delay means another family at risk.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. Every death is a policy failure. Call Senator Comrie. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves the state budget, and represents districts like SD 14.
      ▸ Where does SD 14 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27 and assembly district AD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 14?
      It includes the Forest Hills, Kew Gardens Hills, Jamaica Hills-Briarwood, Kew Gardens, Jamaica, South Jamaica, St. Albans, Hollis, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, and Queens CB13 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 24, District 27, District 29, and District 31, and Assembly Districts AD 27, AD 28, AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 14?
      Cars and trucks killed 7 and seriously injured 10 pedestrians in SD 14 over the past three years. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no pedestrian deaths or serious injuries. Bicycles caused no pedestrian deaths and 1 serious injury (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These are not random accidents. The data shows patterns—speed, failure to yield, and street design—pointing to preventable causes.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, support automated enforcement, and back laws that hold dangerous drivers accountable.

      Comrie Opposes Exemptions to Safety Boosting Congestion Pricing
      ▸

      State Sen. Leroy Comrie slammed calls for more congestion pricing exemptions. He warned that carving out special breaks guts the plan’s purpose. Comrie said every exemption means less money for transit. He wants the city to hold the line. No more carve-outs.

      On January 19, 2022, State Sen. Leroy Comrie, chair of the MTA committee, spoke at a legislative hearing on congestion pricing exemptions. The hearing addressed whether to grant more carve-outs to Manhattan’s congestion tolls. Comrie declared, 'I don't think there should be any exemptions, and I'm going to continue to say that there should be no exemptions.' He pushed back against senators seeking discounts for suburban drivers and city workers. Comrie warned that opening the door to more exemptions would undermine the law’s intent and threaten revenue for transit. He said, 'Once you open up that can of worms, you create major problems, because everybody wants to be exempted for something.' Comrie’s stance: keep the plan strict, protect transit funding, and don’t weaken congestion pricing with special favors.
