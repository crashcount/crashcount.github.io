      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 14?

      Blood on Linden, Silence in Albany: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written July 4, 2025

      SD 14: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 4, 2025

      Death on the Asphalt

      In Senate District 14, the road does not forgive. In the last twelve months, seven people died and 1,881 were injured in crashes. Seventeen suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, parents, children. Their names vanish, but the blood on the street remains.

      Just last week, a 39-year-old man on a scooter was killed at 116th Avenue and Nashville Boulevard. The van turned left. The man was thrown to the ground. He died at Long Island Jewish Medical Center. Police said, “As the van attempted to turn left onto Nashville Boulevard, it collided with the scooter, immediately propelling Lagredelle off the device,” according to amNY.

      On Linden Boulevard, a 62-year-old man tried to cross. He was struck by a hit-and-run driver, then run over by another car. He died the next day. Police are still searching for the first driver who fled the scene, according to Patch.

      The Usual Weapons: Cars, SUVs, and Speed

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. In this district, cars and SUVs caused six deaths and over 800 injuries to pedestrians in recent years. Trucks and buses add to the toll. The numbers do not lie. Speed and inattention are the common threads. The street is a gauntlet for anyone not behind a windshield.

      Senator Comrie: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Senator Leroy Comrie has taken some steps. He voted yes on a bill to require safer street design for all users (S9718). He co-sponsored bills for pedestrian safety ratings and a yellow alert system for hit-and-runs. But when it came to the “Idaho stop” bill, which would have made it safer for cyclists at intersections, Comrie opposed it, citing concerns for seniors (see the debate).

      The pace of change is slow. The dead keep coming. The city studies, the state debates, and families bury their own.

      Call to Action: Demand More, Demand Now

      Seven dead in a year. Hundreds broken. This is not fate. This is policy. Call Senator Comrie. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesign. Demand action before another name is lost.

      The road will not change itself.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 14.
      ▸ Where does SD 14 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27 and assembly district AD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 14?
      It includes the Forest Hills, Kew Gardens Hills, Jamaica Hills-Briarwood, Kew Gardens, Jamaica, South Jamaica, St. Albans, Hollis, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, and Queens CB13 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 24, District 27, District 29, and District 31, and Assembly Districts AD 27, AD 28, AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 14?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians in SD 14: 6 deaths, 619 minor injuries, 182 moderate injuries, and 10 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no deaths but 5 minor and 7 moderate injuries. Bikes caused no deaths, 7 minor injuries, 3 moderate injuries, and 1 serious injury.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and stronger enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for safer street design, lower speed limits, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can also push for more protected crossings and bike lanes.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
