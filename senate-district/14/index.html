      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 14?

      Nine Dead. Thousands Hurt. Who’s Next, Senator Comrie?

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, cleaned citations, and ensured proper link formatting.)

      SD 14: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A man on a scooter, thrown to the ground. A moped, crushed by a BMW. A 76-year-old woman, killed in the back seat on the Cross Island Parkway. In the last twelve months, nine people have died and over 2,000 have been injured in crashes across Senate District 14. Fifteen suffered serious injuries. These are lives cut short, families left with empty chairs.

      Just weeks ago, Shaun Lagredelle was riding his scooter on 116th Avenue. A van turned left. The collision threw him off. He died at the hospital. Police said, “As the van attempted to turn left onto Nashville Boulevard, it collided with the scooter, immediately propelling Lagredelle off the device before he hit the ground”. No arrests. The investigation drags on.

      On June 29, two people riding a moped collided with a BMW in Jamaica. Both were injured. The NYPD said only, “two people were riding the moped when it collided with a BMW”. No word on charges. No word on change.

      The System Favors Speed, Not Safety

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. In the past three years, they killed seven and injured hundreds of pedestrians. Trucks and buses killed one. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll. The dead include the old, the young, the ones just trying to cross the street.

      What Has Senator Comrie Done?

      Senator Leroy Comrie has voted for safer street design—he backed a bill to require complete streets for all users. He co-sponsored a bill for public pedestrian safety ratings on cars. He also sponsored a “yellow alert” system to help catch hit-and-run drivers. But when it came to the “Idaho stop” bill, which would have given cyclists safer options at intersections, Comrie opposed it, citing concerns for seniors.

      The pace of change is slow. Each delay means another family waits for news that never comes. Each compromise leaves the most vulnerable exposed.

      Call to Action: Demand More, Demand It Now

      Contact Senator Comrie. Contact your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people on foot, on bikes, on scooters. The dead cannot speak. You can. The time for waiting is over.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents districts across New York.
      ▸ Where does SD 14 sit politically?
      It belongs to Queens, city council district 27, and assembly district 29. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 14?
      It includes Forest Hills, Kew Gardens Hills, Jamaica Hills-Briarwood, Kew Gardens, Jamaica, South Jamaica, St. Albans, Hollis, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, and Queens CB13 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts 24, 27, 29, and 31, and Assembly Districts 27, 28, 29, 32, and 33. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 14?
      Cars and SUVs: 7 deaths, 642 minor injuries, 192 moderate injuries, 10 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 1 death, 32 minor injuries, 14 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 5 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 7 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. The same streets see the same tragedies year after year. Better design, lower speeds, and stronger laws can prevent deaths.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand automated enforcement, and support laws that protect people walking and biking.
