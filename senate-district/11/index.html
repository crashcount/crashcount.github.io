      Six Dead. Hundreds Hurt. Call Stavisky and Demand Safer Streets—Now.

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 11: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Bodies Left Behind

      A man steps off the curb. A truck turns left. He does not make it home. In the last twelve months, six people died on the roads of Senate District 11. Nineteen more were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In total, 1,622 people were hurt—children, elders, workers, neighbors. The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do.

      A 29-year-old was crushed crossing with the light on Hillside Avenue. The truck kept turning. The record says only this: “Crush Injuries. Apparent Death.” NYC Open Data.

      A 15-year-old on a moped was thrown from his seat and died under the wheels of a bus. The record does not mention his name. Only his age, and that he wore no helmet. NYC Open Data.

      The Vehicles That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. In three years, they killed at least three people and seriously injured seventeen more. Trucks and buses killed one, left three with life-changing wounds. Motorcycles and mopeds took no lives, but left one person with a serious injury. Not a single bike killed or seriously hurt anyone.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Senator Toby Stavisky voted yes on a bill to force safer street design for all users, not just drivers. The bill passed. It calls for “redesigning streets to protect all users, not just drivers.” legislation. Stavisky also co-sponsored a bill to rate cars for pedestrian safety, but it has not moved. The streets remain the same.

      The dead do not care about bills that sit in committee. The families do. The record shows delay. Five months passed before police charged a driver who killed a 78-year-old man in Bellerose. The charge: failure to yield. The penalty: a desk ticket. A driver has been arrested five months after fatally striking a 78-year-old man crossing a heavily traveled Queens intersection, police said Wednesday.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day the city delays lowering speed limits, every day a bill sits unsigned, another family waits for a knock at the door. Call Senator Stavisky. Call your council member. Demand streets that put people first.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for making and passing laws. Senators represent districts across the state and vote on bills that affect all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 11 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 19 and assembly district AD 26.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 11?
      It includes the Rikers Island, College Point, Whitestone-Beechhurst, Bay Terrace-Clearview, Fort Totten, Jamaica Estates-Holliswood, Douglaston-Little Neck, Oakland Gardens-Hollis Hills, Alley Pond Park, Glen Oaks-Floral Park-New Hyde Park, and Queens CB80 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19, District 21, District 22, and District 23, and Assembly Districts AD 24, AD 26, AD 27, AD 33, and AD 35.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 11?
      Cars and SUVs killed at least three people and seriously injured seventeen more in the past three years. Trucks and buses killed one and left three with serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused one serious injury but no deaths. Bikes caused no deaths or serious injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents—they are the result of choices, policies, and street design. Lower speeds, safer crossings, and better enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, and hold reckless drivers accountable. They can also push for more funding and oversight to protect people walking and cycling.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
