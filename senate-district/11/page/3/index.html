      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 11?

      Six Dead. Hundreds Hurt. Call Stavisky and Demand Safer Streets—Now.

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 11: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Bodies Left Behind

      A man steps off the curb. A truck turns left. He does not make it home. In the last twelve months, six people died on the roads of Senate District 11. Nineteen more were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In total, 1,622 people were hurt—children, elders, workers, neighbors. The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do.

      A 29-year-old was crushed crossing with the light on Hillside Avenue. The truck kept turning. The record says only this: “Crush Injuries. Apparent Death.” NYC Open Data.

      A 15-year-old on a moped was thrown from his seat and died under the wheels of a bus. The record does not mention his name. Only his age, and that he wore no helmet. NYC Open Data.

      The Vehicles That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. In three years, they killed at least three people and seriously injured seventeen more. Trucks and buses killed one, left three with life-changing wounds. Motorcycles and mopeds took no lives, but left one person with a serious injury. Not a single bike killed or seriously hurt anyone.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Senator Toby Stavisky voted yes on a bill to force safer street design for all users, not just drivers. The bill passed. It calls for “redesigning streets to protect all users, not just drivers.” legislation. Stavisky also co-sponsored a bill to rate cars for pedestrian safety, but it has not moved. The streets remain the same.

      The dead do not care about bills that sit in committee. The families do. The record shows delay. Five months passed before police charged a driver who killed a 78-year-old man in Bellerose. The charge: failure to yield. The penalty: a desk ticket. A driver has been arrested five months after fatally striking a 78-year-old man crossing a heavily traveled Queens intersection, police said Wednesday.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day the city delays lowering speed limits, every day a bill sits unsigned, another family waits for a knock at the door. Call Senator Stavisky. Call your council member. Demand streets that put people first.

      Take action now.
