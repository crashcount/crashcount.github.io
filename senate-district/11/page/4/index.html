      Queens Bleeds: Streets Kill, Lawmakers Stall

      Written July 13, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 11: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 11, the road does not forgive. Since 2022, 20 people have died and 59 have been seriously injured in crashes. These are not just numbers—they are fathers, daughters, neighbors. In the last twelve months alone, six more lives ended on these streets. Cars and SUVs did most of the killing—three deaths by SUV, one by truck, none by bike. NYC crash data

      Just last week, a crane slammed into a city bus in Queens Village. Four people were hurt. Emily McKinnon was on the bus. “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us,” she said. “I was holding on for my life back there.” McKinnon described the crash

      A few days before, a police chase ended with a man on an e-bike dead under the wheels of a Lexus. The police said, “He was pronounced dead at the scene.” police reported

      The Vulnerable Pay the Price

      The most vulnerable—pedestrians, cyclists, children—bear the brunt. In three years, over 5,000 people have been injured in SD 11. The dead include a 19-year-old moped rider, a 29-year-old pedestrian crushed by a truck, and a 64-year-old driver. The street is a lottery. The odds are bad.

      App-based workers gathered in Little Bay Park to mourn their dead. “For every name we read aloud at this memorial, there are families left grieving and coworkers left fearing the same fate,” said Adaligisa Payero. “These deaths were not inevitable; they happened because billion-dollar companies chose profit over safety.” Payero said at the memorial

      What Has Senator Stavisky Done?

      Senator Toby Stavisky has voted for bills that matter. She voted yes to require safer street designs, to expand school speed cameras, and to boost driver education on pedestrian and cyclist safety. She co-sponsored a bill to create a public safety rating for cars. She voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, which targets repeat dangerous drivers.

      But the deaths keep coming. The bills are not enough. The street does not care about good intentions.

      What Next?

      Call Senator Stavisky. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand enforcement against repeat speeders. Demand streets built for people, not just cars.

      Every day of delay is another family broken. The road will not wait. Neither should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York.
      ▸ Where does SD 11 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 19 and assembly district AD 26.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 11?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Rikers Island, College Point, Whitestone-Beechhurst, Bay Terrace-Clearview, Fort Totten, Jamaica Estates-Holliswood, Jamaica Hills-Briarwood, Cunningham Park, Douglaston-Little Neck, and Queens CB80 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19, District 21, District 22, and District 23, and Assembly Districts AD 24, AD 26, AD 27, AD 33, and AD 35.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 11?
      Most deaths and injuries to pedestrians in SD 11 came from Cars and SUVs (3 deaths, 408 minor injuries, 132 moderate, 19 serious), followed by Trucks (1 death, 28 minor injuries, 14 moderate, 3 serious), Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 3 minor injuries, 1 moderate), and Bikes (no deaths, 7 minor injuries).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street designs, and enforcement against repeat dangerous drivers can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand speed and red-light cameras, redesign streets for safety, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable.
