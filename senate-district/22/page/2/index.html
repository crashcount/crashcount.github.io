      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 22?

      Senator Sutton: How Many Dead Before You Act?

      Written July 7, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, and ensured citation accuracy.)

      SD 22: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Death Toll Grows, Week by Week

      In Senate District 22, the road keeps taking. Ten people killed in the last year. Eighteen left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. In the same stretch, over 1,200 hurt. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie.

      Just weeks ago, a 17-year-old boy died on East 8th Street. He rode a moped. A car turned left. The crash threw him and his passenger into the street. “Jhoan later died at the hospital,” wrote the Daily News. No arrests. No comfort for his family. The street is quiet again, but the loss remains.

      On Avenue N, a man on a motorcycle was killed. On Avenue L, a 73-year-old woman crossing with the signal was struck and killed by an SUV. The stories repeat. The faces change. The outcome does not.

      Broken Bodies, Broken System

      The violence does not spare the young or the old. Three children under 18 killed in the last year. Five more seriously hurt. The old are not safe either. One woman, 73, crossing with the light, was killed by a turning SUV. The driver kept his license. The woman lost her life.

      The carnage is not random. Most deaths come from cars and SUVs—17 killed, 11 seriously hurt in three years. Trucks and buses took four more lives. Motorcycles and mopeds killed none but caused 1 serious injury; bikes caused no deaths or serious injuries. The city counts the dead. The city moves on.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Silence

      State Senator Sam Sutton holds the seat. The record shows no bold action, no public push for the bills that would slow cars or hold repeat speeders to account. Albany passed Sammy’s Law, giving New York City the power to lower speed limits. The city can act. The Senator can lead.

      But the silence is heavy. The dead do not vote. The living wait for change.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Thoughts and Prayers

      Call Senator Sutton. Demand action. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand support for the Stop Super Speeders Act. Demand streets where a child can cross without fear. The toll will not stop until the laws do.

      Do not wait for another name to join the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 22.
      ▸ Where does SD 22 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 48 and assembly district AD 41.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 22?
      It includes the Borough Park, Mapleton-Midwood (West), Midwood, Gravesend (East)-Homecrest, Madison, Sheepshead Bay-Manhattan Beach-Gerritsen Beach, Marine Park-Plumb Island, Brooklyn CB12, Brooklyn CB14, and Brooklyn CB15 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 44, District 46, and District 48, and Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 48, and AD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 22?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 17 deaths and 11 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds killed none but caused 1 serious injury; bikes caused no deaths or serious injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and holding repeat dangerous drivers accountable can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can support laws to lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, and require real penalties for repeat dangerous drivers. They can also push for safer street designs and more funding for pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
