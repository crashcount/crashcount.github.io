      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 22?

      SD 22: Dead Kids, Broken Bodies, and Silence from Senator Sutton

      Written June 27, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned citations, and ensured proper link formatting.)

      SD 22: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Human Cost in SD 22

      A 17-year-old boy rode north on East 8th Street. A 71-year-old man turned left. The moped slammed into the car. The boy flew from the seat. He died at Maimonides. His passenger, 14, was left broken and unconscious. There were no arrests. The police said Jhoan later died at the hospital. The street was quiet again, but the loss stayed.

      A week earlier, a drunk driver in a black Suburban tore through Coney Island Avenue. He hit a Volvo, then a marked NYPD car. Two officers were left with broken bones and head trauma. A passenger was thrown from the SUV, left in critical condition. The driver was charged with DWI and reckless endangerment. Police reported one officer suffered a broken pelvis and arm, as well as head trauma, while another suffered a broken hip.

      The Numbers: Relentless and Unforgiving

      In the last 12 months, SD 22 saw 10 deaths and 1,306 injuries on its streets. Three children died. Nineteen people suffered serious injuries. The violence did not spare the old or the young. Cars and SUVs did most of the killing—17 deaths. Trucks and buses took four more. Motorcycles and mopeds, two. Bikes, none. The numbers do not lie. They do not comfort.

      Leadership: Action or Absence?

      State Senator Sam Sutton holds the seat. The record shows no public push, no bill, no quote, no stand in the last year to stop the bleeding. The silence is loud. The dead cannot vote. The living wait for action.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. Every crash is a policy failure. Every death is a choice. Call Senator Sutton. Demand a vote for real change—lower speed limits, more enforcement, safer streets for children and elders. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Act now. The blood on the street is not yet dry.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for making and passing laws. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect every resident.
      ▸ Where does SD 22 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 48 and assembly district AD 41.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 22?
      It includes the Borough Park, Mapleton-Midwood (West), Midwood, Gravesend (East)-Homecrest, Madison, Sheepshead Bay-Manhattan Beach-Gerritsen Beach, Marine Park-Plumb Island, Brooklyn CB12, Brooklyn CB14, and Brooklyn CB15 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 44, District 46, and District 48, and Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 48, and AD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 22?
      Cars and SUVs caused 17 deaths and 842 injuries. Trucks and buses caused 4 deaths and 74 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths and 27 injuries. Bikes caused no deaths and 22 injuries. These numbers reflect the toll on pedestrians in SD 22, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents—they are the result of choices, policies, and street design. Every death and injury can be prevented with better laws and safer infrastructure.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass laws to lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, support automated enforcement, and hold reckless drivers accountable. They can also listen to residents and act before another life is lost.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
