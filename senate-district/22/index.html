      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 22?

      How Many Dead Kids Before Sutton Wakes Up?

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 22: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 22, the street does not forgive. Since 2022, 35 people have died in crashes. 41 more were left with serious injuries. In the last twelve months alone, 10 lives ended on these roads. Three were children. The numbers do not tell you about the shoes left at the curb or the sirens at midnight. But they do not lie.

      Just last week, a 17-year-old boy died on East 8th Street. He was riding a moped. A car turned left. The moped slammed into it. The boy and his passenger were thrown. “A 17-year-old boy died and an elderly man was in critical condition after a moped collision Monday in Brooklyn,” reported the NY Daily News. No arrests. The street stays the same.

      Days before, a drunk driver in a Chevy Suburban tore through Coney Island Avenue. He hit a Volvo, then a police car. Two officers and a passenger were hospitalized. “One police officer suffered a broken pelvis and arm, as well as head trauma, while another suffered a broken hip,” said police. The driver was charged with DWI. The intersection is unchanged.

      Who Pays the Price

      The dead are not numbers. They are children, elders, neighbors. In the last year, three children died. The oldest was 17. The youngest was five. Most were crossing with the light. Some were walking home. The cars that killed them were mostly SUVs and sedans. Trucks and taxis did their share. No helmet or crosswalk could save them.

      Leadership: Words and Silence

      State Senator Sam Sutton represents this district. The record shows no recent public action, bill, or statement from Sutton on traffic violence. The silence is loud. The deaths continue. The law now allows New York City to lower speed limits. The city can act. The Senator can push. But the streets stay fast, and the bodies pile up.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by leaders who look away. Call Senator Sutton. Demand action. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real penalties for repeat offenders. Demand streets built for people, not for speed. Do not wait for another child to die.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 22.
      ▸ Where does SD 22 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 48 and assembly district AD 41.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 22?
      It includes the Borough Park, Mapleton-Midwood (West), Midwood, Gravesend (East)-Homecrest, Madison, Sheepshead Bay-Manhattan Beach-Gerritsen Beach, Marine Park-Plumb Island, Brooklyn CB12, Brooklyn CB14, and Brooklyn CB15 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 44, District 46, and District 48, and Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 48, and AD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 22?
      Most deaths and injuries to pedestrians in SD 22 came from SUVs and cars (17 deaths, 618 injuries), followed by trucks and buses (4 deaths, 45 injuries), and motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes (no pedestrian deaths, 22 injuries each). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and real accountability can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can push for lower speed limits, support enforcement against repeat dangerous drivers, and fund safer street redesigns.

      Map Reveals City’s Worst Repeat Drivers
      ▸

      Ten drivers rack up hundreds of speed tickets. Their cars haunt the same corners. Fines pile up. Lives end. Lawmakers stall. Danger remains. The city knows who they are. Still, they drive. Still, people die.

      Streetsblog NYC (April 16, 2025) reports on a Transportation Alternatives analysis mapping the city’s most dangerous drivers. One driver collected 563 speed-camera tickets in Sheepshead Bay; another, 474 in Manhattan. The article notes, 'A few weeks ago, one of those [recidivist] drivers sped through a red light and killed a mother and her two young daughters.' Most fines go unpaid. Advocates urge Albany to pass the 'Stop Super Speeders Bill,' which would require speed limiters for drivers with six or more automated tickets in a year. Data show vehicles with over 20 violations are five times likelier to cause deadly or severe crashes. Some legislators resist, arguing ticket thresholds are too low. The report highlights a small group’s outsized threat and the policy gap that lets them keep driving.
