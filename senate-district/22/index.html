      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 22?

      Kids, Elders, Dead—Still No Votes for Safety

      Written July 21, 2025 (Revised July 21, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, cleaned citations, and ensured proper inline link formatting.)

      SD 22: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 21, 2025

      Children and Elders in the Crosshairs

      Three days ago, an 11-year-old boy was hit by a car near a playground on Bedford Avenue in Sheepshead Bay. He survived. Many do not. In the last year alone, five children have died on these streets. Elders fare no better. On July 8, a 90-year-old man, Zhuo Xie, was killed crossing East 14th Street. The moped rider stopped, looked, and left him dying in the crosswalk. “He hit the guy and he left,” a witness told the press.

      Last week, Dov Broyde, 70, was struck and killed by a Smart Car while crossing Avenue I. The driver stayed. No charges. The street stayed the same NY Daily News.

      In the past 12 months, 14 people have died and 1,508 have been injured in SD 22. Seven of the dead were children or elders. The numbers do not flinch.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and sedans do most of the harm. In three years, they killed 21 people and injured over 650 more on foot or bike. Trucks and buses killed four. Motorcycles and mopeds killed none, but left dozens hurt. The street is a gauntlet. The most vulnerable—children, elders, people on foot—pay the price.

      Leadership: Present or Absent?

      State Senator Sam Sutton missed the vote on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill that would force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed-limiting devices Open States. He missed votes on bills to protect school zones and bus lanes. He was present for a bill to boost street safety and access, and voted yes. But when the hardest votes came, he was not there.

      “We want to make sure what happened doesn’t happen again,” said a neighbor after the latest death. The streets do not change themselves. The dead do not vote.

      What Now?

      Call Senator Sutton. Demand action. Demand he show up for every vote that protects the people who walk, bike, and play in SD 22. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real consequences for repeat offenders. The silence of power is not safety. The next name could be someone you love.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, including those that affect street safety, for all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 22 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 48 and assembly district AD 41.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 22?
      It includes the Borough Park, Mapleton-Midwood (West), Midwood, Gravesend (East)-Homecrest, Madison, Sheepshead Bay-Manhattan Beach-Gerritsen Beach, Marine Park-Plumb Island, Brooklyn CB12, Brooklyn CB14, and Brooklyn CB15 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 44, District 46, and District 48, and Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 48, and AD 59. Data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 22?
      Cars and trucks—including SUVs, sedans, trucks, and buses—caused the most harm, with 25 deaths and over 700 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no pedestrian deaths but dozens of injuries. Bikes caused no deaths and a handful of injuries. Source: NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and holding repeat dangerous drivers accountable can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, support bills that target repeat dangerous drivers, and fund street redesigns that protect people walking and biking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Sutton misses committee vote on school speed zone safety bill.
      ▸

      Senate passes S 8344. School speed zone rules in New York City get extended. Lawmakers make technical fixes. The bill keeps pressure on drivers near schools. Streets stay a little safer for kids.

      Bill S 8344, titled 'Extends provisions and makes technical corrections to school speed zones in NYC; repealer,' moved through the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. Sponsored by Senator Andrew Gounardes, the bill passed Senate votes on June 12 and June 13, and cleared the Assembly on June 17. The measure extends and corrects school speed zone laws in New York City, repealing outdated provisions. Gounardes led the push, with strong support from most Senate Democrats and a split Assembly.       S 4045
      Sutton misses committee vote on bill improving road safety for all.
      ▸

      Senate backs S 4045. Repeat speeders face forced installation of speed assistance tech. Eleven points or six camera tickets triggers action. Law targets reckless drivers. Streets may get safer for those outside the car.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on June 11 and June 12, 2025. The bill, titled 'Relates to requiring the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices for repeated violation of maximum speed limits,' mandates these devices for drivers who rack up eleven or more points in 24 months, or six speed or red light camera tickets in a year. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving. Senators including Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, and others voted yes. The bill targets drivers with a pattern of violations, seeking to cut risk for pedestrians and cyclists by limiting repeat speeding.
