      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 22?

      Sutton’s Silence, Brooklyn’s Dead—Who Pays for His Absence?

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed headline duplication, tightened prose, cleaned citations, and ensured all links and attributions are correct.)

      SD 22: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      Blood on the Crosswalks

      In Senate District 22, the bodies keep coming. Eleven dead in the last year. Twenty-two left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. The old, the young, the ones just trying to cross the street. In March, a mother and her two daughters were killed in the crosswalk on Ocean Parkway. In July, a 90-year-old man was struck by a masked moped rider who fled the scene. A witness said the rider hit the man and left. The man died in the hospital. The street remembers. The families do not forget.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the last twelve months, 1,358 people have been injured in crashes here. Three of the dead were children. The old are not spared. Cars and SUVs do most of the killing—18 deaths, 633 minor injuries, 227 moderate injuries, 12 serious injuries—but mopeds, motorcycles, trucks, and even taxis leave their own scars. The numbers are not just numbers. They are the cost of inaction, paid in flesh and bone. NYC crash data

      Leadership: Present or Absent?

      State Senator Sam Sutton missed key votes on bills that would have made streets safer. On June 12, Sutton was absent for the committee vote on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the law. The bill passed committee, but Sutton’s absence is a silence that echoes. The families of the dead do not get to be absent. They show up at the hospital, at the morgue, at the empty chair at dinner.

      The Call: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Senator Sutton. Demand he show up for the living. Demand he fight for the dead. Tell him: every missed vote is another name on a stone. Every delay is a child who does not come home. The time for silence is over. The time for action is now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, including those that affect traffic safety, and is made up of elected senators from districts across the state. See the Stop Super Speeders Act for an example.
      ▸ Where does SD 22 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 48 and assembly district AD 41. See NYC crash data for more.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 22?
      It includes the Borough Park, Mapleton-Midwood (West), Midwood, Gravesend (East)-Homecrest, Madison, Sheepshead Bay-Manhattan Beach-Gerritsen Beach, Marine Park-Plumb Island, Brooklyn CB12, Brooklyn CB14, and Brooklyn CB15 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 44, District 46, and District 48, and Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 48, and AD 59. See NYC crash data for details.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 22?
      Cars and SUVs were responsible for 18 deaths and hundreds of injuries. Motorcycles, mopeds, trucks, and taxis caused additional deaths and injuries, while bikes were involved in a smaller number of incidents. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with better street design, lower speeds, and stronger enforcement. They are not random—they are the result of choices and policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and support laws that lower speed limits, crack down on repeat dangerous drivers, and fund safer street designs. Showing up for key votes and pushing for enforcement that protects the most vulnerable is essential.

      S 4045
      Sutton misses committee vote on bill improving road safety for all.
      ▸

      Senate backs S 4045. Repeat speeders face forced installation of speed assistance tech. Eleven points or six camera tickets triggers action. Law targets reckless drivers. Streets may get safer for those outside the car.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on June 11 and June 12, 2025. The bill, titled 'Relates to requiring the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices for repeated violation of maximum speed limits,' mandates these devices for drivers who rack up eleven or more points in 24 months, or six speed or red light camera tickets in a year. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving. Senators including Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, and others voted yes. The bill targets drivers with a pattern of violations, seeking to cut risk for pedestrians and cyclists by limiting repeat speeding.
