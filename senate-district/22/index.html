      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 22?

      No More Waiting: Blood in the Crosswalk, Silence in Albany

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, tightened prose, deduped and cleaned citations.)

      SD 22: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      Death in the Crosswalk

      In Senate District 22, the street is a gauntlet. On July 8, a 90-year-old man tried to cross Avenue U at East 14th. A blue moped hit him. The rider stopped, looked, then fled. The man died in the hospital. His grandson pleaded, “We hope the authorities can find the person who did this. We are also concerned about the motorcycles running rampant in crowded residential areas.”

      The day before, a 70-year-old man was struck by a Smart Car while crossing Avenue I. He died at Maimonides. The driver stayed. No charges were filed. The street stayed the same.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      In the last 12 months, 11 people died and 1,366 were injured in crashes here. Three of the dead were children. Forty-four people suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Cars and SUVs did most of the killing—18 deaths—but mopeds, motorcycles, and trucks all left bodies and broken lives behind. NYC crash data

      Leadership: Action or Silence?

      State Senator Sam Sutton represents this district. Albany passed new laws: speed cameras renewed, the city allowed to lower speed limits. But the carnage continues. Assemblyman Michael Novakhov, after the moped killing, said, “This can be avoided because if we would require any vehicles, any motorized vehicles, moving over a speed of 25 mph to be registered, to be insured, it would be much easier to find.” Sutton’s record on these measures is not in the news. The silence is loud.

      What Comes Next

      Every crash is preventable. Every delay is a choice. Call Senator Sutton. Demand he back laws that slow cars, stop repeat offenders, and hold reckless drivers to account. Tell him: you want action, not condolences. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is the cost of waiting.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for making and passing state laws. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on legislation that affects all residents. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Where does SD 22 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 48 and assembly district AD 41. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 22?
      It includes the Borough Park, Mapleton-Midwood (West), Midwood, Gravesend (East)-Homecrest, Madison, Sheepshead Bay-Manhattan Beach-Gerritsen Beach, Marine Park-Plumb Island, Brooklyn CB12, Brooklyn CB14, and Brooklyn CB15 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 44, District 46, and District 48, and Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, AD 48, and AD 59. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 22?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 18 deaths and hundreds of injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in several deaths and dozens of injuries; bikes were involved in injuries but no deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows these deaths and injuries are preventable with better laws, safer streets, and real enforcement. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, and require real accountability for reckless drivers. They can push for street redesigns that protect people, not just cars. NYC Open Data
