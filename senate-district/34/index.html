      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 34?

      No More Names on Asphalt: Bronx Streets Are Killing Us

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 16, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      SD 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      Blood on the Parkway

      The Bronx does not sleep. The engines do not rest. In Senate District 34, the road keeps taking. In the last twelve months, 12 people died and 1,165 were injured in crashes. Eighteen were left with wounds so deep they are called serious. The numbers are not just numbers. They are fathers, daughters, neighbors.

      Just weeks ago, a 31-year-old man rode his motorcycle down Pelham Parkway. A Chevrolet SUV turned left. The crash was sudden. He died at the hospital. The driver stayed. There was no arrest. The police are still investigating. “The Equinox driver stayed at the scene, and both she and Martinez were transported by EMS to Jacob Medical Center, where Martinez died from severe internal injuries.”

      On the Hutchinson River Parkway, an 18-year-old woman was thrown from a moped at 4 a.m. She landed on the asphalt. The driver fled. She was left fighting for her life. “Police said the moped driver sped away from the 3:45 a.m. crash… leaving the young woman to die on the road.”

      The Old and the Young

      The violence does not spare the old or the young. In the past year, two children died. Two people over 65 died. The street does not care how old you are. It only cares that you are in its path.

      SUVs and cars are the main killers. They caused 9 deaths and over 445 injuries to pedestrians and cyclists. Trucks and buses, motorcycles, mopeds, bikes—they all play their part. But the weight of the harm is steel and speed.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Senator Nathalia Fernández has voted yes on bills to curb repeat speeders and extend school speed zones. She backed the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force the worst offenders to install speed-limiting devices. She voted to protect children near schools. These are steps. But the blood on the road says it is not enough.

      Call to Action: Demand More

      Twelve dead in a year. Hundreds maimed. The road is not safe. Call Senator Fernández. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement. Demand streets where a child can cross without fear. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York State.
      ▸ Where does SD 34 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 13 and assembly district AD 82.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 34?
      It includes the Soundview-Bruckner-Bronx River, Soundview-Clason Point, Castle Hill-Unionport, Parkchester, Westchester Square, Throgs Neck-Schuylerville, Pelham Bay-Country Club-City Island, Hart Island, Ferry Point Park-St. Raymond Cemetery, Morris Park, and Bronx CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 13 and District 18, and Assembly Districts AD 82, AD 85, AD 87, AD 88, and AD 91.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 34?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians, with 9 deaths and 466 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 13 incidents. Bikes were involved in 11 incidents. Trucks and buses were involved in 41 incidents.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and real enforcement save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, support automated enforcement, and push for safer street designs that protect people walking and cycling.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Fernandez votes yes to extend school speed zones, improving child pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Senate passes S 8344. School speed zone rules in New York City get extended. Lawmakers make technical fixes. The bill keeps pressure on drivers near schools. Streets stay a little safer for kids.

      Bill S 8344, titled 'Extends provisions and makes technical corrections to school speed zones in NYC; repealer,' moved through the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. Sponsored by Senator Andrew Gounardes, the bill passed Senate votes on June 12 and June 13, and cleared the Assembly on June 17. The measure extends and corrects school speed zone laws in New York City, repealing outdated provisions. Gounardes led the push, with strong support from most Senate Democrats and a split Assembly. The bill's technical fixes aim to keep protections in place for children and other vulnerable road users near schools. No safety analyst note was provided.

      S 4045
      Fernandez votes yes in committee, boosting street safety by curbing repeat speeders.
      ▸

      Senate backs S 4045. Repeat speeders face forced installation of speed assistance tech. Eleven points or six camera tickets triggers action. Law targets reckless drivers. Streets may get safer for those outside the car.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on June 11 and June 12, 2025. The bill, titled 'Relates to requiring the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices for repeated violation of maximum speed limits,' mandates these devices for drivers who rack up eleven or more points in 24 months, or six speed or red light camera tickets in a year. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving. Senators including Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, and others voted yes. The bill targets drivers with a pattern of violations, seeking to cut risk for pedestrians and cyclists by limiting repeat speeding.
