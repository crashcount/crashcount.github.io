      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Pennsylvania and Hegeman: one man down, the street keeps moving

      SD 19: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 19, 2025

      Just before 10 AM on Sep 14, a driver hit a man at Pennsylvania and Hegeman in East New York; police later recorded unsafe speed and unsafe lane changing, and the driver was unlicensed (NYC Open Data; ABC7).

      In the past month, he was one of many hurt here. Since Jan 1, 2022 in Senate District 19, 37 people have been killed and 10,988 injured in traffic crashes (NYC Open Data).

      This Week

      • Sep 14: At Pennsylvania Avenue and Hegeman Avenue, a driver hit a 71‑year‑old man; police cited unsafe speed and lane change, and recorded the driver as unlicensed (NYC Open Data; ABC7).
      • Aug 28: At Remsen Avenue and Avenue J, a driver in an SUV hit a person on a bike; police noted driver inattention and inexperience (NYC Open Data).

      Pennsylvania Avenue keeps taking people

      On Jan 24, 2025, at Pennsylvania and Blake, a driver making a left turn failed to yield and killed a 57‑year‑old woman who was crossing with the signal (NYC Open Data). The corner is wide. The walk signal was not enough.

      Two weeks ago, a man stood by the curb at Pennsylvania and Hegeman. Debris covered the street after. Police marked speed as a factor (ABC7; NYC Open Data).

      The toll does not let up

      Year to date, crashes in this district total 2,856, with 2,119 injured and 8 killed; over the same stretch last year, crashes were 3,168, injuries 2,126, and deaths 4 (NYC Open Data). Fewer wrecks. More funerals.

      People walking are not spared. Since 2022, drivers of sedans and SUVs are tied to most pedestrian deaths and injuries in this district’s records; trucks and buses trail far behind in these counts (NYC Open Data).

      Who answers for this

      There is a bill in Albany to force the worst repeat speeders to slow down. The Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045) would require intelligent speed assistance for drivers with a pattern of violations; State Senator Roxanne Persaud voted yes in committee on May 20 and again on Jun 12 (Open States).

      Your Assembly Member here is Nikki Lucas. The record provided to us does not list her as a sponsor. The crashes keep coming. So do the left turns, the speed, the crosses of Pennsylvania Avenue.

      Lowering speeds on local streets is also on the table. City leaders already have tools to set safer limits; they have begun to use them, but the deaths in this district show the gap between promise and pavement (NYC Open Data).

      Act so the next man at the curb gets to go home. Take one minute today: tell your lawmakers to pass the speed‑limiter bill and back safer speed limits.

      Jun 12 - Senate backs S 4045. Repeat speeders face forced installation of speed assistance tech. Eleven points or six camera tickets triggers action. Law targets reckless drivers. Streets may get safer for those outside the car.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on June 11 and June 12, 2025. The bill, titled 'Relates to requiring the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices for repeated violation of maximum speed limits,' mandates these devices for drivers who rack up eleven or more points in 24 months, or six speed or red light camera tickets in a year. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving. Senators including Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, and others voted yes. The bill targets drivers with a pattern of violations, seeking to cut risk for pedestrians and cyclists by limiting repeat speeding.
