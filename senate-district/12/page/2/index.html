      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 12?

      Astoria’s Streets Run Red—Speed Kills, Leaders Stall

      Written August 13, 2025

      SD 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 13, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Recent Toll

      Three men died in Astoria last Tuesday. An 84-year-old driver sped through a stop sign at 42nd Street and 19th Avenue, hitting two men standing by a food truck. The car was going about 60 miles an hour. “It was catastrophic. It just wiped out everything. He must have been gassed to the floor.” The force severed a foot. The truck owner survived by a step. “My truck is totaled, but I still have my life. I’m just grateful that something moved me out of the way.”

      In the last 12 months, SD 12 saw 5 deaths and 27 serious injuries on its streets. Over 2,000 people were hurt. The dead include the old and the young. The numbers keep coming. The pain does not end.

      Patterns of Harm: Who Pays the Price

      Cars and SUVs do the most damage. In this district, they killed 5, seriously hurt 12, and injured hundreds more. Trucks and buses killed 5. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 1. Bikes killed 1. The bodies pile up, but the story is the same: speed, steel, and a moment’s loss.

      Leadership: What Gianaris Has Done—and What Comes Next

      State Senator Michael Gianaris has moved. He co-sponsored and voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed-limiting devices. He backed dedicated bike and pedestrian paths on the Queensboro Bridge after years of delay. He called for stronger penalties for reckless drivers. But the carnage continues. Promises and votes are not enough. Streets remain deadly.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Senator Gianaris. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph default speed limit. Demand the city and state act now, not after the next body. Every day of delay is another family broken.

      Cars and SUVs killed 5 and seriously injured 12. Trucks and buses killed 5 and seriously injured 2. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 1 and seriously injured 1. Bikes killed 1 and seriously injured 2. Source
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and holding repeat dangerous drivers accountable can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, require speed-limiting devices for repeat offenders, and redesign streets to protect people walking and biking.

      Half Of NYC Taxis Now Wheelchair Accessible
      ▸

      Half the city’s taxis now take wheelchairs. Ten years of pressure forced change. The fleet stands as the most accessible in the nation. Policy, not luck, drove this. Disabled riders gain ground. Streets shift. Access grows.

      amNY reported on June 5, 2025, that New York City’s Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) reached a milestone: 50% of active taxis are now wheelchair accessible. TLC Commissioner David Do said, “We made that today. It is the most accessible fleet in the entire country.” This achievement follows a decade-long push by disability advocates and a 2013 settlement requiring half the medallion fleet to be accessible. The transition faced delays, including the COVID-19 pandemic and medallion owner financial struggles. Policy changes, such as alternating accessible vehicles and funding support, drove the shift. The article highlights systemic progress, not individual driver actions, and underscores the impact of sustained advocacy and regulatory pressure.

      Michael Gianaris Welcomes Safety Boosting Queensboro Bridge Bike Pedestrian Split
      ▸

      Queensboro Bridge now splits bikes and walkers. Years of shared chaos end. Cyclists get the north lane. Pedestrians get the south. Advocates fought for this. Delays angered many. Michael Gianaris welcomes the change. Safety for vulnerable users finally comes to the bridge.

      On May 13, 2025, New York City completed a long-stalled plan to separate cyclists and pedestrians on the Queensboro Bridge. Gianaris also criticized the unexplained delays. The plan ends years of conflict and danger for vulnerable road users forced to share a narrow lane. The change follows similar improvements on the Brooklyn Bridge in 2021.

      Gianaris Backs Safety Boosting Fare Free Bus Funding
      ▸

      A new study finds fare-free buses would speed up rides by 12 percent and draw 169 million more riders yearly. Faster boarding slashes dwell times. Riders save time. Streets get safer, air gets cleaner. The city must weigh the cost—and the gains.

      On April 14, 2025, a policy analysis by Charles Komanoff examined the impact of fare-free buses in New York City. The report, covered by Streetsblog NYC, found that eliminating fares would cut bus trip times by 12 percent and boost annual ridership by 169 million, a 23 percent jump. The study states: 'Each dollar of city government support would produce more than two dollars worth of benefits for residents, primarily by removing the burden of fare payments while providing faster and more reliable bus service.' Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani and State Senator Michael Gianaris, both of Queens, have pushed the MTA to consider free buses. Komanoff’s analysis shows that faster buses mean less time waiting, cleaner air, fewer crashes, and a small but real drop in car trips. The report adds fuel to the debate over city funding for free bus service.
