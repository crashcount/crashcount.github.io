      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 12?

      No More Dead Kids: Streets Are Killing, Leaders Are Stalling

      Written July 18, 2025

      SD 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Human Cost

      In Senate District 12, the streets do not forgive. Five people have died in the last year. One was a child. Another was seventy-five. In the same period, 1,651 people were hurt—nineteen of them so badly their lives will never be the same. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old, walking or riding. They only rise.

      Just this spring, a 7-year-old girl was left with a broken femur and a head wound outside her school. The driver did not have a license. The car jumped the curb and took her down. “Three people in the path of the sedan were struck, including the 7-year-old girl, who also suffered a head injury,” reported police. The sidewalk was no shield.

      In October, a cyclist was run down and killed in Astoria. The driver was fleeing police. She was coming home from a ride. “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. The street swallowed her whole.

      Who Bears the Risk

      Cars and trucks do the most harm. In three years, they killed 5, hurt 550, and left 11 with serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds took 1 life and injured 19 more. Bikes killed 1, injured 16. The numbers are not equal, but the pain is. Each crash is a family broken, a life cut short, a name turned into a number.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Miles to Go

      State Senator Michael Gianaris has moved. He co-sponsored and voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit. He pushed for more speed cameras near schools. He stood with families who lost loved ones. But the blood keeps flowing. The law is not yet enough. The streets are not yet safe.

      Act: Demand More Than Words

      Call your leaders. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real penalties for repeat offenders. Demand streets where a child can walk to school and come home alive.

      Every day we wait, another family waits for a knock at the door.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 12. Source
      ▸ Where does SD 12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 30. Source
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 12?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Sunnyside, Woodside, Sunnyside Yards (South), Calvary & Mount Zion Cemeteries, Elmhurst, Maspeth, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22, District 25, District 26, and District 30, and Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 34, AD 37, and AD 38. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 12?
      Cars and trucks killed 5 and injured 550, with 11 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 1 and injured 19. Bikes killed 1 and injured 16.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Policies like lower speed limits, better street design, and enforcement against repeat offenders can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can also push for safer street designs and hold agencies accountable.

      Gianaris Opposes Harmful Delay of Queensboro Bridge Path
      ▸

      Seven lawmakers demand Mayor Adams open the Queensboro Bridge pedestrian path. The project sits finished. Cyclists and walkers still cram into a narrow, crash-prone lane. Delays keep thousands at risk. City Hall stalls. Advocates plan protest. Danger lingers.

      On April 9, 2025, seven elected officials—including Council Members Julie Won and Julie Menin—sent a public letter demanding Mayor Adams open the long-promised Queensboro Bridge pedestrian path. The Department of Transportation had planned to open the dedicated walkway on March 16, but the mayor's office intervened, citing the need for a briefing. The lawmakers wrote, 'The reasons given for this delay are not satisfactory, as all communications from the DOT have indicated that the project is complete and ready to open to the public.' They warned, 'Any further delays to this project that is otherwise ready to open will unnecessarily put at risk the thousands of New Yorkers who cycle and walk the current shared path every day.' The project, in the works since at least 2017, remains stalled. Cyclists and pedestrians are forced to share a cramped, hazardous lane. City Hall insists on more review, while advocates plan a protest ribbon-cutting.
