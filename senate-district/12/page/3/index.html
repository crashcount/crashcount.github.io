      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 12?

      Blood on the Blacktop: How Many More Must Die Before We Act?

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 12, the numbers do not lie. Thirty-three dead. Sixty-eight seriously hurt. Over 5,300 injured. These are not just numbers. They are people—children, elders, neighbors—struck down on streets meant to carry them home. In the last year alone, four people died and 17 suffered serious injuries. The disaster does not slow.

      Just months ago, a cyclist named Amanda Servedio was killed in Astoria. She was riding home from a cycling event. A pickup, fleeing police, ran her down at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. A witness said, “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.” witness described the crash. Her father said, “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood.” her father criticized the police pursuit.

      A 7-year-old girl was crushed outside her school by an unlicensed driver. Unlicensed driver struck children. A 94-year-old woman was pinned beneath a postal van in a crosswalk USPS van pinned elderly woman. The old and the young, the careful and the quick, all at risk. No one is safe while cars rule the street.

      Who Pays the Price

      SUVs and cars do the most harm. They killed five and seriously hurt eleven in the last three years. Trucks and buses killed four. Motorcycles and mopeds killed one. Bikes killed one. The street does not care who you are. It only matters who is behind the wheel.

      Leadership: Steps and Stalls

      State Senator Michael Gianaris has taken some steps. He voted yes on a bill to require safer street designs supported S 9718. He co-sponsored bills for advanced vehicle safety tech and fare-free buses. He called for stronger penalties for repeat reckless drivers, saying registrations should be suspended for those who endanger others called for stronger penalties. He pushed for the long-delayed Queensboro Bridge pedestrian path to open, warning that delays “unnecessarily put at risk the thousands of New Yorkers who cycle and walk the current shared path every day” warned about Queensboro Bridge delays.

      But the deaths keep coming. The bills are not enough. The streets are not safe. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call your leaders. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real penalties for repeat reckless drivers. Demand streets built for people, not cars.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The disaster is slow, but it is relentless. Only action will stop it.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 12.
      ▸ Where does SD 12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 30.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 12?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Sunnyside, Woodside, Sunnyside Yards (South), Calvary & Mount Zion Cemeteries, Elmhurst, Maspeth, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22, District 25, District 26, and District 30, and Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 34, AD 37, and AD 38.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 12?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm, with 5 deaths and 11 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 4. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 1. Bikes killed 1. These numbers are from 2022 to mid-2025 in SD 12, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Many could be prevented with lower speed limits, safer street design, and real penalties for reckless drivers.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, safer street designs, and stronger penalties for repeat offenders. They can also push for infrastructure that protects people walking and biking.
