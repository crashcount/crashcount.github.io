      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 12?

      Queens Bleeds: Safe Streets Now, Not After the Next Funeral

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Toll in Queens: Lives Lost, Streets Unforgiving

      In Senate District 12, the numbers do not lie. Five people killed, nineteen left with serious injuries, and over 1,600 hurt in crashes in the last year alone (NYC Open Data). The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The living carry scars—broken bones, lost work, empty chairs at dinner.

      Just months ago, a cyclist named Amanda Servedio was struck and killed at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She was coming home from a ride. A pickup, fleeing police, hit her and kept going. A witness described the horror: “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.” Her father blamed the police chase: “It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened.”

      A seven-year-old girl was crushed outside her school on 35th Avenue. She left with a broken femur and a head wound. The driver was unlicensed. The street was busy with children. Police charged the driver with reckless endangerment, reckless driving, and driving without a license.

      Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and cars do the most harm. In three years, they killed five, seriously injured eleven, and hurt hundreds more. Trucks and buses killed four. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes also left bodies and broken lives. The old, the young, the ones on foot or on wheels—they pay the price for streets built for speed.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Senator Michael Gianaris has backed some steps. He voted for complete street design changes and pushed for fare-free buses, which can cut car use and make streets safer. He called for stronger penalties for repeat reckless drivers. He stood with families after children died and demanded new signals and crosswalks. But the blood still runs. The streets are not safe. The laws are slow. The deaths keep coming.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a policy failure. Call Senator Gianaris. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real penalties for repeat offenders. Demand streets where children can walk home alive.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 12 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does SD 12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 30.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 12?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Sunnyside, Woodside, Sunnyside Yards (South), Calvary & Mount Zion Cemeteries, Elmhurst, Maspeth, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22, District 25, District 26, and District 30, and Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 34, AD 37, and AD 38.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 12?
      Cars and trucks killed 5 and seriously injured 11. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 1 and seriously injured 3. Bikes killed 1 and seriously injured 2.NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Every crash is preventable. These are failures of policy, design, and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, pass laws to penalize repeat reckless drivers, and push for enforcement that protects people walking and biking.

      Seven-Car Pileup on BQE Fueled by Speed
      ▸

      Seven cars crashed on the Brooklyn Queens Expressway. One flipped. Metal screamed. A 21-year-old man bled from his arm, half-ejected. Two others hurt. Speed drove the chaos. The road did not forgive. The city counted the wounded.

      Seven vehicles collided eastbound on the Brooklyn Queens Expressway. According to the police report, 'Seven cars collided. One flipped. Metal shrieked. A 21-year-old man, half-thrown from his seat, bled from his arm. He stayed awake. Speed was the cause. Speed, again.' Three people were injured: a 21-year-old male driver suffered severe bleeding and partial ejection; a 28-year-old female front passenger and a 53-year-old female driver both reported pain and nausea. The police report lists 'Unsafe Speed' as the contributing factor for both primary vehicles. No other driver errors or helmet or signal issues were cited. The crash left twisted metal and pain in its wake.

      SUV Slams Parked Truck on Booth Street
      ▸

      A Ford SUV crashed into a parked tractor-trailer on Booth Street. The driver’s leg was crushed. He stayed conscious, belted in. The truck did not move. Night air held the sound. Metal met metal. The street did not forgive.

      A Ford SUV struck the rear of a parked tractor-trailer on Booth Street in Queens. According to the police report, the SUV driver’s leg was crushed in the crash. He remained conscious and was wearing a lap belt and harness. The tractor-trailer was stationary and unoccupied at the time. The police report lists 'Driver Inattention/Distraction' and 'Traffic Control Disregarded' as contributing factors. No pedestrians or cyclists were involved. Other occupants in the SUV were not seriously hurt. The impact was severe, crushing the driver’s lower leg. The report makes clear: driver error and disregard for traffic control led to this violent collision.
