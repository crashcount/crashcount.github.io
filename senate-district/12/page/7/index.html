      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 12?

      No More Bodies in the Road: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Senate District 12, the violence does not stop. In the last twelve months, four people died and 1,498 were injured in traffic crashes. Seventeen suffered serious injuries—lives changed in a moment, bones broken, futures lost. Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians are struck down on streets meant to carry them home.

      Just months ago, a cyclist named Amanda Servedio was killed in Astoria. She was riding home from a cycling event. A man in a pickup, fleeing police, ran her down. A witness said, “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.” NY Daily News. Her father’s words cut deeper: “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened.” NY Daily News.

      A few blocks away, an unlicensed driver reversed onto a sidewalk outside a school. A seven-year-old girl was left with a broken femur and a head wound. Her classmate and a man were also hit. The driver was charged, but the pain remains New York Post.

      The Numbers Behind the Names

      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm: 5 deaths, 536 minor injuries, 143 moderate, 11 serious. Trucks and buses killed 4, injured 36, and left 2 with serious wounds. Motorcycles and mopeds, 1 death, 18 minor injuries, 9 moderate, 1 serious. Even bikes killed 1 and injured 25. The numbers do not lie. The danger is everywhere.

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      Senator Michael Gianaris has backed bills for safer streets. He voted yes on a law to force “complete street” design, making roads safer for all Open States. He pushed for fare-free buses, which can pull cars off the road Open States. He called for tougher penalties on repeat reckless drivers Streetsblog NYC. But the blood on the street says it is not enough. The city still allows high-speed chases in crowded neighborhoods. The most dangerous drivers keep their keys.

      The Call

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Senator Gianaris. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand an end to reckless chases. Demand action before another child is left bleeding on the curb.

      Do not wait for another name on the list.

      ▸

      Seven cars crashed on the Brooklyn Queens Expressway. One flipped. Metal screamed. A 21-year-old man bled from his arm, half-ejected. Two others hurt. Speed drove the chaos. The road did not forgive. The city counted the wounded.

      Seven vehicles collided eastbound on the Brooklyn Queens Expressway. According to the police report, 'Seven cars collided. One flipped. Metal shrieked. A 21-year-old man, half-thrown from his seat, bled from his arm. He stayed awake. Speed was the cause. Speed, again.' Three people were injured: a 21-year-old male driver suffered severe bleeding and partial ejection; a 28-year-old female front passenger and a 53-year-old female driver both reported pain and nausea. The police report lists 'Unsafe Speed' as the contributing factor for both primary vehicles. No other driver errors or helmet or signal issues were cited. The crash left twisted metal and pain in its wake.

      SUV Slams Parked Truck on Booth Street
      ▸

      A Ford SUV crashed into a parked tractor-trailer on Booth Street. The driver’s leg was crushed. He stayed conscious, belted in. The truck did not move. Night air held the sound. Metal met metal. The street did not forgive.

      A Ford SUV struck the rear of a parked tractor-trailer on Booth Street in Queens. According to the police report, the SUV driver’s leg was crushed in the crash. He remained conscious and was wearing a lap belt and harness. The tractor-trailer was stationary and unoccupied at the time. The police report lists 'Driver Inattention/Distraction' and 'Traffic Control Disregarded' as contributing factors. No pedestrians or cyclists were involved. Other occupants in the SUV were not seriously hurt. The impact was severe, crushing the driver’s lower leg. The report makes clear: driver error and disregard for traffic control led to this violent collision.
