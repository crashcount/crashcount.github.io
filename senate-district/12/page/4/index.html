      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 12?

      Astoria morning. Food, then the roar. Three dead.

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Astoria, curbside breakfast. Then the roar.

      Just after 8:30 AM, an 84-year-old in a Corolla going north on 42nd Street struck two men by a food truck at 19th Avenue, then hit another car. Both pedestrians died. The driver died. Police said the car “careened at a high speed into a nearby food truck, ramming into two men.” Gothamist reported the time and toll. amNY published that quote.

      “Someone screamed,” the food truck owner said. “The car must have been going 50, 60 miles an hour.” He told the Daily News. “My truck is totaled, but I still have my life.” Same source.

      “It should have been me,” said the son of one victim. “It shouldn’t have been my dad.” New York Post.

      “I have never seen anything like this,” a witness said. amNY.

      The men killed were named by police as Joaquin Venancio-Mendez, 41, and Santiago Baires, 70. amNY. Law enforcement sources told reporters the driver had a stroke two weeks earlier and was advised by a doctor not to drive. amNY.

      This district’s ledger

      A 55-year-old motorcyclist died on Woodhaven Blvd at 60th Drive on Apr 5, 2025. NYC Open Data (CrashID 4803498).

      Names, ages, corners. The list keeps its own time.

      Chases, corners, and a bridge that stays shut

      In the 114th Precinct, Astoria, high-speed police chases climbed. Policy says stop when risk outweighs reward; enforcement failed. “Drivers who are repeatedly caught by speed and red-light cameras currently face no consequences other than a fine in the mail. Repeat reckless drivers must be held to account,” Comptroller Brad Lander said. Streetsblog.

      On the Queensboro Bridge, a safe path was ready to open. City Hall stalled. “The reasons given for this delay are not satisfactory,” lawmakers wrote. “Any further delays… will unnecessarily put at risk” the people who walk and bike there now. Streetsblog.

      The lanes stay wide. The bodies are soft.

      Slow the cars. Stop the worst.

      Lower the citywide default speed limit. Mandate speed limiters for repeat offenders. Albany has a bill to force intelligent speed assistance on drivers with patterns of violations. See S4045.

      One step you can take now: push the city to slow streets and rein in repeat speeders. Take action.

Their plan includes expanded Fair Fares, new discounts for seniors and disabled riders, and a weekly CityTicket for unlimited intracity rail travel. Advocates and lawmakers argue these steps are critical for equity and access, especially as the city prepares to charge drivers entering Manhattan below 60th Street. The proposals now enter negotiations with Governor Hochul.
