      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 12?

      No More Dead Kids: Demand Action on Queens’ Killer Streets

      Written July 7, 2025

      SD 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Human Cost in SD 12

      In Senate District 12, the numbers do not lie. Four people killed. Seventeen left with serious injuries. In the last twelve months, 1,490 neighbors have been hurt in 2,469 crashes. The dead include a 16-year-old girl, a 28-year-old woman on a bike, and a 75-year-old man in the back seat of a taxi. The youngest injured is seven. The oldest, ninety-four. No one is spared.

      On June 26, a sedan making a left turn struck and killed a 16-year-old girl crossing at 46th Street and 47th Avenue. She died at the intersection, her life ended by a car with a clear view obstructed. NYC Open Data records the loss. Days before, a cyclist was killed in Astoria when a pickup, fleeing police, ran her down. “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. Amanda Servedio was coming home from a cycling event. She never made it back.

      Children are not safe. In April, an unlicensed driver spun out of control outside a school in Astoria, crushing a seven-year-old girl’s leg and injuring two others. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment and driving without a license. The sidewalk is no refuge. “Three people in the path of the sedan were struck, including the 7-year-old girl, who also suffered a head injury,” police said.

      Leadership: Progress and Pressure

      State Senator Michael Gianaris has voted for and sponsored bills to make streets safer. He backed the Complete Streets bill and co-sponsored measures for advanced vehicle safety technology and fare-free buses. He called for stronger penalties for repeat reckless drivers, saying registrations should be suspended for those who rack up violations. But the carnage continues. The tools exist. The pace is slow.

      What Next: Demand Relentless Action

      Every day of delay means another family shattered. Call Senator Gianaris. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real consequences for repeat offenders. Demand streets built for people, not just cars. Do not wait for another child’s name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 12. See the Complete Streets bill for an example.
      ▸ Where does SD 12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 30. See the Complete Streets bill for legislative context.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 12?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Sunnyside, Woodside, Sunnyside Yards (South), Calvary & Mount Zion Cemeteries, Elmhurst, Maspeth, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22, District 25, District 26, and District 30, and Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 34, AD 37, and AD 38. See the Complete Streets bill for more.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 12?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 693 crashes involving SUVs and cars and 60 involving trucks and buses. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 29 crashes, while bikes were involved in 28. See NYC Open Data for details.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Patterns repeat: left turns, speeding, unlicensed drivers, and dangerous streets. Proven policies can prevent them.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, pass laws to hold repeat offenders accountable, and ensure enforcement targets the most dangerous behavior.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Gianaris Applauds DOT Steps to Improve Street Safety
      ▸

      Over 200 Astoria residents packed a DOT workshop after a spike in traffic deaths. Cyclists and pedestrians have died. Drivers speed, double-park, and ignore signals. Councilwoman Cabán and others demand urgent action. DOT vows to return with a safety plan.

      On September 14, 2023, the Department of Transportation held a public street safety workshop in Astoria, Queens, following a surge in traffic violence. The event, covered on September 18, 2023, drew over 200 residents and was organized by Western Queens elected officials. The workshop addressed a 'significant increase in traffic-related deaths, including several high-profile fatalities involving cyclists and pedestrians.' Councilwoman Tiffany Cabán, State Senator Kristen Gonzalez, and Assemblymember Zohran Mamdani called the deaths preventable and demanded urgent changes. Cabán stated, 'If it saves lives, it’s worth doing.' DOT officials, including Queens Borough Commissioner Nicole Garcia and senior program manager Kyle Gorman, presented plans for 31st Avenue and collected resident feedback on dangerous driving, lack of protected bike lanes, and unsafe intersections. The DOT pledged to return with a proposal. No formal bill number or committee was cited; the event focused on immediate community engagement and systemic change.
