      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 12?

      Another Body on the Asphalt—When Will Queens Streets Stop Killing Us?

      Written July 16, 2025

      SD 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A cyclist, Amanda Servedio, was killed last fall. She was riding home from a cycling event when a pickup, fleeing police, ran her down at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She flew thirty feet. She did not scream. “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.” The driver left her in the street. Her father said they were upset police didn’t stop the chase: “It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened.”

      A few months later, an unlicensed driver reversed into a box truck, spun onto the sidewalk outside a school, and hit three people. A seven-year-old girl broke her femur. A fourteen-year-old limped away. A man was bruised. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment and driving without a license. Three people in the path of the sedan were struck, including the 7-year-old girl, who also suffered a head injury.

      In the last twelve months, SD 12 saw 5 deaths and 19 serious injuries from crashes. There were 2,673 crashes and 1,657 injuries. The bodies pile up. The numbers do not lie.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 5 deaths, 11 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds killed and maimed too. Bikes killed one, seriously injured two. No one is safe. Not the old, not the young. Not on the sidewalk, not in the crosswalk.

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      State Senator Michael Gianaris has spoken for safety. He co-sponsored and voted for the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit. He called for stronger penalties for repeat reckless drivers. He pushed for safer crossings after a child was killed. He demanded the city open the Queensboro Bridge pedestrian path. But the deaths keep coming. The streets are not safe. The laws are not enough. The city moves slow. The blood dries fast.

      What You Can Do

      Call your leaders. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real penalties for repeat offenders. Demand streets built for people, not cars.

      Every day you wait, someone else will not come home. Act now. Take action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 12.
      ▸ Where does SD 12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 30. Source
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 12?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Sunnyside, Woodside, Sunnyside Yards (South), Calvary & Mount Zion Cemeteries, Elmhurst, Maspeth, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22, District 25, District 26, and District 30, and Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 34, AD 37, and AD 38. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 12?
      Cars and trucks caused 5 deaths and 11 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 1 death and 3 serious injuries. Bikes caused 1 death and 2 serious injuries. Source
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and real accountability for dangerous driving.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, pass laws to stop repeat dangerous drivers, and demand street redesigns that protect people walking and biking.

      Michael Gianaris Supports Safety Boosting Fare Free Bus Expansion
      ▸

      Lawmakers push to triple fare-free bus lines. Fifteen routes, more buses, faster rides. Focus on low-income neighborhoods. The move aims to shift New Yorkers from cars to transit. Congestion pricing looms. Riders want speed, reliability, and access.

      Senator Michael Gianaris introduced the 'Congestion Pricing Done Right' bill on March 4, 2024. The bill, now before the legislature, seeks to expand New York City's fare-free bus pilot from five to fifteen lines, with three in each borough. The measure promises $45 million for better bus reliability and frequency, targeting low-income and disadvantaged communities. The bill summary states: 'expand and extend New York City's fare-free bus pilot program.' Gianaris, as sponsor, aims to boost mass transit before congestion pricing takes effect. Supporters, including Assemblymember Zohran Mamdani, Riders Alliance, and the Transport Workers Union, stress that affordable, frequent buses are vital for safety and equity. MTA Chair Janno Lieber backs more service but warns that bus lanes lag behind. The bill focuses on routes that can handle more buses without new infrastructure, aiming to make public transit a real alternative to cars.
