      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 12?

      Names, Not Numbers: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Toll in SD 12: Names Become Numbers

      The streets of Senate District 12 do not forgive. In the past year, 4 people died and 1,491 were injured in crashes. Seventeen of those injuries were serious—lives changed in a blink. Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians all paid the price. A 16-year-old girl was killed crossing at 46th Street and 47th Avenue. A cyclist, Amanda Servedio, was struck and killed while returning from a ride. She “went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. The driver fled. The family waits for justice.

      No one is safe. In April, an unlicensed driver spun onto the sidewalk outside a school, breaking a 7-year-old girl’s femur and injuring two others. “Bah was arrested and charged with reckless endangerment, reckless driving and driving without a license,” reported the press.

      Leadership: Progress and Pressure

      Senator Michael Gianaris has taken steps. He voted for the Complete Streets bill, pushing for safer street design. He co-sponsored bills for fare-free buses and advanced vehicle safety tech. He called for stronger penalties for repeat reckless drivers and demanded action after children died at dangerous crossings. “We fix Newtown Road where this happened,” he pledged.

      But the carnage continues. Bills and promises do not slow a speeding car. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program has expired. Repeat offenders still roam the streets. The city installs traffic lights after children die, not before.

      The Next Fight: Action, Not Excuses

      Every crash is preventable. The blood on the street is not fate. It is policy. It is delay. It is silence. Call Senator Gianaris. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real penalties for repeat offenders. Demand streets that put people first.

      Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for making and passing laws. Senators represent districts across the state and vote on bills that affect all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 30.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 12?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, Sunnyside, Woodside, Sunnyside Yards (South), Calvary & Mount Zion Cemeteries, Elmhurst, Maspeth, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22, District 25, District 26, and District 30, and Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 34, AD 37, and AD 38.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 12?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 693 incidents, 5 deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 29 incidents, 1 death. Bikes were involved in 28 incidents, 1 death. (Source: NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Every crash is preventable. The numbers are not fate—they are the result of choices, policies, and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, pass and enforce laws against repeat dangerous drivers, fund street redesigns, and support policies that protect people walking and cycling.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
