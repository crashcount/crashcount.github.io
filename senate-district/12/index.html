      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 12?

      Blood on 31st Avenue: No More Dead Kids, No More Excuses

      Written June 25, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      Five dead. Seventeen seriously hurt. In the last year, Senate District 12 saw 2,492 crashes. The bodies are not numbers. A cyclist, Amanda Servedio, was struck and killed in Astoria by a pickup fleeing police. She was coming home from a ride. She had the right of way. A witness described the impact: “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.”

      A 7-year-old girl was hit outside her school by an unlicensed driver. She left with a broken femur and a head wound. The car jumped the curb. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment and driving without a license. Three people were struck.

      The old and the young. Cyclists, walkers, children. The street does not care.

      Who Pays? Who Acts?

      Cars and trucks do the killing. In this district, they caused the most deaths and injuries to pedestrians. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes also took lives, but the numbers are smaller. The street is a gauntlet. The most vulnerable pay the price.

      Senator Gianaris: Action and Silence

      Senator Michael Gianaris has taken steps. He voted yes on a bill to force safer street design statewide. He backed penalties for repeat reckless drivers. He called for suspending registrations for the worst offenders. He supported the push to open a safe path on the Queensboro Bridge, ending years of delay. Gianaris praised the completion of new pedestrian paths: “After years of advocacy, many of us were excited to see [the city’s Department of Transportation] complete the work to open new, dedicated pedestrian paths on the Queensboro Bridge.”

      But the blood on the street says it is not enough. The laws are slow. The deaths are fast.

      What Next? No More Waiting

      Every day, another family waits for a call that never comes.

      Call Senator Gianaris. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real penalties for repeat speeders. Demand streets built for people, not cars.

      Do not wait for another child’s shoe in the road. Do not wait for another ghost bike.

      Act now.

      Half Of NYC Taxis Now Wheelchair Accessible
      ▸

      Half the city’s taxis now take wheelchairs. Ten years of pressure forced change. The fleet stands as the most accessible in the nation. Policy, not luck, drove this. Disabled riders gain ground. Streets shift. Access grows.

      amNY reported on June 5, 2025, that New York City’s Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) reached a milestone: 50% of active taxis are now wheelchair accessible. TLC Commissioner David Do said, “We made that today. It is the most accessible fleet in the entire country.” This achievement follows a decade-long push by disability advocates and a 2013 settlement requiring half the medallion fleet to be accessible. The transition faced delays, including the COVID-19 pandemic and medallion owner financial struggles. Policy changes, such as alternating accessible vehicles and funding support, drove the shift. The article highlights systemic progress, not individual driver actions, and underscores the impact of sustained advocacy and regulatory pressure.

      On May 13, 2025, New York City completed a long-stalled plan to separate cyclists and pedestrians on the Queensboro Bridge.       Gianaris Backs Safety Boosting Fare Free Bus Funding
      ▸

      A new study finds fare-free buses would speed up rides by 12 percent and draw 169 million more riders yearly. Faster boarding slashes dwell times. Riders save time. Streets get safer, air gets cleaner. The city must weigh the cost—and the gains.

      On April 14, 2025, a policy analysis by Charles Komanoff examined the impact of fare-free buses in New York City. The report, covered by Streetsblog NYC, found that eliminating fares would cut bus trip times by 12 percent and boost annual ridership by 169 million, a 23 percent jump. The study states: 'Each dollar of city government support would produce more than two dollars worth of benefits for residents, primarily by removing the burden of fare payments while providing faster and more reliable bus service.' Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani and State Senator Michael Gianaris, both of Queens, have pushed the MTA to consider free buses. Komanoff’s analysis shows that faster buses mean less time waiting, cleaner air, fewer crashes, and a small but real drop in car trips. The report adds fuel to the debate over city funding for free bus service.
