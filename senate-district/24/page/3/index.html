      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Evening blood on Hylan

      SD 24: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 24, 2025

      Early evening at Hylan Boulevard and Sharrott Avenue, a 73‑year‑old driver was recorded with head trauma and severe bleeding. Police logged driver inattention at the scene. Source.

      This Week

      • At Hylan and Sharrott, an eastbound sedan going straight left a 73‑year‑old driver injured, with severe bleeding noted by responders. Source

      The toll does not let up

      Since Jan 1, 2022 in Senate District 24, crashes have killed 38 people and injured 5,810 more. Source.

      In the past 12 months alone: 9 dead, 1,762 injured, 18 seriously hurt. Source.

      This year to date: 5 dead, 1,259 injured, 14 seriously hurt. Source.

      Staten Island names, Staten Island streets

      Hylan Boulevard keeps coming up. On Mar 7, 2025, Chaosheng Wu, 80, was hit while crossing at Benton Avenue and later died, police said. Officials were still reviewing speed and signals. Gothamist.

      Last year and the year before, people walking were killed at New Dorp Lane, Jefferson Avenue, and Forest Avenue. Police listed driver inattention and failure to yield in several of those deaths. NYC Open Data.

      Power and choices

      The state renewed New York City’s 24‑hour school‑zone speed‑camera program in June. Senator Andrew Lanza voted no. Streetsblog NYC. The Assembly did the same; Assembly Member Sam Pirozzolo also voted no. Streetsblog NYC. In 2022 debate on expanding cameras to 24/7, Lanza dismissed the program as a “cash register.” Streetsblog NYC.

      A bill to curb repeat speeders by requiring speed‑limiters after repeated violations advanced this session. Senator Lanza voted yes in committee on June 11, then no the next day. Open States.

      City leaders send mixed messages too. At a Staten Island event crushing illegal mopeds, the mayor said, “The rise of the moped era … has terrorized many of our pedestrians,” and linked those vehicles to robberies. amNY. The machines were smashed. The bigger killers on these streets remain cars, SUVs, and trucks. NYC Open Data.

      What will actually stop the bleeding?

      Sammy’s Law gives the city power to lower speeds. The city can set more 20 MPH zones now. The cameras are alive around the clock. The Legislature has a bill to fit repeat offenders’ cars with speed‑limiters. Our Take Action. Open States. The votes from local lawmakers are on record. Streetsblog NYC.

      One more evening on Hylan. One more person hurt, or dead. It does not have to continue.

      Take one step: push for lower default speeds and mandatory limiters for repeat speeders — start here.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where is this?
      New York State Senate District 24 on Staten Island. It covers neighborhoods including Great Kills–Eltingville, Annadale–Huguenot–Prince’s Bay–Woodrow, and parts of others, and overlaps Council Districts 49, 50, and 51.
      ▸ What changed with speed cameras this year?
      In June 2025, state lawmakers reauthorized New York City’s 24‑hour school‑zone speed‑camera program. Senator Andrew Lanza and Assembly Member Sam Pirozzolo voted no, according to Streetsblog NYC.
      ▸ What is the Stop Super Speeders bill?
      Senate bill S4045 would require installation of intelligent speed‑assistance devices after repeated violations. Senator Andrew Lanza voted yes in committee on June 11 and no the next day, per the state bill record.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We analyzed NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets for crashes, persons, and vehicles, filtered to Senate District 24 and the period Jan 1, 2022–Sep 24, 2025. We used injury severity fields to count deaths, injuries, and serious injuries. Data were accessed Sep 24, 2025. You can view the base datasets here, with linked Persons and Vehicles tables.
