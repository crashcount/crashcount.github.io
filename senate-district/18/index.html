      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 18?

      Streets Run Red. City Shrugs. Demand Action Now.

      Written August 8, 2025 (Revised August 8, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 18: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 8, 2025

      Blood on Broadway, Silence on Morgan Avenue

      Just last week, a 47-year-old man was struck and dragged more than fifty feet at Broadway and Suydam. The driver fled. Police found the man dead in the street. No name released. No arrest. The car kept going. The city kept moving.

      On Morgan Avenue, the bodies keep coming. Three years. Three deaths. Trucks and cars cut through the only north-south route in North Brooklyn. Cyclists and pedestrians cross at their own risk. “I was sad and angry at the same time because I still feel that these are things that can be prevented,” said Juan Ignacio Serra. The city has not acted.

      The Numbers: Relentless and Rising

      In the last 12 months in SD 18:

      • 14 people killed
      • 1,905 injured
      • 40 seriously hurt

      Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors. The living carry scars.

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. Trucks and buses killed five. Motorcycles and mopeds, too. No one is safe when the street is built for speed.

      Leadership: Votes, Words, and Waiting

      State Senator Julia Salazar has voted for bills to curb repeat speeders and extend school speed zones. She co-sponsored the push for speed limiters on dangerous drivers. She signed on to the fight for congestion pricing and for e-bike access for working New Yorkers. “When the federal government falls short, it really does fall on the state to step up and take responsibility,” Salazar said.

      But the streets are still killing. Promises and votes do not stop trucks from turning or cars from fleeing. The city drags its feet on redesigns. The blood dries. The danger stays.

      Call to Action: Demand More, Demand Now

      Call Senator Salazar. Call your council member. Call the mayor.

      Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand real barriers, not broken promises. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect everything from traffic safety to education.
      ▸ Where does SD 18 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 37 and assembly district AD 53.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 18?
      It includes the Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Bushwick (West), Bushwick (East), The Evergreens Cemetery, Cypress Hills, Highland Park-Cypress Hills Cemeteries (South), Highland Park-Cypress Hills Cemeteries (North), and Brooklyn CB4 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 32, District 33, District 34, and District 37, and Assembly Districts AD 38, AD 50, AD 53, and AD 54.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 18?
      Cars and SUVs caused most injuries to pedestrians in SD 18. Trucks and buses killed five pedestrians. Motorcycles and mopeds caused additional injuries. Bicycles were involved in 36 pedestrian injuries, but no pedestrian deaths.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speed limits, and enforcement against dangerous driving. They are not random acts of fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, support automated enforcement against repeat offenders, and demand street redesigns that put people before cars.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      DOT Stalls on Meeker Avenue Bike Lane
      ▸

      Meeker Avenue’s bike lane lies unfinished. Signals missing. Markings faded. Trucks block crossings. Eleven killed or hurt. DOT delays. Cyclists and pedestrians left exposed under the BQE.

      Streetsblog NYC (2025-07-18) reports that the Department of Transportation has failed to complete a protected bike lane under the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway, despite years of known danger. From 2014 to 2020, eleven people were killed or severely injured on Meeker Avenue. The lane remains incomplete, with 'unsignalized intersections, incomplete lane markings, a dangerously designed right-of-way, and an overall lack of proper maintenance.' Trucks block crossings, and drivers stop in bike paths. DOT has not added the route to the official bike map, citing safety concerns. The agency’s inaction leaves a critical corridor unsafe for vulnerable road users.
