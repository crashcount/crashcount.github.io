      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Four crashes in two weeks, one dead on Morgan Avenue. The toll keeps climbing in SD 18.

      SD 18: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 12, 2025

      Just after 3 AM at Cypress Ave and Troutman St on Aug 30, a driver hit a 32‑year‑old on a bike. Police records list head wounds and severe cuts (NYC Open Data).

      This Week

      • Aug 18, Varet St at White St: a driver in an SUV hit a 30‑year‑old crossing outside an intersection; he bled badly but was conscious (NYC Open Data).
      • Aug 17, near 965 Flushing Ave: a driver turned right and a 22‑year‑old on a bike was ejected and in shock (NYC Open Data).
      • Aug 6, Morgan Ave at Maspeth Ave: a box‑truck driver going straight killed a 46‑year‑old man in the roadbed (NYC Open Data).

      Morgan Avenue: three years, three deaths

      A truck driver killed a pedestrian at Morgan and Maspeth on Aug 6. It was the third life taken on Morgan Avenue in three years, and still no redesign. “I was very frustrated that nothing has been done in more than three years since Daniel Vidal was killed,” said Juan Ignacio Serra (Streetsblog NYC).

      The same report quotes a federal transportation official on what happens when homes and bikes meet trucks on old industrial streets: “we’re unfortunately seeing a lot of collisions with cyclists and trucks” (Streetsblog NYC).

      A hit‑and‑run at Broadway and Suydam

      Just before dawn on Aug 2, a driver headed north on Broadway hit a 47‑year‑old and dragged him more than 50 feet. He died there. Detectives are still looking for the driver (Gothamist). Early reports suggested a garbage truck; police had not confirmed the vehicle when the story ran (NY Daily News).

      The toll in SD 18

      Since Jan 1, 2022, SD 18 has recorded 12,026 crashes, 6,211 injuries, and 27 deaths (NYC Open Data).

      Year to date, crashes rose to 2,370 from 1,994 last year. Injuries climbed to 1,375 from 1,035. Deaths increased to 10 from 6. Serious injuries doubled to 29 from 14 (NYC Open Data).

      What leaders have done — and haven’t

      State Senator Julia Salazar co‑sponsored and voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act (S 4045) in June 2025 (Open States). The bill would force repeat violators to use speed‑limiting tech. It passed committee votes on June 11–12 (Open States).

      Assembly Member Maritza Davila represents much of this area. The record here does not show her as a sponsor of the Assembly companion. What gives?

      Council Member Sandy Nurse and DOT can also act. Under Sammy’s Law, NYC can set safer default speeds. Our detailed brief lays out the case for a citywide 20 MPH limit and curbing repeat speeders — and how to press them to do it now (Take Action).

      Next step

      Lower speeds save lives. Habitual speeders can be stopped. Tell City Hall to set 20 MPH and tell Albany to pass speed limiters for repeat offenders. Start here.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How many crashes and deaths has SD 18 seen in this period?
      From Jan 1, 2022 to Sep 12, 2025, SD 18 recorded 12,026 crashes, 6,211 injuries, and 27 deaths, based on NYC Open Data’s crash, person, and vehicle tables.
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We pulled the NYC Open Data crash datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4), filtered to Senate District 18 and the window 2022-01-01 to 2025-09-12, and rolled up counts of crashes, injuries, serious injuries, and deaths. You can view the base datasets here. Data accessed Sep 12, 2025.
      ▸ What changed on Morgan Avenue?
      According to Streetsblog, a box‑truck driver killed a pedestrian near Sharon St on Aug 6, 2025 — the third death on Morgan Avenue in three years. Advocates and local officials have pushed the city to add a protected bike lane and crossings. The city has not delivered a redesign yet.
      ▸ What can stop repeat dangerous driving?
      State bill S 4045 would require intelligent speed assistance devices for drivers with repeated violations. Senator Julia Salazar co‑sponsored and voted yes in June 2025, according to Open States. The Assembly must pass its companion before it can take effect.

      ▸

      Aug 23 - Lawmakers say Hochul broke the law. They filed a brief. They argue the governor has no right to halt congestion pricing. The MTA Board, not Albany, holds the power. The pause sows confusion. Streets stay clogged. Vulnerable New Yorkers pay the price.

      On August 23, 2024, a group of New York State legislators—Assembly Members Phil Steck and Andrew Hevesi, State Senator Julia Salazar, and former Assembly Member Dick Gottfried—filed a legal brief against Governor Hochul’s decision to pause congestion pricing. The matter, as described: “the legislature never gave her or any governor the power to do away with the traffic toll.” The brief cites the 2019 Traffic Mobility Act, arguing only the Traffic Mobility Review Board and the MTA can make such decisions. The lawmakers warn that letting the governor override the MTA would “make impossibly opaque the actual responsibility for MTA decisions.” Their action supports the MTA Board’s independence and opposes executive interference. No safety analyst has assessed the direct impact on vulnerable road users, but the legislators’ stance highlights the risk of political meddling in life-and-death street policy.
