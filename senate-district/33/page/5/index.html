      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 33?

      SD 33: Streets That Keep Taking

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Recent killings

      On Aug. 11, police said a Mercedes hit two men on scooters on the Bronx River Parkway; both suffered fatal injuries “struck two men on scooters, who then suffered fatal injuries”. On June 28, a multivehicle crash sent five to the hospital; police said the driver fled “injured several pedestrians”. On May 10, police searched for a driver who hit and killed a pedestrian and left “after striking and killing a pedestrian”.

      A road rage case turned deadly on June 16. “An arrest was made after Keino Campbell, 27, was shot and killed over the weekend in New York City,” said Erica Lunsford.

      Open data logs another loss: Jan. 2, 2024, a moped and sedan collision at Mosholu Parkway and Bainbridge Ave left one person dead (CrashID 4692380).

      The toll

      Since 2022 in SD 33, 19 people are dead, 68 suffered serious injuries, and 4,632 were hurt in 7,839 crashes (district totals). The bodies and the broken bones say what policy has not.

      Rivera’s record

      State Sen. Gustavo Rivera co‑sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act (S 4045) and voted yes in committee in June 2025 (bill page). The bill would require speed‑limiting tech for drivers with repeated violations.

      He also co‑sponsored S 1675 to create a pedestrian safety rating for vehicles (bill page). In 2023, powerful institutions tried to halt Fordham Road bus fixes; the pressure to delay safety was real (Fordham Rd letter).

      What to push now

      The pattern is clear. Lower the NYC default speed limit. Pass and enforce speed limiters for repeat speeders. Reward the yes votes. Demand passage and rollout that sticks.

      Act

      Tell City Hall and Albany to slow the streets and stop the worst drivers. Start here: take action. Do it now. The road won’t wait.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      It’s the state’s upper house. Senators pass statewide laws with the Assembly, then the Governor signs or vetoes. Committees vet bills; floor votes decide them. Local safety tools like speed limiters move only when Albany acts.
      ▸ Where does SD 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 11 and assembly district AD 80.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 33?
      It includes the Mount Hope, Fordham Heights, Belmont, Bedford Park, Norwood, Riverdale-Spuyten Duyvil, Pelham Parkway-Van Nest, Morris Park, Allerton, and Bronx CB6, with overlaps in Council Districts District 11, District 13, District 14, District 15, and Assembly Districts AD 78, AD 80, AD 81, AD 86.
      ▸ Which politicians should I contact to fix things in SD 33?
      Your local contacts include Council Member Eric Dinowitz and Assembly Member John Zaccaro. For state action, contact State Senator Gustavo Rivera. Use our take action page to get started.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 33?
      Cars and Trucks: 850 cases total, 7 deaths, 20 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 51 total, 0 deaths, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 24 total, 0 deaths, 1 serious injury. (2022–Aug. 2025).
      ▸ What does it take to change traffic laws here?
      Real change takes city and state action. Street fixes need support from Council Member Eric Dinowitz, a City Council vote, and the mayor’s signature. Automated tools and penalties (like speed limiters in S 4045) need passage by the State Assembly and Senate and the Governor’s signature. Local officials can press, but they can’t act alone.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
