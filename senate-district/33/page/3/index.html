      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 33?

      No More Names: End the Killing on Bronx Streets

      Written August 12, 2025 (Revised August 12, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      SD 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 12, 2025

      The Death Toll Grows

      In Senate District 33, the numbers do not lie. Nineteen people killed. Sixty-six left with serious injuries. Over 4,500 hurt since 2022. The dead do not get a second chance. The wounded carry scars that do not heal. Most victims are not in cars. They are walking, riding, or crossing. They are children, elders, neighbors.

      Just weeks ago, a driver struck and killed two men on scooters on the Bronx River Parkway. Police made an arrest, but the loss is permanent. Police said a Mercedes sedan driver struck two men on scooters, who then suffered fatal injuries. The story is always the same. The car keeps moving. The bodies do not.

      Hit-and-runs are routine. In May, a pedestrian was killed and the driver fled. Police are searching for a driver who allegedly fled after striking and killing a pedestrian in the Bronx early Saturday morning. The city shrugs. The street is washed clean. But the blood remains.

      The Policy Response: Rivera’s Record

      Senator Gustavo Rivera has not been silent. He voted yes on bills to curb repeat speeders and extend school speed zones. He co-sponsored a bill to rate cars for pedestrian safety. In June, he backed the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force the worst repeat offenders to install speed limiters. He voted yes in committee, boosting street safety by curbing repeat speeders.

      But the crisis does not wait for Albany. The streets demand more. Cameras catch speeders, but the city’s default speed limit is still too high. The most dangerous drivers keep driving. The most vulnerable keep dying.

      The Next Step: Demand Action Now

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a policy choice. The numbers are not fate. They are the result of laws, votes, and silence.

      Call Senator Rivera. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real consequences for repeat offenders. Join the fight. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 33 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does SD 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 11 and assembly district AD 80.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 33?
      It includes the Mount Hope, Fordham Heights, Belmont, Bedford Park, Norwood, Riverdale-Spuyten Duyvil, Pelham Parkway-Van Nest, Morris Park, Allerton, Woodlawn Cemetery, and Bronx CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 11, District 13, District 14, and District 15, and Assembly Districts AD 78, AD 80, AD 81, and AD 86.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 33?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians in SD 33, with 5 deaths and 18 serious injuries. Trucks and buses were involved in 2 deaths and 1 serious injury. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 1 serious injury but no deaths. Bikes were involved in 1 serious injury and no deaths.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Every crash is preventable. The numbers are the result of policy choices, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, pass and enforce laws against repeat dangerous drivers, and support street redesigns that protect people walking and biking.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      ▸

      Bronx business groups and local institutions want Mayor Adams to stop bus lane improvements on Fordham Road. They claim the changes will hurt traffic and business. Data shows most shoppers arrive by transit, walking, or biking—not by car. The city weighs next steps.

      On May 30, 2023, Bronx business leaders and institutions sent a letter opposing the Department of Transportation’s plan to expand bus lanes on Fordham Road. The matter, titled 'Bronx Business Leaders and Local Institutions Want to Halt Bus Fixes on Fordham Rd,' highlights their demand for Mayor Adams to halt improvements meant for 85,000 daily bus riders. The Belmont and Fordham Business Improvement Districts, along with the Bronx Zoo, New York Botanical Garden, Fordham University, Monroe College, and St. Barnabas Hospital, argue the changes would 'significantly impact crucial traffic flow' and burden side streets. State Sen. Gustavo Rivera voiced support for transit equity but awaits a traffic study. DOT claims to work with the community. Despite business concerns, advocates and city data show most shoppers use transit, walking, or biking, not cars. Similar fears about bus improvements hurting business have not come true elsewhere.
