      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 59?

      Eighteen Dead. No More Excuses.

      Written July 6, 2025

      SD 59: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 59, the numbers do not lie. Eighteen people killed. Thirty-six left with serious injuries. More than 3,700 hurt since 2022. The wounds are not abstract. They are broken bodies, empty beds, and families who will never be whole again. Just this spring, a 49-year-old man was killed crossing India Street in Greenpoint, struck by an unlicensed e-bike rider who ran a stop sign. The police found him bleeding in the road. He did not get up. NYC Open Data

      On 34th Avenue, a woman on a bike was hit and killed. On Bedford Avenue, a crash left a driver dead and a passenger with head injuries. The pattern is clear. Speed, inattention, and reckless driving keep taking lives.

      “Lowering the Speed Limit and Enforcing Real Accountability”

      The calls for change are not new. After a repeat speeding driver struck a woman in Greenpoint, local leaders spoke out. “Lowering the speed limit and enforcing real accountability for dangerous drivers are urgent steps we must take to protect New Yorkers,” said a joint statement from Senator Kristen Gonzalez and others.

      The same coalition demanded Albany pass laws to force the worst repeat offenders to slow down and to let the city set its own speed limits. They called for raised crosswalks and daylighting at deadly corners. “We’re calling on the New York State legislature to pass two critical bills that would lead to fewer incidents of traffic violence,” they said.

      Leadership: Progress and Pressure

      Senator Kristen Gonzalez has not been silent. She backed bills for complete street design and automated bike lane enforcement. She called for lower speed limits and better infrastructure after each new death. She co-sponsored laws to require advanced safety tech in cars and to keep drivers out of bike lanes. But the pace is slow. The bodies pile up while the bills wait.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Every death is preventable. The street does not care who you are. It only takes. Call Senator Gonzalez. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand enforcement against repeat speeders. Demand streets that protect the people who walk and ride. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 59.
      ▸ Where does SD 59 sit politically?
      It belongs to Queens, in City Council District 26 and Assembly District 37. See crash data
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 59?
      It includes Greenpoint, Williamsburg, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, United Nations, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Astoria Park, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts 2, 4, 22, 26, and 33, and Assembly Districts 36, 37, 50, and 74. See crash data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 59?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 1 death and over 600 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 42 injuries. Bikes were involved in 2 deaths and 50 injuries. See crash data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are not random. Lower speeds, better street design, and real enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass laws to lower speed limits, require safety tech in cars, fund safer street designs, and enforce rules against repeat dangerous drivers.
