      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 59?

      Another Body, Another Delay—How Many More Will Die Before They Act?

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 59: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 59, the numbers do not lie. Eighteen people are dead since 2022. Three thousand seven hundred twenty-one are injured. Thirty-six suffered wounds so grave they may never walk the same. The dead do not get a second chance. The living carry scars.

      Just this spring, a man was killed by an e-bike rider who ran a stop sign in Greenpoint. A cyclist died on 34th Avenue, struck by a truck. A 15-year-old boy fell from a subway train at Queensboro Plaza. His family never planned for a funeral. “We were blindsided. We never planned for a funeral for a child. They’re kids, they think they are having fun, they think it doesn’t affect other people but then it does,” said his mother.

      The violence is relentless. In the last 12 months alone, five more are dead. Over a thousand are hurt. Children, elders, cyclists, pedestrians. The street does not care who you are.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      State Senator Kristen Gonzalez has not been silent. She has called for lower speed limits, for speed control, for real enforcement. After a woman was killed by a repeat speeder in Greenpoint, Gonzalez joined others to demand two critical bills that would lead to fewer incidents of traffic violence—one to force repeat speeders to install speed-limiting devices, and another to let the city lower speed limits. “Lowering the speed limit and enforcing real accountability for dangerous drivers are urgent steps we must take to protect New Yorkers,” she said.

      Gonzalez has voted yes on bills to require safer street design and co-sponsored laws for automated bike lane enforcement and advanced vehicle safety tech. She backed the push for a 20 mph slow zone in Greenpoint and Williamsburg, and demanded the city open the Queensboro Bridge pedestrian path. But the pace of change is slow. The bodies keep coming.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every delay, every watered-down law, every blocked redesign means another mother, another child, another cyclist is left in the street. Call your lawmakers. Demand the 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat offenders. Demand streets built for people, not just cars.

      Do not wait for another funeral.
