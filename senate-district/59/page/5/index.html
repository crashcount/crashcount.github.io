      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 59?

      Still Bleeding: No More Waiting While Our Streets Kill

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, removed duplicate headline, cleaned and deduped citations, and ensured proper inline link formatting.)

      SD 59: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      The streets of Senate District 59 do not forgive. Since 2022, 19 people have died and 3,850 have been injured in crashes here. Forty-one were left with wounds so deep they are called serious injuries. The dead do not get second chances. The living carry scars.

      Last spring, a cyclist was killed on India Street in Greenpoint. A bike ran a stop, a man died. The city called it a statistic. His family called it a life. The record shows: “Severe bleeding” and “Apparent Death” for a man crossing the street, struck by a rider who did not stop, according to NYC Open Data.

      A few months before, a woman in Greenpoint was hit by a driver with 26 prior speed camera violations. She died. The driver kept his license until the day he killed. Officials said, “Lowering the speed limit and enforcing real accountability for dangerous drivers are urgent steps we must take to protect New Yorkers.”

      Who Pays, Who Acts

      The pain is not spread even. Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and trucks did the most harm: 628 pedestrian injuries, 5 deaths. Bikes killed 2, injured 53. Motorcycles and mopeds, 42 injuries, no deaths. The numbers do not lie. The city’s most vulnerable are left to fend for themselves.

      Senator Gonzalez: Votes, Words, and the Waiting

      Senator Kristen Gonzalez has not been silent. She co-sponsored the bill to force repeat speeders to install speed limiters. She voted yes. She called for lower speed limits and for the city to act. After the Greenpoint death, she said, “We’re calling on the New York State legislature to pass two critical bills that would lead to fewer incidents of traffic violence.”

      She backed the 20 mph slow zone for Greenpoint and Williamsburg. She signed the letter to open the Queensboro Bridge path for walkers and cyclists. She has voted for school speed zones. The bills are good. The deaths keep coming.

      What Now

      The street is still hungry. The law moves slow. The city waits. The dead do not wait. Call your council member. Call your senator. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed limiters for repeat offenders. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York. For more information, visit the official New York State Senate website.
      ▸ Where does SD 59 sit politically?
      It belongs to Queens, city council district 26, and assembly district 37, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 59?
      It includes Greenpoint, Williamsburg, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, United Nations, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Astoria Park, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts 2, 4, 22, 26, and 33, and Assembly Districts 36, 37, 50, and 74. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 59?
      Cars and trucks caused 5 deaths and 628 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 42 injuries. Bikes caused 2 deaths and 53 injuries, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and real accountability for dangerous drivers can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, require speed limiters for repeat offenders, and fund safer street designs that protect people walking and biking.
