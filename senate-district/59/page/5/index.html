      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 59?

      Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written July 28, 2025

      SD 59: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 28, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      Twenty people have died on the streets of Senate District 59 since 2022. Over 4,000 have been hurt. Forty-four suffered injuries so grave they may never walk the same. The numbers do not slow. In the last year alone, deaths more than doubled. Five dead, 734 injured, 15 left with wounds called “serious” by the city.

      Just last week, a firefighter died on the FDR Drive. He fell from his motorcycle. A car hit him and kept going. Police said, “he was struck by an unknown vehicle that did not remain on the scene” (ABC7). No arrest. No answers. The road is fast, the city is silent.

      A cyclist was struck on McGuinness Boulevard. The driver turned and hit him. There was no protected lane. “The fact is that this wouldn’t be a problem if Mayor Adams had just done what he had promised to do, which was, make all of McGuinness safe,” said a local advocate (Streetsblog NYC).

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and trucks caused 655 injuries or deaths to people on foot. Motorcycles and mopeds, 42. Bikes, 55. The city blames distraction. The city blames the victim. The dead cannot answer.

      What Has Senator Gonzalez Done?

      Senator Kristen Gonzalez has not stood still. She co-sponsored and voted for the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install devices that keep their cars from breaking the law (Open States). She backed the extension of school speed zones. She signed on to bills for automated bike lane enforcement and called for lower speed limits after deadly crashes. She joined other officials to demand the city open safe paths for walkers and cyclists and to back protected bike lanes in Astoria (Streetsblog NYC).

      But the blood on the street says it is not enough. Every day of delay is another family broken.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call Senator Gonzalez. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for every street. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The city will not save you unless you force its hand.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 59. For example, Senator Kristen Gonzalez represents SD 59 and has voted on key street safety legislation. Open States
      ▸ Where does SD 59 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 37. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 59?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, United Nations, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Astoria Park, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 4, District 22, District 26, and District 33, and Assembly Districts AD 36, AD 37, AD 50, and AD 74. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 59?
      Cars and trucks caused 655 injuries or deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 42. Bikes caused 55. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speed, lack of protection, and repeat offenders. These deaths and injuries can be prevented with better laws and safer streets.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes and pedestrian crossings, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable.

      Gonzalez co-sponsors bill boosting cyclist safety with automated bike lane enforcement.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 3304 aims to keep cars out of bike lanes. Photo devices would catch violators. Sponsors say it’s time to protect cyclists. The bill stands at the sponsorship stage.

      Senate bill S 3304, sponsored by Brad Hoylman-Sigal with co-sponsors Jabari Brisport, Kristen Gonzalez, Liz Krueger, and Julia Salazar, was introduced on January 30, 2023. The bill is in the sponsorship stage. It seeks to 'establish in the city of New York a bicycle lane safety program to enforce certain restrictions on the use of protected bicycle lanes by means of bicycle lane photo devices.' The measure targets drivers who block bike lanes, aiming to keep space clear for cyclists. No safety analyst note is available. The bill has not yet advanced to committee or vote.

      Gonzalez Blames Poor Street Design for Traffic Violence
      ▸

      A cement truck driver turned right and struck a woman on a Citi Bike in Astoria. She died at the scene, less than a mile from home. This marks the fourth cyclist killed in the area in under three years. Streets remain deadly.

      On January 5, 2023, a cement truck driver fatally struck 62-year-old Tamara Chuchi Kao as she rode a Citi Bike in Astoria, Queens. The driver turned right from 24th Avenue onto 29th Street, hitting Kao and killing her instantly. Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani called the crash 'absolutely heartbreaking,' noting Kao was the fourth cyclist killed in Astoria in 2.5 years. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez blamed 'poorly designed streets,' saying, 'traffic violence is the outcome of poorly designed streets and is a solvable problem.' The area has almost no protected bike lanes. In 2022, Community Board 1 saw 1,979 crashes, injuring 120 cyclists and 163 pedestrians. Politicians and residents demand safer streets, better enforcement, and an end to car dominance. The deadly toll continues.
