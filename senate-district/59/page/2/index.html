      Nineteen Dead, Still No Peace: Demand Action on Deadly Streets Now

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      SD 59: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      The streets of Senate District 59 do not forgive. Since 2022, 19 people have died in traffic crashes here. Forty-one more suffered serious injuries. In the last year alone, six people died, and over a thousand were hurt. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old. They only rise.

      A man is killed by a bus at an intersection. A cyclist is struck down by a turning truck. A pedestrian is left bleeding on the asphalt. The details change. The outcome does not. Every death is a family broken, a life ended for nothing.

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      Cars and trucks do most of the killing. They account for the largest share of deaths and injuries. But the danger is everywhere. Bikes, mopeds, buses—all can maim or kill. The city adds bike lanes, lowers speed limits, installs cameras. Still, the bodies pile up.

      After a woman was killed by a repeat speeder in Greenpoint, leaders called for action. “Lowering the speed limit and enforcing real accountability for dangerous drivers are urgent steps we must take to protect New Yorkers,” said a coalition including Senator Kristen Gonzalez (called for action). But the speeders keep coming. The crosswalks are still unprotected. The law moves slow. The cars move fast.

      Leadership: Progress and Pressure

      Senator Kristen Gonzalez has backed bills to curb repeat speeders and clear bike lanes. She voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force chronic violators to install speed limiters. She co-sponsored a bill for automated bike lane enforcement. She joined calls for lower speed limits and safer street design after fatal crashes.

      But laws on paper do not stop a car in the crosswalk. Advocates must keep the pressure on. Every delay means another name on the list. Every compromise is another mother left waiting at the hospital. “We’re calling on the New York State legislature to pass two critical bills that would lead to fewer incidents of traffic violence,” Gonzalez and others said after another death.

      Act Now: Demand More Than Words

      The crisis is not over. Call Senator Gonzalez. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed limiters for repeat offenders. Demand daylighted intersections and protected bike lanes. Do not wait for another funeral.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing state laws and overseeing statewide policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect everything from traffic safety to education.
      ▸ Where does SD 59 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 37.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 59?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, United Nations, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Astoria Park, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 4, District 22, District 26, and District 33, and Assembly Districts AD 36, AD 37, AD 50, and AD 74.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 59?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 3 deaths and over 600 injuries to pedestrians. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 42 crashes. Bikes were involved in 53 crashes, including 2 deaths.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, protected crossings, and real accountability for dangerous drivers can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, speed limiters for repeat offenders, protected bike lanes, and safer intersections. They can also push for better enforcement against reckless driving.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      ▸
