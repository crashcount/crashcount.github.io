      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 59?

      Another Body, Another Excuse—Time to End the Blood on Our Streets

      Written August 9, 2025

      SD 59: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 9, 2025

      The Toll, This Year and Every Year

      The streets of Senate District 59 do not rest. Since January 2022, 21 people have died and 4,135 have been injured in crashes here. Forty-five of those injuries were serious. In the last twelve months alone, eight people lost their lives. The dead do not get a second chance. The injured carry it with them.

      Just last week, a 65-year-old man rode his e-bike down Second Avenue. A 21-year-old, driving a Nissan Rogue without a license, struck him and fled. The cyclist was left with severe head trauma, “unconscious when paramedics arrived and they immediately started doing CPR and quickly put him in the ambulance” (Streetsblog NYC). The driver later told police he ran because “he did not have a license” (West Side Spirit).

      A week before, a firefighter died on the FDR Drive. He fell from his motorcycle and was struck by a car. The driver did not stay. The city lost a man who had once pulled others from fire. Now, his name is another line in a report.

      Patterns That Kill

      The numbers do not lie. Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 1 death, 475 minor injuries, 183 moderate injuries, 6 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 0 deaths, 32 minor injuries, 11 moderate. Bikes: 2 deaths, 37 minor, 13 moderate, 3 serious. The violence is not random. It is relentless.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Senator Kristen Gonzalez has not been silent. She co-sponsored and voted yes on a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed-limiting devices. She backed the push for a 20 mph slow zone in Greenpoint and Williamsburg, writing to DOT with other officials after a deadly year. She stood with local leaders to demand protected bike lanes in Astoria, even as businesses sued to stop them.

      But the blood keeps coming. The city and state move slow. Each delay is another family broken.

      What You Can Do—Now

      Call Senator Gonzalez. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed limiters for repeat offenders. Demand protected bike lanes and daylight at every corner.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The street will not wait. Neither should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect everything from traffic safety to education.
      ▸ Where does SD 59 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 37. Source
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 59?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, United Nations, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Astoria Park, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 4, District 22, District 26, and District 33, and Assembly Districts AD 36, AD 37, AD 50, and AD 74. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 59?
      Cars and trucks caused 1 death, 475 minor injuries, 183 moderate injuries, and 6 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths, 32 minor injuries, and 11 moderate injuries. Bikes caused 2 deaths, 37 minor injuries, 13 moderate injuries, and 3 serious injuries. Source
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Patterns repeat—speeding, unlicensed drivers, missing protections. Proven policies can prevent deaths, but only if leaders act.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, require speed limiters for repeat offenders, fund protected bike lanes, and demand rapid street redesigns that put people first.

      Gonzalez Supports Safety Boosting 20MPH Slow Zone Plan
      ▸

      Community Board 1 voted 18-15 to cut speed limits in Greenpoint and Williamsburg to 20 mph. Residents and officials demand action after deadly crashes. DOT has yet to respond. The fight pits safety against drivers. Lives hang in the balance.

      On March 6, 2025, Brooklyn Community Board 1 voted 18-15 to request a 20 mph speed limit for Greenpoint and Williamsburg. The measure, supported by 40 groups and nearly all local elected officials, marks the first neighborhood-wide slow zone request since the passage of Sammy's Law. The board's action follows letters from Sen. Kristen Gonzalez, Assembly Member Emily Gallagher, Council Member Jennifer Gutierrez, and U.S. Rep. Nydia Velazquez to DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. They cited CB1's high fatality rate—3.27 deaths per 10,000 residents, above the city average. The board's vote was contentious, with pro-driving members opposing the change. DOT spokesperson Mona Bruno acknowledged the support and promised more information on slow zones for outer boroughs. Council Member Lincoln Restler later joined the call for lower speeds. The measure now awaits DOT action.
