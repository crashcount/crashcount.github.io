      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 59?

      Death Is Winning on SD 59’s Streets—Who Will Stop the Bleeding?

      Written August 12, 2025 (Revised August 12, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      SD 59: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 12, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Crashes in SD 59

      Seven dead. Sixteen seriously hurt. In just the first eight months of this year, the streets of Senate District 59 have claimed more lives than last year—a 250% jump in deaths. The numbers do not flinch: since January 2022, 22 people have died and 45 have suffered life-altering injuries in 8,414 crashes. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, parents, children. On July 29, a 65-year-old man riding his e-bike on Second Avenue was struck by a 21-year-old unlicensed driver in a Nissan SUV. The driver fled, leaving the man with a shattered skull on the pavement. Police found him later. He told them he ran because he had no license. He now faces charges of leaving the scene of an accident that caused serious injury and driving without a license.

      The Pattern: Who Pays the Price

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. They caused 666 injuries or deaths to pedestrians here. Trucks and buses added 60 more. Motorcycles and mopeds, 43. Bikes, 55. The old, the young, the ones on foot or two wheels—they pay the price. The street does not care if you are careful. It only cares if you are exposed.

      Leadership: What Has Been Done, What Must Be Done

      State Senator Kristen Gonzalez has not been silent. She co-sponsored and voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit. She backed the push for a 20 mph slow zone in Greenpoint and Williamsburg, where the death rate is among the city’s worst. She joined calls to open the Queensboro Bridge pedestrian path and to defend protected bike lanes in Astoria, even as businesses sued to block them. The city, she said with others, must act: “Any further delays to this project that is otherwise ready to open will unnecessarily put at risk the thousands of New Yorkers who cycle and walk the current shared path every day.”

      But the blood keeps coming. The numbers keep rising. No law, no ribbon-cutting, no press release will bring back the dead.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call your senator. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand the Stop Super Speeders Act become law. Demand protected bike lanes and open bridges.

      Every day you wait is another day someone does not come home.

      Take action now.

      Gonzalez Supports Safety Boosting 20MPH Slow Zone Plan
      ▸

      Community Board 1 voted 18-15 to cut speed limits in Greenpoint and Williamsburg to 20 mph. Residents and officials demand action after deadly crashes. DOT has yet to respond. The fight pits safety against drivers. Lives hang in the balance.

      On March 6, 2025, Brooklyn Community Board 1 voted 18-15 to request a 20 mph speed limit for Greenpoint and Williamsburg. The measure, supported by 40 groups and nearly all local elected officials, marks the first neighborhood-wide slow zone request since the passage of Sammy's Law. The board's action follows letters from Sen. Kristen Gonzalez, Assembly Member Emily Gallagher, Council Member Jennifer Gutierrez, and U.S. Rep. Nydia Velazquez to DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. They cited CB1's high fatality rate—3.27 deaths per 10,000 residents, above the city average. The board's vote was contentious, with pro-driving members opposing the change. DOT spokesperson Mona Bruno acknowledged the support and promised more information on slow zones for outer boroughs. Council Member Lincoln Restler later joined the call for lower speeds. The measure now awaits DOT action.
