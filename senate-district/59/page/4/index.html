      Six Dead, 1,100 Broken—Time to End NYC’s Traffic Bloodletting

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      SD 59: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Human Cost

      Just last month, a cyclist was killed by a turning sedan on East 41st Street. A week before, a convertible struck a child crossing East 14th. In the last year alone, six people have died and over 1,150 have been injured in crashes across Senate District 59. Twenty-one suffered injuries so severe they may never fully recover. The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do.

      Patterns That Kill, Policies That Stall

      The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, children, parents. A 36-year-old woman, crushed by a pickup on 34th Avenue. A 49-year-old man, struck by a bus at 3rd Avenue and 28th Street. A pedestrian, a cyclist, a child—each erased in a moment. Cars and trucks caused the most harm, but buses, bikes, and mopeds all left bodies behind. The violence is relentless. It does not pause for policy.

      Leadership: Votes, Bills, and the Work Ahead

      State Senator Kristen Gonzalez has not been silent. She co-sponsored and voted for the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed limiters. She backed the extension of school speed zones. After a deadly crash in Greenpoint, she joined other officials to demand lower speed limits and real accountability for reckless drivers. Their words were plain: “Lowering the speed limit and enforcing real accountability for dangerous drivers are urgent steps we must take to protect New Yorkers.” demanded lower speed limits and accountability.

      But the work is not done. Speed kills. Delay kills. Every day without stronger laws and safer streets is another day someone does not come home.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call your lawmakers. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand passage of the Stop Super Speeders Act. Demand streets where a child can cross without fear.

      No more waiting. No more names turned into numbers. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Change it.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York, including SD 59. source
      ▸ Where does SD 59 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 37. source
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 59?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, United Nations, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Astoria Park, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 4, District 22, District 26, and District 33, and Assembly Districts AD 36, AD 37, AD 50, and AD 74. source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 59?
      Cars and trucks caused the most injuries and deaths, followed by motorcycles and mopeds, then bikes. Each group left people dead or hurt in the district. source
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and real accountability for dangerous driving. They are not random acts of fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable, and redesign streets to protect people walking and biking.

      ▸

      On July 18, 2023, Council Member Shahana Hanif and other Brooklyn officials called for 'equalized tolling on all crossings into Manhattan' as congestion pricing nears. Their letter to the Traffic Mobility Review Board states: 'No one community should disproportionately bear the burden of the cars and trucks passing through their neighborhood.' Politicians and residents demand safer streets, better enforcement, and an end to car dominance. The deadly toll continues.
