      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 59?

      Eighteen Dead—How Many More Before They Act?

      Written July 8, 2025

      SD 59: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 8, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 59, the numbers do not lie. Eighteen people dead. Thirty-six left with serious injuries. Over 3,700 hurt since 2022. The streets run through Greenpoint, Astoria, Murray Hill, and more. The pain is spread wide, but it lands hardest on the most vulnerable—pedestrians, cyclists, children.

      Just this spring, a 49-year-old man was killed crossing India Street in Greenpoint. The e-bike that struck him kept going. He died from severe bleeding, his body broken on the pavement. The crash report reads only: “Apparent Death” and “Severe Bleeding” (NYC Open Data).

      A few months earlier, a woman in Astoria was struck and killed while riding her bike. The truck kept moving. Her helmet could not save her. The record says: “Ejected. Apparent Death.”

      The violence is relentless. Cars, trucks, bikes, mopeds—all play their part. But the deadliest wounds come from the largest machines. Cars and trucks caused most of the deaths. Bikes and mopeds, too, have left families grieving.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      State Senator Kristen Gonzalez has not been silent. She has backed bills to force safer street design, to bring cameras to bike lanes, and to require new safety tech in cars. She voted yes on a law to make streets safer for everyone, not just drivers. She co-sponsored a bill to use cameras to keep cars out of bike lanes.

      After a repeat speeder left a woman dying in Greenpoint, Gonzalez joined others to demand “lowering the speed limit and enforcing real accountability for dangerous drivers”. She called for raised crosswalks, daylighting, and one-way conversions to protect people on foot and bike.

      But laws move slow. Streets change slower. Each day of delay is another day of risk. The city and state have the tools. They must use them now.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call Senator Gonzalez. Call your council member. Demand lower speed limits. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand action, not words. The blood on the street is not yet dry.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York.
      ▸ Where does SD 59 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 37.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 59?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, United Nations, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Astoria Park, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 4, District 22, District 26, and District 33, and Assembly Districts AD 36, AD 37, AD 50, and AD 74.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 59?
      Cars and Trucks: 1 death, 5 serious injuries, 605 total injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 42 injuries. Bikes: 2 deaths, 3 serious injuries, 50 injuries. (NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not random. They happen where streets are unsafe and laws are not enforced. Every crash is a policy failure.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, pass laws for safer street design, and demand real enforcement against reckless drivers.
