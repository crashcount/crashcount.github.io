      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 59?

      Death Is Winning on SD 59’s Streets—Who Will Stop the Bleeding?

      Written August 12, 2025 (Revised August 12, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      SD 59: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 12, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Crashes in SD 59

      Seven dead. Sixteen seriously hurt. In just the first eight months of this year, the streets of Senate District 59 have claimed more lives than last year—a 250% jump in deaths. The numbers do not flinch: since January 2022, 22 people have died and 45 have suffered life-altering injuries in 8,414 crashes. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, parents, children. On July 29, a 65-year-old man riding his e-bike on Second Avenue was struck by a 21-year-old unlicensed driver in a Nissan SUV. The driver fled, leaving the man with a shattered skull on the pavement. Police found him later. He told them he ran because he had no license. He now faces charges of leaving the scene of an accident that caused serious injury and driving without a license.

      The Pattern: Who Pays the Price

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. They caused 666 injuries or deaths to pedestrians here. Trucks and buses added 60 more. Motorcycles and mopeds, 43. Bikes, 55. The old, the young, the ones on foot or two wheels—they pay the price. The street does not care if you are careful. It only cares if you are exposed.

      Leadership: What Has Been Done, What Must Be Done

      State Senator Kristen Gonzalez has not been silent. She co-sponsored and voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit. She backed the push for a 20 mph slow zone in Greenpoint and Williamsburg, where the death rate is among the city’s worst. She joined calls to open the Queensboro Bridge pedestrian path and to defend protected bike lanes in Astoria, even as businesses sued to block them. The city, she said with others, must act: “Any further delays to this project that is otherwise ready to open will unnecessarily put at risk the thousands of New Yorkers who cycle and walk the current shared path every day.”

      But the blood keeps coming. The numbers keep rising. No law, no ribbon-cutting, no press release will bring back the dead.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call your senator. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand the Stop Super Speeders Act become law. Demand protected bike lanes and open bridges.

      Every day you wait is another day someone does not come home.

      Take action now.

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across the state and vote on bills that affect all New Yorkers. source
      ▸ Where does SD 59 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 37. source
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 59?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, United Nations, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Astoria Park, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 4, District 22, District 26, and District 33, and Assembly Districts AD 36, AD 37, AD 50, and AD 74. source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 59?
      Cars and trucks caused 666 injuries or deaths, motorcycles and mopeds 43, and bikes 55. The greatest harm comes from cars and trucks. source
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Patterns show repeat offenders and dangerous street designs, not random chance.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, support protected bike lanes, back bills like the Stop Super Speeders Act, and demand safer street design.

      Gonzalez votes yes to extend school speed zones, improving child pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Senate passes S 8344. School speed zone rules in New York City get extended. Lawmakers make technical fixes. The bill keeps pressure on drivers near schools. Streets stay a little safer for kids.

      Bill S 8344, titled 'Extends provisions and makes technical corrections to school speed zones in NYC; repealer,' moved through the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. Sponsored by Senator Andrew Gounardes, the bill passed Senate votes on June 12 and June 13, and cleared the Assembly on June 17.       S 4045
      Gonzalez votes yes in committee, boosting street safety by curbing repeat speeders.
      ▸

      Senate backs S 4045. Repeat speeders face forced installation of speed assistance tech. Eleven points or six camera tickets triggers action. Law targets reckless drivers. Streets may get safer for those outside the car.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on June 11 and June 12, 2025. The bill, titled 'Relates to requiring the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices for repeated violation of maximum speed limits,' mandates these devices for drivers who rack up eleven or more points in 24 months, or six speed or red light camera tickets in a year. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving. Senators including Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, and others voted yes. The bill targets drivers with a pattern of violations, seeking to cut risk for pedestrians and cyclists by limiting repeat speeding.
