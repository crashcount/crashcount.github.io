      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 59?

      No More Dead Ends: Demand Streets That Don’t Kill

      SD 59: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Crashes in SD 59

      The streets of Senate District 59 do not forgive. In the last twelve months, six people died and twenty-one suffered serious injuries in crashes here. The dead do not get second chances. The injured carry scars the rest of us cannot see. In all, there were 2,285 crashes and 1,167 injuries—each one a life bent or broken.

      A man was killed by a bus at E 28th and 3rd. A cyclist died on 34th Avenue. A pedestrian was struck down in Greenpoint. The numbers do not flinch: cars and trucks caused the most harm, but bikes and mopeds left their mark too. The disaster is slow, but it is relentless.

      Leadership: Words, Votes, and the Weight of Action

      State Senator Kristen Gonzalez has not been silent. She backed the call for a 20 mph slow zone in Greenpoint and Williamsburg, joining other officials to demand lower speeds after a string of deaths. She co-sponsored bills for automated bike lane enforcement and advanced vehicle safety tech. She voted yes on a law to force safer street design. When a repeat speeder struck a woman in Greenpoint, Gonzalez and others said: “Lowering the speed limit and enforcing real accountability for dangerous drivers are urgent steps we must take to protect New Yorkers.”

      But the work is not done. The city drags its feet on opening safe paths. Promises pile up. The dead do not wait.

      The Numbers That Do Not Lie

      Most victims are not in cars. In the last year, children, cyclists, and pedestrians paid the highest price. A single crash can end a life or leave a family shattered. The numbers are not just numbers. They are names, faces, and empty chairs at dinner tables.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Call your leaders. Demand action. Lower the speed limit. Build real protection for people on foot and on bikes. Enforce the laws against the drivers who do the most harm. Do not let another year pass with the same old blood on the street.

      Contact Senator Gonzalez. Contact your council member. Join the fight.

      The disaster is not fate. It is policy. It can be stopped.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect every resident.
      ▸ Where does SD 59 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 26 and assembly district AD 37. Data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 59?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, United Nations, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Astoria Park, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 4, District 22, District 26, and District 33, and Assembly Districts AD 36, AD 37, AD 50, and AD 74. Data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 59?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for the most harm to pedestrians, with 1 death and 5 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths and 0 serious injuries. Bikes caused 2 deaths and 3 serious injuries. Data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not fate. They are the result of policy choices, street design, and enforcement. The numbers show that change is possible.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, fund and build protected bike lanes, enforce laws against reckless drivers, and pass bills that prioritize the safety of people walking and cycling.

      Gonzalez Supports Safety Boosting 20MPH Slow Zone Plan
      ▸

      Community Board 1 voted 18-15 to cut speed limits in Greenpoint and Williamsburg to 20 mph. Residents and officials demand action after deadly crashes. DOT has yet to respond. The fight pits safety against drivers. Lives hang in the balance.

      On March 6, 2025, Brooklyn Community Board 1 voted 18-15 to request a 20 mph speed limit for Greenpoint and Williamsburg. The measure, supported by 40 groups and nearly all local elected officials, marks the first neighborhood-wide slow zone request since the passage of Sammy's Law. The board's action follows letters from Sen. Kristen Gonzalez, Assembly Member Emily Gallagher, Council Member Jennifer Gutierrez, and U.S. Rep. Nydia Velazquez to DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. They cited CB1's high fatality rate—3.27 deaths per 10,000 residents, above the city average. The board's vote was contentious, with pro-driving members opposing the change. DOT spokesperson Mona Bruno acknowledged the support and promised more information on slow zones for outer boroughs. Council Member Lincoln Restler later joined the call for lower speeds. The measure now awaits DOT action.
