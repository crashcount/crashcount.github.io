      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 20?

      No More Dead Kids: Hold Myrie to the Blood on Brooklyn’s Streets

      Written July 13, 2025

      SD 20: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Senate District 20, the numbers do not lie. Four people killed, fifteen seriously injured, and nearly 1,500 hurt in crashes in the last year alone (NYC Open Data). Children, elders, cyclists, pedestrians—no one is spared. On June 28, an eight-year-old boy was crossing Eastern Parkway with his sister. A black SUV hit him. Witnesses saw him dragged from under the car. “I just saw a lot of blood gushing out of his ears, his mouth,” a neighbor said. The boy died on the street. The driver stayed. No arrest. The city moved on.

      Just weeks earlier, a 101-year-old woman was killed crossing with the signal at Montgomery Street. An SUV making a left turn ended her life. The driver was unlicensed (NYC Open Data).

      These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by a city that moves too slow.

      The Vehicles That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the damage. In the last three years, they killed five pedestrians and left hundreds more injured in SD 20 (NYC Open Data). Trucks and buses killed three. Motorcycles and mopeds, bikes—they hurt, but they do not kill like the big machines do (NYC Open Data).

      What Has Been Done—And What Hasn’t

      State Senator Zellnor Myrie has spoken for safer streets. He has called for more protected bike lanes, busways, and congestion pricing (called for more protected bike lanes, busways, and congestion pricing). “We should be making this as easy as possible and as safe as possible for as many people as possible,” he said after riding through Brooklyn’s broken bike lanes. He has backed bills to expand enforcement and redesign streets (backed bills to expand enforcement and redesign streets).

      But when the Stop Super Speeders Act came up—a bill to force the worst repeat offenders to slow down—Myrie missed the key committee vote (missed the key committee vote). The bill passed committee anyway, but the absence was felt. The dead do not get a second chance.

      The Call

      The blood on the street is not washed away by words. Call Senator Myrie. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action on repeat offenders. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves the state budget, and represents districts like SD 20.
      ▸ Where does SD 20 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35 and assembly district AD 57.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 20?
      It includes the Park Slope, Windsor Terrace-South Slope, Prospect Heights, Crown Heights (North), Lincoln Terrace Park, Crown Heights (South), Prospect Lefferts Gardens-Wingate, East Flatbush-Erasmus, East Flatbush-Rugby, Prospect Park, and Brooklyn CB8 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 35, District 36, District 39, District 40, and District 41, and Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 52, AD 56, AD 57, and AD 58.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 20?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians in SD 20, with five pedestrian deaths and hundreds of injuries in the last three years. Trucks and buses killed three pedestrians. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes caused injuries but far fewer deaths.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. They happen because of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by city policy—not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable. They can support bills like the Stop Super Speeders Act and push for citywide 20 mph limits.

      Myrie Urges Immediate Action on Safety Boosting Speed Bill
      ▸

      After a crash killed a mother and two daughters in Gravesend, advocates and Council Member Shahana Hanif rallied for the Stop Super Speeders bill. The law would force repeat reckless drivers to use speed-limiting tech. Survivors demand action. Lawmakers promise change.

      On April 1, 2025, Council Member Shahana Hanif joined a rally at Brooklyn Borough Hall demanding passage of the Stop Super Speeders bill. The bill, sponsored in Albany by State Sen. Andrew Gounardes and Assemblymember Emily Gallagher, would require drivers with repeated violations to install intelligent speed assistance (ISA) devices. These devices cap speed at 5 mph over the limit for those with 11 or more license points in 24 months or six camera tickets in a year. The rally followed a fatal Gravesend crash that killed a mother and her two daughters. Hanif and other lawmakers called current enforcement—ticketing, suspensions, fines, jail—ineffective. Assembly Member Jo Anne Simon said, 'The speed limiter technology is available to us. Let’s use it. It will save lives.' The bill is modeled on EU and Virginia laws. Some opposition remains, but supporters say the measure is urgent and practical.

      ""Congestion pricing is working – and is critical for the investments we need to make in our public transit system. We need leadership that's not afraid to stand up for us, or stand up against Donald Trump."" -- Zellnor Myrie

      On February 19, 2025, the Trump administration withdrew federal approval for New York City's congestion pricing program. U.S. DOT Secretary Sean Duffy cited burdens on working-class drivers and questioned the legality of using toll revenue for transit. The MTA immediately filed a lawsuit to challenge the move, arguing the program is settled law and already reduces congestion. State Senators Andrew Gounardes and Zellnor Myrie condemned the action, warning, 'Trump will be directly responsible for more traffic, more crashes, more polluted air, slower buses and less funding for our transit system.' The withdrawal aligns with New Jersey's opposition and threatens transit upgrades like elevators for elderly and disabled riders. Local officials and advocates say ending congestion pricing will harm vulnerable road users and worsen street danger.
