      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 20?

      When Words Fail, Children Die: Hold Myrie Accountable for Parkway Bloodshed

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 24, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      SD 20: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      Children, Blood, and the Parkway

      Just weeks ago, an eight-year-old boy tried to cross Eastern Parkway with his sister. He did not make it. A black Honda Pilot struck him at the intersection with Albany Avenue. Witnesses saw a woman drag the boy from under the SUV. “I just saw a lot of blood gushing out of his ears, his mouth,” said one witness. The driver stayed. The boy died. No arrest was made. The street stayed open.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the last twelve months, SD 20 saw 4 deaths and 1,489 injuries from crashes. Fifteen people suffered serious injuries. Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians all paid. SUVs and cars did most of the harm—5 deaths and 784 injuries to pedestrians in recent years. Trucks and buses killed three more. Bikes and mopeds took lives too, but the carnage comes on four wheels, not two. The disaster is slow, but it does not stop.

      Leadership: Words, Votes, and Missed Chances

      State Senator Zellnor Myrie has stood with advocates for safer streets. He co-sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed limiters. But when the bill came to a vote in June, he was marked “excused”—absent, not voting on both June 11 and June 12. Myrie has spoken for protected bike lanes and busways, saying, “We should be making this as easy as possible and as safe as possible for as many people as possible” on a recent ride. But words do not stop cars. Votes do. Laws do.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a policy failure. Call Senator Myrie. Tell him to show up and vote for the bills that save lives. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action, not sympathy. Do not wait for another child to die. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing statewide policy. Senators represent districts and vote on bills that affect all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 20 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35 and assembly district AD 57.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 20?
      It includes the Park Slope, Windsor Terrace-South Slope, Prospect Heights, Crown Heights (North), Lincoln Terrace Park, Crown Heights (South), Prospect Lefferts Gardens-Wingate, East Flatbush-Erasmus, East Flatbush-Rugby, Prospect Park, and Brooklyn CB8 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 35, District 36, District 39, District 40, and District 41, and Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 52, AD 56, AD 57, and AD 58.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 20?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 5 deaths and 784 injuries to pedestrians. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in fewer incidents, while bikes accounted for 39 injuries but no deaths. The greatest danger comes from four-wheeled vehicles. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just "accidents"?
      Crashes are not random accidents—they are the result of policy choices, street design, and enforcement. Safer laws and infrastructure can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and support laws that lower speed limits, hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable, and fund street redesigns that protect people walking and cycling.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
