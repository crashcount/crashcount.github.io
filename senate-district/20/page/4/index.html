      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 20?

      No More Towels: Streets Are Killing Kids and Seniors—Who’s Next?

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      SD 20: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      Children, Blood, and the Parkway

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy tried to cross Eastern Parkway at Albany Avenue. A black Honda Pilot hit him. His sister watched. Neighbors ran for towels. They tried to stop the blood. “I saw the kid being dragged from underneath the car by a woman,” a witness said. The boy died. The driver stayed. No arrest. The street stayed the same.

      Just weeks before, a 101-year-old woman was killed crossing Montgomery Street at Brooklyn Avenue. A GMC SUV, left turn, unlicensed driver. She had the signal. She did not make it across. In the last year, four people have died on these streets. One was a child. One was a centenarian. One was a cyclist. One was a man crushed by a truck. No one is safe.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the last twelve months, SD 20 saw 4 deaths and 15 serious injuries. 1,495 people were hurt. Children, elders, cyclists, walkers. Cars and SUVs did most of the killing—5 deaths and 18 serious injuries citywide in the period, just from these vehicles according to NYC Open Data. Trucks and buses crushed three more. Bikes and mopeds left bodies too. The violence is steady. The names change. The blood does not.

      Leadership: Words, Votes, and the Waiting

      State Senator Zellnor Myrie talks about safer streets. He rides a bike for the cameras. He says, “We should be making this as easy as possible and as safe as possible for as many people as possible.” He co-sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed limiters. But when the bill came up for a vote in June, he was marked “excused”—absent. The bill passed committee. The streets did not change.

      The Call: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another risk. Call Senator Myrie. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action on repeat speeders. Demand streets where children and elders can cross and live. Do not wait for another towel, another body, another name.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees state policy for all New Yorkers. Open States
      ▸ Where does SD 20 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35 and assembly district AD 57. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 20?
      It includes the Park Slope, Windsor Terrace-South Slope, Prospect Heights, Crown Heights (North), Lincoln Terrace Park, Crown Heights (South), Prospect Lefferts Gardens-Wingate, East Flatbush-Erasmus, East Flatbush-Rugby, Prospect Park, and Brooklyn CB8 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 35, District 36, District 39, District 40, and District 41, and Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 52, AD 56, AD 57, and AD 58. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 20?
      Cars and SUVs were responsible for most deaths and serious injuries to pedestrians in SD 20, with 5 deaths and 18 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 3 deaths and 5 serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 39 incidents, with no deaths and one serious injury. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement. The steady toll of deaths and injuries shows these are not random accidents, but the result of choices and policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, support bills like the Stop Super Speeders Act, fund protected bike lanes, and demand redesigns of dangerous intersections. They can also push for automated enforcement and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable.

      Myrie Supports Safety Boosting Universal Daylighting with Barriers
      ▸

      Seven Brooklyn officials urge DOT to clear cars from corners. They want boulders, planters, and bike corrals—not just paint. Their call follows deadly crashes. They press the city to use state law and federal funds. DOT promises review. Advocates back the push.

      On January 17, 2024, seven Brooklyn elected officials—including Council Member Shahana Hanif, Borough President Antonio Reynoso, Assembly Members Marcela Mitaynes, Jo Anne Simon, Robert Carroll, and State Senators Zellnor Myrie and Andrew Gounardes—issued a joint letter to the NYC Department of Transportation. They called for 'universal daylighting with hardened materials such as boulders, planters, and bike corrals' at intersections. The officials cited recent fatal crashes and urged the city to opt into a state law banning parking within 20 feet of corners. They want federal funds used for these changes. The group opposes DOT’s slow pace and reliance on paint, demanding physical barriers. Community board leaders and advocates support the move. DOT says it will review the letter and remains committed to evidence-based daylighting.
