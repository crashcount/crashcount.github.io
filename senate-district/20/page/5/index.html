      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 20?

      No More Blood on Eastern Parkway—Make Them Change the Street Before It Kills Again

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, deduped citations, and ensured proper inline citation formatting.)

      SD 20: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      Children in the Crosswalk, Blood on the Asphalt

      In Senate District 20, the crisis does not pause. In the last twelve months, four people died on these streets. One was a child. Fifteen more suffered serious injuries. These are not just numbers—they are bodies on the ground, families torn open, lives ended at the curb.

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy was crossing Eastern Parkway with his sister when a black SUV hit him. He died there, in the intersection. A witness said, “I just saw a lot of blood gushing out of his ears, his mouth” (NY Daily News). The driver stayed. No arrest. The street stayed dangerous. Another neighbor said, “Yeah, this street is dangerous” (ABC7).

      In the past year, 1,493 people were hurt in crashes here. Most were walking, biking, or just trying to live. Cars and SUVs did the most harm—killing, maiming, leaving scars that never heal.

      Leadership: Promises, Votes, and the Long Road

      State Senator Zellnor Myrie has not been silent. He voted yes on a bill to force safer street designs for all users. He has backed more protected bike lanes, busways, and daylighting. He rode a bike through Brooklyn and said, “We should be making this as easy as possible and as safe as possible for as many people as possible” (Streetsblog NYC).

      But the bodies keep coming. The bills are not enough. Every week, another crash. Every month, another family in mourning.

      What Now: The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. The deaths are preventable. The pain is a choice made by those in power, or by those who do nothing. Call Senator Myrie. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for every crosswalk and bike lane. Do not wait for another child to die.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect all residents.
      ▸ Where does SD 20 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35 and assembly district AD 57.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 20?
      It includes the Park Slope, Windsor Terrace-South Slope, Prospect Heights, Crown Heights (North), Lincoln Terrace Park, Crown Heights (South), Prospect Lefferts Gardens-Wingate, East Flatbush-Erasmus, East Flatbush-Rugby, Prospect Park, and Brooklyn CB8 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 35, District 36, District 39, District 40, and District 41, and Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 52, AD 56, AD 57, and AD 58.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 20?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm to pedestrians in SD 20, with 5 deaths and 18 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 0 deaths and 0 serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 0 deaths and 1 serious injury (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Policies like lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, and safer street designs can save lives and stop the next tragedy.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, redesign dangerous intersections, and hold reckless drivers accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
