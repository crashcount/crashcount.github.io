      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 20?

      Eastern Parkway Kills Kids. Who Will Stop the Bloodshed?

      Written June 29, 2025

      SD 20: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      Children, Elders, and the Unprotected

      In Senate District 20, the street is a gauntlet. In the past year, two people died and 1,298 were hurt in crashes. Sixteen suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. The dead include a 101-year-old woman, a boy of eight, and a 68-year-old grandmother. Their lives ended at the curb, in the crosswalk, or crossing with the light.

      Just days ago, an eight-year-old boy was killed crossing Eastern Parkway with his sister. The driver stayed at the scene. The street stayed dangerous. “Yeah, this street is dangerous,” said a neighbor. The boy’s name was Mordica Keller. He was walking home. He never made it.

      In May, Maurette Lafleur, 68, was hit in a crosswalk in East Flatbush. She had the signal. The driver turned left and struck her. “The lady spin around and sped through,” said Lorna Johnson, who watched her die. The driver faced no charges.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the damage. In three years, they killed four pedestrians and left hundreds more with broken bodies. Trucks and vans killed three more. Bikes and mopeds hurt dozens, but the carnage comes on four wheels. The numbers are plain: 18 killed, 5,027 injured, 66 left with wounds that will not heal.

      Leadership: Words, Votes, and What’s Missing

      State Senator Zellnor Myrie has voted for safer street design, backing a bill to require “complete streets” that protect everyone, not just drivers. He has called for more protected bike lanes, busways, and an end to delays. “We should be making this as easy as possible and as safe as possible for as many people as possible,” he said after riding the streets himself. He has supported congestion pricing and speed limiters for repeat offenders. But the blood on the street says it is not enough. Promises do not stop cars. Votes do not slow SUVs. Every week without action is another funeral.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call your council member. Call Senator Myrie. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings. Demand that the killing stop.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York.
      ▸ Where does SD 20 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35 and assembly district AD 57. Source
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 20?
      It includes the Park Slope, Windsor Terrace-South Slope, Prospect Heights, Crown Heights (North), Lincoln Terrace Park, Crown Heights (South), Prospect Lefferts Gardens-Wingate, East Flatbush-Erasmus, East Flatbush-Rugby, Prospect Park, and Brooklyn CB8 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 35, District 36, District 39, District 40, and District 41, and Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 52, AD 56, AD 57, and AD 58. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 20?
      Most injuries and deaths came from Cars and SUVs (4 deaths, 549 minor injuries, 198 moderate injuries, 18 serious injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (3 deaths, 43 minor injuries, 12 moderate injuries, 5 serious injuries). Motorcycles and Mopeds caused injuries but no deaths. Bikes caused injuries but no deaths. Source
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. The same streets see deaths and injuries year after year—proof that better design, lower speeds, and enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes and crossings, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable. They can also push for street redesigns that put people before cars.

      Myrie Opposes Harmful NYPD Bike Force at Protests
      ▸

      Senator Zellnor Myrie told a city panel how NYPD officers used bikes as weapons during a protest. He described tires on his back, threats of pepper spray, and trauma that lingers. The Civilian Complaint Review Board found the officer abused his authority.

      On June 30, 2022, State Senator Zellnor Myrie testified at an NYPD disciplinary hearing for Officer Michael Kovalik, a member of the Strategic Response Group. The hearing followed a Civilian Complaint Review Board (CCRB) investigation, which found Kovalik abused his authority by threatening Myrie and others with pepper spray during a protest on May 29, 2020. The matter centered on 'excessive use of force with department-issued bikes' and the officer's actions at a protest outside Barclays Center after George Floyd's murder. Myrie, detained with zip ties, said, 'I felt what felt like bike tires on my back and on my arms.' He described lasting trauma from the incident. The CCRB prosecutor, Andre Applewhite, argued video evidence confirmed the threats. Myrie and another lawmaker have filed a federal lawsuit against the NYPD. The case highlights the dangers faced by peaceful protesters and the use of police bicycles as tools of force.
