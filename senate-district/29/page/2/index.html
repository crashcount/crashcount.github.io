      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 29?

      Sidewalks Aren’t Safe—And Politicians Are Letting It Happen

      Written August 12, 2025 (Revised August 12, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 29: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 12, 2025

      The Sidewalk Is Not Safe

      Just last month, a Ford Mustang jumped the curb at East 149th and Courtlandt. Six people were walking. The car hit one, then five more, before smashing into scaffolding. The driver and passenger ran. The street was left with pain and panic. “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting,” said Vivian Cole. All six went to the hospital. They survived. This time.

      The Numbers Don’t Lie

      In the last twelve months, SD 29 saw 8 deaths and over 2,000 injuries from crashes. Thirty-two people were seriously hurt. The bodies pile up. The stories blur. A cyclist killed by a bus on 149th. A pedestrian crushed by a truck on Oak Point. A man dead under the wheels of an SUV. The sidewalk, the crosswalk, the bike lane—none of them safe.

      Leadership: Votes and Silence

      State Senator Jose Serrano has voted yes on bills to create safety zones and extend school speed cameras. He co-sponsored and voted for the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from going too fast. These are steps. But the blood on the street says it is not enough. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not. The most dangerous drivers keep driving.

      The Voices in the Street

      After the Mustang hit, the fear was raw. “We thought it was a bomb or something, because we are all panicking around here,” said Christina Sieh. The city moves on. The sidewalk is swept. The pain stays.

      Call to Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Senator Serrano. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real consequences for repeat offenders. Do not wait for another body on the sidewalk.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees state policy for all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 29 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 8 and assembly district AD 84. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 29?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, North & South Brother Islands, Concourse-Concourse Village, Highbridge, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Soundview Park, Upper East Side-Yorkville, and Bronx CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8, District 16, District 17, and District 18, and Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 77, AD 84, and AD 85. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 29?
      SUVs and cars caused the most harm to pedestrians in SD 29, with 8 deaths and 18 serious injuries. Trucks and buses were involved in 3 deaths and 6 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 2 deaths and 2 serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 1 death and 3 serious injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speed limits, and real enforcement. They are not accidents—they are the result of choices and policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, pass laws to stop repeat dangerous drivers, and fund safer street designs. They can also hold agencies accountable for enforcement and transparency.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
