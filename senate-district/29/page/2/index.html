      No One Is Safe on the Sidewalk—Hold Serrano’s Feet to the Fire

      Written July 21, 2025 (Revised July 29, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 29: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 21, 2025

      The Week the Sidewalk Wasn’t Safe

      Just weeks ago, a Ford Mustang tore through the intersection at East 149th and Courtlandt. The driver lost control, mounted the curb, and plowed into six people. He left the car and ran. Six bodies on the ground, sirens in the heat. All sent to Lincoln Hospital. The police are still looking for him. “Once I heard the noise I turned around and it was already on the curb. It immediately backed up and fled.” said Melvin Pemberton, a witness. The city called it minor injuries. There is nothing minor about a car on the sidewalk.

      The Toll: Death and Injury, Year After Year

      In the last twelve months, SD 29 saw 10 deaths, 25 serious injuries, and 1,881 people hurt in 3,189 crashes. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The numbers do not stop. They only grow. In the last year, crashes rose nearly 24%. Injuries climbed by 18%. The faces change, the pain repeats.

      Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and cars did the most harm—8 deaths, 16 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 3, left 6 with life-changing wounds. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes took more. The sidewalk, the crosswalk, the street—none are safe. Police say all six victims were taken to Lincoln Hospital with minor injuries.

      What Has Senator Serrano Done?

      State Senator Jose Serrano voted yes on bills to extend school speed zones and create safety zones. He co-sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force repeat speeders to install speed-limiting devices. He voted yes in committee. But the law is not yet passed. The dead cannot wait for Albany.

      Call to Action: Demand More, Now

      Call Senator Serrano. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand the Stop Super Speeders Act. Demand streets where a child can walk and live.

      The sidewalk is not safe. The crosswalk is not safe. Only action will make it so.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, including those that affect traffic safety and street design.
      ▸ Where does SD 29 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 8 and assembly district AD 84. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 29?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, North & South Brother Islands, Concourse-Concourse Village, Highbridge, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Soundview Park, Upper East Side-Yorkville, and Bronx CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8, District 16, District 17, and District 18, and Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 77, AD 84, and AD 85. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 29?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 8 deaths and 16 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 2 deaths and 1 serious injury. Bikes were involved in 1 death and 3 serious injuries. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and stronger enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, support speed cameras, and require speed limiters for repeat offenders. They can fund safer street redesigns and hold dangerous drivers accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
