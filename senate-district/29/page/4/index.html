      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 29?

      Five Dead, Thirteen Broken—Streets Still Built for Blood

      Written June 29, 2025

      SD 29: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      The Toll This Year

      Five dead. Thirteen seriously hurt. That is just since January in Senate District 29. These numbers are not names, but every one was a life. In the last twelve months, eleven people killed, twenty-five left with life-changing wounds (NYC Open Data).

      Last week, a driver ran a red light at Hunts Point Avenue and hit three men on the sidewalk. One is still fighting for his life. Police said the driver “slammed on the gas and sped off east on Hunts Point Ave., blowing through a red light” (NY Daily News). He ran, but the blood stayed behind.

      A few months back, a cyclist was killed by a turning MTA bus on East 149th. In April, a man crossing Oak Point Avenue was crushed by a box truck. The stories do not stop. The street does not forgive.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. In three years, cars and trucks killed at least eight. Motorcycles and mopeds killed two. Bikes killed one (NYC Open Data). The dead are old and young, men and women. Some were walking home. Some were riding to work. Some were just in the wrong place when a driver did not stop.

      The city blames the walker, the biker, the weather. But the street is built for speed, not for life. The numbers do not lie. SUVs and cars caused the most harm—8 deaths, 745 injuries. Trucks and buses killed 3. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes killed fewer, but the pain is the same (NYC Open Data).

      What Has Been Done

      State Senator Jose Serrano voted yes on a bill to force safer street design (Open States S 9718). The bill says streets must be built for all, not just for cars. It passed. But the blood keeps flowing. There are no public quotes. No press releases. No comfort for the families left behind.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by someone in power. Call Senator Serrano. Call your council member. Demand streets that do not kill. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York.
      ▸ Where does SD 29 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 8 and assembly district AD 84.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 29?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, North & South Brother Islands, Concourse-Concourse Village, Highbridge, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Soundview Park, Upper East Side-Yorkville, and Bronx CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8, District 16, District 17, and District 18, and Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 77, AD 84, and AD 85.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 29?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 8 deaths and 745 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 2 deaths and 38 injuries. Bikes caused 1 death and 59 injuries (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for safer street design, lower speed limits, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can also listen to advocates and act quickly after each crash.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
