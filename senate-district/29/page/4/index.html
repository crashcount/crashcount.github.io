      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 29?

      Blood on Serrano’s Streets: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      Written June 21, 2025 (Revised June 20, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      SD 29: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 21, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Twelve dead. Twenty-five left with injuries that will never heal. In the last year alone, Senate District 29 saw 2,560 crashes. These are not just numbers—they are neighbors, children, workers. In three years, 36 people have died: five killed by SUVs, three by trucks, one by a bike, the rest by cars, motorcycles, and buses. The streets do not forgive.

      The Human Cost

      A 15-year-old boy on a scooter was struck by a motorcyclist who ran a red light and sped away, leaving him fighting for his life. Police said, “A 15-year-old boy riding his scooter was critically hurt when a Bronx motorcyclist blew through a red light, slammed into him and sped away” (NY Daily News).

      A man sleeping under a box truck in Hunts Point was crushed when the driver pulled away. “Nobody checks under their car [to see] if someone is there,” said a local worker after the man died at Lincoln Hospital (NY Daily News).

      Children, the elderly, the homeless—none are spared.

      What Has Been Done—And What Hasn’t

      Senator Jose Serrano voted yes on a bill to force safer street design for all users, not just drivers (Open States). The bill passed. It is a start. But the carnage continues. Cameras catch speeders, but the law that keeps them running is always under threat. The city can lower speed limits, but waits. The dead keep coming.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call Senator Serrano. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand permanent speed cameras. Demand streets built for people, not just cars. Every day of delay is another family broken.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, including those that affect street safety and traffic enforcement. Open States
      ▸ Where does SD 29 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 8 and assembly district AD 84. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 29?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, North & South Brother Islands, Highbridge, Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Soundview Park, East Harlem (South), Randall’S Island, and Bronx CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8, District 16, District 17, and District 18, and Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 77, AD 84, and AD 85. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 29?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for most deaths and injuries (8 deaths, 741 injuries), motorcycles and mopeds caused 3 deaths and 37 injuries, and bikes were involved in 1 death and 58 injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents—they are the result of policy, street design, and enforcement. The numbers show that change is possible when leaders act.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, make speed cameras permanent, and redesign streets to protect people walking and biking. They can pass and enforce laws that put safety before speed.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
