      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 29?

      Twelve Dead, Zero Excuses: Serrano Must Act Now

      Written June 25, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 29: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Human Cost in SD 29

      A man steps onto Harlem River Drive at dawn. A black Nissan Rogue comes fast. Six months later, police arrest the driver. Willow Hall is dead. The driver was unlicensed. He stayed at the scene, but charges came only after half a year. As the paper put it, “Donald Pinnock was charged Sunday with aggravated unlicensed operation of a motor vehicle” (NY Daily News).

      In Central Park, a cyclist swerves to avoid a pedestrian. He falls. His head hits the curb. Salvador Nico-Garcia, 43, does not get up. “Officials say Nico-Garcia was on an e-bike and was not wearing a helmet at the time he was thrown” (ABC7). The pedestrian walks away with a scraped hand. The city keeps counting bodies.

      In the last 12 months, SD 29 saw 12 deaths, 25 serious injuries, and over 1,500 people hurt on its streets. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. The numbers are not just numbers. They are lives cut short, families broken, futures erased.

      Who Pays, Who Acts?

      Most deaths come from cars and trucks. SUVs killed five. Trucks killed three. Bikes killed one. The pattern is clear. The biggest vehicles do the most harm. The most vulnerable pay the price.

      Leadership: What Has Serrano Done?

      Senator Jose Serrano voted yes on a bill to bring complete street design to New York. The bill aims to make roads safer for everyone, not just drivers. It passed. That is a start. But the blood keeps flowing. The city and state have the power to do more. Lower speed limits. Enforce the law. Build streets that protect people, not just cars.

      Call to Action: Make Them Hear You

      Twelve dead in a year. Hundreds more broken. This is not fate. It is policy. It is delay. It is silence. Call Senator Serrano. Call your council member. Demand streets where a man can cross at dawn and make it home. Demand action before another name is added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York State.
      ▸ Where does SD 29 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 8 and assembly district AD 84.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 29?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, North & South Brother Islands, Highbridge, Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Soundview Park, East Harlem (South), Randall’S Island, and Bronx CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8, District 16, District 17, and District 18, and Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 77, AD 84, and AD 85.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 29?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm to pedestrians in SD 29 (8 deaths, 745 injuries), followed by motorcycles and mopeds (2 deaths, 37 injuries), and bikes (1 death, 59 injuries). See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The patterns are clear and preventable. Most deaths come from the largest vehicles and the lack of safe street design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, and push for real accountability for dangerous drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
