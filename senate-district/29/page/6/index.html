      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 29?

      Blood on the Asphalt: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written June 20, 2025 (Revised June 20, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, deduped and sorted citations.)

      SD 29: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      Twelve dead. Twenty-five left with injuries that will not heal. In the last year alone, SD 29 saw 1,556 people hurt and 12 killed on its streets. The youngest victim was a child. The oldest, a man who never made it home. Crashes are not rare here. They are routine.

      A 15-year-old boy on a scooter was struck by a motorcyclist who ran a red light and sped away. The boy was left in critical condition. Police reported that “A 15-year-old boy riding his scooter was critically hurt when a Bronx motorcyclist blew through a red light, slammed into him and sped away.” No one stopped. No one helped.

      A man sleeping under a box truck in Hunts Point was crushed to death when the driver pulled away. A worker nearby said, “Nobody checks under their car [to see] if someone is there.”

      SUVs, trucks, bikes, mopeds, buses. The machines change, the outcome does not.

      Who Pays the Price

      The most vulnerable pay first. In the past year, 127 children were injured. Older adults, too: 24 people over 75. Pedestrians and cyclists are struck by SUVs, trucks, and even buses. In three years, 36 people have died. Eighty suffered serious injuries.

      The numbers are not just numbers. They are families, friends, neighbors. A body on the curb. A bike left twisted in the road.

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      Senator Jose Serrano voted yes on a bill to redesign streets for safety—“Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles”. It passed. But the deaths keep coming. No press quotes. No public fight.

      Laws alone do not save lives. Streets must change. Cameras must stay on. Speeds must drop. The city has the power. The state has the money. Still, the bodies pile up.

      What You Can Do

      Call Senator Serrano. Call your council member. Demand real change. Lower the speed limit. Keep the cameras running. Redesign the streets. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, including those that affect traffic safety in New York City.
      ▸ Where does SD 29 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 8 and assembly district AD 84.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 29?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, North & South Brother Islands, Highbridge, Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Soundview Park, East Harlem (South), Randall’S Island, and Bronx CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8, District 16, District 17, and District 18, and Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 77, AD 84, and AD 85.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 29?
      SUVs and Cars: 8 deaths, 203 moderate injuries, 15 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 2 deaths, 6 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 1 death, 23 moderate injuries, 3 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 3 deaths, 21 moderate injuries, 6 serious injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The numbers show a pattern. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, keep speed cameras running, and redesign streets to protect people walking and biking.
