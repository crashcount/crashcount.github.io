      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 29?

      Leggett and Bruckner: a woman down, a driver gone

      Written August 31, 2025 (Revised August 31, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 29: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 31, 2025

      Just after 12 AM on Aug 26, 2025, at Bruckner Blvd and Leggett Ave in Mott Haven, a driver hit 32-year-old Dilmania Lopez de Rodriguez in the crosswalk and fled, police said (ABC7; NY Daily News).

      She is one of 37 people killed on streets in this Senate district since Jan 1, 2022, alongside 6,064 injuries across 10,962 crashes, according to NYC’s collision data (NYC Open Data: Crashes, Persons, Vehicles).

      The count keeps rising

      Year-to-date, this district has logged 2,291 crashes, up 31.1% from 1,747 at this point last year. Injuries rose to 1,350 from 1,051. Serious injuries rose to 23 from 17. Deaths stand at 6, compared with 7 last year (NYC Open Data: Crashes, Persons).

      The numbers say what the corner already knows.

      Left turn, six bodies on the sidewalk

      On Jul 3, 2025, a Ford Mustang failed a left at E 149 St and Courtlandt Ave, jumped the curb, and hit six pedestrians. The driver ran, police said. “People were yelling… crying,” a witness said (New York Post; also NY Daily News and CBS New York).

      The same precinct logged a fatal hit-and-run at Bruckner and Leggett. The same hospital gets the calls. The same families wait.

      The policy lever sits on the desk

      State Senator Jose Serrano represents this district. He co-sponsored and voted yes in committee for bill S 4045, which would require intelligent speed assistance for drivers who rack up 11 DMV points in 24 months or six speed or red‑light camera tickets in a year (Open States).

      Assembly Member Amanda Septimo represents AD 84 here. The Assembly must move its version. Will she co-sponsor and push it?

      Citywide, lowering the default speed and reining in repeat speeders are the tools on hand. Use them. Our full call to action is here.

      What happens next

      The woman in the crosswalk is gone. The driver left. The data says more crashes, more injuries, steady death.

      We know where the fixes sit. Pass the speed limiters. Lower the speeds. Start now. See how to act here.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets — Crashes (h9gi-nx95), Persons (f55k-p6yu), and Vehicles (bm4k-52h4) — filtered to Jan 1, 2022 through Aug 31, 2025 and spatially clipped to New York State Senate District 29. CrashCount applies a spatial filter to NYPD geocoded collision points within the SD 29 boundary. Totals reported here (crashes, injuries, serious injuries, deaths; and year-to-date comparisons) come from those filtered records as of Aug 31, 2025. You can view the base datasets here, here, and here.
      ▸ Who was killed at Bruckner and Leggett?
      Police identified the victim as 32-year-old Dilmania Lopez de Rodriguez. She was struck in the crosswalk by a driver heading north on Bruckner Blvd, who fled the scene (ABC7; NY Daily News).
      ▸ What changed this year in SD 29?
      Year-to-date, crashes rose to 2,291 from 1,747 (up 31.1%). Injuries rose to 1,350 from 1,051. Serious injuries rose to 23 from 17. Deaths are 6, compared with 7 last year over the same period (NYC Open Data: Crashes and Persons).
      ▸ Who represents this area?
      State Senator Jose Serrano represents Senate District 29; Assembly Member Amanda Septimo represents Assembly District 84; Council Member Diana Ayala represents Council District 8 (context: SD 29 overlaps these areas).
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ What can I do right now?
      Ask city leaders to lower speeds citywide and back state action on speed limiters for repeat violators. Start with our steps here.
