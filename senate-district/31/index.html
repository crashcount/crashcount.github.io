      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 31?

      Six Dead, 1,400 Injured—Senator, How Many More?

      Written July 24, 2025 (Revised July 24, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, removed duplicate headline, and cleaned citations.)

      SD 31: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 24, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      Six people killed. Over 1,400 injured. That is the price paid on the streets of Senate District 31 in the past year alone (NYC Open Data). The wounds are not numbers. They are broken bodies, families left with silence where laughter used to be. Just this spring, a 65-year-old man was killed crossing with the signal at University Avenue and West Kingsbridge Road. He was crushed by a van making a right turn. He had the light. He did not make it home (NYC Open Data CrashID 4801942).

      On the Major Deegan Expressway, a driver stranded by a dead battery was hit from behind and left to die. His friend recalled, “He called to get a jump, and after that, he got hit from behind, a hit-and-run… He called to let them know, I’ve been hit. I can’t breathe.” The driver who struck him ran. The city moved on.

      The Pattern: No End in Sight

      Crashes keep coming. In the last twelve months, SD 31 saw 2,342 crashes, 25 serious injuries, and 6 deaths (NYC Open Data). Pedestrians and cyclists bear the brunt. Cars and SUVs are the main killers—7 deaths and 22 serious injuries from these vehicles since 2022 (NYC Open Data). Trucks and buses killed 3 more. Motorcycles and mopeds, 1 serious injury. Bikes, 1 serious injury. The violence is steady, the pain routine.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Miles to Go

      State Senator Robert Jackson has backed some measures. He voted yes to curb repeat speeders with mandatory speed limiters and supported extending school speed zones. He co-sponsored a fare-free bus pilot and a bill to rate vehicles for pedestrian safety. These are steps. But the bodies keep coming. The bills are not enough. The streets are not safe.

      The Call: Demand More

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by leaders who allow speed, chaos, and neglect. The Bronx District Attorney said, “The defendant allegedly drove her car over the victim… then after looking under her vehicle… she allegedly drove away without reporting what happened. Her alleged actions are egregious and show a lack of humanity.” The horror is not rare. It is routine.

      Call Senator Jackson. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Demand streets where children, elders, and everyone else can cross and live.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees statewide policy, including traffic safety.
      ▸ Where does SD 31 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 10, and assembly district AD 72 (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 31?
      It includes the University Heights (South)-Morris Heights, University Heights (North)-Fordham, Bedford Park, Kingsbridge Heights-Van Cortlandt Village, Kingsbridge-Marble Hill, Washington Heights (South), Washington Heights (North), Inwood, Highbridge Park, Inwood Hill Park, and Bronx CB7 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts 10, 11, and 14, and Assembly Districts 71, 72, 77, 78, 81, and 86 (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 31?
      Cars and SUVs caused 7 deaths and 22 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 3 more. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 1 serious injury. Bikes caused 1 serious injury (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Policies, street design, and enforcement choices make them predictable and preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, crack down on repeat dangerous drivers, fund safer street designs, and pass laws that protect people walking and cycling.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      ▸

      Senate passes S 8344. School speed zone rules in New York City get extended. Lawmakers make technical fixes. The bill keeps pressure on drivers near schools. Streets stay a little safer for kids.

      Bill S 8344, titled 'Extends provisions and makes technical corrections to school speed zones in NYC; repealer,' moved through the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. Sponsored by Senator Andrew Gounardes, the bill passed Senate votes on June 12 and June 13, and cleared the Assembly on June 17. The bill's technical fixes aim to keep protections in place for children and other vulnerable road users near schools. No safety analyst note was provided.

      S 4045
      Jackson votes yes in committee, boosting street safety by curbing repeat speeders.
      ▸

      Senate backs S 4045. Repeat speeders face forced installation of speed assistance tech. Eleven points or six camera tickets triggers action. Law targets reckless drivers. Streets may get safer for those outside the car.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on June 11 and June 12, 2025. The bill, titled 'Relates to requiring the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices for repeated violation of maximum speed limits,' mandates these devices for drivers who rack up eleven or more points in 24 months, or six speed or red light camera tickets in a year. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving. Senators including Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, and others voted yes. The bill targets drivers with a pattern of violations, seeking to cut risk for pedestrians and cyclists by limiting repeat speeding.
