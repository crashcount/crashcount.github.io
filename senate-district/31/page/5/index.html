      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 31?

      Lower Speed, Save Lives—No More Names Turned Into Numbers

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, deduped and cleaned citations.)

      SD 31: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Senate District 31, the road does not forgive. Since 2022, 29 people have died in crashes. Sixty-nine have been seriously injured. These are not just numbers—they are fathers, daughters, neighbors. In the last twelve months alone, six more lives ended on these streets. Over 1,200 people were hurt. The pain is not spread evenly. Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and SUVs do the most damage, but trucks, motorcycles, and bikes all leave scars (NYC Open Data).

      Just this spring, a 39-year-old man was stranded on the Major Deegan. He called friends for help. A Mercedes slammed into him. He called again, said, “I can’t breathe.” His friends found him dying. The driver ran. The police are still looking. “He called to get a jump, and after that, he got hit from behind, a hit-and-run,” said a friend.

      The System Responds—But Not Fast Enough

      Senator Robert Jackson has voted for safety. He backed the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed limiters (Stop Super Speeders Act). He voted to extend school speed zones (school speed zones). He co-sponsored bills for safer buses and a pedestrian safety rating for vehicles. He has spoken up for 24/7 speed cameras, saying, “Cars speeding in New York kill New Yorkers, injure New Yorkers.”

      But the deaths keep coming. The cameras catch speeders, but the worst drivers keep driving. The law moves slow. The street moves fast. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so citywide. The most dangerous drivers are still on the road.

      The Work Left to Do

      Every delay costs lives. The families know it. “To think he was in that car suffering for that long. One minute’s too long. Imagine more than 15 minutes,” said a stepmother after police left a burning crash scene without helping.

      This is not fate. It is policy.

      Call to Action

      Demand more. Call Senator Jackson. Call your council member. Tell them: Lower the speed limit. Pass the bills. Enforce the laws. Protect the people who walk and ride. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians were caused by Cars and Trucks (7 deaths, 21 serious injuries), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 1 serious injury), and Bikes (no deaths, 1 serious injury). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These crashes are not random accidents. They are the result of policy choices, street design, and enforcement decisions that can be changed to save lives. Streetsblog NYC

      They can lower speed limits, pass and enforce safety laws, fund safer street designs, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable. Open States
