      Major Deegan, Minor Consequences: Blood on the Streets, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 8, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      SD 31: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      A man stranded on the Major Deegan called his friends for help. The battery was dead. A black Mercedes slammed into him from behind. He called again, this time to say he could not breathe. He died before help arrived. The driver ran. Police have not found him. “He called to get a jump, and after that, he got hit from behind, a hit-and-run. They were on their way to come give him a jump and he got hit waiting for them. And then he called to let them know, I’ve been hit. I can’t breathe.”

      In the last twelve months, eight people died in Senate District 31. Twenty-four were seriously injured. Over a thousand were hurt. The dead include the old and the young. A 65-year-old man was killed crossing with the signal. A 24-year-old woman was struck in a crosswalk. The street does not care who you are.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. In three years, they killed seven pedestrians, left twenty with serious injuries, and caused 190 moderate injuries. Trucks and buses killed three. Motorcycles and mopeds caused one serious injury and sixteen moderate injuries. Bikes caused one serious injury and eight moderate injuries. The numbers do not lie. The street is a battlefield, and the weapons are heavy and fast.

      What Has Been Done, What Has Not

      Senator Robert Jackson has voted for safer streets. He backed the law to force complete street design, to protect people on foot and on bikes. He voted yes. He co-sponsored bills for fare-free buses and for public safety ratings on cars. He supported 24/7 speed cameras. “Cars speeding in New York kill New Yorkers, injure New Yorkers.”

      But the blood keeps coming. The cameras do not cover every street. The laws do not slow every car. The city has the power to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not done so. The dead wait for action.

      Call to Action: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand 20 mph on every street. Demand cameras that never sleep. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. The street will not wait for you.

      S 9718
      Jackson votes yes, boosting street safety with complete street design.
      ▸

      Senate Bill S 9718 passed. It pushes for complete street design. The aim: safer roads for everyone. Pedestrians, cyclists, and passengers stand to gain. The vote split. Some senators said no. But the bill moved forward. Streets may change.

      Senate Bill S 9718, titled "Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles," advanced through the Senate with committee votes on May 28 and June 3, 2024. The bill, sponsored by Sean Ryan (District 61) and co-sponsored by Liz Krueger, Monica Martinez, Jack M. Martins, Anthony H. Palumbo, and Julia Salazar, passed both votes despite opposition. The measure calls for redesigning streets to protect all users, not just drivers. The vote was not unanimous—several senators voted no, but the majority carried it through. The bill’s focus is clear: safer streets for people on foot, on bikes, and in vehicles. No safety analyst note was provided, but the intent is to cut danger at the curb and crosswalk.

      Jackson Supports Safety Boosting Automated Street Cleaning Enforcement
      ▸

      A new state bill would let New York City street sweepers use cameras to catch cars blocking cleaning routes. Repeat offenders rack up most tickets. Lawmakers say dirty streets and blocked drains endanger everyone. The law would sunset in 2029.

      Assembly Bill (unnumbered) was introduced by Brooklyn Assembly Member Brian Cunningham on April 25, 2024. The bill is pending in the state legislature. It would allow New York City to mount enforcement cameras on street sweepers to ticket cars parked illegally during street cleaning hours. The bill summary states it 'fulfills the Sanitation Department's longstanding ask for help getting vehicles out of the way of road-cleaning operations.' Cunningham, who sponsors the bill, said, 'Dirty streets are totally unacceptable.' Sanitation Commissioner Jessica Tisch supports the change, noting that state law must allow automated ticketing. Uptown Manhattan State Senator Robert Jackson introduced a similar Senate bill. The law would phase in after a year and expire by mid-2029, giving officials time to assess its impact. No formal safety analysis for vulnerable road users was provided.
