      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 30?

      Blood on Their Hands—How Many More Must Die on Harlem’s Streets?

      Written July 8, 2025

      SD 30: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 8, 2025

      The Toll Mounts, Week After Week

      In Senate District 30, the streets do not forgive. Since 2022, 20 people have died in crashes. Sixty-seven more suffered serious injuries. Nearly 4,000 have been hurt. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old, walking or riding. They only rise.

      SUVs and cars did most of the killing—eight deaths, eleven serious injuries. Trucks and buses took two more lives. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes left dozens battered, but the greatest harm came from heavy, fast machines. The names fade, but the pain stays. A cyclist ejected, head bleeding on East 135th. A pedestrian crushed on Harlem River Drive. A child struck crossing Manhattan Avenue. The city moves on. The families do not.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      The violence does not slow. On June 2, a pedestrian was killed at the RFK Bridge exit. In late May, an NYPD squad car hit two people eating outdoors on Broadway. The police said, “The NYPD squad car struck two pedestrians Monday afternoon while trying to avoid a collision with a Manhattan taxi.” Both diners and officers went to the hospital. The taxi driver got a summons for failure to yield. The investigation is ongoing. The street remains the same.

      In February, a fire from an uncertified e-bike battery killed journalist Fazil Khan in Harlem. Councilmember Keith Powers called it “a critical measure to keep New Yorkers safe. Fires from unregulated e-bike batteries have already caused too much harm, including tragic loss of life.”

      Leadership: Progress and Pressure

      State Senator Cordell Cleare has voted yes on bills to require safer street design and co-sponsored legislation to put vulnerable users first. These votes matter. But the crisis is not over. The laws are not enough until the dying stops.

      What Comes Next: Demand More

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every death is a choice made by someone in power. Call Senator Cleare. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking. The street will not change itself. Only pressure works. Only action saves lives.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect every resident.
      ▸ Where does SD 30 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 9 and assembly district AD 70.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 30?
      It includes the Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Morningside Heights, Manhattanville-West Harlem, Hamilton Heights-Sugar Hill, Harlem (South), Harlem (North), East Harlem (North), Central Park, Manhattan CB10, and Manhattan CB64. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 6, District 7, District 8, and District 9, and Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 69, AD 70, and AD 71.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 30?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians: 8 deaths and 11 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 2 deaths and 1 serious injury. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no pedestrian deaths but were involved in 28 injuries. Bicycles were involved in 36 injuries, but no deaths.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These crashes are preventable. Policy, design, and enforcement choices decide who lives and who dies on our streets.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking—not just drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
