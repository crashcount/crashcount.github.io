      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 30?

      No More Empty Chairs: Stop Traffic Deaths Now

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      SD 30: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Senate District 30, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, 22 people have died and 71 have been seriously injured on these streets (NYC crash data). The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. Just last month, a 43-year-old man was killed riding his e-bike through Central Park. In May, a 73-year-old man died crossing West 135th Street, struck by an SUV. A baby, not yet a year old, was killed on the RFK Bridge exit in June.

      The violence is relentless. Every week brings new names, new families left with empty chairs at the table. The numbers are not just numbers. They are lives cut short.

      No Safe Place—Not Even the Sidewalk

      The danger does not stop at the curb. In May, an NYPD squad car swerved to avoid a taxi and crashed into two people sitting at an outdoor dining table on Broadway and 112th (NY Daily News, New York Post). The police said, “The two diners, along with the individuals in the police vehicle, were taken to St. Luke’s Hospital in stable condition” (NY Daily News). The cab driver was given a summons for failure to yield. The investigation is ongoing. The street is a battlefield, and no one is safe.

      Leadership: Votes, Progress, and the Work Ahead

      State Senator Cordell Cleare has voted for bills to curb repeat speeders and extend school speed zones. She co-sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force the worst repeat offenders to install speed-limiting devices. She voted yes to create more safety zones and expand ignition interlock monitoring. These are steps forward. But the deaths keep coming. Policy must move faster than the next crash.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call Senator Cleare. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand that repeat speeders are taken off the road. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect every resident.
      ▸ Where does SD 30 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 9 and assembly district AD 70.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 30?
      It includes the Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Morningside Heights, Manhattanville-West Harlem, Hamilton Heights-Sugar Hill, Harlem (South), Harlem (North), East Harlem (North), Central Park, Manhattan CB10, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 6, District 7, District 8, and District 9, and Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 69, AD 70, and AD 71.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 30?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 30 were caused by SUVs and cars (9 deaths, 12 serious injuries), followed by trucks and buses (2 deaths, 1 serious injury), motorcycles and mopeds (no deaths, 30 injuries), and bikes (no deaths, 39 injuries).
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not random accidents—they are the result of choices about speed, street design, and enforcement. Every death and injury can be prevented with the right policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, remove repeat dangerous drivers, redesign streets for safety, and fund enforcement that targets the most dangerous behavior.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
