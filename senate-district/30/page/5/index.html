      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 30?

      No More Bodies in the Crosswalk

      Written July 7, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 30: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Senate District 30, the violence does not stop. Since 2022, 20 people have died in crashes. Sixty-seven more were seriously injured. Nearly 4,000 have been hurt. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about the weather or the time of day. They only rise.

      Just last month, an NYPD squad car struck two people eating outdoors in Morningside Heights. The police swerved to avoid a taxi. The taxi driver got a summons for failure to yield. The diners went to the hospital. The police did too. The investigation is not over. The story is not new. The Daily News reported that “the NYPD squad car struck two pedestrians Monday afternoon while trying to avoid a collision with a Manhattan taxi.” The city keeps building outdoor dining. The cars keep coming.

      In February, a Harlem apartment burned. A lithium-ion battery exploded. A young journalist died. Twenty-two others were hurt. Councilmember Keith Powers said, “Fires from unregulated e-bike batteries have already caused too much harm, including tragic loss of life.”

      The Numbers That Do Not Lie

      SUVs and cars did most of the killing: 8 deaths, 11 serious injuries, 607 other injuries. Trucks and buses killed 2. Motorcycles and mopeds hurt dozens. Bikes were not blameless, but the toll is smaller. The bodies pile up at intersections, on crosswalks, in the middle of the block. Children, elders, workers. No one is safe.

      What Has Senator Cleare Done?

      Senator Cordell Cleare has not been silent. She voted yes on a bill to force safer street design for all users, not just drivers. The bill passed. It is a start. She also co-sponsored a law to require every new project with public money to consider the safety of people on foot and bike. These are not empty gestures. But the blood on the street says more is needed.

      What Next? Who Will Act?

      Every day, someone else is hit. The city can lower speed limits. The state can force the worst repeat offenders off the road. The law can change. But only if you demand it. Call Senator Cleare. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Do not wait for another body in the road.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York.
      ▸ Where does SD 30 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 9 and assembly district AD 70.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 30?
      It includes the Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Morningside Heights, Manhattanville-West Harlem, Hamilton Heights-Sugar Hill, Harlem (South), Harlem (North), East Harlem (North), Central Park, Manhattan CB10, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 6, District 7, District 8, and District 9, and Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 69, AD 70, and AD 71.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 30?
      Cars and trucks caused 10 deaths and 12 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no deaths but several injuries. Bikes were involved in 36 crashes, with no deaths and no serious injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not random acts of fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
