      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 30?

      Blood on the Crosswalk: Demand Action Before More Lives Are Lost

      Written July 6, 2025

      SD 30: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 30, the street is a gauntlet. Since 2022, 20 people have died and 67 have been seriously injured in crashes (NYC crash data). In the last year alone, deaths tripled compared to the year before. Three people are dead since January.

      The bodies are not numbers. A cyclist, ejected and bleeding, on E 135th Street. A pedestrian crushed on the Harlem River Drive. A man on a scooter, killed by a fleeing driver near St. Nicholas Avenue (Scooter rider killed fleeing traffic stop). The names fade. The pain does not.

      No Shelter for the Vulnerable

      Children, elders, and people on foot or bike pay the highest price. In the past twelve months, two children died. One elder, crossing at night, did not make it home. SUVs and trucks do the most damage. Cars and SUVs killed 8 people and injured over 600. Trucks and buses killed 2. Bikes, mopeds, and motorcycles left dozens more hurt.

      The street does not forgive. “Fires from unregulated e-bike batteries have already caused too much harm, including tragic loss of life,” said Councilmember Keith Powers. But the real fire is on the asphalt, where speed and steel meet flesh.

      Leadership: Progress and Pressure

      State Senator Cordell Cleare has voted for safer streets. She backed a bill to require complete street design—roads built for people, not just cars. She co-sponsored another to force every project with public money to put safety first (co-sponsored safety bill). These are steps. But the blood on the crosswalk says it is not enough.

      The city and state have the power to lower speed limits, redesign deadly corners, and rein in repeat offenders. The tools are there. The clock is running.

      Call to Action: Demand More

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. Call Senator Cleare. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit, real street redesigns, and an end to the carnage. The street belongs to the living. Make them prove it.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 30 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does SD 30 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 9 and assembly district AD 70.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 30?
      It includes the Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Morningside Heights, Manhattanville-West Harlem, Hamilton Heights-Sugar Hill, Harlem (South), Harlem (North), East Harlem (North), Central Park, Manhattan CB10, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 6, District 7, District 8, and District 9, and Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 69, AD 70, and AD 71.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 30?
      Cars and SUVs killed 8 people and injured over 600. Trucks and buses killed 2. Bikes were involved in 36 injury crashes, and motorcycles and mopeds caused dozens of injuries (NYC crash data).
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Every death and serious injury is a failure of policy, design, or enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous roads, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking instead of blaming them.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      S 9718
      Cleare votes yes, boosting street safety with complete street design.
      ▸

      Senate Bill S 9718 passed. It pushes for complete street design. The aim: safer roads for everyone. Pedestrians, cyclists, and passengers stand to gain. The vote split. Some senators said no. But the bill moved forward. Streets may change.

      Senate Bill S 9718, titled "Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles," advanced through the Senate with committee votes on May 28 and June 3, 2024. The bill, sponsored by Sean Ryan (District 61) and co-sponsored by Liz Krueger, Monica Martinez, Jack M. Martins, Anthony H. Palumbo, and Julia Salazar, passed both votes despite opposition. The measure calls for redesigning streets to protect all users, not just drivers. The vote was not unanimous—several senators voted no, but the majority carried it through. The bill’s focus is clear: safer streets for people on foot, on bikes, and in vehicles. No safety analyst note was provided, but the intent is to cut danger at the curb and crosswalk.
