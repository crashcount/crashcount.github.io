      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 30?

      Harlem Streets Are Killing Fields—How Many More Must Die?

      Written July 28, 2025 (Revised August 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed citation formatting, and deduped citations.)

      SD 30: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 28, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      Ten people killed. Over 1,200 injured. In the last year alone, the streets of Senate District 30 have not been safe. A 73-year-old man died crossing 135th Street. A cyclist was crushed by a bus on Lenox Avenue. A pedestrian was left dead on Harlem River Drive. The numbers do not flinch: 24 deaths, 4,168 injuries, 72 serious injuries since 2022 (NYC crash data).

      Just this May, an NYPD squad car swerved to avoid a taxi and plowed into two people sitting outside on Broadway. The police and the diners went to the hospital. The cab driver got a summons for failing to yield. “The chaos erupted as the police cruiser, with lights flashing and sirens blaring, was traveling north on Broadway… when a cab heading south attempted a leisurely left turn onto West 112th Street,” said an NYPD spokesperson.

      Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and cars do the most harm. They killed 10 people and injured 655 more. Trucks and buses killed 2, motorcycles and mopeds injured 31, and bikes were involved in 43 injury cases. The victims are often the most vulnerable: children, the elderly, people just trying to cross the street or ride a bike. The city keeps counting bodies. The pain is not abstract. “Kirk was taken from me just as we were starting our new life together. He was the love of my life,” said Shauntea Weaver, after a drunk teen killed her fiancé in a wrong-way crash.

      What Has Senator Cleare Done?

      Senator Cordell Cleare has voted for safety. She co-sponsored and voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed-limiting devices (Stop Super Speeders Act). She also voted to extend school speed zones, a move to protect children on their way to class. These are steps, but the blood on the street says more is needed. The deaths keep coming.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call Senator Cleare. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat speeders. The city has the power. The bodies on the street are proof that waiting is not an option.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 30 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does SD 30 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 9 and assembly district AD 70.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 30?
      It includes the Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Morningside Heights, Manhattanville-West Harlem, Hamilton Heights-Sugar Hill, Harlem (South), Harlem (North), East Harlem (North), Central Park, Manhattan CB10, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 6, District 7, District 8, and District 9, and Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 69, AD 70, and AD 71.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 30?
      Cars and trucks killed 12 people and injured 655. Motorcycles and mopeds injured 31. Bikes were involved in 43 injury cases (NYC crash data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with lower speeds, better street design, and real enforcement. The same deadly patterns repeat year after year.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, support speed cameras, pass laws against repeat dangerous drivers, and fund safer street redesigns. They can also push for enforcement that targets reckless driving, not pedestrians or cyclists.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
