      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 36?

      Six Dead in a Year—Bronx Streets Still Bleed, Lawmakers Still Stall

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 36: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Toll in SD 36: Lives Lost, Families Shaken

      The Bronx does not forget. In the last twelve months, six people have died and 942 have been injured in crashes across Senate District 36. Nine of those injuries were serious—lives changed in a heartbeat. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, parents, children.

      Just days ago, a 71-year-old woman died in Co-op City. A 79-year-old driver crashed into two cars and a pole. “I saw one lady was out on the ground. They was giving her medical attention, checking her body. She was laid out,” said Samuel Cherry. Seven others were hurt. The street was quiet, except for the sirens.

      Patterns That Kill: Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and cars do most of the harm. In three years, they killed six people and injured over 465 more on foot or bike. Trucks and buses killed one. Motorcycles and mopeds, four injuries. Bikes, four. The numbers do not lie. The weight of steel falls hardest on the unprotected.

      Pedestrians die crossing with the light, waiting at the curb, or just walking home. Cyclists are crushed by sedans and SUVs. The cause is always the same: a system that lets it happen again and again.

      Senator Bailey: Action and the Road Ahead

      Senator Jamaal Bailey has voted for safety. He backed the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit. He voted yes to extend school speed zones. He co-sponsored bills to create safety zones and expand ignition interlock monitoring. But the deaths keep coming. The work is not done.

      “The cause of the crash was not immediately known,” police said. But the cause is always known. Streets built for speed. Laws that let the reckless drive again. Enforcement that falls hardest on the least protected.

      Call your leaders. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real action against repeat offenders. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 36 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does SD 36 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 12 and assembly district AD 83.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 36?
      It includes the Co-Op City, Pelham Gardens, Allerton, Williamsbridge-Olinville, Eastchester-Edenwald-Baychester, Wakefield-Woodlawn, Bronx CB12, and Bronx CB11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 11, District 12, and District 13, and Assembly Districts AD 80, AD 81, AD 82, AD 83, and AD 89.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 36?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 6 deaths and 475 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 8 injuries. Bikes were involved in 4 injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and action against repeat dangerous drivers can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, pass laws to stop repeat offenders, fund safer street designs, and ensure enforcement targets the most dangerous driving.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
