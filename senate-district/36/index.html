      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 36?

      Blood on Bailey’s Watch: Streets Still Kill

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised July 7, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      SD 36: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 18, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Senate District 36, the numbers do not lie. Twelve people are dead. Forty-three suffered serious injuries. In three and a half years, there have been nearly 5,000 crashes. The wounds are not just numbers. They are heads split on concrete, bodies crushed at crosswalks, lives ended mid-step. A 76-year-old woman, struck by an SUV on White Plains Road, did not make it home. A 24-year-old cyclist, crushed on E 233rd Street, left a bike mangled and a family waiting for a call that never came.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay first. Cars and SUVs killed five. Trucks and buses took one more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes left others broken. The old, the young, the ones just crossing the street. In the last year alone, five people died and 864 were hurt. The pain is not spread evenly. It falls hardest on those with the least steel around them.

      What Leadership Has Done—and Not Done

      Senator Jamaal Bailey has moved on street safety. He voted yes on a bill to force safer street design for all users, not just drivers. The bill’s aim is clear: “Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles” (bill text). Bailey also co-sponsored a bill to require complete street design on projects using public money (co-sponsored legislation). But not every move protects the vulnerable. Bailey sponsored a bill to carve out traffic law exemptions for bus drivers in large co-ops, a step that could leave pedestrians and cyclists at risk (traffic law exemptions).

      The work is not done. The blood on the street says so. “An accomplice handed Aracena the gun used to shoot Campbell in the chest as the victim sat behind the wheel” (NY Daily News report). The violence does not end with the crash.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every day without safer streets is a choice. Call Senator Bailey. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesign. Demand that every carve-out for drivers is struck from the law. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      S 9718
      Bailey votes yes, boosting street safety with complete street design.
      ▸

      Senate Bill S 9718 passed. It pushes for complete street design. The aim: safer roads for everyone. Pedestrians, cyclists, and passengers stand to gain. The vote split. Some senators said no. But the bill moved forward. Streets may change.

      Senate Bill S 9718, titled "Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles," advanced through the Senate with committee votes on May 28 and June 3, 2024. The bill, sponsored by Sean Ryan (District 61) and co-sponsored by Liz Krueger, Monica Martinez, Jack M. Martins, Anthony H. Palumbo, and Julia Salazar, passed both votes despite opposition. The measure calls for redesigning streets to protect all users, not just drivers. The vote was not unanimous—several senators voted no, but the majority carried it through. The bill’s focus is clear: safer streets for people on foot, on bikes, and in vehicles. No safety analyst note was provided, but the intent is to cut danger at the curb and crosswalk.
