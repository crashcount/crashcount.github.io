      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 23?

      No More Excuses: Streets Are Killing Us

      Written June 26, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 23: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      Six dead. Nine left with wounds that will not heal. In the last year alone, SD 23 saw 1,410 people injured and 2,534 crashes (NYC Open Data). The dead are not numbers. They are the 80-year-old man struck at an intersection on Hylan Boulevard. The 64-year-old woman crushed by an SUV while crossing Mason Avenue. The 58-year-old man killed by a moped on Targee Street. A 17-year-old, helmet on, dead on the Belt Parkway (NYC Open Data).

      Most victims are on foot. Most vehicles are cars and SUVs. The street does not forgive. The curb is no shield. The crosswalk is a promise broken again and again.

      Leadership: Votes and Silence

      State Senator Jessica Scarcella-Spanton voted yes on a bill to redesign streets for safety. But when it mattered most, she voted against renewing New York City’s speed camera program—a tool proven to save lives. She sponsored a bill to repeal congestion pricing, a move that would put more cars and more danger on city streets.

      When the MTA Board heard from the public about the loss of funding for accessible transit, Scarcella-Spanton praised the pause on congestion pricing, calling it “the wrong plan for my constituents.” The voices of the disabled, the elderly, and the families of the dead were drowned out. The money for ramps and elevators vanished. The risk stayed.

      The Cost of Delay

      Speed kills. Delay kills. The numbers do not lie. SUVs and cars caused eight deaths and 700 injuries to pedestrians and cyclists in this district since 2022. Trucks and buses killed three more. Motorcycles and mopeds, none. Bikes, none. The street is a gauntlet for the old, the young, the walker, the cyclist.

      “We want to make sure that our maintenance workers and contractors are safe,” said MTA Bridges and Tunnels President Catherine Sheridan, as speed cameras finally came to city crossings. But on the streets of SD 23, the cameras that could save lives are still up for debate.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      Contact Senator Scarcella-Spanton. Demand a vote for life, not delay. Tell her: every day without speed cameras, every day with more cars, is another day someone does not come home. Join forces with local groups. Call. Write. Show up. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      S 9718
      Scarcella-Spanton votes yes, boosting street safety with complete street design.
      ▸

      Senate Bill S 9718 passed. It pushes for complete street design. The aim: safer roads for everyone. Pedestrians, cyclists, and passengers stand to gain. The vote split. Some senators said no. But the bill moved forward. Streets may change.

      Senate Bill S 9718, titled "Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles," advanced through the Senate with committee votes on May 28 and June 3, 2024. The bill, sponsored by Sean Ryan (District 61) and co-sponsored by Liz Krueger, Monica Martinez, Jack M. Martins, Anthony H. Palumbo, and Julia Salazar, passed both votes despite opposition. The measure calls for redesigning streets to protect all users, not just drivers. The vote was not unanimous—several senators voted no, but the majority carried it through. The bill’s focus is clear: safer streets for people on foot, on bikes, and in vehicles. No safety analyst note was provided, but the intent is to cut danger at the curb and crosswalk.
