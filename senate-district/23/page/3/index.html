      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Bay Street, Hylan, Belt: the toll in SD 23 keeps climbing

      SD 23: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 20, 2025

      On Sep 15, 2025, another crash went on the books. No fanfare. Just a case number.

      The pattern is the point

      Since 2022, 31 people have been killed and 6,399 injured in crashes across this district, in 12,276 total collisions — our analysis of NYC Open Data shows it plainly.

      The names in the data are heavy. On Mar 7, 2025, a driver in an SUV going north on Hylan Boulevard hit an 80‑year‑old man at Benton Avenue. He died at the intersection (NYC Open Data, CrashID 4797079).

      Weeks earlier, on Dec 11, 2024, a driver turning left in a 2023 SUV killed a 64‑year‑old woman crossing Mason Avenue near 242 Mason. Police recorded failure to yield by the driver (NYC Open Data, CrashID 4777953).

      On Mar 3, 2023, a pickup driver going west on Mermaid Avenue hit two people crossing with the signal at West 24th Street. A 41‑year‑old woman died; an 18‑year‑old man was hurt (NYC Open Data, CrashID 4609976).

      Streets that don’t forgive

      Some deaths sit at the door of heavy vehicles and left turns. In Sheepshead Bay on Jun 2, 2025, police said a 34‑year‑old bus driver made a left and struck an 87‑year‑old man, pinning him under the bus at East 12th Street and Avenue Z. He was taken to the hospital in critical condition (Gothamist).

      On the Belt Parkway before dawn on Nov 4, 2022, two westbound car drivers hit a 58‑year‑old man outside a crosswalk. He died at the scene (NYC Open Data, CrashID 4578958).

      Not all harm comes from lawless speed. Confusion kills too. Staten Island Borough President Vito Fossella put it bluntly about Hylan Boulevard’s bus lane signs: “That’s one accident every four days where somebody perhaps unwittingly thinks they must turn from the middle lane” (amNY). The reporting counted 32 such crashes this year tied to bad turns.

      The count this year

      In the year to date, 6 people have been killed in crashes here, up from 5 at this point last year, with 1,432 injuries in 2,311 crashes, by our cut of NYC Open Data.

      Pedestrians bear the brunt. Since 2022, drivers of sedans and SUVs account for most recorded pedestrian harm in this district’s data cuts — hundreds injured, with deaths in both groups — per our analysis of the city datasets (NYC Open Data).

      Votes, tools, and the gap

      Albany renewed NYC’s 24‑hour school‑zone speed cameras this June, but Staten Island’s own Sen. Jessica Scarcella‑Spanton voted no, as did Assembly Member Sam Pirozzolo in his chamber, according to Streetsblog. The program is a proven brake on speeding, and these votes mattered.

      At the same time, Scarcella‑Spanton voted yes in committee for the Stop Super Speeders Act (S 4045) — a bill to require intelligent speed assistance for repeat offenders (Open States). That tool targets the small set of drivers who do the most harm.

      The fixes are on the table. Sammy’s Law lets the city lower speed limits. The superspeeder bill would force chronic speeders to slow down. The record here shows why those steps cannot wait.

      What must happen next

      Lower speeds save lives. Habitual speeders need hard limits. Call your representatives and press for both — a lower default speed and passage of S 4045. The ask and contacts are laid out here.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We analyzed NYC’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4) for Senate District 23 from 2022-01-01 to 2025-09-20. We filtered by the district’s geography as defined in our site’s SD 23 boundary and counted totals for crashes, injuries, serious injuries, and deaths. Data were accessed on Sep 19–20, 2025. You can view the base crash dataset here.
      ▸ Who represents this area?
      State Senator Jessica Scarcella‑Spanton (SD 23), Assembly Member Sam Pirozzolo (AD 63), and Council Member Kamillah Hanks (District 49).
      ▸ What policies could change this now?
      Two citywide moves stand out in the record: use Sammy’s Law authority to lower more streets to 20 MPH, and pass the Stop Super Speeders Act (S 4045) to require speed limiters for repeat offenders. See details and contacts here.
      ▸ Why mention bus lane signs on Hylan?
      Because confusing signs have been linked to repeated crashes on that corridor; as Borough President Vito Fossella said, “That’s one accident every four days,” in coverage of Hylan’s bus lanes (amNY).
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
