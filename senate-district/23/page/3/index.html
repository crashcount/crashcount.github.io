      Another Body, Same Street: How Many More Will Albany Ignore?

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 23: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A man on a motorcycle. A woman crossing the street. An old man pinned under a bus. In Senate District 23, the story repeats. In the last twelve months, six people have died and over 1,500 have been injured in crashes. Thirteen suffered serious injuries. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about age. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared.

      Just last week, on July 5th, Jeremy Claudio, 34, was killed on Bay Street. He hit the door of a Toyota making a K-turn. The police said the driver was “making a K-turn from the northbound lane… into its southbound lane when the collision occurred” (amNY). Claudio died at the hospital. The driver stayed. No arrests. The street is quiet again, but the blood is not washed away.

      On June 2nd, an 87-year-old man was pinned under an MTA bus in Sheepshead Bay. He was “standing near the corner when the driver made a left onto the avenue and hit him” (Gothamist). He left in critical condition. The bus was empty. The corner was not.

      The Numbers Behind the Names

      Cars and SUVs killed eight. Trucks and buses killed three. Motorcycles and mopeds, none. Bikes, none. But the injuries run higher: over 500 hurt by cars and SUVs, dozens by trucks and buses. The pattern is clear. The danger is heavy and fast.

      Leadership: Votes, Silence, and Missed Chances

      State Senator Jessica Scarcella-Spanton has voted for some safety bills. She backed the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit (Open States). But when it mattered most, she voted against renewing the city’s speed camera program—a tool proven to save lives (Streetsblog NYC). The cameras are still here, but not because of her.

      She has also stood against congestion pricing, a policy that would cut traffic and make streets safer for everyone outside a car (amNY). The voices of the dead do not reach Albany.

      What Now? The Blood Is Not Dry

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day, another crash. Another family waits in the hospital. Call Senator Scarcella-Spanton. Demand she fight for speed cameras, safer crossings, and real change. Do not wait for another name to join the list. The street will not forgive our silence.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 23.
      ▸ Where does SD 23 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Staten Island, city council district District 49 and assembly district AD 63. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 23?
      It includes the Fort Hamilton, Gravesend (South), Coney Island-Sea Gate, Brighton Beach, Calvert Vaux Park, Sheepshead Bay-Manhattan Beach-Gerritsen Beach, St. George-New Brighton, Tompkinsville-Stapleton-Clifton-Fox Hills, Rosebank-Shore Acres-Park Hill, West New Brighton-Silver Lake-Grymes Hill, and Brooklyn CB15 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts 47, 48, 49, and 50, and Assembly Districts 45, 46, 61, 63, and 64. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 23?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 8 deaths and over 500 injuries. Trucks and buses killed 3, motorcycles and mopeds caused no pedestrian deaths, and bikes caused none. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Policies like speed cameras, safer crossings, and lower speed limits can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can support speed cameras, lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, and pass laws that hold dangerous drivers accountable.
