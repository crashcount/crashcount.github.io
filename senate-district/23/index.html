      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 23?

      No More Excuses: Staten Island Streets Are Killing Us

      Written July 13, 2025

      SD 23: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Toll: Death and Injury on Staten Island and South Brooklyn

      The streets of Senate District 23 do not forgive. Since 2022, 22 people have died in crashes here. 4,599 have been injured. Thirty-four suffered wounds so grave they are called serious injuries in the city’s ledger. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. They are the man pinned under a bus in Sheepshead Bay, the motorcyclist thrown from his bike on Bay Street, the pedestrian struck crossing Hylan Boulevard.

      Just last week, Jeremy Claudio, 34, died on Bay Street. He was riding his motorcycle. A driver made a K-turn. Claudio hit the door. He was rushed to the hospital. He did not come home. Police said, “a 40-year-old woman was operating the Toyota, making a K-turn from the northbound lane… when the collision occurred” (amNY).

      In June, an 87-year-old man was pinned under an MTA bus. He was standing near the corner. The driver turned left. The man was taken to the hospital in critical condition. Police said, “officers who responded to the scene discovered an 87-year-old man pinned under the city bus… The man was standing near the corner when the driver made a left onto the avenue and hit him” (Gothamist).

      The Record: What Senator Scarcella-Spanton Has Done

      Senator Jessica Scarcella-Spanton has voted for some safety bills. She backed school speed cameras. She voted yes on a bill for safer street design. She also voted yes to curb repeat speeders.

      But she has also stood in the way. She voted against reauthorizing the city’s speed camera program. She sponsored bills to repeal congestion pricing, a move that would keep traffic thick and streets hostile for the most vulnerable.

      The Pattern: Who Pays the Price

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. Of the deaths and injuries to pedestrians, most come from sedans and SUVs. Trucks and buses kill and maim too. Motorcycles and mopeds add to the toll. Bikes are almost never the cause of death.

      The pattern is clear. The most vulnerable—pedestrians, cyclists, the old, the young—pay the price for inaction and delay. Each crash is a policy failure. Each death is a choice made by those in power.

      Call to Action: Demand Relentless Change

      This does not have to continue. Call Senator Scarcella-Spanton. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras on every dangerous street. Demand action, not excuses. The dead cannot call. The living must.

      Scarcella-Spanton Opposes Congestion Pricing Citing Health Safety Concerns
      ▸

      Congestion pricing cuts traffic jams across Manhattan and the metro. Streets clear. Delays drop. Fewer cars mean more space for people. The city breathes. Vulnerable road users gain ground. Data shows real relief, not empty promises.

      On June 18, 2025, the Regional Plan Association released a report on congestion pricing’s impact. The study, covered by Barbara Russo-Lennon, found a 25% drop in Manhattan traffic jams, with smaller but real declines in the Bronx and New Jersey. The report states: 'Congestion pricing is delivering clear benefits, saving people time and the aggravation of being stuck in traffic.' Council Member Frank Morano and State Sen. Jessica Scarcella-Spanton criticized the program, citing Staten Island’s smaller gains and health concerns. MTA Chair Janno Lieber defended the policy, pointing to improved drive times and transit use. Safety analysts note congestion pricing reduces car traffic, lowers crash risk for pedestrians and cyclists, encourages mode shift, and supports safer, more equitable streets. The program’s future now rests with the federal courts.

      S 4045
      Scarcella-Spanton votes yes in committee, boosting street safety by curbing repeat speeders.
      ▸

      Senate passes S 4045. Drivers with too many points or camera tickets must install speed assistance tech. Law targets repeat speeders. Streets may get safer for people on foot and bike.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on May 20, June 11, and June 12, 2025. The bill requires drivers who rack up eleven or more points in two years, or six speed/red light camera tickets in one year, to install intelligent speed assistance devices. The bill summary reads: 'Requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Gounardes and colleagues pushed for action. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving and protect vulnerable road users.
