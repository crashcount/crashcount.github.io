      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 13?

      Queens SD 13: Red Lights, Broken Bodies, and the Fix We Keep Dodging

      Written August 24, 2025 (Revised August 24, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 24, 2025

      Another driver. Same ending.

      • On the Long Island Expressway, a 24‑year‑old motorcyclist hit an SUV and was run over by a second driver who kept going, police said. The SUV driver stayed. The second driver is missing. The crash was at 3:45 p.m., near 97th St. in Corona. Police named the rider: Kahyl Pena. NY Daily News.
      • Two girls, 4 and 8, crossed 37th Ave. at 73rd St. in the crosswalk. An SUV went around a stopped car, blew the red, hit them, and fled. They went to Elmhurst Hospital. NY Daily News.
      • At Northern Blvd. and 107th St., prosecutors say an off‑duty firefighter ran a red at 83 mph in a 25 zone, t‑boned a BMW, and killed 23‑year‑old Justin Diaz. BAC .156. Drugs in his system, they said. “Our shared roadways are not a racetrack,” said DA Melinda Katz. ABC7 and NY Daily News.

      In this Senate district since 2022, crashes pile up. In the last 12 months: 2,295 crashes, 1,355 injuries, 4 deaths. This year to date: 1,406 crashes, 820 injuries, 3 deaths. Younger people take the brunt. Ages 18–24 saw the most deaths in the period. NYC Open Data.

      Pedestrians are struck most by SUVs and sedans. In this area, SUVs are linked to 277 pedestrian injuries, including 5 deaths; sedans to 321 injuries. Trucks killed too. NYC Open Data.

      A woman walking outside an intersection on 37th Ave. at 90th St. was killed by a motorcycle in 2024. The dataset lists her as a 44‑year‑old pedestrian. NYC Open Data.

      The pattern doesn’t blink

      • LIE, westbound. Shoulder pass. Impact. Body thrown to the center lane. Second vehicle rolls over him. The second driver leaves. NY Daily News.
      • 37th Avenue, school‑day corner. A red light means nothing to a fleeing SUV. Two little girls on the way to grow up. EMS to Elmhurst. NY Daily News.
      • Northern and 107th. Night air. A BMW with the light. A Mercedes without it. One family at Elmhurst. Another family hires a lawyer. ABC7 and NY Daily News.

      Queens parents have begged for crossing guards after two students were killed. “You would think that this is one of the most basic ways to protect the safety of our children,” said State Senator Jessica Ramos. “And yet that seems like an insurmountable task.” Gothamist.

      Officials know what works — do they?

      Albany gave the city power to lower speeds. The city can set 20 mph on local streets. The cameras now run 24/7 through 2030. We have the tools to slow cars and catch repeat speeders. See our call to action: lower the default speed and rein in repeat offenders.

      One bill goes at the worst of the worst. The Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045) would require speed limiters for repeat violators. It moved in committee in June. Sen. Ramos co‑sponsored and voted yes on May 20; she missed a later committee vote noted as excused. Open States.

      Three corners. One fix.

      • Northern Blvd. at 107th St.: one dead, charges filed. ABC7.
      • 37th Ave. at 73rd St.: two children struck in the crosswalk; driver fled. NY Daily News.
      • LIE near 97th St.: a rider dead, a driver gone. NY Daily News.

      Slow the cars. Save the lives. Lower the default to 20 mph and force speed limiters on repeat speeders. Start here. Then keep going. Act now.

      Ramos Calls for Safer Street Design Not Criminalization
      ▸

      Delivery workers rallied against Hochul’s e-bike crackdown. Council weighs bills to license e-bikes, raise pay, and study conditions. DOT opposes registration. Advocates warn of criminalization. City Hall silent. Streets stay dangerous for those on foot and bike.

      On February 27, 2025, the City Council considered three bills: Intro 606 (register and license all e-bikes), Intro 1133 (expand minimum wage, study delivery worker conditions), and Intro 1135 (set minimum pay for grocery delivery). Intro 606 faces opposition from DOT, which says, 'there is no evidence that registration improves safety.' The matter’s summary warns that Hochul’s plan would let NYPD enforce bike lane speed limits and reclassify heavy e-bikes as mopeds, requiring licenses and registration. Council members are split: Brad Lander supports regulation targeting app companies, not workers; Jessica Ramos and Zohran Mamdani call for better street design; Michael Blake urges targeted safety solutions and more infrastructure. Advocates like Ligia Guallpa say these measures would criminalize delivery workers and create a 'ticketing-to-deportation pipeline.' The Council press office did not comment. The debate exposes a city divided on how to protect its most vulnerable road users.
