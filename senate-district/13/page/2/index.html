      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 13?

      Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in Albany

      Written August 5, 2025 (Revised August 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 5, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      Four people killed. Fifteen left with life-changing injuries. In the last year alone, the streets of Senate District 13 have not let up. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, children. Just this July, a 24-year-old motorcyclist died on the Long Island Expressway. He hit an SUV, was thrown into the road, and was run over by another driver who did not stop. Police said, “A 24-year-old motorcyclist was killed… when he crashed into an SUV and was then run over by a driver who did not remain at the scene” (NY Daily News).

      In February, a drunk off-duty firefighter sped through a red light at 83 mph and killed a 23-year-old man on his way to work. The District Attorney said, “An FDNY firefighter who was off duty is accused of driving under the influence, running a steady red light and slamming into a 23-year-old motorist, killing him, as he drove more than three times the posted speed limit… Our shared roadways are not a racetrack” (ABC7).

      Children are not spared. In May, two little girls, ages 4 and 8, were hit in a crosswalk by an SUV that ran a red light. The driver fled. The girls survived. Many do not (NY Daily News).

      The Pattern: Cars, Speed, and No Consequences

      SUVs and cars do most of the harm. In the last three years, cars and SUVs killed six people and injured hundreds more on foot or bike. Trucks and buses killed one. Motorcycles and mopeds left dozens hurt. The pattern is clear. The danger is not random. It is built into the streets, the laws, and the lack of enforcement.

      What Has Senator Ramos Done?

      State Senator Jessica Ramos has taken steps. She co-sponsored and voted for the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit (Open States). She has called for more crossing guards, safer street design, and bus lanes. She said, “You would think that this is one of the most basic ways to protect the safety of our children. And yet that seems like an insurmountable task” (Gothamist).

      But the deaths keep coming. The fixes are slow. The streets do not forgive delay.

      Act Now: Demand Action

      Call your council member. Call Senator Ramos. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat offenders. Demand safe crossings at every school.

      Every day of delay is another family broken. Do not wait for the next siren.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves the state budget, and represents districts across New York.
      ▸ Where does SD 13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 21 and assembly district AD 34.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 13?
      It includes the Jackson Heights, East Elmhurst, North Corona, Elmhurst, Corona, Queens CB3, and Queens CB4 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 21, District 22, and District 25, and Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 35, and AD 39.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 13?
      SUVs and Cars: 6 deaths, 530 minor injuries, 146 moderate injuries, 10 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 42 minor injuries, 9 moderate injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 29 minor injuries, 9 moderate injuries. Trucks and Buses: 1 death, 32 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Patterns show that speed, red-light running, and lack of safe crossings are to blame—not chance.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, fund safe crossings, enforce laws against repeat offenders, and redesign streets to protect people walking and biking.
