      Blood on Bedford: NYC Streets Kill, Politicians Stall

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 18, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 25: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Senate District 25, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-two people killed. Fifty-eight left with serious injuries. Over 5,200 hurt since 2022. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors. A 32-year-old woman crushed by an SUV on Van Buren Street in May. A 55-year-old woman struck by another SUV on Washington Avenue days later. The street does not care if you are young or old. It takes what it wants.

      SUVs and cars are the main killers. They caused seven deaths and over 250 serious or moderate injuries to pedestrians and cyclists in this district alone. Trucks and buses killed at least one. Bikes and mopeds hurt, but they do not kill like steel and speed do. NYC Open Data

      The Politics of Safety

      The city talks about safety. Sometimes it acts. Sometimes it does not. On Bedford Avenue, the city ripped out a protected bike lane after complaints and a crash involving a child and a cyclist. The mayor called it a response to “community concerns.” Council Member Lincoln Restler called it what it was: “This is just pure politics. That is not how safety decisions on our streets should be made.”

      The cost of delay is paid in blood. The city’s own data showed this stretch had the second largest number of pedestrian deaths before the lane went in. “Before this bike lane from 2018 to 2022 this is the area that had the second largest numbers of pedestrian fatalities and crashes in the city,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez.

      What Has Brisport Done?

      Senator Jabari Brisport has not been silent. He voted yes on a bill to force safer street design for all users, not just drivers. He co-sponsored a bill to use cameras to keep bike lanes clear of cars. He stood with advocates demanding congestion pricing to fund transit and cut traffic. These are steps. But the bodies keep coming.

      What Next? Demand More

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every day the city delays, another family is broken. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call Senator Brisport. Tell them: protect the vulnerable, not the parking. Build streets for people, not for cars.

      Take action now.

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees state policy for all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 25 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 36 and assembly district AD 56.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 25?
      It includes the Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Bedford-Stuyvesant (East), Ocean Hill, Brooklyn CB16, and Brooklyn CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 35, District 36, District 37, and District 41, and Assembly Districts AD 52, AD 55, AD 56, and AD 57.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 25?
      Cars and trucks caused 7 deaths and 252 serious or moderate injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths and 10 injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths and 20 injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents. The numbers show patterns—speed, street design, and enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and fund laws for safer street design, expand automated enforcement, and prioritize people over parking. They can listen to data, not just complaints.

      Flatbush Avenue Bus Lanes, Pedestrian Islands Planned
      ▸

      Flatbush Avenue will lose car lanes. Bus lanes and pedestrian islands will take their place. Fifty-five killed or badly hurt since 2019. Buses crawl. Pedestrians dodge traffic. The city moves to fix a deadly, clogged corridor.

      Gothamist reported on June 4, 2025, that New York City's Department of Transportation plans to overhaul Flatbush Avenue between Livingston Street and Grand Army Plaza. The redesign replaces two car lanes with 24/7 bus-only lanes and adds pedestrian islands. DOT officials said, 'almost 70,000 daily bus riders are stuck waiting too long for slow buses, drivers are caught in a mess of traffic and pedestrians are left crossing intersections clogged with vehicles.' Since 2019, 55 people have been killed or severely injured in crashes along this stretch. The plan removes curbside parking and bans cars from bus lanes, aiming to speed up twelve bus routes and protect people on foot. Most residents in the area do not own cars and depend on slow buses. The proposal reflects a shift toward prioritizing vulnerable road users and addressing systemic danger on one of Brooklyn's busiest corridors.
