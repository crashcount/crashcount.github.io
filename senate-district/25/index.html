      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 25?

      Hit-and-Run City: Blood on Broadway, Silence at City Hall

      Written July 14, 2025

      SD 25: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 14, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A man tried to cross Broadway at East New York Avenue just before 10 p.m. on July 3. A gray Ford hit him and kept going. He died at Brookdale Hospital. The driver vanished into the night. Police said it was the second hit-and-run in the city in 24 hours. Fifty-five pedestrians have been killed crossing city streets this year. That is eight fewer than last year, but the number is still a wound that will not close. As the Daily News reported, “A hit-and-run driver struck and killed a 36-year-old man crossing a Brooklyn street, officials said Friday.”

      On the same day, a 55-year-old motorcyclist died on the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway. He lost control and was thrown from his bike. The road was closed for hours. According to police, “The victim was zipping west on the expressway on a Kawasaki KLR650 when he lost control near Emerson Place in Clinton Hill just before 2 a.m.”

      The Numbers Behind the Names

      In the last twelve months, 12 people have died and 24 have been seriously injured in SD 25. There have been 2,677 crashes and 1,587 injuries. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, children, parents. Cars and SUVs did most of the killing—10 deaths and 14 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed one. Motorcycles and mopeds, three serious injuries. Bikes, one serious injury. The violence is steady. It does not care about the weather or the hour.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      State Senator Jabari Brisport has voted for and co-sponsored bills to curb repeat speeders, expand speed cameras, and require driver education on pedestrian and cyclist safety. He voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force the worst repeat offenders to install speed-limiting devices. He co-sponsored bills for bike lane camera enforcement and more complete street designs. But the blood keeps running. The laws are not enough. The city still waits for a 20 mph speed limit. Bike lanes are still ripped out for politics. The most vulnerable are still left to fend for themselves.

      Call to Action: Demand More, Now

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Senator Brisport. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for every person on foot or bike. Do not wait for another body in the street. The time to act is now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 25.
      ▸ Where does SD 25 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 36 and assembly district AD 56. See NYC Open Data for more.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 25?
      It includes the Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Bedford-Stuyvesant (East), Prospect Heights, Ocean Hill, Brownsville, Brooklyn CB16, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 35, District 36, District 37, and District 41, and Assembly Districts AD 52, AD 55, AD 56, and AD 57. See NYC Open Data for details.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 25?
      Cars and SUVs were responsible for most deaths and serious injuries (10 deaths, 14 serious injuries). Trucks and buses killed one. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 3 serious injuries. Bikes caused 1 serious injury. Source: NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. These deaths and injuries are preventable. They are the result of policy choices, street design, and enforcement—not random chance.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, fund safer street designs, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable.

      S 4045
      Brisport votes yes in committee, boosting street safety by curbing repeat speeders.
      ▸

      Senate passes S 4045. Drivers with too many points or camera tickets must install speed assistance tech. Law targets repeat speeders. Streets may get safer for people on foot and bike.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on May 20, June 11, and June 12, 2025. The bill requires drivers who rack up eleven or more points in two years, or six speed/red light camera tickets in one year, to install intelligent speed assistance devices. The bill summary reads: 'Requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Gounardes and colleagues pushed for action. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving and protect vulnerable road users.

      Flatbush Avenue Bus Lanes, Pedestrian Islands Planned
      ▸

      Flatbush Avenue will lose car lanes. Bus lanes and pedestrian islands will take their place. Fifty-five killed or badly hurt since 2019. Buses crawl. Pedestrians dodge traffic. The city moves to fix a deadly, clogged corridor.

      Gothamist reported on June 4, 2025, that New York City's Department of Transportation plans to overhaul Flatbush Avenue between Livingston Street and Grand Army Plaza. The redesign replaces two car lanes with 24/7 bus-only lanes and adds pedestrian islands. DOT officials said, 'almost 70,000 daily bus riders are stuck waiting too long for slow buses, drivers are caught in a mess of traffic and pedestrians are left crossing intersections clogged with vehicles.' Since 2019, 55 people have been killed or severely injured in crashes along this stretch. The plan removes curbside parking and bans cars from bus lanes, aiming to speed up twelve bus routes and protect people on foot. Most residents in the area do not own cars and depend on slow buses. The proposal reflects a shift toward prioritizing vulnerable road users and addressing systemic danger on one of Brooklyn's busiest corridors.
