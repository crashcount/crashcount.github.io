      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 25?

      Hit-and-Run City: Twelve Dead, Still No Safe Passage

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 20, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      SD 25: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Losses in SD 25

      Just two weeks ago, a 36-year-old man tried to cross Broadway at East New York Avenue. A gray Ford hit him and kept going. He died at Brookdale Hospital. The driver vanished into the night. Police are still searching. “A hit-and-run driver struck and killed a 36-year-old man crossing a Brooklyn street, officials said Friday.”

      He was not alone. In the last twelve months, 12 people have died and 1,582 have been injured on the streets of SD 25. Twenty-four of those injuries were serious—lives changed in a moment. Most victims were walking. Most drivers kept driving.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm. In this district, they killed 10 people and injured hundreds more. Trucks and buses killed one. Motorcycles and mopeds, none. Bikes, none. The street is not safe for the old, the young, or anyone in between.

      Leadership: Action and Silence

      State Senator Jabari Brisport has not stood still. He co-sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed limiters. He voted yes—twice. He also backed bills to extend school speed zones and create new safety zones. These are steps in the right direction. But the blood keeps flowing.

      The city rips out bike lanes. The drivers keep fleeing. The dead do not come back. As one advocate said, “The fight to save the Bedford Avenue safety improvements continues, and we won’t back down until everyone can get home safely.”

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every day, another family waits for a call that never should come. Call Senator Brisport. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for every person on foot or bike. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Act now. The street will not forgive delay.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 25 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does SD 25 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 36 and assembly district AD 56. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 25?
      It includes the Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Bedford-Stuyvesant (East), Prospect Heights, Ocean Hill, Brownsville, Brooklyn CB16, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 35, District 36, District 37, and District 41, and Assembly Districts AD 52, AD 55, AD 56, and AD 57. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 25?
      Cars and SUVs killed 10 people and injured hundreds. Trucks and buses killed one. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no pedestrian deaths. Bikes caused no pedestrian deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not fate. They are the result of policy, street design, and enforcement. They can be prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws to slow traffic, protect bike lanes, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable. They can fight for lower speed limits and real protection for people walking and biking.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

The 2019 law mandates the state and MTA 'shall' implement congestion pricing, not 'may.' U.S. Rep. MTA board members, caught off guard, warned that without toll revenue, modernization projects—like electric buses and ADA stations—will be deprioritized. State Sen. Jabari Brisport and Riders Alliance noted a surge in public support for the program after Hochul’s decision. Hochul cited safety and community concerns, but advocates say her move robs New Yorkers of a vital tool to fund transit and cut traffic and pollution. The program’s future remains uncertain, with vulnerable road users facing continued risk from unchecked car traffic.
