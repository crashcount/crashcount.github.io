      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 25?

      Twelve Dead, No Excuses: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      SD 25: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Losses in SD 25

      In the last twelve months, twelve people have died and twenty-four have been seriously injured on the streets of Senate District 25. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about hope or habit. They count the bodies and move on.

      On July 3rd, a 36-year-old man tried to cross Broadway at East New York Avenue. A gray Ford hit him and kept going. He died at Brookdale Hospital. The driver did not stop. “The driver of the Ford sped off, never stopping, cops said. He remained at large on Saturday.” Just another night in Brownsville.

      That same day, a 55-year-old man died on the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway. He lost control of his motorcycle and hit the concrete. “The victim was zipping west on the expressway on a Kawasaki KLR650 when he lost control near Emerson Place in Clinton Hill just before 2 a.m., cops said.”

      In the past year, SD 25 has seen 985 injuries and 1,624 crashes.

      Who Pays the Price

      The dead do not get a second chance. In this district, the most vulnerable pay first. Pedestrians and cyclists are struck by cars, SUVs, trucks, and motorcycles. SUVs and cars alone have killed ten and seriously injured fourteen pedestrians since 2022.

      The stories repeat. A woman crossing Washington Avenue. A pedestrian on Van Buren. The details change. The outcome does not.

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      Senator Jabari Brisport has voted for and co-sponsored bills to make streets safer. He backed S 9718, which demands streets be designed for all users, not just drivers. He co-sponsored S 5008, pushing for automated bike lane enforcement to keep cars out of lanes meant for cyclists. He also supports S 131, requiring complete street design on every project that takes public money.

      But the blood keeps coming. Policy on paper is not safety on the street.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Every day without change is another day someone does not come home. Call Senator Brisport. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for bike lanes and sidewalks. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 25.
      ▸ Where does SD 25 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 36 and assembly district AD 56.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 25?
      It includes the Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Bedford-Stuyvesant (East), Prospect Heights, Ocean Hill, Brownsville, Brooklyn CB16, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 35, District 36, District 37, and District 41, and Assembly Districts AD 52, AD 55, AD 56, and AD 57.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 25?
      Since 2022, SUVs and cars (including sedans) have killed 10 and seriously injured 14 pedestrians; trucks and buses have killed 1 and seriously injured 2; motorcycles and mopeds have caused 3 serious injuries; bikes have caused 1 serious injury.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The same streets see the same tragedies year after year. These deaths and injuries can be prevented with safer design, lower speeds, and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund and enforce protected bike lanes, redesign dangerous intersections, and pass laws that put people before cars. They can act now, not after another death.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
