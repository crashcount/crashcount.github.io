      Thirteen Dead, No Excuses: Senate District 25 Bleeds While Leaders Stall

      Written July 31, 2025

      SD 25: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 31, 2025

      Death on the Pavement

      Thirteen dead. Thirty seriously hurt. That’s the toll in Senate District 25 in the last year and a half. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about hope or habit. They only count the bodies and the broken.

      Just this July, a man tried to cross Broadway at East New York Avenue. A gray Ford hit him and kept going. He died at Brookdale Hospital. The driver never stopped. Police are still looking. “A hit-and-run driver struck and killed a 36-year-old man crossing a Brooklyn street,” officials said.

      A day earlier, a motorcyclist lost control on the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway. He died on the road. “The victim was zipping west on the expressway…when he lost control,” cops said.

      This is not a string of accidents. This is a pattern.

      The Usual Weapons: Cars, SUVs, Trucks

      Most deaths come from cars and SUVs. In this district, cars and trucks have killed 12, hurt 933, and left 16 with serious injuries since 2022. Motorcycles and mopeds took another life and left 24 hurt. Bikes injured 48. The street is a field of risk. The sidewalk is no refuge.

      Leadership: Votes, Bills, and the Long Road

      State Senator Jabari Brisport has voted for bills to curb repeat speeders and extend school speed zones. He co-sponsored a bill for automated bike lane enforcement. He stood with advocates for congestion pricing and transit upgrades. But the blood on the street does not care about votes. Every delay is another family waiting for a call that never should come.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call Senator Brisport. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat speeders. Demand streets built for people, not cars. The dead cannot speak. You must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 25 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does SD 25 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 36 and assembly district AD 56.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 25?
      It includes the Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Bedford-Stuyvesant (East), Prospect Heights, Ocean Hill, Brownsville, Brooklyn CB16, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 35, District 36, District 37, and District 41, and Assembly Districts AD 52, AD 55, AD 56, and AD 57.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 25?
      Cars and Trucks: 12 deaths, 933 injuries, 16 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 24 injuries, 3 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 48 injuries, 1 serious injury. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These are not random accidents. The same patterns repeat—speed, reckless driving, and streets built for cars, not people.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, target repeat dangerous drivers, and fund street redesigns that protect people instead of cars.

      Brisport Supports Safety‑Boosting 24/7 Speed Camera Expansion
      ▸

      Albany stalls on S5602. The mayor stays quiet. Speed cameras hang in the balance. Some senators push for 24/7 enforcement. Others hedge. Council members want camera cash for roads. The clock runs out. Streets stay dangerous. Lives hang on the vote.

      Senate bill S5602, sponsored by Sen. Andrew Gounardes, would expand New York City’s speed camera program to 24/7 operation. The legislative session nears its end with no clear action. State Sen. Jabari Brisport supports the bill, calling for round-the-clock enforcement. Sen. John Liu backs maintaining cameras but won’t commit to S5602. Some City Council members demand that camera revenue fund local road improvements. Assembly Member Dick Gottfried doubts the Council will send the required home rule message in time, while Assembly Member William Magnarelli promises to advance the bill if they do. Mayor Adams’ team claims safety was discussed in Albany, but the mayor himself stayed silent. The bill faces opposition over increased penalties and insurance notifications. Without action, the city’s speed cameras—and the safety of its streets—are at risk.
