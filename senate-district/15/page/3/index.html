      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Rockaway and 97th: a child hit in the crosswalk, and a district on edge

      SD 15: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 19, 2025

      Just after 8 AM on Aug 27, at Rockaway Boulevard and 97th Street, a driver in a sedan went straight and hit a 10-year-old who was crossing with the signal. Police recorded driver inexperience and following too closely. NYC Open Data crash record (CrashID 4839983).

      This Week

      • Aug 27: A driver hit a 10-year-old in the crosswalk at Rockaway Blvd and 97th St; police noted driver inexperience and following too closely. NYC Open Data (CrashID 4839983).
      • Jul 31: A Florida-plated sedan driver struck and killed a 23-year-old man near 101st Street; police recorded unsafe speed. NYC Open Data (CrashID 4832080).

      The count does not stop

      Since Jan 1, 2022, in this district, there have been 9,298 crashes, 5,441 injuries, and 37 deaths. NYC Open Data.

      Year to date, crashes are 1,638 and injuries are 1,061. Deaths are 11, up from 6 at this point last year. That is an 83.3% rise. NYC Open Data.

      Children, older adults, everyone in between—no one gets a pass. A person on a bike, 23, died at 63rd Drive and Queens Boulevard this spring. Police cited a red-light disregard and distraction. NYC Open Data (CrashID 4799953).

      Queens cases that tell the truth

      “Our roadways are not the place to settle disputes,” Queens DA Melinda Katz said after charging a BMW driver with murder for slamming into a motorcyclist in April. NY Daily News.

      At Atlantic Avenue and 87th Street in March, two people walking were killed. Police listed both as outside an intersection. Three vehicles were involved, including a moving SUV and parked cars. NYC Open Data (CrashID 4801846).

      A 73-year-old woman, crossing in a marked crosswalk at 86th Street and 107th Avenue, was killed by the driver of a turning bus. Police recorded failure to yield by the driver. NYC Open Data (CrashID 4677970).

      Who moves, and who waits

      Albany passed a bill to force help on the worst repeat speeders. The Senate’s S4045 advanced in June; Senator Joe Addabbo voted yes in committee on Jun 11 and Jun 12, 2025. NYS Senate. The Senate also extended school speed zones; Addabbo voted yes. NYS Senate.

      City leaders already have the power to lower speeds. Sammy’s Law is on the books. The city has begun dropping limits on select streets. A 20 MPH default is still a choice, not a fact. Take Action.

      Your Assembly Member here is Jenifer Rajkumar. Your Council Member is Joann Ariola. Addabbo has voted to rein in repeat speeders. Will the Assembly match it this session? NYS Senate.

      What it will take now

      Lower the speed. Stop the worst repeat offenders. The tools exist. The record is written in blood and asphalt.

      One small step you can take today: tell City Hall and Albany to slow the streets and pass the speed-limiter bill. Start here.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets on NYC Open Data (Crashes, Persons, Vehicles) filtered to Jan 1, 2022–Sep 19, 2025 and to New York State Senate District 15. Counts (crashes, injuries, deaths) and year‑to‑date comparisons come from those records as compiled for CrashCount’s SD 15 page. You can review the source datasets here: Crashes here, Persons here, and Vehicles here. Data last accessed Sep 19, 2025.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ Who represents this area?
      This is New York State Senate District 15. The State Senator is Joe Addabbo. The City Council Member is Joann Ariola (District 32). The Assembly Member is Jenifer Rajkumar (AD 38).
      ▸ What recent crashes stand out?

Senator Addabbo voted yes on S4045 in June 2025; the Assembly can act next. See details here and take action here.
