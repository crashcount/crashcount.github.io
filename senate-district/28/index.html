      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      Eleven Dead, No Excuses: Streets Still Kill in Krueger’s District

      Written June 25, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Eleven dead. Over twelve hundred injured. That’s the cost of traffic violence in Senate District 28 in the last year alone. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors. An 83-year-old woman, crossing with the signal at West 58th and Sixth, struck and killed by an SUV making a left turn. A 90-year-old woman, crushed by a taxi and an SUV on York Avenue. Two cyclists, both 67, ejected and killed on Fifth Avenue. The street does not forgive.

      Recent Crashes: No One Is Safe

      The carnage does not slow. In February, a Tesla sedan on the FDR Drive crashed at speed. The driver, a woman, was ejected and killed. On East 72nd, a pedestrian was killed by a taxi and SUV, her body broken in the crosswalk. The city’s own data shows the pattern: SUVs and cars are the main killers, with 11 deaths and hundreds of injuries. Trucks and buses follow. Bikes and mopeds injure, but they do not kill like steel and glass do. NYC Open Data

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      State Senator Liz Krueger has moved on safety. She voted yes on a bill to force safer street design for all users, not just drivers. The bill’s aim is clear: “safer streets for people on foot, on bikes, and in vehicles.” Krueger also co-sponsored a bill for automated bike lane enforcement, targeting drivers who block the only safe space for cyclists. She backed a law to rate cars for pedestrian safety, to shine a light on the danger at the curb. But the work is not done. The deaths keep coming.

      Krueger has not been silent on the failures of leadership above her. When Governor Hochul delayed congestion pricing, Krueger called it “a staggering error” and warned it would leave the streets clogged and dangerous. But bills and words are not enough. The blood on the street is not washed away by votes.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand It Now

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. Call Senator Krueger. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand automated enforcement for bike lanes. Demand that every street is built for the child crossing, not the car speeding. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Take action now.

      Congestion Pricing Tolls Face Legal Showdown
      ▸

      Tolls still hang over Third Avenue. Deadlines pass. Courts hold the answer. Fewer cars enter Manhattan. Millions flow to transit. The fight is bureaucratic, not on the street. Riders wait. The city’s pulse slows, but the outcome is uncertain.

      West Side Spirit reported on May 23, 2025, that New York’s congestion pricing tolls remain in place despite three missed federal deadlines to remove them. The article details a standoff between the MTA, New York State, and U.S. DOT Secretary Sean Duffy, who demands the tolls be taken down, threatening to withhold highway funds. The MTA argues the legality of the tolls and seeks a court injunction, stating, 'congestion pricing is legal and proper.' The system, operational since January 2025, has reduced daily vehicle entries into Manhattan’s core by 76,000 in April and raised $159 million in the first quarter. The dispute highlights tensions over funding priorities and the impact on lower-income drivers, but the courts, not drivers or pedestrians, will decide the fate of the tolls.

      Krueger Opposes Harmful Delay of Queensboro Bridge Path
      ▸

      Seven lawmakers demand Mayor Adams open the Queensboro Bridge pedestrian path. The project sits finished. Cyclists and walkers still cram into a narrow, crash-prone lane. Delays keep thousands at risk. City Hall stalls. Advocates plan protest. Danger lingers.

      On April 9, 2025, seven elected officials—including Council Members Julie Won and Julie Menin—sent a public letter demanding Mayor Adams open the long-promised Queensboro Bridge pedestrian path. The project, in the works since at least 2017, remains stalled. Cyclists and pedestrians are forced to share a cramped, hazardous lane. City Hall insists on more review, while advocates plan a protest ribbon-cutting.
