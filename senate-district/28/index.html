      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      Streets of Blood: How Many More Must Die Before We Act?

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, deduped and cleaned citations, fixed FAQ citation.)

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 28, the street does not forgive. Since January 2022, at least 26 people have died and 106 have been seriously injured in crashes (NYC Open Data). These are not just numbers—they are mothers, sons, neighbors. In the last year alone, 11 people were killed and 29 suffered life-changing wounds. The old and the young fall alike. Five people over 75 are dead. No child under 18 has died, but 43 have been hurt.

      The carnage does not slow. On February 5, a 90-year-old woman was struck and killed by an SUV and a taxi on York Avenue. She was crossing the street. She did not make it to the other side. On February 11, an 83-year-old woman was killed by a left-turning SUV at Sixth Avenue and 58th Street. She was crossing with the signal. The driver turned left. She never finished her walk.

      The Policy Response: Votes and Voices

      Senator Liz Krueger has taken steps. She voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed-limiting devices. She co-sponsored a bill to enforce bike lane safety with cameras. She backed insurance for delivery workers and pedestrians, but critics warn that insurance is not prevention. “Focusing on insurance rather than speed limits shifts responsibility away from prevention and system design,” safety analysts said. “It fails to reduce crash risk and potentially discourages mode shift by not addressing the root causes of danger for pedestrians and cyclists.” Read the full critique.

      Krueger has also spoken out against delays to congestion pricing, calling Governor Hochul’s move “a staggering error” that leaves streets clogged and dangerous. See her stance.

      The Road Ahead: Who Pays the Price?

      Cars and trucks do most of the killing. In this district, SUVs and cars caused 11 deaths and 23 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 3 and seriously injured 9. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes together caused no deaths, but bikes left 8 people with serious injuries. The street is not safe for anyone outside a car.

      The city and state have tools. They can lower speed limits. They can redesign streets. They can take away the keys from repeat offenders. But every day they wait, another family pays the price.

      Call your lawmakers. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against reckless drivers. Demand streets where the most vulnerable can cross and live.

      Do not wait for the next name to be carved into stone.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees state policy for all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4 and assembly district AD 73.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 28?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Upper East Side-Yorkville, Central Park, Manhattan CB5, Manhattan CB6, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, District 5, and District 6, and Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 75, and AD 76.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 28?
      Cars and trucks caused 14 deaths and 32 serious injuries to pedestrians in SD 28. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no deaths but 6 serious injuries. Bikes caused no deaths but 8 serious injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and real enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws to keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      ▸

      Buses crawl. Riders wait. The city weighs a busway on 34th Street. Some cheer for faster trips and safer crossings. Others fear cars will flood side streets. The board backs the plan. The street waits for change.

      West Side Spirit reported on June 15, 2025, that the NYC Department of Transportation’s plan to convert 34th Street into a busway drew mixed reactions at a Community Board 6 meeting. The board voted 31-5 in favor. Supporters called current bus speeds 'painfully slow' and urged the city to 'prioritize pedestrians and transit users.'       S 8344
      Krueger votes yes to extend school speed zones, improving child pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Senate passes S 8344. School speed zone rules in New York City get extended. Lawmakers make technical fixes. The bill keeps pressure on drivers near schools. Streets stay a little safer for kids.

      Bill S 8344, titled 'Extends provisions and makes technical corrections to school speed zones in NYC; repealer,' moved through the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. Sponsored by Senator Andrew Gounardes, the bill passed Senate votes on June 12 and June 13, and cleared the Assembly on June 17. The measure extends and corrects school speed zone laws in New York City, repealing outdated provisions. Gounardes led the push, with strong support from most Senate Democrats and a split Assembly. The bill's technical fixes aim to keep protections in place for children and other vulnerable road users near schools. No safety analyst note was provided.

      S 4045
      Krueger votes yes in committee, boosting street safety by curbing repeat speeders.
      ▸

      Senate backs S 4045. Repeat speeders face forced installation of speed assistance tech. Eleven points or six camera tickets triggers action. Law targets reckless drivers. Streets may get safer for those outside the car.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on June 11 and June 12, 2025. The bill, titled 'Relates to requiring the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices for repeated violation of maximum speed limits,' mandates these devices for drivers who rack up eleven or more points in 24 months, or six speed or red light camera tickets in a year. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving. Senators including Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, and others voted yes. The bill targets drivers with a pattern of violations, seeking to cut risk for pedestrians and cyclists by limiting repeat speeding.

      ▸

      City will rip up Fifth Avenue. Sidewalks will double. Lanes for cars will shrink. Trees, benches, and light will fill the space. Pedestrians, long squeezed, will finally get room to breathe. The city bets big on feet, not fenders.

      amNY reported on May 21, 2025, that New York City will begin a $400 million redesign of Fifth Avenue in 2028, stretching from Bryant Park to Central Park.       City Plans Busway On 34th Street
      ▸

      City will ban most cars from 34th Street. Only buses and trucks allowed. Bus speeds crawl at 3 mph. Officials promise faster rides, safer crossings. Pedestrians get shorter, brighter crosswalks. Policy echoes 14th Street’s busway success.

      Gothamist reported on May 19, 2025, that New York City plans to restrict most passenger vehicles from 34th Street, Manhattan, between Third and Ninth Avenues. The Department of Transportation aims to allow only buses and trucks, forcing cars to turn off quickly. The M34 bus, used by 28,000 daily riders, currently averages just 3 mph during rush hour.
