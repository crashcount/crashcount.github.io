      New York Streets Are Killing Our Elders—Who Will Stop It?

      Written June 20, 2025

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Bodies in the Crosswalks

      A woman, 90, struck down on York Avenue. An 83-year-old killed crossing with the light at Sixth and 58th. Two cyclists, both 67, dead on Fifth Avenue. Eleven killed in the last year. Most were over 65. The old die crossing the street. The young are left to watch. In twelve months: 1,261 injured, 35 seriously. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs killed eleven. Trucks and buses killed three. Cars, taxis, mopeds, bikes, all took their share. The city counts the bodies. The city moves on. “A 43 year-old Bronx resident…died on June 18 after flying from an e-bike and striking his head on the curb” wrote West Side Spirit. The pedestrian he hit walked away with a cut hand. The cyclist did not walk away at all.

      What Has Been Done, What Has Not

      Senator Liz Krueger has voted for safer streets. She backed a bill to force street redesigns for all users, not just drivers see S 9718. She co-sponsored a bill for automated bike lane enforcement see S 5008. She called Governor Hochul’s delay of congestion pricing “a staggering error” according to Streetsblog. She has not stopped the killing. No one has.

      What Must Come Next

      Every day, another crash. Every week, another family broken. The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has the power to build barriers and redesign streets. It has the power to act. Call Senator Krueger. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings. Demand action before another name becomes a number.

      The street does not care who you are. The street only takes.

      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4 and assembly district AD 73. See official context

      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Manhattan CB5, and Manhattan CB8 neighborhoods.       ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 28?
      Cars and trucks (including SUVs, sedans, taxis, buses, and trucks) were responsible for the most deaths and injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused fewer but still significant injuries. Bikes also injured pedestrians, but with no deaths reported. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Street design, speed limits, and enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to reduce traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, support automated enforcement, and hold drivers accountable. They can also push for protected crossings and prioritize safety over convenience.

      Congestion Pricing Tolls Face Legal Showdown
      ▸

      Tolls still hang over Third Avenue. Deadlines pass. Courts hold the answer. Fewer cars enter Manhattan. Millions flow to transit. The fight is bureaucratic, not on the street. Riders wait. The city’s pulse slows, but the outcome is uncertain.

      West Side Spirit reported on May 23, 2025, that New York’s congestion pricing tolls remain in place despite three missed federal deadlines to remove them. The article details a standoff between the MTA, New York State, and U.S. DOT Secretary Sean Duffy, who demands the tolls be taken down, threatening to withhold highway funds. The MTA argues the legality of the tolls and seeks a court injunction, stating, 'congestion pricing is legal and proper.' The system, operational since January 2025, has reduced daily vehicle entries into Manhattan’s core by 76,000 in April and raised $159 million in the first quarter. The dispute highlights tensions over funding priorities and the impact on lower-income drivers, but the courts, not drivers or pedestrians, will decide the fate of the tolls.

      Queensboro Bridge Splits Paths For Safety
      ▸

      Cyclists and walkers get space at last. Years of crowding end. The bridge’s narrow lane forced conflict. Now, riders and pedestrians move apart. The city takes a lane from cars. The danger shrinks. The span breathes easier.

      amNY reported on May 13, 2025, that New York City will separate cyclists and pedestrians on the Ed Koch-Queensboro Bridge. Starting May 18, the north outer roadway becomes bike-only, while the south outer roadway, once for vehicles, opens to pedestrians. The article notes, “Advocates have fought to open a separate pedestrian path, citing dangerous overcrowding that has led to conflicts among cyclists, pedestrians and micromobility users.” The bridge was the last city-owned East River crossing without split paths. Manhattan Community Board 6 urged the city to act, pressing DOT to open the path despite construction delays. The change doubles space for non-drivers and removes a vehicle lane, addressing years of systemic risk from forced mixing of vulnerable users.
