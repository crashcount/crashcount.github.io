      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      York and 72nd. A body on the asphalt. A city that won’t slow down.

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      Just before 8 PM on Sep 4, at 5th Ave and E 66th, a bus driver turned right and hit a 61‑year‑old woman in the crosswalk. She was taken away conscious, with crush injuries to her leg (NYC Open Data).

      In the past month, a taxi driver hit and killed a person crossing near York Ave and E 72nd at about 5 AM on Aug 30 (NYC Open Data). Police later arrested a 71‑year‑old driver in connection with that fatal crash, according to amNY.

      Since Jan 1, 2022, this Senate district has recorded 37 deaths, 136 serious injuries, and 11,348 crashes (NYC Open Data). The toll does not let up.

      Two hits in one week, same neighborhood

      • Sep 4: A bus driver turning right hit a woman in the crosswalk at E 66th and 5th Ave (NYC Open Data).
      • Aug 30: A taxi driver hit and killed a person near York Ave and E 72nd (NYC Open Data).

      This is the pattern. Year‑to‑date, deaths here stand at 13, versus 6 at this point last year (NYC Open Data).

      Streets built for speed, bodies pay the price

      On Feb 11, a driver in an SUV turned left at Avenue of the Americas and W 58th and killed an 83‑year‑old woman who was crossing with the signal (NYC Open Data). On Feb 5, a 90‑year‑old woman was hit and killed near E 72nd and York by drivers going straight (NYC Open Data).

      Each one is a person with a life cut short. The ledger only shows “killed.” Families live with the rest.

      Leaders know the gridlock. They said so.

      When Gov. Hochul delayed congestion pricing, State Sen. Liz Krueger called it “a staggering error” (Streetsblog NYC). The delay kept cars flooding Manhattan and starved transit of funding. The danger stayed on the street.

      Tools are on the table. Use them.

      Albany advanced the Stop Super Speeders Act. The Senate bill S4045 would force drivers with a record of violations to use speed limiters. Sen. Krueger co‑sponsored it and voted yes in committee (Open States). The Assembly version is A2299C. It needs a vote.

      City Hall has power too. Sammy’s Law lets New York City lower the default speed limit. A citywide 20 MPH limit is ready to go. Our own coverage explains the path and why it matters (Take Action).

      The road out is not mystery. It is will.

      • Lower the speed on every block. Enforce it.
      • Pass and implement S4045/A2299C. Stop the repeat offenders.

      A woman was hit at 5th and 66th. A man died near York and 72nd. It does not have to happen again. Act now: Take Action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What area does this report cover?
      New York State Senate District 28, which includes Midtown South–Flatiron–Union Square, Midtown–Times Square, East Midtown–Turtle Bay, the Upper East Side (Lenox Hill, Carnegie Hill, Yorkville, Roosevelt Island), and Central Park.
      ▸ How many people have been hurt or killed here since 2022?
      Since Jan 1, 2022, the district has recorded 11,348 crashes, 37 deaths, and 136 serious injuries, based on NYC Open Data crash records analyzed for SD 28.
      ▸ Which recent crashes stand out?
      Sep 4, 2025: a bus driver turning right hit a 61‑year‑old woman at 5th Ave and E 66th. Aug 30, 2025: a taxi driver hit and killed a person crossing near York Ave and E 72nd. Both are recorded in NYC’s crash database.
      ▸ Where do local officials stand on speeding reforms?
      State Sen. Liz Krueger co‑sponsored and voted yes on S4045 to require speed limiters for repeat violators. The Assembly version is A2299C and awaits action. The city can also lower the default speed limit under Sammy’s Law; see our Take Action page.
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets for Crashes, Persons, and Vehicles, filtered to incidents from 2022‑01‑01 to 2025‑09‑18 within Senate District 28. We counted total crashes, deaths (persons where injury severity = fatal), and serious injuries (as reported in the Persons table). Data was accessed Sep 18, 2025. You can view the base datasets here, with linked Persons and Vehicles.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      S 4045 Krueger votes yes in committee, boosting street safety by curbing repeat speeders.
      ▸

      Jun 12 - Senate backs S 4045. Repeat speeders face forced installation of speed assistance tech. Eleven points or six camera tickets triggers action. Law targets reckless drivers. Streets may get safer for those outside the car.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on June 11 and June 12, 2025. The bill, titled 'Relates to requiring the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices for repeated violation of maximum speed limits,' mandates these devices for drivers who rack up eleven or more points in 24 months, or six speed or red light camera tickets in a year. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving. Senators including Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, and others voted yes. The bill targets drivers with a pattern of violations, seeking to cut risk for pedestrians and cyclists by limiting repeat speeding.

      City Eyes 34th Street Car Ban Plan
      ▸

      Jun 8 - City wants cars off 34th Street. Residents fear traffic will flood side streets. Bus riders crawl at five miles an hour. Officials tout safety gains from 14th Street. Tension rises between speed, safety, and neighborhood calm.

      The New York Post (2025-06-08) reports that the city proposes restricting cars on 34th Street to create a busway between 3rd and 9th Avenues. Residents worry rerouted vehicles will jam local streets and worsen safety. Jessica Lavoie of the Murray Hill Neighborhood Association warns, "diverting traffic from this critical corridor onto narrow residential streets would lead to increased congestion, safety hazards, and diminished quality of life." The Department of Transportation aims to replicate the 14th Street busway, which "reduced congestion, sped up bus travel and curbed accidents."
