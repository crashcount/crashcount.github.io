      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      Another Name on the Stone: How Many Deaths Before Action?

      Written July 6, 2025

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 28, the toll does not stop. Twenty-six people have died on these streets since 2022. Four thousand twenty-one have been injured. One hundred five suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again. These are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. The old man struck crossing with the light. The cyclist thrown from his bike. The child who never made it home.

      Just last month, a 47-year-old man died in Central Park after his e-bike collided with a pedestrian. “Nico Garcia was thrown from his electric bike by the impact and slammed his head on the curb,” reported the NY Daily News. The pedestrian walked away with a cut hand. Nico Garcia did not walk away at all.

      On East 72nd Street, a 90-year-old woman was killed by an SUV and a taxi. She was crossing. The drivers kept going straight. She did not.

      The Policy Response: Promises and Pressure

      Senator Liz Krueger has taken some steps. She co-sponsored a bill to enforce bike lane safety with cameras, targeting drivers who block lanes and endanger cyclists. She also backed a bill to create a public safety rating for cars, exposing which vehicles are most deadly to people on foot. She voted yes on a law to require safer street designs for all users, not just drivers. But the pace is slow. The danger is not.

      When Governor Hochul delayed congestion pricing, Krueger called it “a staggering error” and warned it would keep streets clogged and unsafe as reported in Streetsblog. She joined other lawmakers demanding the city open the Queensboro Bridge pedestrian path, writing, “Any further delays to this project that is otherwise ready to open will unnecessarily put at risk the thousands of New Yorkers who cycle and walk the current shared path every day” as quoted in Streetsblog.

      But the deaths keep coming.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Senator Krueger. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand the Stop Super Speeders Act. Demand streets that put people first. Every day of delay is another name on a stone.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect all residents. See how the Senate works.
      ▸ Where does SD 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4 and assembly district AD 73. See collision data for SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 28?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Upper East Side-Yorkville, Central Park, Manhattan CB5, Manhattan CB6, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, District 5, and District 6, and Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 75, and AD 76. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 28?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for the most harm, with 14 deaths and 31 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 6 serious injuries but no deaths. Bikes were involved in 8 serious injuries and no deaths. See vehicle crash data.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, safer street designs, and automated enforcement. They can also push for funding and policies that protect people walking and biking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
