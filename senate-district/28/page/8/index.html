      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      SD 28: Death at the Crosswalk, Silence at City Hall

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      York Avenue does not forgive. On February 5, a 90‑year‑old woman was hit and killed at E 72nd Street. Police coded the cause as driver distraction. Two southbound vehicles, an SUV and a taxi, kept going straight. She did not. The state calls this CrashID 4790720. The record says “crush injuries.”

      On February 11, at Sixth Avenue and West 58th, an 83‑year‑old woman crossing with the signal was struck by a left‑turning SUV. She died there. The city database marks it as a fatal pedestrian strike at W 58th and Sixth. No citation text. Just “making left turn.”

      Three blocks mean nothing to a bumper. A life is a line in a table.

      Two cyclists. One parked SUV. Both gone.

      February 18, West 51st and Fifth. Two 67‑year‑old men on e‑bikes collided with a parked SUV. Both were ejected. Both died. The vehicle shows “no damage.” The people show “head” and “internal.” This is CrashID 4796322.

      There is no note, no why. Only time, place, and bodies.

      The count climbs

      The district’s year‑to‑date toll is worse than last year: crashes up 37%, injuries up 32%, deaths up from 3 to 11. Serious injuries hold flat at 20. That’s in the city’s open data. Over the last 12 months, 13 people died here, with seniors bearing the brunt: 65–74, three dead; 75 and older, six dead.

      Pedestrians face SUVs most: 12 pedestrian deaths and hundreds of injuries tied to SUVs and sedans in this period set. Bikes hurt people too, but the scale is different. The roll‑up shows SUVs and cars linked to 910 pedestrian injury events, bikes to 185, within the same dataset.

      Numbers are clean. The corners are not.

      Steel into scaffolding

      On July 31, Madison Avenue between 84th and 85th turned to shrapnel. A car and an SUV crashed. One hit the scaffolding. Eight people were injured. None critically, police said. There’s no word on the cause. No charges yet.

      Two weeks earlier, Midtown cops opened a van after a crash and found dozens of propane tanks. The fire risk rode our streets unnoticed until metal met metal.

      Promises on paper, blood on asphalt

      Albany moved. Senator Liz Krueger voted yes in committee to advance a bill to force speed limiters on repeat violators, known as S 4045. The measure targets drivers with a pattern of tickets or points. She also backed extending the 24/7 school‑zone camera program, voting yes on S 8344. Those are votes. The bodies are here.

      On Third Avenue, advocates begged for space to live. “I don’t like riding on this avenue. It’s terrifying, it’s bad,” one said at a 2022 town hall held by Sen. Krueger. “We deserve much wider sidewalks so we can get around, not just comfortably, but safely.” The plea is on the record at Streetsblog.

      City Hall says a remake is coming to 14th Street. The goal, they told NY1, is to “improve the pedestrian experience”. Design money is lined up. Next year. The dead are tallied this year.

      What will stop the next siren?

      • Lower speeds save lives. The city has the power to set them. Use it. Make 20 mph the rule on local streets.
      • Stop the worst drivers with mandatory speed limiters for repeat offenders. The Senate bill is on the table: S 4045.

      The victims can’t wait for ribbon cuttings. Act now. Go to Take Action.

      ▸ How many people have died here this year compared to last year?
      Year‑to‑date deaths rose from 3 to 11, while crashes rose 37% and injuries 32%, per NYC Open Data’s comparative stats in the provided period.
      ▸ What kinds of vehicles are most often linked to pedestrian harm here?
      SUVs and sedans account for the largest share of pedestrian injuries and deaths in this dataset window, with 910 pedestrian injury events tied to those vehicle types, compared with 185 tied to bikes.
      ▸ What is being done by local officials now?

      Krueger Supports Safety Boosting Third Avenue Redesign and Enforcement
      ▸

      The city will redraw Third Avenue. Fewer car lanes. More bus lanes. A protected bike path. Wider sidewalks. Advocates and Council Member Gale Brewer pushed for change. DOT’s Ed Pincar says a proposal is coming. NYPD and Sen. Krueger focus on bike enforcement.

      On July 27, 2022, city officials announced at a virtual town hall that a long-awaited redesign of Manhattan’s Third Avenue is in the works. The event, hosted by State Senator Liz Krueger, featured DOT Manhattan Borough Commissioner Ed Pincar, who said, “We are looking very closely… we may be able to present a proposal maybe even later this year.” The redesign aims to transform Third Avenue into a safer boulevard with fewer car lanes, more bus lanes, a separated bike path, and wider sidewalks. Council Member Gale Brewer and advocates like Paul Krikler have long demanded these changes. Krueger and the NYPD, however, focused on enforcement against cyclists and e-bike riders, citing constituent concerns. Krueger has previously pushed for harsher penalties for electric-vehicle riders. NYPD data shows more enforcement against cyclists than drivers, even though drivers cause most injuries. No formal safety analysis was provided.
