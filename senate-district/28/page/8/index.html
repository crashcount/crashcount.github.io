      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      Blood on the Asphalt: How Many More Must Die Before Streets Change?

      Written June 25, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations for deduplication and order.)

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 28, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-six people killed. Over 100 seriously injured. Nearly 8,000 crashes since 2022. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, parents, children. A 90-year-old woman, crushed while crossing York Avenue. An 83-year-old, struck in a crosswalk on Sixth. Two cyclists, ejected and killed on Fifth Avenue. The street does not forgive. The street does not care.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs do most of the damage. In the last three years, they killed 11, seriously injured 21, and hurt hundreds more. Trucks and buses killed 3. Motorcycles and mopeds left 6 with serious wounds. Even bikes, in a city built for cars, left 8 with life-changing injuries. The pattern is clear. The bigger the vehicle, the greater the harm. The street is a battlefield, and the unprotected lose.

      Leadership: Words, Votes, and the Waiting

      Senator Liz Krueger has not been silent. She voted yes on a bill to force safer street designs. She co-sponsored a bill for automated bike lane enforcement. She backed a plan to rate cars for pedestrian safety. She called Governor Hochul’s delay of congestion pricing “a staggering error”. She joined the push to open the Queensboro Bridge path, warning that delay “will unnecessarily put at risk the thousands of New Yorkers who cycle and walk the current shared path every day.”

      But bills and letters do not stop cars. The deaths keep coming. The city delays. The state debates. The street stays the same.

      What Comes Next

      The crisis is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by those in power. Contact Senator Krueger. Contact your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, open bridges, and enforcement that targets the reckless—not the vulnerable. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      The dead cannot speak. You can.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for making and passing state laws. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect the entire state.
      ▸ Where does SD 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4 and assembly district AD 73.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 28?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Manhattan CB5, and Manhattan CB8 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, District 5, and District 6, and Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 75, and AD 76.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 28?
      Cars and SUVs killed 11 and caused 21 serious injuries to pedestrians in SD 28. Trucks and buses killed 3. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 6 serious injuries. Bikes caused 8 serious injuries, but no deaths.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by city planners, by lawmakers. Prevention is possible with safer streets, lower speeds, and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, open pedestrian paths, and target reckless drivers. They can refuse to delay or water down safety measures.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
