      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      Death on Repeat: How Many More Before They Act?

      Written June 22, 2025 (Revised June 22, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, clarified and cited vehicle injury data, deduped and sorted citations.)

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 22, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A woman, age 90, struck down by a taxi and SUV on York Avenue. An 83-year-old, killed by an SUV turning left as she crossed with the signal at Sixth Avenue. Two cyclists, both 67, ejected and killed in a crash with an SUV on Fifth Avenue. In the last year alone, 11 people died and 34 suffered serious injuries on the streets of Senate District 28. The old, the young, the ones just trying to cross the street. The numbers do not flinch: 1,255 injured, 2,279 crashes in twelve months. The city calls them accidents. The families know better.

      Who Pays the Price

      The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors. They are the ones who walk, ride, and wait for the light to change. Most killed are over 65. Some are children. The streets do not forgive. Cars and SUVs are the main killers—11 deaths, 21 serious injuries—but trucks, bikes, and mopeds all leave bodies behind. The city records the wounds. The pavement remembers the rest.

      What Has Liz Krueger Done?

      Senator Liz Krueger has moved on some fronts. She voted yes on a bill to force safer street designs, aiming to protect everyone, not just drivers. The bill calls for “redesigning streets to protect all users, not just drivers” in the legislation. She co-sponsored a bill to put cameras in bike lanes, targeting drivers who block and endanger cyclists with enforcement. She backed a measure to rate cars for pedestrian safety, to show who is most at risk with a new rating system. When Governor Hochul delayed congestion pricing, Krueger called it “a staggering error” in her statement. She joined others to demand the Queensboro Bridge pedestrian path open now, warning, “Any further delays…will unnecessarily put at risk the thousands of New Yorkers who cycle and walk the current shared path every day” in a public letter.

      But the deaths keep coming. The bills move slow. The streets do not wait.

      What Next? The Streets Demand More

      The crisis is not over. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It can redesign every deadly intersection. It can fund protected bike lanes and open the bridges. It can act now. Or it can wait for the next name, the next number, the next family left with nothing but a memory and a patch of blood on the curb.

      Call your leaders. Demand action. The dead cannot speak. You can. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts across New York.
      ▸ Where does SD 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4 and assembly district AD 73.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 28?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Manhattan CB5, and Manhattan CB8 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, District 5, and District 6, and Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 75, and AD 76.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 28?
      Cars and SUVs caused 11 deaths and 21 serious injuries to pedestrians in SD 28. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 6 serious injuries. Bikes caused 8 serious injuries but no deaths. Source: NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are not random. Most deaths and injuries in SD 28 happen on streets that could be made safer with lower speeds, better design, and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, open protected bike and pedestrian paths, and pass laws that hold dangerous drivers accountable.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Krueger Supports Safety Boosting Third Avenue Redesign and Enforcement
      ▸

      The city will redraw Third Avenue. Fewer car lanes. More bus lanes. A protected bike path. Wider sidewalks. Advocates and Council Member Gale Brewer pushed for change. DOT’s Ed Pincar says a proposal is coming. NYPD and Sen. Krueger focus on bike enforcement.

      On July 27, 2022, city officials announced at a virtual town hall that a long-awaited redesign of Manhattan’s Third Avenue is in the works. The event, hosted by State Senator Liz Krueger, featured DOT Manhattan Borough Commissioner Ed Pincar, who said, “We are looking very closely… we may be able to present a proposal maybe even later this year.” The redesign aims to transform Third Avenue into a safer boulevard with fewer car lanes, more bus lanes, a separated bike path, and wider sidewalks. Council Member Gale Brewer and advocates like Paul Krikler have long demanded these changes. Krueger and the NYPD, however, focused on enforcement against cyclists and e-bike riders, citing constituent concerns. Krueger has previously pushed for harsher penalties for electric-vehicle riders. NYPD data shows more enforcement against cyclists than drivers, even though drivers cause most injuries. No formal safety analysis was provided.
