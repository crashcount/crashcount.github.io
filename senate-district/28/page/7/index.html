      Blood in Her District: Still No Safe Passage

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 20, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned citations, and ensured proper inline links.)

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Senate District 28, the numbers do not lie. Since January 2022, 26 people have died and 4,176 have been injured in 8,183 crashes. Among them: children, elders, cyclists, pedestrians. One hundred six suffered serious injuries. Each number is a body. Each body is a life changed or ended.

      Just this year, deaths have more than tripled compared to last year. Ten people are already gone. Serious injuries are down, but the dead do not heal.

      The Recent Blood on the Streets

      On February 5, a 90-year-old woman was killed crossing York Avenue. She was struck by an SUV and a taxi. The crash report lists “driver inattention/distraction” as a cause. She died in the street, crushed. See NYC Open Data crash records.

      On February 11, an 83-year-old woman was killed at the intersection of Avenue of the Americas and 58th. She was crossing with the signal. The SUV turned left. She did not make it to the other side. See NYC Open Data crash records.

      On February 4, a woman was ejected and killed on the FDR Drive. The cause: unsafe speed. The car was demolished. See NYC Open Data crash records.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      State Senator Liz Krueger has taken steps. She co-sponsored and voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed limiters. The bill targets those who rack up violations and points, aiming to keep killers off the road. As the bill summary states, the legislation targets drivers with a pattern of violations, seeking to cut risk for pedestrians and cyclists by limiting repeat speeding.

      Krueger also backed the extension of school speed zones, a move to protect children walking to class. She voted yes to extend school speed zones, improving child pedestrian safety.

      But the carnage continues. Bills move slow. Streets change slower. The dead do not wait.

      The Fight Ahead

      The bodies pile up. The numbers grow. The only thing that stops this is action. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand the city use the power it has. Demand the state pass the Stop Super Speeders Act.

      Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees state policy for all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4 and assembly district AD 73.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 28?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Upper East Side-Yorkville, Central Park, Manhattan CB5, Manhattan CB6, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, District 5, and District 6, and Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 75, and AD 76.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 28?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 11 deaths and 830 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 71 incidents. Bikes were involved in 168 incidents, with no deaths but 8 serious injuries. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just accidents?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement against repeat dangerous drivers can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, require speed limiters for repeat offenders, and fund safer street redesigns. They can also push city agencies to act faster and hold them accountable.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Krueger Supports Safety Boosting Third Avenue Redesign Plan
      ▸

      DOT will present its Third Avenue redesign. Seven lanes for cars have left little for walkers, cyclists, or bus riders. Since 2019: one pedestrian killed, 198 injured. Advocates demand fewer car lanes, protected bike paths, and wider sidewalks. Change is overdue.

      ""I don't like riding on this avenue. It's terrifying, it's bad," Paul Krikler said during a July town hall hearing held by state Sen. Liz Krueger (D-Upper East Side). "We deserve much wider sidewalks so we can get around, not just comfortably, but safely."" -- Liz Krueger

      On October 12, 2022, the Department of Transportation (DOT) will unveil its Third Avenue redesign proposal to a Manhattan panel. The plan marks the Adams administration's first major street project. The current avenue, with seven northbound car lanes, has seen 703 crashes, one pedestrian death, and 198 injuries since 2019. The matter summary calls for 'reallocation of street space, prioritizing cycling, walking, and public transit over cars.' Advocates like Kate Fillin-Yeh (NACTO) and Paul Krikler (Community Board 8) urge protected bike lanes, busways, and wider sidewalks. Krikler says, 'We deserve much wider sidewalks so we can get around, not just comfortably, but safely.' Transportation Alternatives and Community Board 6 echo these demands. The redesign aims to shift space from cars to people, following models like Paris's Rue de Rivoli. The panel's response will shape the future of vulnerable road user safety on Third Avenue.
