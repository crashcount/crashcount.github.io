      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      No More Excuses: Deadly Streets, Deadlier Delays

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, tightened prose, and cleaned citations.)

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Senate District 28, the numbers do not lie. Eleven people killed. Thirty seriously hurt. In the last twelve months alone, 1,356 injured, 2,486 crashes. The old and the young, all at risk. Five people over 75 lost their lives. Three more between 65 and 74. A woman, 90, struck crossing York Avenue, did not make it home.

      SUVs and trucks do the most damage. In this district, SUVs killed eleven, trucks killed two, buses killed one. Bikes and mopeds hurt, but the deadliest machines are always bigger, heavier, faster. The street is not safe for anyone on foot or on two wheels.

      The Policy Response: Promises and Pressure

      Senator Liz Krueger has moved on some fronts. She voted yes on a bill to force safer street designs, aiming to protect all users, not just drivers (S 9718). She co-sponsored a bill for automated bike lane enforcement, to keep cars out of the lanes meant for cyclists (S 5008). She backed a bill for public pedestrian safety ratings for vehicles (S 1675). These are steps, not leaps.

      Krueger called Governor Hochul’s delay of congestion pricing “a staggering error”—a move that keeps streets clogged and dangerous, and leaves transit underfunded. “A staggering error,” she said. She joined others demanding the Queensboro Bridge pedestrian path open now, warning that delay “will unnecessarily put at risk the thousands of New Yorkers who cycle and walk the current shared path every day” warned Krueger and others.

      But the deaths keep coming. The bills move slow. The streets stay dangerous.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure of policy, not fate. The city and state have the tools. They must use them. Lower the speed limit. Build real protection for people outside cars. Enforce the laws that matter—against the drivers who kill, not the people who walk or ride.

      Call your leaders. Demand action. Do not let another name become a number. The time for patience is over.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect everything from street safety to transit funding.
      ▸ Where does SD 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4 and assembly district AD 73.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 28?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Upper East Side-Yorkville, Central Park, Manhattan CB5, Manhattan CB6, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, District 5, and District 6, and Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 75, and AD 76.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 28?
      Cars and trucks (including SUVs and buses) caused the most harm: 14 deaths and hundreds of injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds injured dozens, while bikes caused injuries but no deaths. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Every crash is preventable. The data shows patterns—speed, heavy vehicles, unsafe streets—not random chance.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, enforce laws against reckless drivers, and support bills that protect people outside cars. They can act now, not wait.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
