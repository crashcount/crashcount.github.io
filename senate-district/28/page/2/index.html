      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      Blood on the Crosswalk. Silence in Albany.

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      Eleven dead. Twenty-nine left with injuries that change a life. In the last year alone, Senate District 28 saw 1,220 people hurt in 2,250 crashes. The old, the young, the ones just trying to cross the street. Five people over 75 did not make it home. Three more between 65 and 74. A 90-year-old woman was killed crossing York Avenue. She was struck by an SUV and a taxi. She never stood a chance. NYC crash data

      On February 11, an 83-year-old woman was hit by an SUV while crossing with the signal at West 58th and Sixth. She died. The driver was making a left turn. No one called it a crime. No one called it anything at all.

      In Central Park, a man on an e-bike hit a pedestrian in a crosswalk. He flew from his bike, struck his head on the curb, and died. The pedestrian walked away with a cut. The city keeps count. The dead do not. As reported by West Side Spirit, “A 43 year-old Bronx resident…died on June 18 after flying from an e-bike and striking his head on the curb.”

      Who Bears the Weight

      Most deaths come from cars and trucks. SUVs killed eleven. Trucks killed two. Taxis, sedans, and vans add to the toll. Bikes and mopeds hurt, but they do not kill like steel and speed do. The numbers do not lie. The street does not forgive.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      State Senator Liz Krueger has moved on some fronts. She co-sponsored a bill for automated bike lane enforcement, aiming to keep cars out of lanes meant for cyclists. She backed a measure to post pedestrian safety ratings for vehicles. She voted yes on a bill to force safer street designs. She called Governor Hochul’s congestion pricing delay “a staggering error”, warning it would keep streets clogged and dangerous.

      But the blood keeps coming. The bills are not enough. The dead do not care about press releases.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call your council member. Call Senator Krueger. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against reckless drivers. Demand streets built for people, not for speed.

      Every day you wait, someone else does not come home.
