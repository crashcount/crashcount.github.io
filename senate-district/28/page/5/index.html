      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      Blood on the Asphalt: New Yorkers Die While Lawmakers Stall

      Written July 14, 2025

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 14, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 28, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-six people killed. Over four thousand injured. More than a hundred suffered wounds so grave they will never be the same. These are not numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the price of inaction.

      Just weeks ago, a cyclist in Central Park hit a pedestrian and died after striking his head on the curb. The pedestrian walked away with a cut hand. The cyclist did not walk away at all. The 43-year-old Bronx resident died on June 18 after flying from an e-bike and striking his head on the curb. The NYPD is still investigating. No arrests. No answers. Only another family left with a hole that will not close.

      On York Avenue, a 90-year-old woman was killed crossing the street. The crash report lists driver inattention and distraction. She was struck by an SUV and a taxi. She did not make it to the other side. No law protected her. No street design slowed the cars.

      Who Bears the Risk

      The old and the young pay the highest price. In the last twelve months, five people over 75 died. Three more between 65 and 74. Children are not spared. Forty-three injured, one seriously. The city calls these accidents. But the pattern is clear. SUVs and cars are the main killers—eleven deaths, hundreds injured. Trucks and buses killed three. Bikes and mopeds injured, but did not kill, in this period. The street is a gauntlet. The most vulnerable are left to fend for themselves.

      Leadership: Steps and Stalls

      State Senator Liz Krueger has backed some measures for safer streets. She voted yes on a bill to require speed-limiting devices for repeat dangerous drivers, a move meant to stop the worst offenders. The bill requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points or receives six speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period. She co-sponsored bills for automated bike lane enforcement and bus lane cameras. She called the governor’s congestion pricing delay “a staggering error,” warning it would keep streets clogged and dangerous (congestion pricing delay). But the blood on the street says more is needed. Every delay, every watered-down law, means another family grieves.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call Senator Krueger. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against reckless drivers. Demand streets built for people, not cars. The toll will not stop until the policies change. The dead cannot speak. You must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 28 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does SD 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4 and assembly district AD 73. Source
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 28?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Upper East Side-Yorkville, Central Park, Manhattan CB5, Manhattan CB6, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, District 5, and District 6, and Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 75, and AD 76. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 28?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 11 deaths and over 800 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds injured 71 people, while bikes injured 168 but caused no deaths in this period. Source
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      The patterns are clear: most deaths and injuries come from cars and trucks, and many crashes involve distraction or unsafe speed. These are not random accidents—they are preventable with better laws, design, and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, require speed-limiting devices for repeat offenders, fund safer street designs, and hold drivers accountable. Every delay costs lives.
