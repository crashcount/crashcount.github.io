      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      No More Excuses: Streets Are Bleeding, and Albany Looks Away

      Written August 5, 2025 (Revised August 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 5, 2025

      The Blood on the Asphalt

      Just last week, eight people were hurt when a car and SUV crashed on Madison Avenue, sending one vehicle into scaffolding. The news said, “There is no word on the cause of the crash. So far, no charges have been filed” (ABC7). No one died that morning, but the street was left broken, and so were the people who walked it.

      In the last twelve months, 12 people died and 1,562 were injured in crashes across Senate District 28. Thirty-five suffered serious injuries—the kind that do not heal. The dead are old and young. A 90-year-old woman was killed crossing York Avenue (NYC Open Data). Two cyclists, both 67, died together on Fifth Avenue. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie.

      The System Grinds On

      The violence is not random. Cars, SUVs, trucks, taxis—they do the most harm. In this district, cars and trucks injured or killed over 900 people. Bikes and mopeds hurt fewer, but the street is not safe for anyone. The city adds more lanes, more speed, more risk. The sidewalk is no refuge. The crosswalk is no shield.

      Senator Krueger: Votes and Silence

      State Senator Liz Krueger has voted for some measures that matter. She co-sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit (Open States). She voted to extend school speed zones, trying to keep children safe. She called the governor’s delay of congestion pricing “a staggering error” (Streetsblog NYC).

      But the blood keeps flowing. The bills move slow. The streets do not wait.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Call Senator Krueger. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat offenders. Demand streets built for people, not cars.

      Every day of delay is another name on the list. The street does not forgive. Neither should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws and oversees statewide policy, including traffic safety.
      ▸ Where does SD 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4 and assembly district AD 73. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 28?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Upper East Side-Yorkville, Central Park, Manhattan CB5, Manhattan CB6, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, District 5, and District 6, and Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 75, and AD 76. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 28?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with over 900 injuries and deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 75, while bikes were involved in 178 cases. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      The pattern is clear: the same streets, the same vehicles, the same outcomes. These are not accidents—they are preventable with safer speeds, better enforcement, and streets built for people.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, support bills that target repeat dangerous drivers, and fund street redesigns that protect people walking and cycling.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
