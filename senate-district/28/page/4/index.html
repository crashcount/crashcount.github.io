      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      No More Excuses: Eleven Dead, and the Streets Still Kill

      Written July 13, 2025

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt

      In the last twelve months, eleven people died in Senate District 28. More than 1,350 were hurt. Twenty-nine were left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. The dead are old and young. Five were over 75. Three were between 65 and 74. One was a child. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are careful or quick.

      Just this June, a cyclist was thrown from his e-bike in Central Park. He struck his head on the curb and died. The city called it an accident. The West Side Spirit wrote, “A 43 year-old Bronx resident… died on June 18 after flying from an e-bike and striking his head on the curb.” The pedestrian he hit walked away with a scraped hand. The cyclist did not walk away at all.

      On York Avenue, a 90-year-old woman was killed crossing the street. She was hit by an SUV and a taxi. She never made it to the other side. The data says she was crossing without a signal. The data does not say if she was in a hurry, or if she was tired, or if she was thinking about dinner.

      Who Bears the Weight

      Cars and trucks do most of the killing. In three years, SUVs and sedans took eleven lives and left hundreds battered. Trucks and buses killed three. Bikes and mopeds hurt many, but killed none. The numbers are clear. The danger is not shared equally.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      State Senator Liz Krueger has voted for bills to slow repeat speeders and fund safer streets. She backed the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force the worst drivers to install speed-limiting tech. She co-sponsored bills for bike lane enforcement and bus lane cameras. She called Governor Hochul’s congestion pricing delay “a staggering error”.

      But the deaths keep coming. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not. The most dangerous drivers still roam free. The bridge path stays closed. The sidewalk is still too narrow. The curb is still too high.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call your council member. Call Senator Krueger. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand the city open safe paths and redesign deadly streets. The numbers will not change until the streets do. The dead will not come back. The living can still be saved.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees statewide policy, including traffic safety. Learn more at the New York State Senate.
      ▸ Where does SD 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4 and assembly district AD 73.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 28?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Upper East Side-Yorkville, Central Park, Manhattan CB5, Manhattan CB6, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, District 5, and District 6, and Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 75, and AD 76.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 28?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 11 deaths and hundreds of injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused dozens of injuries but no deaths. Bikes injured many but killed none.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement. They are not random; they follow patterns that policy can change.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, support automated enforcement, and pass laws to keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road.
