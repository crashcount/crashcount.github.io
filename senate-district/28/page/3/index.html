      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      Senate District 28: Blood on the Streets, Action Long Overdue

      Written June 21, 2025 (Revised June 20, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, deduped and sorted citations.)

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 21, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 28, the numbers do not lie. Eleven people killed. Thirty-four left with serious injuries. More than 1,200 hurt in crashes in the last year alone, according to NYC Open Data. The dead are old and young. A 90-year-old woman, crushed by an SUV and taxi on York Avenue. An 83-year-old, struck in the crosswalk by a turning SUV on Sixth. Cyclists ejected, pedestrians left on the pavement, families waiting for news that never comes.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the damage. In three years, they killed 11, seriously injured 21, and left hundreds more with broken bodies, according to NYC Open Data. Trucks and buses killed three. Bikes and mopeds, far fewer, but every crash leaves scars. The street is a gauntlet. The curb is no refuge.

      What Liz Krueger Has Done

      Senator Liz Krueger has not been silent. She voted yes on a bill to force safer street designs, putting people before cars. She co-sponsored a law to use cameras to keep bike lanes clear, so drivers cannot block the only safe space for cyclists. She backed a bill to rate cars for pedestrian safety, to show which machines are most deadly. When Governor Hochul delayed congestion pricing, Krueger called it “a staggering error” that would keep streets clogged and dangerous.

      But some bills only measure, not fix. Insurance for delivery workers is good, but it does not stop the crash. A rating system is not a barrier at the curb. The city needs action, not just more studies.

      “Gov. Hochul revoked her support for congestion pricing less than a month before it was supposed to start,” Krueger said. “A staggering error.”

      On street design, the bill’s focus is clear: “safer streets for people on foot, on bikes, and in vehicles.”

      The Next Step Is Yours

      The disaster is slow, but it is not fate. Call Senator Krueger. Call your council member. Demand real change: lower speed limits, more protected lanes, enforcement that targets drivers, not victims. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees state policy. For more, see crash data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Where does SD 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4 and assembly district AD 73. See NYC Open Data crash statistics.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 28?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Manhattan CB5, and Manhattan CB8 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, District 5, and District 6, and Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 75, and AD 76. See NYC Open Data crash statistics.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 28?
      Cars and trucks killed 11 and seriously injured 21 pedestrians. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 6 serious injuries but no deaths. Bikes caused 8 serious injuries and no deaths. See NYC Open Data crash statistics.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are not inevitable. Lower speeds, protected lanes, and better enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries. See NYC Open Data crash statistics.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, and support automated enforcement that targets dangerous driving. See NYC Open Data crash statistics.
