      No More Dead Neighbors: Demand Safe Streets in SD 28 Now

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 19, 2025

      The Toll in SD 28: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      A woman, 83, crossing with the signal at West 58th. Struck by an SUV turning left. She never made it to the other side. A 90-year-old woman, crushed by an SUV and a taxi on York Avenue. A cyclist, 67, killed on Fifth Avenue. These are not numbers. They are lives ended in the crosswalk, on the curb, in the street. In the last twelve months, 11 people died and 35 suffered serious injuries in SD 28. The old, the young, the ones just trying to get home. NYC Open Data.

      Who Pays the Price: The Most Vulnerable

      SUVs killed more pedestrians here than any other vehicle. Cars, trucks, taxis, bikes—each left their mark. But the pattern is clear. The bigger the vehicle, the harder the blow. The city counts 273 pedestrian injuries from SUVs and cars, 38 from trucks and buses, 37 from motorcycles and mopeds, and 70 from bikes in this district since 2022. The sidewalk is no refuge. The crosswalk is no guarantee.

      What Has Liz Krueger Done?

      Senator Liz Krueger has backed some steps. She co-sponsored a bill for automated bike lane enforcement—cameras to catch drivers blocking the lanes that keep cyclists alive. She also co-sponsored a bill to rate cars for pedestrian safety, putting pressure on automakers to build safer vehicles. She voted yes on a bill to force safer street designs, saying streets must protect everyone, not just drivers. But when Governor Hochul delayed congestion pricing, Krueger called it “a staggering error”. She has spoken up, but the bodies keep piling up. Every delay, every watered-down law, means another family left with a hole that will never close.

      “Gov. Hochul revoked her support for congestion pricing less than a month before it was supposed to start.” Krueger said.

      “We deserve much wider sidewalks so we can get around, not just comfortably, but safely.” said Paul Krikler, Community Board 8.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Senator Krueger. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, wider sidewalks, and real enforcement. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for making and passing laws. Senators represent districts across the state and vote on bills that affect all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to Manhattan, city council district 4, and assembly district AD 73. See crash data.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 28?
      It includes Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Manhattan CB5, and Manhattan CB8 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts 3, 4, 5, and 6, and Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 75, and AD 76. See crash data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 28?
      Since 2022, SUVs and cars caused 273 injuries and 11 deaths, trucks and buses caused 38 injuries and 3 deaths, motorcycles and mopeds caused 37 injuries, and bikes caused 70 injuries. The largest toll comes from SUVs and cars. See crash data.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and real enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes and wider sidewalks, and hold dangerous drivers accountable.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Krueger Criticizes Lack of Alternative Funding for Transit
      ▸

      Governor Hochul froze Manhattan’s congestion pricing. The $15 billion for subways, buses, and rail hangs in limbo. Riders face broken elevators, old signals, and crowded trains. Streets stay clogged. No plan B. Vulnerable New Yorkers lose safe, reliable transit.

      On June 6, 2024, Governor Kathy Hochul halted New York’s congestion pricing program, which was set to start June 30. The plan, designed to charge most drivers $15 to enter Manhattan below 60th Street, would have funded $15 billion in MTA capital improvements. The City Council and transit advocates express deep disappointment and concern for New York’s future.
