      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 28?

      Ten Dead This Year. How Many More Will You Let Them Kill?

      Written July 7, 2025

      SD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Senate District 28, the numbers do not lie. Since January 2022, 26 people have died and 105 have been seriously injured in crashes. Over 4,000 have been hurt. The dead include the old and the young. In the last year alone, deaths more than tripled—from 3 to 10. The pace is relentless. The wounds do not heal.

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay the highest price. In February, an 83-year-old woman was killed crossing with the signal at Avenue of the Americas and West 58th. She was struck by an SUV making a left turn. She died at the intersection, her right of way ignored. A week later, a 90-year-old woman was killed on York Avenue, hit by a southbound SUV and taxi. The crash report lists “driver inattention/distraction” as a cause. There are no second chances for the dead.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the harm. In this district, SUVs and sedans have killed 11, causing 22 serious injuries and over 500 minor injuries. Trucks and buses killed 3. Bikes, mopeds, and motorcycles injured dozens, but did not kill. The numbers are cold. The impact is not.

      What Has Senator Krueger Done?

      Senator Liz Krueger has taken some steps. She voted yes on a bill to require complete street design and co-sponsored measures for bike lane enforcement and pedestrian safety ratings. She called Governor Hochul’s congestion pricing delay “a staggering error”, warning it leaves streets clogged and dangerous. But bills and words do not bring back the dead. The city still waits for a default 20 mph speed limit. The most dangerous drivers still roam free.

      The Call

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call Senator Krueger. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand enforcement against repeat offenders. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Policies like lower speed limits, street redesign, and enforcement against dangerous drivers can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, and target repeat dangerous drivers. They can also fund and support automated enforcement and safe infrastructure.
