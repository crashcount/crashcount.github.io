      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Brooklyn’s SD 17: Crosswalks, sirens, and the same old news

      SD 17: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 24, 2025

      Another driver. Same ending.

      • On 7th Avenue at 44th Street, a sedan turned left and hit a 70‑year‑old woman who had the signal. She died. Police cited failure to yield. That was January 13, 2025 (NYC Open Data).
      • On Cropsey and 24th, a 95‑year‑old woman and her aide were struck by a turning cargo van. She died. Police later charged a man with failure to yield and failure to exercise due care, after her death made headlines (Gothamist).
      • On 60th at 4th Avenue, a box truck going straight hit a 30‑year‑old man. He died in the street (NYC Open Data).
      • On 86th Street at 18th Avenue, a 76‑year‑old woman was killed at the intersection. The record lists a traffic control disregarded (NYC Open Data).

      Four deaths in this Senate district in the last 12 months. Most of the injured are not young. The toll keeps climbing: 1,857 crashes, 1,190 injuries, 4 deaths in the last year alone across SD 17 (PeriodStats).

      The names fade. The corners don’t.

      “He didn’t stop. He just kept going,” said a son after a hit‑and‑run killed his father on New Utrecht Avenue this June (NY Daily News).

      Left turns. Big vehicles. Old bones.

      —

      The crosswalk is not a shield

      • The 70‑year‑old on 7th and 44th had the walk. The driver turned left. She never got up (NYC Open Data).
      • Mayya Gil, 95, crossed near her home on Cropsey. A van turned. “She was a very active lady,” her daughter said. Police charged a man months later (Gothamist).

      SUVs and trucks do the most harm. This district’s pedestrian injuries stack up behind them: SUVs lead, then sedans, then trucks. Bikes injure too, but the body count comes from larger vehicles (PeriodStats).

      Seniors carry the grief. Two deaths for residents 75+, another for 65–74, in the past year here. Older bodies do not survive impacts. The figures do not lie (PeriodStats).

      —

      We already know what works — do we use it?

      Albany moved one bill. The Senate voted on the Stop Super Speeders Act, which forces habitual violators to install speed limiters. It passed committee votes in June (Open States). The idea is simple: cap the speed, cut the risk.

      City Hall has another tool. The city can lower speeds. A default 20 mph makes crashes more survivable. Advocates say do it now. The path is laid out in our own call to action (Take Action).

      Insurance for delivery workers advanced. Helpful after the crash. It does not slow the car before it hits you (Streetsblog NYC).

      —

      Three corners. One fix.

      • 44th Street at 7th Avenue: left‑turn protection and slow turns. A woman with the signal died there (NYC Open Data).
      • Cropsey at 24th Avenue: turn‑calming and tighter corners. A 95‑year‑old died after a turning van hit her (Gothamist).
      • 60th at 4th Avenue: truck traffic and speed. A man died there under a box truck’s path (NYC Open Data).

      Slow them. Save them. The rest is noise.

      —

      What must change, right now

      • Lower the city’s default speed limit to 20 mph. Fewer sirens. Fewer funerals (Take Action).
      • Pass state law to put intelligent speed assistance on cars of the worst repeat offenders. The Senate has the bill on the table (Open States).

      One call can help move both. Do it today (Take Action).
