      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 17?

      Brooklyn Bleeds While Lawmakers Stall

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, deduped and reordered citations, tightened prose, and ensured proper citation formatting.)

      SD 17: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      In Senate District 17, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people killed. Nearly 3,000 injured. In just the last twelve months, three more deaths, almost a thousand hurt. The dead are not numbers. They are elders, children, workers, neighbors. A 95-year-old woman, Mayya Gil, was crossing Cropsey Avenue with her home health aide. A van turned left. Gil never made it home. Her daughter said, “She was a very active lady.” The police did not charge the driver.

      On June 22, a man named Pascual Tziquin was crossing New Utrecht Avenue. A red Dodge Charger hit him and kept going. His son watched. “He didn’t stop. He just kept going.” Eight days later, Tziquin died in the hospital. The street stayed the same.

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. SUVs and cars did most of the killing—six deaths, 659 injuries. Trucks and buses killed two, bikes killed one. The old and the young are not spared. In the last year, one person over 65 died, another over 75. Children under 18 were hurt 103 times.

      A van carrying adults with special needs crashed into a tree in Bath Beach. Fourteen people hurt. “It’s sad because they definitely need a lot of attention more than anyone else. I hope that they’re all OK at the end of the day,” said a neighbor. The cause is still under investigation.

      State Senator Steve Chan voted yes on bills to curb repeat speeders and expand speed cameras. He voted no on complete streets and safer school zones. He backed insurance for delivery workers, but not the laws that would slow cars or redesign streets. He has not led the fight for a 20 mph citywide speed limit or for redesigning the roads that kill.

      The crisis is not fate. It is policy. Every delay, every watered-down law, every silent leader means another family waits for a loved one who will never come home.

      Demand more. Call Senator Chan. Tell him to fight for lower speed limits, for real street redesign, for laws that protect people, not just drivers. Join Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Make them choose life.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 17 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does SD 17 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 43 and assembly district AD 49.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 17?
      It includes the Sunset Park (Central), Green-Wood Cemetery, Dyker Heights, Bensonhurst, Bath Beach, Gravesend (West), Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Kensington, Brooklyn CB11, and Brooklyn CB7 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 43, and District 47, and Assembly Districts AD 47, AD 49, and AD 51.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 17?
      Cars and Trucks: 6 deaths, 659 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 25 injuries. Bikes: 1 death, 42 injuries. SUVs and cars are the main killers. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. They are the result of policy, street design, and enforcement. They can be prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, pass laws to rein in repeat dangerous drivers, and ensure enforcement focuses on those who do the most harm.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
