      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 32?

      Webster Avenue Bleeds—City Hall Lets It Happen

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 21, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      SD 32: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Blood on Webster Avenue

      Just weeks ago, a man stood in a crosswalk on Webster Avenue. A black Mercedes came fast. It did not stop. It hit him, threw him into the air, dragged him half a block, and sped away. His mother said, “That car deliberately went straight speeding, didn’t stop, hit my son all the way up into the air and came down, dragged him half a block, they killed my son” (ABC7).

      He was a father. He was loved. His sister said, “Great father, great person—everybody in the community loved him. Everybody knew Kelvin. He grew up in his neighborhood his whole life and he’s gonna be greatly missed” (NY Daily News).

      The Numbers That Do Not Lie

      In the last 12 months, 6 people died and 1,636 were injured on the streets of SD 32. Twenty-five suffered serious injuries. Since 2022, the district has seen 26 deaths and over 5,200 injuries. Most victims are people on foot. Most drivers keep going.

      SUVs and sedans do the most harm. Cars and trucks caused 9 deaths and over 850 injuries to pedestrians. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll. The violence is steady. The pain is spread wide.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      State Senator Luis Sepúlveda has voted for and co-sponsored bills to curb repeat speeders and extend speed cameras in school zones (Open States). He backed the “Stop Super Speeders Act” and measures to keep cameras rolling. These are steps. But the streets are still killing. The laws are not enough. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph now. It has not. The blood keeps running.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat offenders. Join groups like Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name on a stone.

      The dead cannot speak. You can. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 32. See the legislative record.
      ▸ Where does SD 32 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 15 and assembly district AD 79. See collision data.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 32?
      It includes the Melrose, Longwood, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Claremont Park, Mount Hope, and Bronx CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 15, District 16, and District 17, and Assembly Districts AD 77, AD 79, AD 85, AD 86, and AD 87. See collision data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 32?
      Cars and trucks caused 9 deaths and over 850 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused dozens of injuries. Bikes were involved in a handful of cases. See collision data.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement against repeat offenders can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand camera enforcement, and pass laws that target repeat dangerous drivers. They can also push for safer street designs.
