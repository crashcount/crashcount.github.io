      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 32?

      Five Dead in a Year. Who’s Next, Senator Sepúlveda?

      Written July 7, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      SD 32: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Death Toll Grows

      Five dead. Twenty-seven seriously hurt. That is the cost of traffic violence in Senate District 32 in the last year alone (NYC Open Data). The numbers do not bleed, but the people do. In May, a black Mercedes sped down Webster Avenue. It hit Kelvin Mitchell, a father of three, and dragged him half a block. The driver did not stop. Mitchell’s mother demanded justice. His sister mourned the loss: “Everybody is missing Kelvin. It’s not gonna be the same over here without him.”

      Just days ago, a car slammed into a sidewalk shed in Melrose. Six more people were hurt. The sidewalk was not safe. The shed was not enough.

      Patterns in the Wreckage

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. In the last three years, cars and SUVs caused 8 deaths and 839 injuries to pedestrians here. Trucks and buses hurt dozens more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes are in the numbers, but the real threat comes on four wheels.

      Children are not spared. In the past year, 154 people under 18 were hurt. Two were seriously injured. The old are not spared either. One person over 65 died. The violence is steady, the names change.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Senator Luis Sepúlveda has voted for safer street design, backing a bill to make streets work for everyone, not just cars. He co-sponsored a bill to rate cars for pedestrian safety. But he also sponsored a bill that would force e-scooter riders to get licenses and insurance, a move that puts up barriers for the most vulnerable and does nothing to slow the real killers—speeding cars and reckless drivers.

      There is no record of a push for a 20 mph citywide speed limit. No public demand to end repeat speeding offenders. No call for more automated enforcement. The silence is loud.

      Call to Action

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Senator Sepúlveda. Demand action for lower speed limits, real street redesign, and laws that target the drivers who do the most harm. Do not wait for another family to lose a son, a father, a child. The blood is on the street. It does not wash away.

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 32. Open States S 9718
      ▸ Where does SD 32 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 15 and assembly district AD 79. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 32?
      It includes the Melrose, Longwood, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Claremont Park, Mount Hope, and Bronx CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 15, District 16, and District 17, and Assembly Districts AD 77, AD 79, AD 85, AD 86, and AD 87. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 32?
      Cars and Trucks: 8 deaths, 839 injuries; Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 52 injuries; Bikes: 0 deaths, 20 injuries. The vast majority of deaths and injuries come from cars, SUVs, trucks, and buses. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and holding reckless drivers accountable can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws that target repeat dangerous drivers instead of blaming vulnerable road users.
