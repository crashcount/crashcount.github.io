      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 32?

      Webster Avenue Bleeds—How Many More Must Die Before Streets Change?

      Written July 9, 2025

      SD 32: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Blood on Webster Avenue

      A man stood in the crosswalk. The car did not stop. It hit him, dragged him half a block, and kept going. His name was Kelvin Mitchell. He was a father. His mother said, “That car deliberately went straight speeding, didn’t stop, hit my son all the way up into the air and came down, dragged him half a block, they killed my son” (ABC7).

      This was not the only death. In the last twelve months, five people died in Senate District 32. Twenty-five more were seriously injured. The numbers do not stop. In the same year, there were 2,131 crashes. 1,491 people hurt. The dead do not come back. The wounded carry it forever.

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and the Cost

      Most deaths came from cars and SUVs. Eight killed, 13 seriously hurt. Trucks and buses left seven people with grave injuries. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes added to the toll. No one is safe. Not the young, not the old. Children under 18 were injured 156 times in a year. The street does not care who you are.

      Leadership: Votes, Bills, and the Weight of Delay

      Senator Luis Sepúlveda voted for a bill to redesign streets for safety (S 9718). He co-sponsored a bill to rate cars for pedestrian safety (S 1675). He also sponsored a bill to require e-scooter riders to get licenses and insurance (S 5801). Some bills help. Some shift blame to those with the least power. The street is still deadly.

      A sister stood at the scene and said, “Great father, great person—everybody in the community loved him… Everybody is missing Kelvin. It’s not gonna be the same over here without him” (NY Daily News).

      The Call: Demand More, Demand Now

      The deaths will not stop until the streets change. Call Senator Sepúlveda. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real redesigns, not more rules for the powerless. Every day of delay is another family broken.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts and vote on bills that affect all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 32 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 15 and assembly district AD 79.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 32?
      It includes the Melrose, Longwood, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Claremont Park, Mount Hope, and Bronx CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 15, District 16, and District 17, and Assembly Districts AD 77, AD 79, AD 85, AD 86, and AD 87.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 32?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 8 deaths, 13 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 2 serious injuries. Bikes caused 2 serious injuries but no deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speed limits, and stronger enforcement. They are not random acts—they follow patterns that policy can change.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass laws to lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can also oppose bills that shift blame to pedestrians and cyclists.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
