      No More Names on the Street: Demand 20 MPH Now

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, ensured proper link formatting, and cleaned citations.)

      SD 32: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll in SD 32

      Six dead. Twenty-five left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In the last year alone, 1,636 people were hurt in 2,320 crashes across Senate District 32. The numbers do not slow. The pain does not fade. NYC Open Data

      On July 3, a car jumped the curb in Melrose and slammed into a sidewalk shed. Six people were hurt. The shed was built to shield pedestrians from falling bricks, not from cars. The street was quiet. Then it was not. CBS New York

      In May, a black Mercedes tore down Webster Avenue. It hit Kelvin Mitchell, a father of three, and dragged him half a block. The driver did not stop. “That car deliberately went straight speeding, didn’t stop, hit my son all the way up into the air and came down, dragged him half a block, they killed my son,” said his mother. His sister stood on the sidewalk and said, “Great father, great person—everybody in the community loved him. Everybody knew Kelvin. He grew up in his neighborhood his whole life and he’s gonna be greatly missed.” NY Daily News

      Who Pays the Price

      SUVs and sedans do the most harm. In this district, cars and SUVs killed nine people and injured 851 more. Trucks and buses left 62 injured. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes added to the toll. The dead include children, elders, and workers. The living carry scars.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      State Senator Luis Sepúlveda has voted yes on bills to curb repeat speeders and extend school speed zones. He co-sponsored a bill to expand camera enforcement. These are steps. But the blood on the street says it is not enough. The city now has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so. The most dangerous drivers still roam free.

      What Comes Next

      Every day without action is another day of loss. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call Senator Sepúlveda. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat offenders. Demand streets where children can walk without fear.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like SD 32 in Albany. See legislation details.
      ▸ Where does SD 32 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 15 and assembly district AD 79. See NYC Open Data for more.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 32?
      It includes the Melrose, Longwood, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Claremont Park, Mount Hope, and Bronx CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 15, District 16, and District 17, and Assembly Districts AD 77, AD 79, AD 85, AD 86, and AD 87. See NYC Open Data for details.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 32?
      Cars and SUVs: 9 deaths, 851 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 53 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 21 injuries. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, pass laws to rein in repeat dangerous drivers, expand camera enforcement, and fund safer street designs.
