      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 32?

      No More Names on Asphalt: End the Killing on Webster Avenue

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, ensured all quotes have inline links, deduped and sorted citations.)

      SD 32: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Blood on Webster Avenue

      A man stood in the crosswalk. The car came fast. It did not stop. It hit him, dragged him half a block. His name was Kelvin Mitchell. He was a father. His mother said, “That car deliberately went straight speeding, didn’t stop, hit my son all the way up into the air and came down, dragged him half a block, they killed my son” (told ABC7).

      This is not rare. In the last twelve months, six people died on the streets of Senate District 32. 1,636 were injured. 25 were left with serious wounds. Most were walking. Some were children. The numbers do not stop. The pain does not stop.

      The Pattern: Cars, Speed, and Silence

      The cars do not care if you are young or old. SUVs and sedans killed nine. Trucks and buses hit dozens more. In May, a car slammed into a sidewalk shed in Melrose. Six people were hurt. The sidewalk was not safe (reported CBS New York).

      A sister spoke for the dead: “Great father, great person—everybody in the community loved him. Everybody knew Kelvin. He grew up in his neighborhood his whole life and he’s gonna be greatly missed” (she told NY Daily News).

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Senator Luis Sepúlveda voted yes on bills to curb repeat speeders (Open States). He backed more speed cameras in school zones. He co-sponsored bills for safety zones and a vehicle pedestrian safety rating. These are steps. But the killing goes on. The law moves slow. The street moves fast.

      What Must Happen Now

      Every day of delay is another risk. The dead do not get a second chance. Call Senator Sepúlveda. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Demand streets where a man can cross and live.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and shaping policy for New York. Senators represent districts across the state and vote on bills that affect all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does SD 32 sit politically?
      It belongs to the Bronx and overlaps city council districts 15, 16, and 17, as well as assembly districts 77, 79, 85, 86, and 87 (see NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 32?
      It includes the Melrose, Longwood, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Claremont Park, Mount Hope, West Farms, Tremont, and Soundview-Bruckner-Bronx River neighborhoods, as well as Bronx CB6. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts 15, 16, and 17, and Assembly Districts 77, 79, 85, 86, and 87 (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 32?
      Cars and trucks (SUVs, sedans, trucks, buses) caused the most harm: 9 deaths, 600 minor injuries, 251 moderate injuries, and 14 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 53 crashes, with 2 serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 21 crashes, with 2 serious injuries (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Speed, reckless driving, and unsafe streets cause deaths and injuries that could be prevented with better laws and design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, support automated enforcement, fund safer street designs, and pass laws to stop repeat dangerous drivers. They can also listen to residents and act fast when lives are at stake.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
