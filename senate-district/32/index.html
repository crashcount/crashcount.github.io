      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in SD 32?

      Bronx Streets Are Killing Fields. Who Will Stop the Bleeding?

      Written July 11, 2025

      SD 32: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Toll in SD 32

      The streets of Senate District 32 do not forgive. Since 2022, 26 people have died and 74 have been seriously injured in crashes here. In the last twelve months alone, six more lives ended and 26 more bodies were broken. The dead are not numbers. They are sons, daughters, fathers, neighbors.

      Just weeks ago, a car slammed into a sidewalk shed in Melrose, injuring six. The news was brief: “Six people were injured when a car crashed into a sidewalk shed Thursday morning in the Melrose section of the Bronx,” reported CBS New York. No names. No faces. Only pain left behind.

      In May, a black Mercedes sped down Webster Avenue. It hit Kelvin Mitchell, a father of three, and dragged him half a block. The driver did not stop. His mother said, “That car deliberately went straight speeding, didn’t stop, hit my son all the way up into the air and came down, dragged him half a block, they killed my son,” she told ABC7. His sister said, “Everybody is missing Kelvin. It’s not gonna be the same over here without him,” she told the NY Daily News.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists bear the brunt. Most deaths come from cars and SUVs. In this district, SUVs and sedans killed at least nine people on foot. Trucks and buses add to the toll. Bikes and mopeds are not spared, but the street belongs to the biggest and fastest.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      State Senator Luis Sepúlveda has taken some steps. He voted yes on a bill to require “complete street” design, aiming to protect all users, not just drivers. He co-sponsored a bill to post pedestrian safety ratings for cars. But he also sponsored a bill to require e-scooter riders to get licenses and insurance—another barrier for those with the least power, while the deadliest drivers face little change.

      The crisis is not over. The numbers do not lie. The bodies do not lie. The silence of leaders is a choice.

      What Comes Next

      Call Senator Sepúlveda. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns. Demand action against repeat reckless drivers.

      Do not wait for another name on a slab. The street will not change itself.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Senate and how does it work?
      The New York State Senate is the upper chamber of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and overseeing state policy. Senators represent districts across New York and vote on bills that affect all residents.
      ▸ Where does SD 32 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 15 and assembly district AD 79.
      ▸ Which areas are in SD 32?
      It includes the Melrose, Longwood, Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, Crotona Park, Concourse-Concourse Village, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Claremont Park, Mount Hope, and Bronx CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 15, District 16, and District 17, and Assembly Districts AD 77, AD 79, AD 85, AD 86, and AD 87.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in SD 32?
      Cars and trucks (including SUVs and sedans) caused the most harm, with 9 deaths and hundreds of injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused dozens of injuries but no deaths; bikes caused injuries but no deaths, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. Calling them ‘accidents’ hides the fact that policy and infrastructure choices decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, crack down on repeat dangerous drivers, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking instead of blaming them.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
