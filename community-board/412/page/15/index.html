      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Queens CB12: Crosswalks, sirens, and a ledger of the dead

      Queens CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      A 75-year-old man crossing with the signal at Jamaica Ave and 168 St was hit and injured on Sept 12. The city’s own crash log records it plainly. Source.

      This Week

      • A man walking on the Belt Parkway was struck and killed by two drivers near 146th St on Sept 12, police said. Open Data | AMNY
      • A driver in an SUV hit a woman at 89 Ave and 169 St on Sept 8. Source
      • A driver in an SUV hit a woman at Farmers Blvd and 142 Ave on Sept 6. Source

      The count won’t stop

      Since 2022, Queens CB12 has recorded 26 deaths and 6,949 injuries across 11,633 crashes. These are NYPD-reported numbers from the city’s database. NYC Open Data.

      This year to date, the district has 2,032 crashes, 7 deaths, and 1,371 injuries, up from 2 deaths and 1,274 injuries at this point last year. NYC Open Data.

      Deaths cluster in dark hours. The log shows the highest death counts at 3 AM (4 deaths), 5 AM (3), and again around evening, 8 PM (3) and 9 PM (3). NYC Open Data.

      The places we keep losing people

      Belt Parkway leads the board: 4 deaths and 214 injuries. Merrick Boulevard, Sutphin Boulevard, Hillside Avenue, and Farmers Boulevard each carry long lists of hurt. NYC Open Data.

      Police-coded driver actions repeat: failure to yield, disregarding signals, distraction. Those three alone appear with multiple deaths and scores of injuries in this district. NYC Open Data.

      Power knows the corners — will it use them?

      Daylighting — clearing parked cars from crosswalk approaches — sits at City Hall. The Council’s Progressive Caucus says a majority backs Intro 1138, but the Speaker has not brought it to a vote. Streetsblog. Her office has said, “The safety of pedestrians and all street users remains a top priority for Speaker Adams and the council.” AMNY.

      Repeat speeders also have an answer on the table. In Albany, the Senate’s S 4045 moved forward to require intelligent speed assistance devices for drivers with repeated violations; Senator Leroy Comrie (SD 14) co-sponsored and voted yes in committee. Open States.

      Closer to home, Council Member Nantasha M. Williams (District 27) is pushing an enforcement bill on unlicensed commuter vans (Int 1347-2025). Street safety advocates say visibility and speed are the killers at intersections; daylighting still waits. NYC Council Legistar | Streetsblog.

      In June, Assembly Member Alicia Hyndman (AD 29) voted yes to extend school speed zones (S 8344), and Senator Comrie did the same. Cameras are not the cure, but they catch patterns and buy time. Open States.

      What must happen now

      • Clear corners. Pass and implement universal daylighting so people can see and be seen at Merrick, Sutphin, Hillside, and every side street off them. Streetsblog.
      • Stop repeat speeders. Pass the speed limiter bill so the worst drivers cannot keep pushing past the limit here. Open States.

      One man trying to cross at Jamaica Ave is in the hospital. Another man on the Belt Parkway is gone. The fixes sit on desks. Act now: Take action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where is this happening?
      Queens Community Board 12 includes Jamaica, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)–Rochdale Village, St. Albans, and Hollis. It overlaps parts of Council Districts 23, 27, and 28; Assembly Districts 29, 32, and 33; and State Senate Districts 10, 11, and 14.
      ▸ What patterns stand out here?
      Since 2022, the district has 26 deaths and 6,949 injuries across 11,633 crashes. Deaths are highest at 3 AM (4), 5 AM (3), and again at 8 PM (3) and 9 PM (3). Belt Parkway shows 4 deaths and 214 injuries. Sources: NYC Open Data crash datasets.
      ▸ Who can act right now?
      Council Member Nantasha M. Williams (District 27), Assembly Member Alicia Hyndman (AD 29), and State Senator Leroy Comrie (SD 14). Comrie co-sponsored and voted yes on S 4045 (speed limiters) and voted yes on S 8344 (school speed zones). Hyndman voted yes on S 8344. Sources: Open States; NYC Council Legistar.
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4), filtered to incidents within Queens Community Board 12 from 2022-01-01 to 2025-09-18. Totals (crashes, deaths, injuries) and the hourly/locations figures reflect NYPD reports in those datasets as downloaded on Sep 17, 2025. Explore the base datasets here.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
