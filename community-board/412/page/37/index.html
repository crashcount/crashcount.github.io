      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB12?

      Queens Streets Bleed While Politicians Stall

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Queens CB12, the road does not forgive. Twenty people have died since 2022. Thirty-five more have been seriously injured. The numbers do not slow. In the last twelve months alone, seven people died and fourteen were left with life-altering wounds (NYC Open Data).

      The dead are not just numbers. A 45-year-old woman, crushed while crossing 158th Street on Christmas night. A 19-year-old, thrown from his motorcycle on 90th Avenue. A 47-year-old, struck at North Conduit Avenue before dawn. The street swallows them and moves on.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the damage. They killed four, left 12 with serious injuries, and hurt hundreds more. Trucks and buses injured dozens. Motorcycles and mopeds took lives and limbs. Bikes, too, have left their mark, but the carnage comes on four wheels, not two (NYC Open Data).

      The Voices in the Aftermath

      After the crash, the stories come out. “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us,” said Emily McKinnon after a crane struck her bus in Queens Village. She held on for her life. She still takes the bus. She is still afraid.

      A survivor of the Belt Parkway crash said, “They went airborne and into the barrier.” Two died. The rest crawled out through broken glass. No one wore a seatbelt. The car burned.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Council Member Nantasha Williams has voted for daylighting, greenways, and safer streets. She co-sponsored bills for better lighting and crosswalks. But some bills sit stalled in committee. Promises pile up. Streets stay the same.

      Senator Leroy Comrie voted for complete street design but opposed the Idaho Stop, citing seniors. Assembly Member Alicia Hyndman has stayed quiet on the most urgent bills. The pace is slow. The toll is not.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is preventable. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action, not studies. The street will not wait. Neither should you.

      Comrie Mentioned in Debate Over Misguided Speed Hump Delays
      ▸

      Councilmember Rita Joseph blasted DOT for years-long delays on speed hump installations in Flatbush. Twelve sites requested, one approved, none built. Meanwhile, crashes injure cyclists and pedestrians daily. Joseph calls the delay unacceptable. Streets stay dangerous. DOT offers no timeline.

      On November 11, 2022, Councilmember Rita Joseph (District 40) issued a public letter to the Department of Transportation, demanding answers for the slow pace of speed hump installations. The matter, titled "#StuckAtDOT: It Takes Years (and Years) to Get a Speed Hump in this City," details Joseph’s frustration: 'In response to a speed hump request, my office was informed by the DOT that...studies are finalized after two years.' Joseph listed twelve locations needing speed humps; only one was approved, none installed. Eight were denied, several remain under review. Her district saw 1,173 crashes this year, injuring 87 cyclists and 141 pedestrians. Joseph, who lost students to traffic violence, calls the delay 'unacceptable.' DOT says it is reviewing her letter. Advocates demand faster action, saying, 'It should never take two years to implement these straight-forward safety measures.'

      Int 0291-2022
      Adams votes yes, boosting citywide safety with new greenway plan.
      ▸

      The Council passed Int 0291-2022, forcing city agencies to map, plan, and report on greenways. The law demands annual updates and public engagement. It aims to carve out safe, car-free corridors for walkers and cyclists. The mayor returned it unsigned.

      Int 0291-2022, now Local Law 115 of 2022, was enacted by the City Council on November 27, 2022. The bill came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, first introduced April 28, 2022. The law's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to a citywide greenway plan.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers sponsored the bill, joined by dozens of co-sponsors including Rivera, Hudson, and others. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor on October 27, 2022. The law orders the Department of Transportation and Parks to identify, map, and report on greenways, and to consult with community boards. Proposals for new greenway segments or repairs must be presented to affected communities within 60 days. The mayor returned the bill unsigned, but it became law. The measure compels the city to plan and maintain safe routes for non-drivers, with regular public reporting and accountability.
