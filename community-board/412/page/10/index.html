      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB12?

      Queens Streets Bleed While Lawmakers Stall

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB12

      The streets do not forgive. In the last twelve months, seven people died in crashes across Queens CB12. Thirteen more were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In all, 1,460 people were hurt—bodies broken on Linden Boulevard, 116th Avenue, and the rest. The numbers do not flinch. They only grow.

      Last week, a man on a stand-up scooter was hit by a van turning left at 116th Avenue and Nashville Boulevard. He died at the hospital. Police said, “As the van attempted to turn left onto Nashville Boulevard, it collided with the scooter, immediately propelling Lagredelle off the device.” Police said

      Two days before, two moped riders collided with a BMW in Jamaica. Both were injured. The NYPD said, “two people were riding the moped when it collided with a BMW.” The NYPD said

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the worst of it. In three years, cars and SUVs killed four people and injured hundreds more. Trucks and buses left dozens hurt. Motorcycles and mopeds add to the toll. The old and the young are not spared. In the last year, 143 children were injured. Three people aged 18–24 died.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Silence

      The council passed a law to end jaywalking enforcement. Streets are for people, not just cars. The law “lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking.” The law

      But the pace is slow. Promises pile up. Bills to add lighting and redesign streets sit in committee. The dead do not wait.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made upstream. The city can lower speed limits. It can redesign streets. It can end the bloodletting. But only if pushed.

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighted corners and protected crossings. Demand action before another name is added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB12?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, St. Albans, and Hollis neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 28, Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB12?
      Most injuries and deaths came from SUVs and Cars (4 deaths, 12 serious injuries, 753 total incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, and 14 incidents. Bikes caused 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, and 10 incidents. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement can save lives. Every crash is a policy failure, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous intersections, install daylighting and protected crossings, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking.

      S 1675
      Comrie co-sponsors bill for online pedestrian safety ratings, no major safety change.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 1675 would force carmakers to face the facts. It creates a pedestrian safety rating system for all vehicles. The ratings go public on the DMV website. Lawmakers want drivers to see the danger. The streets demand it.

      Senate bill S 1675 was introduced on January 13, 2025. It sits in the sponsorship stage. The bill, titled 'Creates a pedestrian safety rating system for motor vehicles,' would require the Department of Motor Vehicles to post these ratings online. Primary sponsor Andrew Gounardes leads a slate of co-sponsors: Leroy Comrie, Michael Gianaris, Brad Hoylman-Sigal, Robert Jackson, Liz Krueger, Zellnor Myrie, Gustavo Rivera, Julia Salazar, Luis R. Sepúlveda, and Toby Ann Stavisky. The bill aims to expose the risk cars pose to people on foot. By making safety data public, it puts pressure on automakers and drivers alike. The measure has not yet moved to committee or received a vote.
