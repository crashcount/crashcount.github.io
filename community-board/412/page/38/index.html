      Queens Streets Bleed While Lawmakers Stall

      Queens CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB12

      The streets do not forgive. In the last twelve months, seven people died in crashes across Queens CB12. Thirteen more were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In all, 1,460 people were hurt—bodies broken on Linden Boulevard, 116th Avenue, and the rest. The numbers do not flinch. They only grow.

      Last week, a man on a stand-up scooter was hit by a van turning left at 116th Avenue and Nashville Boulevard. He died at the hospital. Police said, “As the van attempted to turn left onto Nashville Boulevard, it collided with the scooter, immediately propelling Lagredelle off the device.” Police said

      Two days before, two moped riders collided with a BMW in Jamaica. Both were injured. The NYPD said, “two people were riding the moped when it collided with a BMW.” The NYPD said

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the worst of it. In three years, cars and SUVs killed four people and injured hundreds more. Trucks and buses left dozens hurt. Motorcycles and mopeds add to the toll. The old and the young are not spared. In the last year, 143 children were injured. Three people aged 18–24 died.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Silence

      The council passed a law to end jaywalking enforcement. Streets are for people, not just cars. The law “lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking.” The law

      But the pace is slow. Promises pile up. Bills to add lighting and redesign streets sit in committee. The dead do not wait.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made upstream. The city can lower speed limits. It can redesign streets. It can end the bloodletting. But only if pushed.

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighted corners and protected crossings. Demand action before another name is added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB12?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, St. Albans, and Hollis neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 28, Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB12?
      Most injuries and deaths came from SUVs and Cars (4 deaths, 12 serious injuries, 753 total incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, and 14 incidents. Bikes caused 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, and 10 incidents. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement can save lives. Every crash is a policy failure, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous intersections, install daylighting and protected crossings, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking.

      Williams co-sponsors bill to speed up street resurfacing, boosting safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force street resurfacing jobs to finish in two weeks. Delays must be explained to neighbors. Streets torn up for months endanger walkers and riders. The bill died at session’s end. The danger remains. The clock keeps ticking.

      Int 0905-2023 was introduced on February 2, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to amend city law to require the Department of Transportation to finish all street resurfacing work within two weeks. If more time was needed, the department would have to notify the community, stating the reason and new timeline. The matter summary reads: 'ensure that all work associated with the resurfacing of any street is finalized within 2 weeks from the start of work.' Council Member Diana I. Ayala sponsored the bill, joined by thirty other members. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. No safety analyst reviewed the impact, but torn-up streets are a known threat to pedestrians and cyclists. The bill is dead. The hazard lives on.

      Sanders Supports Increased Penalties for Negligent Construction Companies
      ▸

      Twenty-two construction workers died last year in New York City. Lawmakers passed Carlos' Law, raising fines for negligent companies to $500,000. State Sen. Jessica Ramos calls it vital as migrants fill non-union jobs. Advocates warn: enforcement and worker awareness still lag.

      Carlos' Law, passed in early 2023, raises the maximum penalty for construction companies convicted of criminal negligence leading to worker injury or death from $10,000 to $500,000. The law, named after Carlos Moncayo, aims to curb a surge in construction worker deaths—22 in the past year, the highest in five years. State Sen. Jessica Ramos, representing District 13, called the law 'critically needed' as thousands of migrants enter non-union construction jobs lacking basic safety protections. Ramos and other lawmakers supported the bill, but advocates and union officials warn that without strong enforcement and education for new arrivals, deaths and injuries will persist. The law passed after years of advocacy, but the minimum fine was removed, and nonprofits struggle to train the influx of new workers. As Ramos said, 'Behind every worker is a family expecting them to return home.'

      Adams Supports Permanent Outdoor Dining Opposes Roadway Dining Sheds
      ▸

      The city’s open restaurants program broke barriers. Outdoor dining spread to 17 neighborhoods that never had it. Blue-collar districts of color gained most. Manhattan’s dominance faded. Brooklyn, Queens, and the Bronx doubled their share. The fight now: who controls the future.

      On January 19, 2023, a new report detailed the impact of New York City’s pandemic-era open restaurants program. The program, debated in the Council and championed by Speaker Adrienne Adams, brought outdoor dining to 17 community boards previously shut out by high fees and red tape. The report states, 'Dining in all neighborhoods [should be] a permanent part of the city’s streetscape.' Brooklyn’s share of outdoor dining rose from 16% to 25%, while Manhattan’s dropped from 70% to 49%. Queens and the Bronx doubled their share. Speaker Adams opposes dining sheds in the roadway and seeks to shift oversight from DOT to the Department of Consumer and Worker Protection. Advocates warn DCWP lacks resources and fear a return to restrictive rules. DOT supports keeping the process simple and affordable, citing equity and vibrant streets. The Council’s decision will shape who benefits—and who is left out.
