      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB12?

      Queens Streets Are Killing Fields—Who’s Going to Stop the Bloodshed?

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 21, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned citations, and ensured all quotes have inline links.)

      Queens CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Recent Toll

      Six dead. Thirteen seriously injured. In just the first half of this year, Queens CB12 saw 1,435 crashes—nearly a thousand people hurt. These are not just numbers. They are bodies on Linden Boulevard, 90th Avenue, Rockaway Boulevard. Families who will never be whole again.

      On April 24, a 62-year-old man was struck and killed by two SUVs while crossing Linden Boulevard. The crash report lists “failure to yield” and “driver inattention” as causes. He died at the scene. No second chances. NYC Open Data

      Just three months earlier, a 23-year-old pedestrian was killed at Rockaway Boulevard and North Conduit Avenue. He was crossing at the intersection. The car kept going. The man did not. NYC Open Data

      Broken Promises and Small Steps

      Local leaders have voted for change. Senator Leroy Comrie backed the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed-limiting devices. He voted yes in committee, helping push the bill forward. voted yes in committee

      Assembly Member Alicia Hyndman and Senator Comrie also voted to extend school speed zones, a move to protect children walking to class. voted to extend school speed zones

      But the carnage continues. SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In this district, SUVs and sedans caused five pedestrian deaths and twelve serious injuries since 2022. Trucks, buses, and motorcycles add to the toll. The street is not safe for the old, the young, or anyone in between.

      The Human Cost

      A bus jumps the curb in Flushing. Eight people hurt. “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus,” said Ken Baur. The MTA pulled the driver from service. The investigation drags on.

      A mother, a father, a child. Each crash leaves a hole. “It must be very devastating for the people that were on the bus,” said Jacqueline Cox.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another family broken. Call your council member. Call your state senator. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat speeders. Demand streets that put people first, not cars.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB12?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, St. Albans, and Hollis neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 28, Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB12?
      Since 2022, SUVs and sedans caused 5 pedestrian deaths and 12 serious injuries in Queens CB12. Trucks, buses, and motorcycles contributed additional injuries. SUVs and sedans are responsible for the majority of deaths and injuries to pedestrians. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These are not random accidents. Most deaths and injuries in Queens CB12 involved driver inattention, failure to yield, or speeding. Policies like lower speed limits, speed cameras, and safer street design can prevent them.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, support speed cameras, pass laws to rein in repeat speeders, and fund safer street designs. They can also hold city agencies accountable for slow progress.
