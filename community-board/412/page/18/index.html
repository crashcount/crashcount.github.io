      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB12?

      Queens Streets Bleed While Lawmakers Stall

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB12

      The streets do not forgive. In the last twelve months, seven people died in crashes across Queens CB12. Thirteen more were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In all, 1,460 people were hurt—bodies broken on Linden Boulevard, 116th Avenue, and the rest. The numbers do not flinch. They only grow.

      Last week, a man on a stand-up scooter was hit by a van turning left at 116th Avenue and Nashville Boulevard. He died at the hospital. Police said, “As the van attempted to turn left onto Nashville Boulevard, it collided with the scooter, immediately propelling Lagredelle off the device.” Police said

      Two days before, two moped riders collided with a BMW in Jamaica. Both were injured. The NYPD said, “two people were riding the moped when it collided with a BMW.” The NYPD said

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the worst of it. In three years, cars and SUVs killed four people and injured hundreds more. Trucks and buses left dozens hurt. Motorcycles and mopeds add to the toll. The old and the young are not spared. In the last year, 143 children were injured. Three people aged 18–24 died.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Silence

      The council passed a law to end jaywalking enforcement. Streets are for people, not just cars. The law “lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking.” The law

      But the pace is slow. Promises pile up. Bills to add lighting and redesign streets sit in committee. The dead do not wait.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made upstream. The city can lower speed limits. It can redesign streets. It can end the bloodletting. But only if pushed.

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighted corners and protected crossings. Demand action before another name is added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB12?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, St. Albans, and Hollis neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 28, Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB12?
      Most injuries and deaths came from SUVs and Cars (4 deaths, 12 serious injuries, 753 total incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, and 14 incidents. Bikes caused 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, and 10 incidents. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement can save lives. Every crash is a policy failure, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous intersections, install daylighting and protected crossings, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking.

      Adams Calls for Community Input on Speed Limits
      ▸

      Council Speaker Adams wavers on using new power to lower speed limits. She calls for district input. Advocates warn: patchwork rules endanger lives. Uniform 20 mph limit saves people. Council delays action as streets stay deadly.

      On July 19, 2024, Council Speaker Adrienne Adams addressed the Council’s authority under Sammy’s Law, which lets New York City lower its speed limit. The matter, discussed in Streetsblog, quotes Adams: “Each Council member is going to have to weigh in on how they feel it should be enacted or should not be enacted in their district.” Adams hesitated to commit to a citywide 20 mph limit, instead suggesting community-by-community decisions. Eric McClure of StreetsPAC called this approach “chaotic and dangerous,” pushing for a uniform 20 mph limit to save lives. Elizabeth Adams of Transportation Alternatives urged a comprehensive, data-driven plan. State Senator Andrew Gounardes and Brad Hoylman-Sigal pressed the Council to use its new powers. The Department of Transportation clarified its limited authority. The Council’s delay leaves vulnerable road users at risk.

      Adams Defends Compromise Policy Reducing Roadway Dining Safety
      ▸

      Outdoor dining in New York City’s roadways is fading fast. New rules force restaurants to pack up curbside setups for four winter months. Only 23 have hearings to join the permanent program. The city keeps most curb space for cars. Sidewalk dining survives.

      The policy, shaped by the City Council and Mayor Adams, phases out pandemic-era roadway dining structures. As of July 12, 2024, only 23 restaurants have public hearings scheduled to join the permanent program, with hundreds more applications pending, according to the Department of Transportation. The matter summary states: 'New York City is likely to see a steep drop in restaurants offering outdoor dining in roadways after its permanent al fresco program phases out the pandemic-era structures next month.' Council Speaker Adrienne Adams and her office defend the compromise, saying it 'strikes the right balance.' Restaurant owners like Thiru Rajamani and Sam say the seasonal pause and costs are too high. Advocates argue the city is missing a chance to reclaim curb space from cars. Sidewalk dining will remain year-round.

      Adams Backs Fair Fares Expansion Boosting Transit Safety
      ▸

      City Council raised Fair Fares eligibility. Now, more low-income New Yorkers get half-price MetroCards. The move adds $10 million to the program. Over one million people now qualify. Councilmember Brannan calls it a step for equity. Advocates want more.

      On June 28, 2024, the City Council expanded the Fair Fares MetroCard program, raising eligibility from 120% to 145% of the federal poverty line. The change, part of the Fiscal Year 2025 budget, was championed by Council Finance Chair Justin Brannan. The bill increases access for individuals earning up to $21,837 and families of four earning $45,240. The council added $10 million in funding, bringing the total to over $100 million annually. Brannan and economist James Parrott wrote, 'By expanding the reach of Fair Fares, we can ensure that all New Yorkers—particularly those who are already struggling during a crisis of affordability—can fully participate in the economic, social, and cultural opportunities of our city.' The expansion boosts eligibility from 932,000 to just over 1 million people. Advocates and Speaker Adrienne Adams say it is a win, but still short of the 200% threshold they seek.
