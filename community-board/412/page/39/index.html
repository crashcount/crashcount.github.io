      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB12?

      Queens Bleeds While City Stalls: Act Now or Count More Bodies

      Written July 6, 2025

      Queens CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt

      Five people are dead in Queens CB12 since January. Seven in the last year. The numbers do not flinch. In the past twelve months, 1,448 people have been hurt in crashes here. Fourteen were left with injuries so severe they may never walk right again.

      A 62-year-old man was killed crossing Linden Boulevard in April. The police report cites “driver inattention/distraction.” He died in the street, not at an intersection, hit by an SUV going straight ahead. No one else died that day, according to NYC Open Data.

      On Christmas night, a woman was killed at 158th Street and 111th Avenue. She was crossing—no signal, no crosswalk. The SUV kept going straight. She did not get up, as reported by NYC Open Data.

      The Toll of Delay

      The city talks about safety. The bodies keep coming.

      Council Member Nantasha Williams has voted for daylighting, traffic calming near seniors, and a greenway plan. She co-sponsored bills for better lighting and safer street design. But some bills remain “laid over in committee”, promises waiting in the dark.

      The council voted to end jaywalking enforcement. Streets are for people, not just cars. As the council stated, “The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.”

      But speed humps and real change come slow. As one advocate put it, “It should never take two years to implement these straight-forward safety measures.”

      Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. Of all pedestrian injuries and deaths, cars and SUVs are the main weapon. Trucks, bikes, and mopeds leave their mark too, but the numbers are smaller. The dead are old and young, crossing at corners or not, walking home or just walking.

      The city says it is acting. The street says otherwise. The numbers do not lie. The pain is not abstract. It is a body in the road, a family waiting for news, a name that will not be spoken again.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns, not studies. Demand action before another name is lost.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB12?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, St. Albans, and Hollis neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 28, Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB12?
      Most injuries and deaths come from Cars and SUVs (4 deaths, 567 minor injuries, 173 moderate injuries, 12 serious injuries). Motorcycles and Mopeds caused fewer injuries (no deaths, 6 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries). Bikes caused no deaths, 9 minor injuries, and 1 serious injury, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These are not random accidents. Most deaths and injuries happen in patterns—same streets, same vehicles, same failures. Delays in safety measures and weak enforcement let it happen again and again.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and fund real street redesigns, lower speed limits, and enforce laws that protect people walking and biking. They can stop stalling bills that would save lives and push DOT to act faster.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      On August 26, 2022, New York officials debated congestion pricing exemptions as the policy neared implementation. The discussion, covered by Streetsblog NYC, highlighted shifting positions. State Senator Leroy Comrie, once opposed to exemptions, now listens to public input. Assembly Member Robert Carroll said, "I think that there should be limited exemptions." State Senator Andrew Gounardes added, "Obviously we want them to be as limited as possible." Manhattan Borough President Mark Levine backed toll credits for tunnel users and taxi exemptions. The Traffic Mobility Review Board will decide on further exemptions after federal review. No direct safety impact for vulnerable road users was assessed.
