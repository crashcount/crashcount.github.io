      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB12?

      Queens Streets, Bloody by Design—Demand Action Now

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Queens CB12, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-one people killed. Thirty-nine left with serious injuries. More than 5,000 hurt since 2022. The streets run through Jamaica, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, St. Albans, and Hollis. The violence does not stop.

      Just this spring, the carnage continued. On April 24, a 62-year-old man was killed crossing Linden Boulevard. Two SUVs, one with an Arizona plate, struck him. The crash report lists “Failure to Yield Right-of-Way” and “Driver Inattention/Distraction” as causes. He died at the scene. No one else was hurt. No one else paid the price. NYC Open Data

      On April 14, a 19-year-old on a motorcycle died at 90th Avenue and 188th Street. He was ejected, crushed, and left dead in the road. The SUV driver survived. NYC Open Data

      The Human Cost

      Every crash leaves a mark. In January, a 45-year-old woman was killed crossing 158th Street at 111th Avenue. The driver was distracted. She was crossing, no signal, no crosswalk. She never made it home. NYC Open Data

      A 23-year-old man died at Rockaway Boulevard and North Conduit Avenue. The crash report says he was crossing against the signal. The car kept going. He did not. NYC Open Data

      On a bus in Flushing, seven people were hurt when the driver lost control and jumped the curb. “It must be very devastating for the people that were on the bus,” said Jacqueline Cox. Another rider, Ken Baur, remembered, “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus.” ABC7

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have taken some steps. State Senator Leroy Comrie voted yes to extend school speed zones and to require speed limiters for repeat dangerous drivers. Assembly Member Alicia Hyndman also voted for school speed zones. But the city still waits for a default 20 mph speed limit. The most dangerous drivers keep their keys. The streets remain deadly.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat speeders. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB12?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, St. Albans, and Hollis neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 28, Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB12?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians here were caused by SUVs and Cars (5 deaths, 12 serious injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (no deaths, 2 serious injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 1 serious injury).
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are not just accidents. The data shows patterns—failure to yield, distraction, speeding. These are choices, not fate. Policy can prevent deaths.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, enforce laws against repeat dangerous drivers, redesign streets for safety, and support automated enforcement. Every vote and every delay has a cost.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
