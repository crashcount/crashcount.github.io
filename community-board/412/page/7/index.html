      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB12?

      Queens Streets Bleed While Lawmakers Stall

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB12

      The streets do not forgive. In the last twelve months, seven people died in crashes across Queens CB12. Thirteen more were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In all, 1,460 people were hurt—bodies broken on Linden Boulevard, 116th Avenue, and the rest. The numbers do not flinch. They only grow.

      Last week, a man on a stand-up scooter was hit by a van turning left at 116th Avenue and Nashville Boulevard. He died at the hospital. Police said, “As the van attempted to turn left onto Nashville Boulevard, it collided with the scooter, immediately propelling Lagredelle off the device.” Police said

      Two days before, two moped riders collided with a BMW in Jamaica. Both were injured. The NYPD said, “two people were riding the moped when it collided with a BMW.” The NYPD said

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the worst of it. In three years, cars and SUVs killed four people and injured hundreds more. Trucks and buses left dozens hurt. Motorcycles and mopeds add to the toll. The old and the young are not spared. In the last year, 143 children were injured. Three people aged 18–24 died.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Silence

      The council passed a law to end jaywalking enforcement. Streets are for people, not just cars. The law “lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking.” The law

      But the pace is slow. Promises pile up. Bills to add lighting and redesign streets sit in committee. The dead do not wait.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made upstream. The city can lower speed limits. It can redesign streets. It can end the bloodletting. But only if pushed.

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighted corners and protected crossings. Demand action before another name is added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB12?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, St. Albans, and Hollis neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 28, Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB12?
      Most injuries and deaths came from SUVs and Cars (4 deaths, 12 serious injuries, 753 total incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, and 14 incidents. Bikes caused 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, and 10 incidents. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement can save lives. Every crash is a policy failure, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous intersections, install daylighting and protected crossings, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking.

      Adams Remains Neutral on Citywide 20 MPH Limit
      ▸

      DOT will cut speed limits to 20 mph in DUMBO, City Island, Broad Channel, and New Brighton. The move targets busy pedestrian areas. Advocates and Council Member Restler back the change. Lower speeds mean fewer deadly crashes. Citywide action still stalled.

      On March 20, 2025, the NYC Department of Transportation announced four new 20 mph speed limit zones. The agency used powers from Sammy's Law, passed last year, to lower speeds in DUMBO (Brooklyn), City Island (Bronx), Broad Channel (Queens), and New Brighton (Staten Island). DOT chose these areas for their heavy pedestrian traffic and clear boundaries. Council Member Lincoln Restler, representing DUMBO, praised the move, saying, "I'm pleased that DOT will be implementing Brooklyn's first Regional Slow Zone in DUMBO—a neighborhood densely crowded with pedestrian foot traffic." DOT plans to cut speeds on 250 street segments this year, focusing on schools and shared streets. Research shows lower speed limits reduce injury severity in crashes. Despite this progress, Council Speaker Adrienne Adams has not brought a citywide 20 mph limit to a vote. Advocates urge more action.

      Adams Backs Harmful Bill Slashing Curbside Dining Space
      ▸

      Council Speaker Adrienne Adams pushed new rules. Only 600 restaurants can use curbside space. That’s an 81 percent drop. Winter dining is banned. Permits now cost more and take longer. Streets return to cars. Small businesses lose out. Critics call it a failure.

      Bill passed by the City Council and signed by Mayor Adams in 2023 slashes the number of restaurants allowed curbside dining to just 600, down 81 percent from the pandemic peak. The legislation, championed by Council Speaker and mayoral candidate Adrienne Adams, bans winter roadway dining, adds yearly fees, and requires public hearings for each curbside setup. The matter summary states, 'outdoor dining, in my perspective, should be sidewalk,' and that 'street extensions ... were designed to be temporary.' Local Council members and community boards now have more power to delay or deny permits. Adams led the push, with DOT officials claiming the new program is still larger than pre-Covid, but restaurateurs and advocates like Sara Lind and Majora Carter call the process burdensome and exclusionary. Small businesses and vulnerable road users lose public space as curbside dining shrinks and car storage returns.

      Adams Supports Increased Funding to Save Open Streets
      ▸

      Open Streets are shrinking. Volunteers warn more cuts loom unless City Hall steps up. Federal relief is gone. Groups want $48 million over three years. Current grants barely keep programs alive. The city’s inaction puts public space and safety at risk.

      On March 14, 2025, a coalition of 16 volunteer open street groups sent a letter to City Council Transportation Chair Selvena Brooks-Powers. They warned, 'The limited funding and decreases in funding that have occurred over the years jeopardize the continuity and future of the program—funding pressure is already leading to downsizing and the disappearance of Open Streets programs.' The groups seek $48 million over three years, separate from the city’s $30 million contract with the Horticultural Society. Current grants max out at $20,000 per year, forcing cutbacks. Speaker Adrienne Adams and Mayor Adams are starting budget talks. Council spokesperson Julia Agos called Open Streets 'critical components of our city's public realm.' The Department of Transportation said suggestions will be reviewed. Volunteer groups handle most operations, but stalled redesigns and lack of resources threaten the program’s survival.
