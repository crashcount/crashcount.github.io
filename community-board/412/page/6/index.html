      No More Excuses: Twenty-One Dead, Thousands Broken on Queens Streets

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Toll: Death and Injury on Familiar Streets

      In Queens CB12, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, twenty-one people have died and 5,011 have been injured in 8,367 crashes. Thirty-nine of those injuries were serious. The dead include children, elders, and people just trying to cross the street. The living carry scars—broken bones, crushed bodies, memories that do not heal.

      Just this spring, a 62-year-old man was killed crossing Linden Boulevard. He was hit by two SUVs. The crash report lists the cause as “failure to yield right-of-way” and “driver inattention.” He died alone, not at an intersection, not in a crosswalk. The street did not forgive him. NYC crash data

      On Christmas night, a woman was struck and killed at 158th Street and 111th Avenue. The driver kept going straight. The report says “driver inattention.” The only thing that stopped the car was her body. NYC crash data

      The Human Cost: Voices from the Scene

      After a bus crash in Flushing, a passenger described the chaos: “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus.” Eight people were hurt. The driver had fallen asleep at the wheel. The MTA pulled him from service. The investigation goes on.

      A mother at the scene told reporters, “I have a baby with me. That would be scary. I’ll be more cautious of my surroundings.” But caution is not armor. The sidewalk is not safe. The curb is not a shield.

      Leadership: Progress and the Long Road Ahead

      Local leaders have taken steps. Senator Leroy Comrie voted yes to extend school speed zones and to require speed limiters for repeat dangerous drivers. These are the right votes. But the deaths keep coming. The city now has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so everywhere. The law is on the books. The blood is on the street.

      Every delay is another family broken.

      What Next: Demand Relentless Action

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit on every street. Demand that repeat speeders are stopped before they kill.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. The crisis is slow, but it is not silent. Make them hear you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB12?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, St. Albans, and Hollis neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 28, Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB12?
      SUVs and Cars: 5 deaths, 582 minor injuries, 182 moderate injuries, 12 serious injuries (total 781 incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 6 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries (total 14 incidents). Bikes: 0 deaths, 9 minor injuries, 1 moderate injury, 1 serious injury (total 11 incidents).
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. The data shows patterns: driver inattention, failure to yield, unsafe speed. These are choices, not fate. Safer street design and lower speed limits can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, expand speed cameras, and support laws that stop repeat dangerous drivers. They can fund safer street designs and hold reckless drivers accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
