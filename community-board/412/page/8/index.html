      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB12?

      Queens Streets: Blood in the Gutter, Silence in City Hall

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Queens CB12, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-one people dead. Thirty-nine left with serious injuries. Five thousand more hurt since 2022. The wounds are not abstract. They are broken bones, crushed bodies, and families who will never be whole again.

      Just this spring, a 62-year-old man was killed on Linden Boulevard. He was crossing. Two cars, an SUV and a sedan, struck him. He died there, on the street, not at an intersection, not in a crosswalk. The cause: driver inattention. The record says only “crush injuries” and “semiconscious” before death. No arrest. No comfort. Only silence (NYC Open Data).

      A 19-year-old on a moped died on 90th Avenue. Ejected. Apparent death. Helmet or not, it did not matter. The SUV kept going straight. The moped was demolished (NYC Open Data).

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the harm. In three years, they killed five, seriously injured twelve, and hurt hundreds more. Trucks and buses injured dozens. Motorcycles and mopeds, too, leave bodies in the street. Bikes are not blameless, but their toll is a fraction. The pattern is clear: the bigger the machine, the greater the damage.

      Words from the Wreckage

      After a crane slammed into a city bus in Queens Village, a survivor recalled, “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us. I was holding on for my life back there”.

      On the Belt Parkway, a BMW went airborne. Passengers were thrown from the car. One survivor said, “They went airborne and into the barrier… Jewel was already dead when she flew out the car”.

      Leadership: Votes, Delays, and the Long Wait

      Council Member Nantasha Williams has voted for bills to daylight intersections, build greenways, and boost safety for seniors. She co-sponsored a bill to bring more light to dark corridors, but it sits stalled in committee. She backed a task force to study e-bike street safety, but the city is still waiting for action. Bills to protect the most vulnerable are too often delayed, studied, or ignored.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay means another body in the street. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns, not just studies. Tell them: the dead cannot wait. The living should not have to.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB12?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, St. Albans, and Hollis neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 28, Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB12?
      Cars and SUVs: 5 deaths, 12 serious injuries, hundreds hurt. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 14 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, 11 injuries. Cars and SUVs are the main killers.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most deaths and injuries come from driver inattention, speeding, and dangerous street design. These are preventable. Every delay in action means more lives lost.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and fund laws for lower speed limits, daylight intersections, redesign streets, and install more pedestrian lighting. They can stop stalling bills that protect people on foot and bike.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
