      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB12?

      Queens Streets Bleed While Politicians Stall

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Queens CB12, the road does not forgive. Twenty people have died since 2022. Thirty-five more have been seriously injured. The numbers do not slow. In the last twelve months alone, seven people died and fourteen were left with life-altering wounds (NYC Open Data).

      The dead are not just numbers. A 45-year-old woman, crushed while crossing 158th Street on Christmas night. A 19-year-old, thrown from his motorcycle on 90th Avenue. A 47-year-old, struck at North Conduit Avenue before dawn. The street swallows them and moves on.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the damage. They killed four, left 12 with serious injuries, and hurt hundreds more. Trucks and buses injured dozens. Motorcycles and mopeds took lives and limbs. Bikes, too, have left their mark, but the carnage comes on four wheels, not two (NYC Open Data).

      The Voices in the Aftermath

      After the crash, the stories come out. “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us,” said Emily McKinnon after a crane struck her bus in Queens Village. She held on for her life. She still takes the bus. She is still afraid.

      A survivor of the Belt Parkway crash said, “They went airborne and into the barrier.” Two died. The rest crawled out through broken glass. No one wore a seatbelt. The car burned.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Council Member Nantasha Williams has voted for daylighting, greenways, and safer streets. She co-sponsored bills for better lighting and crosswalks. But some bills sit stalled in committee. Promises pile up. Streets stay the same.

      Senator Leroy Comrie voted for complete street design but opposed the Idaho Stop, citing seniors. Assembly Member Alicia Hyndman has stayed quiet on the most urgent bills. The pace is slow. The toll is not.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is preventable. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action, not studies. The street will not wait. Neither should you.

      Sanders Pushes QueensLink Rail Funding Despite Park Support
      ▸

      A $117 million federal grant pushes the QueensWay park forward on an old rail line. Rail advocates warn this blocks future transit. City Hall claims both park and rail can coexist. For now, the city’s money backs the park, not the train.

      On March 15, 2024, a $117 million federal grant was awarded for the QueensWay, a 3.5-mile park on an abandoned Long Island Rail Road spur in Queens. This follows Mayor Adams’s earlier $35 million commitment. The matter centers on whether to build a park or restore rail service—"Its purpose is to derail the train," said Rick Horan, QueensLink’s executive director. The city’s investments so far favor the park, leaving the rail proposal in limbo. No safety analyst has assessed the impact on vulnerable road users.

      ▸

      Council calls for unlimited subway and bus transfers within two hours for pay-per-ride users. The move targets long, complex trips that force riders to pay extra. Sponsors say the current policy punishes those with the hardest commutes.

      Resolution 0252-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced March 7, 2024, it urges Albany to pass and sign a law creating unlimited subway and bus transfers within two hours for pay-per-ride users. The matter title reads: 'Resolution calling on the New York State Legislature to pass, and the New York State Governor to sign, legislation, which would create unlimited transfers within the two-hour period of paying the Metropolitan Transportation Authority subway or bus fare for pay-per-ride users.' Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez leads as primary sponsor, joined by Riley, Stevens, Hanif, Won, Feliz, Salaam, Hanks, Farías, De La Rosa, Cabán, Williams, Hudson, Ung, Ossé, Banks, and Holden.       Int 0606-2024
      Williams co-sponsors e-bike and scooter registration bill, reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council wants plates on every e-bike and scooter. The bill sits in committee. Riders face new rules. Lawmakers push for registration. Streets wait. No change yet. The city debates control. Vulnerable users watch. The system holds its breath.

      Int 0606-2024 was introduced on March 7, 2024, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered," would force all e-bikes, e-scooters, and similar vehicles not already covered by DMV rules to register with the Department of Transportation and display a visible plate. Council Member Inna Vernikov led as primary sponsor, joined by Holden, Farías, Riley, Brannan, and many others. The bill was laid over in committee on December 11, 2024. The measure aims to track these vehicles, but its impact on vulnerable road users remains unassessed. The city’s debate continues, with no law yet in force.
