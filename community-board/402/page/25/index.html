      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB2?

      Twelve Dead, Hundreds Broken—Queens Streets Still Bleed While Leaders Stall

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Toll Mounts, One Life at a Time

      In Queens CB2, the numbers do not lie. Twelve people killed. Thirty-five left with serious injuries. Over 2,500 hurt. These are not distant tragedies. They are neighbors, children, elders. In the last year alone, two more lives ended on these streets. Hundreds more were broken—732 injured, nine left with life-altering wounds (NYC Open Data).

      The violence is relentless. A 16-year-old girl was killed crossing at 47th Avenue and 46th Street. A cyclist, 28, died under the wheels of a turning truck on Greenpoint Avenue. An 18-year-old moped rider lost his life on Laurel Hill Boulevard. The list goes on. Each crash a story cut short.

      “Speed May Have Been an Issue”

      Just days ago, a BMW tore across the Belt Parkway, vaulted the divider, and slammed into oncoming cars. Six people were hurt, two left fighting for their lives. Police said speed may have been an issue. The NYPD’s Collision Investigation Squad closed the highway to pick through the wreckage. The horror is routine now. “A BMW driver and a passenger were fighting for their lives Saturday after their out-of-control luxury car vaulted into oncoming traffic… slamming into two unsuspecting motorists in a fiery crash,” the NYPD said.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      Council Member Julie Won has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, require protected bike lanes, and legalize jaywalking. She co-sponsored the universal daylighting bill, which would keep corners clear so drivers can see people crossing. She voted to end jaywalking enforcement, shifting blame off pedestrians. But the pace is slow. The daylighting bill sits in committee. Promises pile up. Streets stay deadly.

      State Senator Michael Gianaris pushed for the Queensboro Bridge bike-pedestrian split. It took years. Each delay meant more close calls, more pain. The bridge is safer now, but the fight is not over.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      The carnage will not stop until leaders feel the heat. Call Council Member Won. Call Senator Gianaris. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand every intersection be daylighted. Demand action, not excuses. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Int 0312-2022
      Won co-sponsors bill raising fines for excessive vehicle noise.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to hike penalties for loud cars and trucks. The measure targeted blaring horns, illegal claxons, and roaring engines. It died at session’s end. No direct safety gains for people walking or biking. Noise remains. Streets stay loud.

      Int 0312-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety. The bill aimed to amend the city’s administrative code to increase penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles, including those under and over 10,000 pounds, and for unauthorized horns and claxons. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to increasing certain penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles.' Council Member Sandra Ung sponsored the bill, joined by 26 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. There is no evidence the bill would have improved safety for vulnerable road users. The city’s noise problem, and its threat to livable streets, remains unresolved.

      Won co-sponsors bill boosting safety by tracking police vehicle force incidents.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0256-2022 would force NYPD to count every time an officer uses a car to control someone. The law closes a reporting loophole. Police vehicles are now named as weapons. The bill was filed at session’s end. No action taken.

      Int 0256-2022, introduced April 28, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety, sought to amend the city code to require NYPD to report when officers use a motor vehicle as force. The bill’s matter title reads: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to use of force incidents involving police department use of a motor vehicle.” Council Member Althea V. Stevens led as primary sponsor, joined by 26 co-sponsors including Hudson, Williams, Restler, Krishnan, and others. The bill would have added 'use of a motor vehicle to gain control of a subject' as a reporting category. It was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. This measure aimed to expose police vehicle violence by demanding full transparency in use-of-force reporting.

      Res 0108-2022
      Won co-sponsors Atlantic Ticket expansion, boosting transit use and street safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to expand and lock in the Atlantic Ticket. They want Far Rockaway and more LIRR stations in the fare program. The move aims to cut commute times and boost transit use. The bill is filed, not enacted.

      Res 0108-2022 was introduced on April 14, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution urges the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to 'expand and make permanent the Atlantic Ticket pilot in order to include Far Rockaway, and other stations along the LIRR corridor, in the fare affordability program.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Yeger, Stevens, Hanif, Bottcher, Krishnan, and many others. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. The Atlantic Ticket pilot, launched in 2018, gave Southeast Queens and Brooklyn riders cheaper LIRR fares. Advocates say expanding it citywide could mean faster, safer commutes and more transit riders. The council’s action highlights the need for affordable, accessible transit—especially for neighborhoods with long, punishing commutes.
