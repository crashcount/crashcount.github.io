      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Staten Island CB1?

      Staten Island Streets Are Killing Us—Who Will Stop It?

      Written July 14, 2025

      Staten Island CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 14, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Staten Island CB1, the road takes what it wants. Seventeen people have died since 2022. Two dozen more left with wounds that do not heal. In the last year alone: four dead, eleven with life-altering injuries.

      The faces change. The story does not. On June 29, a 16-year-old on an e-scooter was struck by a Hyundai Tucson. He died of head trauma. The police said, “Ellis suffered head trauma as a result of the crash.” No arrests. No comfort. Just another family left with a hole in it.

      A month earlier, a 58-year-old man tried to cross Targee Street. An e-scooter hit him. He died at the hospital. The driver was thrown from the scooter. The police said, “upon impact, the driver was violently ejected off the scooter while Gomez-Guallazaca sustained severe trauma.” No charges. The street stays the same.

      The Numbers Are Names

      Since 2022, 2,645 people have been hurt in crashes here.

      Children, elders, workers, neighbors. Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. Trucks, motorcycles, bikes, and now e-scooters join in. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are careful. They do not care if you are loved.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Some leaders act. Assembly Member Charles Fall voted to extend school speed zones, improving child pedestrian safety. He has pushed for safer streets, daylighting, and protected bike lanes. But others block the way. State Senator Andrew Lanza voted no on a bill that would curb repeat speeders. He also opposed reauthorizing speed cameras, proven to save lives.

      Every delay means another family gets the call.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate.

      Call your council member. Call your state senator. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action on repeat dangerous drivers. Join groups like Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Stand with the living. Speak for the dead.

      Do not wait for the next siren. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Staten Island CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Staten Island, city council district District 49, assembly district AD 61 and state senate district SD 24.
      ▸ Which areas are in Staten Island CB1?
      It includes the St. George-New Brighton, Tompkinsville-Stapleton-Clifton-Fox Hills, Rosebank-Shore Acres-Park Hill, West New Brighton-Silver Lake-Grymes Hill, Westerleigh-Castleton Corners, Port Richmond, Mariner’S Harbor-Arlington-Graniteville, and Snug Harbor neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 49 and District 50, Assembly Districts AD 61 and AD 63, and State Senate Districts SD 23 and SD 24.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Staten Island CB1?
      Cars and SUVs: 5 deaths, 293 minor injuries, 137 moderate injuries, 3 serious injuries (438 total incidents). Trucks and Buses: 3 deaths, 33 minor injuries, 9 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury (46 total incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 3 minor injuries, 0 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries (3 total incidents). Bikes: 0 deaths, 4 minor injuries, 3 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries (7 total incidents).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not just accidents. They are the result of choices—speed, street design, enforcement, and policy. Every delay in action means more lives lost or broken.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws to stop repeat dangerous drivers. They can support proven measures and refuse to delay.

      On September 22, 2023, the Port Authority approved an $8.3 million study to examine widening the Outerbridge Crossing, as required by a 2021 state law.
