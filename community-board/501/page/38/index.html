      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Staten Island CB1?

      Staten Island Streets Still Kill—Will Lawmakers Ever Choose Lives Over Cars?

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned up duplicate citations.)

      Staten Island CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      Four people killed. Eight hundred thirty-four injured. Eleven left with wounds so grave they may never heal. That is the toll of traffic violence in Staten Island CB1 over the past year. These are lives cut short, families broken, futures erased.

      On July 5th, Jeremy Claudio, age 34, was riding his motorcycle on Bay Street. A Toyota SUV made a K-turn across traffic. Claudio hit the driver’s door. He died at the hospital. There were no arrests. Police said the investigation continues. The street is quiet now, but the loss remains.

      In May, Jose Luis Gomez-Guallazaca, 58, tried to cross Targee Street. An e-scooter struck him. He died from his injuries. The driver was thrown from the scooter. Paramedics rushed both men to the hospital. Only one came home.

      The Vehicles That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm. In the last three years, they killed five people and injured over 400 more on these streets. Trucks and buses killed three. Motorcycles and mopeds killed none, but injured several. Even bikes injured a handful. No mode is harmless, but the deadliest are the biggest, fastest, and least forgiving.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Failed to Do

      Some leaders have acted. Council Member Kamillah Hanks voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable and did nothing to stop the real danger—drivers who do not yield. The new law says streets belong to people, not just cars.

      But others have failed. State Senator Andrew Lanza voted against reauthorizing speed cameras in school zones. He also voted no on a bill to redesign streets for safety. The record is clear: some protect drivers’ convenience, not children’s lives.

      The Call to Action

      The deaths will not stop until leaders act. Call your council member. Call your senator. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand more speed cameras, not fewer. Demand streets built for people, not just cars. Every day of delay is another family’s grief.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Staten Island CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Staten Island, city council district District 49, assembly district AD 61 and state senate district SD 24.
      ▸ Which areas are in Staten Island CB1?
      It includes the St. George-New Brighton, Tompkinsville-Stapleton-Clifton-Fox Hills, Rosebank-Shore Acres-Park Hill, West New Brighton-Silver Lake-Grymes Hill, Westerleigh-Castleton Corners, Port Richmond, Mariner’S Harbor-Arlington-Graniteville, and Snug Harbor neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 49 and District 50, Assembly Districts AD 61 and AD 63, and State Senate Districts SD 23 and SD 24.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Staten Island CB1?
      Cars and SUVs: 5 deaths, 437 injuries. Trucks and buses: 3 deaths, 46 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 0 deaths, 10 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 7 injuries. Cars and SUVs are the deadliest by far.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, unsafe turns, and failure to yield are choices. Better street design and enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking. They can also refuse to support policies that blame victims or delay real change.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Fall Supports Safety Boosting Permanent Daylight Savings Time
      ▸

      A University of Washington study finds permanent daylight savings time could save 33 human lives and 36,550 deer yearly. Fewer crashes happen when commutes end before dark. Earlier Rutgers research showed a 13 percent drop in pedestrian deaths at dusk and dawn.

      On November 9, 2022, a research report published in Current Biology examined the impact of permanent daylight savings time on road safety. The study, led by University of Washington researchers, argues that 'rolling back the clocks in November may give millions of Americans an extra hour of sleep, but it also costs human and animal lives on U.S. roads that could be saved by making daylight savings time permanent.' The report found that keeping daylight savings time year-round could prevent 33 human deaths and 36,550 deer deaths annually by reducing crashes during dark hours, especially at peak commute times. A 2004 Rutgers study cited in the report found permanent daylight savings time would cut pedestrian deaths by about 13 percent during dusk and dawn. The researchers stress that systemic changes—like mandatory streetlights, traffic-calming infrastructure, and wildlife crossings—are also needed to protect vulnerable road users and wildlife.
