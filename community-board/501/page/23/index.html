      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Staten Island CB1?

      Staten Island Streets Still Kill—Who Will Stop the Bloodshed?

      Written July 13, 2025

      Staten Island CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A man steps into the street. A machine ends his life. In the last twelve months, four people died on Staten Island CB1 roads. Eight hundred thirty-four were hurt. Eleven were left with wounds so deep they will not heal. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about age. Children, elders, workers, neighbors—no one is spared.

      Just weeks ago, a motorcyclist was killed on Bay Street. Police said he collided with a car making a K-turn. A motorcyclist was killed after colliding with a driver awkwardly turning their car on Staten Island during the Fourth of July holiday weekend. The driver stayed. The man died. The street kept moving.

      In May, an e-scooter struck a man crossing Targee Street. The impact threw the rider. The pedestrian, Jose Luis Gomez-Guallazaca, was pronounced dead at the hospital. Upon impact, the driver was violently ejected off the scooter while Gomez-Guallazaca sustained severe trauma throughout his body.

      Who Pays, Who Acts

      The dead do not get a vote. The living do. Local leaders have made moves. Assembly Member Charles Fall voted to extend school speed zones, a step that protects children. Senator Andrew Lanza voted no on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill that would have forced repeat dangerous drivers to slow down. The bill passed anyway. But the message is clear: some leaders stand up for safety, others stand in the way.

      The Machines That Kill

      Most deaths come from cars and trucks. Four killed. Hundreds hurt. Motorcycles and mopeds take lives too. Bikes are not blameless, but their toll is small. The street is a battlefield, and the biggest machines win.

      What Now

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by someone with power. Call your council member. Call your senator. Tell them: slow the cars, redesign the streets, protect the people who walk and ride. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Demand action. Demand it now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Staten Island CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Staten Island, city council district District 49, assembly district AD 61 and state senate district SD 24.
      ▸ Which areas are in Staten Island CB1?
      It includes the St. George-New Brighton, Tompkinsville-Stapleton-Clifton-Fox Hills, Rosebank-Shore Acres-Park Hill, West New Brighton-Silver Lake-Grymes Hill, Westerleigh-Castleton Corners, Port Richmond, Mariner’S Harbor-Arlington-Graniteville, and Snug Harbor neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 49 and District 50, Assembly Districts AD 61 and AD 63, and State Senate Districts SD 23 and SD 24.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Staten Island CB1?
      Cars and Trucks caused the most harm: 5 deaths, 293 minor injuries, 137 moderate injuries, and 3 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds were involved in 0 deaths, 3 minor injuries, and 0 serious injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths, 4 minor injuries, and 3 moderate injuries. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not just accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by policymakers, by those who design and enforce our streets. Every crash can be prevented with better policy and design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      Local politicians can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, expand automated enforcement, and support laws that hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable. They can also oppose bills that shift blame to pedestrians and cyclists.

      Charles Fall Supports Safety Boosting Harlem Trash Containerization Expansion
      ▸

      New York will install fixed trash containers in West Harlem’s curb lanes by spring 2025. Sidewalk garbage bags will vanish. Custom trucks will haul the bins. The plan removes 150,000 parking spots. Streets clear. Rats drop. Sidewalks open for people, not cars.

      On February 1, 2024, the city announced an expansion of trash containerization in West Harlem. The Department of Sanitation will install stationary curbside trash enclosures for buildings with 31 or more units in Manhattan Community Board 9 by spring 2025. The policy, championed by Mayor Adams, follows a pilot that cut rat sightings by 68 percent. Adams called the new side-loading truck 'the future of New York City garbage collection.' The plan replaces about 150,000 parking spots—five percent of the city’s total—with trash containers. Smaller buildings will use wheeled bins on sidewalks. Advocates support the move, urging shared containers to keep sidewalks clear. Some warn that too many bins could harm businesses and streetscapes. The city will study environmental impacts before rollout. The expansion aims to reclaim space from cars, clear sidewalks, and improve safety for pedestrians and other vulnerable road users.

      Fall Supports Safety Boosting Commercial Waste Zone Program
      ▸

      Five years after passage, the city’s commercial waste overhaul crawls forward. Only one zone starts in 2024. Fewer trucks, fewer miles, but delays keep danger rolling. Streets still wait for safer rigs. Cyclists and pedestrians remain exposed.

      Bill creating the Commercial Waste Zone program, sponsored by Antonio Reynoso, passed in 2019 after deadly crashes involving private haulers. Yet, delays persist, especially on installing guard rails to prevent pedestrians and cyclists from being swept under trucks. Justin Wood of NYLPI voiced concern over the slow rollout. The program promises fewer trucks and safer streets, but for now, most neighborhoods wait.
