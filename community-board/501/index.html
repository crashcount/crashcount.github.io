      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Staten Island CB1?

      Staten Island Streets Still Kill—Who’s Next?

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 16, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      Staten Island CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A man steps into the street. A car turns left. The man does not make it home. On January 6, a 68-year-old was killed crossing Forest Avenue. The police report lists the cause: “Failure to Yield Right-of-Way” and “Driver Inattention/Distraction” (NYC Open Data). No arrest. No second chance.

      On May 17, Jose Luis Gomez-Guallazaca, 58, was crossing Targee Street. An e-scooter hit him. “Upon impact, the driver was violently ejected off the scooter while Gomez-Guallazaca sustained severe trauma” (amNY). He died at Richmond University Medical Center. The investigation drags on. The street stays the same.

      On June 29, a 16-year-old riding an electric scooter collided with a Hyundai Tucson. He suffered head trauma and died (The Brooklyn Paper). The driver stayed. The boy did not.

      In the last 12 months, four people have died and 834 have been injured on these streets. Eleven were seriously hurt.

      Who Pays the Price

      The dead are not numbers. They are fathers, sons, neighbors. In three and a half years, 17 people have died in Staten Island CB1. More than 2,600 have been injured. Most were walking, biking, or riding. Cars and SUVs did the most harm—five deaths, 293 minor injuries, 137 moderate injuries, three serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed three more. Motorcycles and mopeds, bikes, and vans add to the toll.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Some leaders act. Assembly Member Charles Fall voted to extend school speed zones, improving child pedestrian safety (Open States). Others do not. State Senator Andrew Lanza voted no on a bill to curb repeat speeders, opposing a measure that would have forced the most dangerous drivers to slow down (Open States).

      The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has not done so citywide. Cameras catch speeders, but some politicians call them a burden. The streets remain deadly.

      Call to Action: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      This is not fate. It is failure. Call your council member. Call your state senator. Tell them to lower speed limits, expand enforcement, and redesign streets for people, not cars. Join Families for Safe Streets. Stand with those who have lost. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Demand change now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Staten Island CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Staten Island, city council district District 49, assembly district AD 61 and state senate district SD 24.
      ▸ Which areas are in Staten Island CB1?
      It includes the St. George-New Brighton, Tompkinsville-Stapleton-Clifton-Fox Hills, Rosebank-Shore Acres-Park Hill, West New Brighton-Silver Lake-Grymes Hill, Westerleigh-Castleton Corners, Port Richmond, Mariner’S Harbor-Arlington-Graniteville, and Snug Harbor neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 49 and District 50, Assembly Districts AD 61 and AD 63, and State Senate Districts SD 23 and SD 24.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Staten Island CB1?
      Cars and SUVs: 5 deaths, 293 minor injuries, 137 moderate injuries, 3 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 3 deaths, 33 minor injuries, 9 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 3 minor injuries, 0 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 4 minor injuries, 3 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Most deaths and injuries happen on the same streets, year after year. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement can prevent them.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign dangerous streets, and pass laws to stop repeat dangerous drivers. They can also support funding for safer crossings and bike lanes.
