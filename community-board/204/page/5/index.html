      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx CB4?

      Hit-and-Run City: Blood on Bronx Streets, Silence at City Hall

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Bronx CB4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Bodies on the Road

      A boy on a scooter, fifteen years old, struck down at Jerome and 167th. The motorcyclist ran the red and kept going. The boy was left in the street, fighting for breath. Police asked for help to find the driver. The boy’s family waits by his hospital bed. Police said a 15-year-old boy riding his scooter was critically hurt when a Bronx motorcyclist blew through a red light, slammed into him and sped away.

      A father, Kelvin Mitchell, crossing Webster Avenue. A black Mercedes, speeding in the bus lane, hit him and kept going. His sister said, “God is going to make sure that my brother sees justice… Everybody is missing Kelvin. It’s not gonna be the same over here without him.”

      In the last twelve months, 1 person died and 526 were injured in crashes in Bronx CB4. Children, elders, workers. One serious injury. Hundreds of broken bodies. The numbers do not heal.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs killed 3 pedestrians here. Sedans killed 1. Buses, bikes, mopeds, taxis, motorcycles—each left someone hurt. The streets are not safe for the old or the young. The danger is not random. It is built into the road.

      The Leaders and the Law

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They say, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” They promise more cameras, lower speed limits, safer crossings. But the pain does not wait for press releases. Sammy’s Law lets the city lower speed limits to 20 mph. The law is there. The will is not. The cameras that catch speeders are at risk of going dark. The city can act. It does not.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Demand justice for the dead and the living.

      Do not wait for another child’s blood on the street. Take action now.

      Int 1050-2024
      Stevens co-sponsors bill capping TLC insurance, potentially reducing street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1050-2024 blocks the Taxi and Limousine Commission from demanding more injury coverage than state law. The measure, laid over in committee, shields cab companies from higher insurance costs. Passengers and crash victims face tighter limits on payouts.

      Int 1050-2024 was introduced on September 26, 2024, and is now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to limiting the amount of liability coverage that the taxi and limousine commission may require for vehicles it licenses," bars the TLC from requiring more personal injury protection than state law demands. Council Member Diana I. Ayala is the primary sponsor, joined by over two dozen co-sponsors including De La Rosa, Banks, Ossé, Marte, and others. The bill was laid over by committee on February 10, 2025. The measure would take effect immediately if passed. By capping required coverage, the bill may leave crash victims with less compensation after collisions involving TLC-licensed vehicles.

      Int 1069-2024
      Stevens co-sponsors bill reducing commercial parking time, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1069-2024 slashes legal parking time for tractor-trailers and similar trucks to 90 minutes. Commercial vehicles clogging curbs will face tighter limits. The measure targets streets where hulking rigs linger, blocking sightlines and endangering walkers and riders.

      Int 1069-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on September 26, 2024. The bill reads: "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to reducing the maximum time commercial vehicles may park." It limits parking for tractor-trailer combinations, tractors, truck trailers, and semi-trailers to 90 minutes, down from three hours. Council Member Kevin C. Riley leads as primary sponsor, joined by Nantasha M. Williams, Darlene Mealy, Sandy Nurse, and others. The bill was referred to committee the day it was introduced. No safety analyst has yet assessed the direct impact on vulnerable road users, but the measure aims to keep large trucks from blocking curbs and crosswalks, where they threaten pedestrians and cyclists.

      Int 0346-2024
      Stevens votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.

      Int 0746-2024
      Stevens votes yes, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety with greener medians.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to turn bare medians green. The city must plant trees or use medians for stormwater. Agencies must report progress. The law targets high-risk areas. Votes showed strong support. The mayor returned it unsigned. Streets will change.

      Int 0746-2024, now Local Law 94 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 12, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the improvement and maintenance of medians through the planting of vegetation or use in stormwater management," requires the city to improve at least one mile of paved medians every two years until 2046. The Department of Transportation, Parks, and Environmental Protection must add planted medians, tree beds, or stormwater infrastructure, focusing on high-priority investment areas. Council Member Shekar Krishnan sponsored the bill, joined by Feliz, Avilés, Nurse, Banks, Restler, Ung, Schulman, Holden, Hudson, and Yeger. The Council passed the bill with broad support, though the mayor returned it unsigned. The law mandates annual progress reports and public tracking of improved medians and responsible agencies.

      Stevens Questions Citi Bike Dock Size Despite Rising Usage
      ▸

      Council Member Stevens doubts Citi Bike demand in the Bronx. She sees full docks, asks if they should shrink. Experts fire back: full docks mean high use. Ridership surges. DOT stands firm. Bike-share stays. Riders keep moving.

      At a May 8, 2024, City Council oversight hearing, Council Member Althea Stevens questioned the need for a Citi Bike dock in her South Bronx district. She said, "If we're seeing that these stations aren't being used adequately ... is there another option, can we make the docks smaller?" The Department of Transportation, led by Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez, defended the current system. Bike-share experts like David Eddington countered that full docks signal popularity, not neglect. Citi Bike General Manager Patrick Knoth explained that point-in-time observations mislead, as ridership is strong and docks are planned based on trends. Citi Bike usage in Stevens's district rose 48 percent from 2022 to 2023, with most riders using reduced fares. The hearing highlighted the need for a comprehensive, accessible bike-share network, especially as e-bikes make hilly areas more reachable.
