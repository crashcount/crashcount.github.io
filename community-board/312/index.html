      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn’s Blood Price: Policy Kills, Leaders Hide

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed duplicate headline in body and cleaned up citations.)

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      In Brooklyn CB12, the street is a gauntlet. Since 2022, 20 people have died. 2,181 have been injured. 17 were left with wounds so deep they are called serious. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The living carry scars you cannot see.

      Just last week, a 17-year-old boy died on a moped in Midwood. He was thrown from the seat. His passenger was left fighting for life. Police said, “Jhoan later died at the hospital” (NY Daily News).

      A year ago, a 16-year-old cyclist was crushed by a turning truck at Coney Island Avenue and Ditmas. The city counted the body. The street stayed the same.

      SUVs and trucks do most of the killing. In three years, SUVs and cars took 7 lives and left 517 injured. Trucks and buses killed 2, injured 45. Bikes and mopeds are not blameless, but their toll is a fraction.

      Leadership: Votes, Silence, and Delay

      The numbers rise. The policies crawl. Council Member Simcha Felder has a record of fighting safety: he blocked speed cameras, opposed lower speed limits, and skipped the funeral for three killed by a speeder. Streetsblog wrote, “Felder has a long record of opposing street safety measures, including Vision Zero, speed cameras, speed limits, and street redesigns” (Streetsblog NYC).

      Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein voted against renewing the city’s speed camera program. He stood with those who called cameras a burden, not a shield. The votes are on the record. The bodies are in the ground.

      Some bills have been filed. Some died in committee. Some passed, but the street does not change overnight. Every day of delay is another risk.

      The Call: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      This is not fate. It is policy.

      Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Tell them: Lower the speed limit. Build real protection. Stop blaming the dead.

      The street remembers every name. Do not let your leaders forget.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      SUVs and Cars: 7 deaths, 374 injuries. Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 30 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 14 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 17 injuries. Most deaths and injuries come from SUVs, cars, and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These are not random accidents. The numbers rise because of choices—speed limits, street design, and enforcement. Every delay in action means more risk.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign streets, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking. They can stop blaming victims and start building real safety.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      City Targets Overweight Trucks On BQE
      ▸

      Heavy trucks pound the BQE. The city will ticket Staten Island-bound rigs. Sensors catch the violators. The road crumbles under weight it cannot bear. Warnings are over. Fines begin. The system aims to cut danger and slow decay.

      Gothamist (2025-06-21) reports that New York City will start ticketing overweight Staten Island-bound trucks on the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway. The Department of Transportation will fine violators $650 per incident, using in-road sensors to detect excess weight. Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, "Overweight trucks cause wear and tear on our roadways and we all pay the price through expensive repairs to our infrastructure." The city issued over 3,000 warnings during a 90-day grace period. Similar enforcement on Queens-bound trucks led to a 60% drop in overweight vehicles. Albany has approved expanding this automated system to more city roadways. The BQE’s aging structure faces risk from trucks exceeding its design limits, raising safety and infrastructure concerns.

      Judge Blocks Bedford Avenue Bike Lane Removal
      ▸

      A Brooklyn judge stopped the city from tearing out a protected bike lane. Cyclists and children face danger when lanes vanish. The city acted fast, without notice. The fight for safe passage on Bedford Avenue continues in court.

      Streetsblog NYC reported on June 18, 2025, that Judge Carolyn Walker-Diallo halted New York City's plan to remove three blocks of the Bedford Avenue protected bike lane. The court found the city may have acted "arbitrarily, capriciously, and illegally" by ordering the removal without proper legal notice. City law requires the Department of Transportation to notify local officials and allow time for public discussion, but, as the court papers state, "DOT did not provide the required notice." Plaintiffs argued that removing the lane would "irreparably" harm cyclists, especially children. The city claimed it would only shift the lane, not remove it, but advocates countered that any removal increases danger. The case highlights the need for transparent processes and the risks when safety infrastructure is hastily altered.

      Flatbush Avenue Bus Lanes, Pedestrian Islands Planned
      ▸

      Flatbush Avenue will lose car lanes. Bus lanes and pedestrian islands will take their place. Fifty-five killed or badly hurt since 2019. Buses crawl. Pedestrians dodge traffic. The city moves to fix a deadly, clogged corridor.

      Gothamist reported on June 4, 2025, that New York City's Department of Transportation plans to overhaul Flatbush Avenue between Livingston Street and Grand Army Plaza. The redesign replaces two car lanes with 24/7 bus-only lanes and adds pedestrian islands. DOT officials said, 'almost 70,000 daily bus riders are stuck waiting too long for slow buses, drivers are caught in a mess of traffic and pedestrians are left crossing intersections clogged with vehicles.' Since 2019, 55 people have been killed or severely injured in crashes along this stretch. The plan removes curbside parking and bans cars from bus lanes, aiming to speed up twelve bus routes and protect people on foot. Most residents in the area do not own cars and depend on slow buses. The proposal reflects a shift toward prioritizing vulnerable road users and addressing systemic danger on one of Brooklyn's busiest corridors.
