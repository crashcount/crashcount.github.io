      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn Streets Bleed While Politicians Stall

      Written July 6, 2025

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Brooklyn CB12, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people killed. Seventeen left with serious injuries. Over 2,100 hurt since 2022. The dead include children, elders, and people just trying to cross the street. In the last year alone, two children died. One was a teenager on a moped. Another was younger, struck while crossing. The pain does not fade. The street remembers.

      SUVs and trucks do the most damage. Seven killed by SUVs and trucks. One by a sedan. The rest are numbers, but each number is a name, a family, a life ended or broken.

      Recent Crashes, Unanswered

      On June 17, 2024, a 67-year-old man was killed at 15th Avenue and 54th Street. The truck kept going straight. The man did not go home. The record notes only this: “Apparent Death” and “Crossing Against Signal.” The driver lived. The pedestrian did not. The system shrugs. NYC Open Data.

      On August 19, 2024, a 16-year-old boy on a bike was crushed by a turning truck at Coney Island Avenue and Ditmas. The truck made a right. The boy was gone. No law, no sign, no camera stopped it.

      Leaders: Votes and Silence

      Local politicians have voted both ways. Council Member Kalman Yeger co-sponsored bills to audit deadly crossings and add speed reducers, but he also voted against safer truck routes, greenway expansion, and the law that ended jaywalking tickets. He was absent for key votes on daylighting and pedestrian safety. When the Assembly voted to renew the city’s speed camera program, Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein voted no.

      The excuses are always the same. At a funeral for a mother and two children killed by a repeat speeder, a local official said, “any driver can get much more than six” speed violations in a year. He called speed cameras a punishment for regular drivers.

      The Call: No More Waiting

      The deaths are not fate. They are policy. Every delay, every no vote, every shrug is a choice. The streets do not forgive. Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns. Demand action, not excuses.

      If you wait, another family will grieve. The street will remember.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      Most deaths and injuries to pedestrians here come from SUVs and Cars (7 deaths, 526 injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (2 deaths, 47 injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 21 injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 25 injuries). SUVs and trucks are the most lethal. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These deaths and injuries are not random. They happen again and again, often at the same crossings, to the same kinds of people. Better street design, lower speed limits, and enforcement can prevent them. The numbers do not lie.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, expand camera enforcement, and fund real safety improvements. They can also stop blocking or delaying proven safety laws.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0578-2022
      Yeger co-sponsors bill boosting pedestrian crossing safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force DOT to study deadly intersections and add signals that give walkers a head start or full crossing time. The bill died at session’s end. Streets stay risky. Pedestrians wait for action.

      Bill Int 0578-2022, introduced July 14, 2022, aimed to amend city law to improve safety at pedestrian crossings. The Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure reviewed it, but the bill was filed without passage at the end of session on December 31, 2023. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to improving safety at pedestrian crossings in the city.' Council Members Robert F. Holden (primary sponsor), Ari Kagan, Justin L. Brannan, Jennifer Gutiérrez, Sandy Nurse, Kalman Yeger, Rita C. Joseph, and Tiffany Cabán sponsored the bill. It would have required DOT to audit the city’s deadliest intersections and consider installing leading pedestrian intervals and exclusive pedestrian phases—signals proven to protect people on foot. The bill’s failure leaves dangerous crossings unchanged.

Council Member Carmen N. De La Rosa led as primary sponsor, joined by Salamanca, Louis, Brewer, Joseph, Yeger, Dinowitz, and Sanchez.       Int 0596-2022
      Yeger co-sponsors bill to boost pedestrian and cyclist safety via curb repairs.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0596-2022 would force DOT to repair broken curbs when streets get resurfaced. Curbs shape the border between sidewalk and street. Broken curbs trip walkers, let cars mount sidewalks, and endanger the frail. The bill died in committee.

      Int 0596-2022 was introduced on July 14, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill required the Department of Transportation to repair any curb deemed a safety hazard during street resurfacing. The matter summary reads: 'requiring that the department of transportation repair broken curbs as part of resurfacing projects.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers sponsored the bill, joined by Schulman, Brewer, Dinowitz, Krishnan, Narcisse, Menin, Yeger, Avilés, Nurse, Gutiérrez, Riley, Brannan, Sanchez, and Louis. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. Broken curbs are a daily threat to pedestrians, especially the elderly and disabled. The bill would have forced the city to address these hazards as part of routine work, but the effort stalled.
