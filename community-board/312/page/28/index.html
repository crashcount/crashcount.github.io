      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn Blood on Their Hands: Politicians Block Lifesaving Action

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Brooklyn CB12, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people are dead. Seventeen more are left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Since 2022, there have been 3,650 crashes. The bodies pile up. The pain does not fade.

      Just this spring, a 61-year-old man was killed crossing Bay Parkway. The SUV hit him at the intersection. He died from internal injuries. There was no second chance. NYC Open Data

      Children are not spared. Two have died. Three more suffered serious injuries. The old are not spared. Four people over 65 are dead. The street does not care who you are.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs do most of the killing. Seven dead. Five with life-changing wounds. Trucks and buses take two more lives. Motorcycles and mopeds injure and kill. Bikes, too, leave scars, but the numbers are smaller. The pattern is clear. The bigger the machine, the greater the harm.

      Words From the Street

      After a moped struck and killed a 90-year-old man, a neighbor said, “They plow right through red lights, no consideration for people crossing. They just don’t care.”

      A witness saw the aftermath: “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open.”

      Leadership: Votes and Silence

      Local leaders have failed to act with urgency. Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein voted against renewing the city’s speed camera program—a tool proven to save lives. Council Member Kalman Yeger has voted no on bills to daylight intersections and redesign truck routes for safety. The bills passed anyway, but the message is clear: some leaders stand in the way of change.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Every death is preventable. The machines do not drive themselves. The laws do not write themselves. Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Tell them to stand for the living, not the dead. Demand safer speeds, more cameras, and streets built for people, not cars. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Int 0203-2022
      Yeger sponsors bill requiring limo safety inspection proof, no overall safety change.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force luxury limo companies to show safety inspection proof at booking. No loopholes. No excuses. Passengers would see if a car passed inspection before stepping in. The bill died at session’s end. Danger remains.

      Bill Int 0203-2022 was introduced on April 14, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill would have amended city law to require luxury limousine services to provide proof of vehicle safety inspections at the time of contract. The matter summary reads: 'This bill would require for-hire luxury limousine companies to provide proof of safety inspections for its licensed vehicles at the time a person contracts with the company for the transportation of passengers.' Council Members Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor, District 44) and Rafael Salamanca, Jr. (co-sponsor, District 17) backed the measure. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided. The bill’s failure leaves passengers and other road users exposed to unchecked vehicles.

      Int 0101-2022
      Yeger co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring quick temporary signs at broken signals.
      ▸

      When a traffic light fails, time matters. This bill orders the city to plant stop or yield signs within an hour. No more waiting. The aim: fewer crashes, fewer bodies in the street. The council filed it. The danger remains.

      Int 0101-2022 was introduced on March 24, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill required the Department of Transportation to place temporary priority regulatory signs—like stop or yield—at intersections within one hour of a reported inoperable traffic signal. The matter summary states, 'requiring the placement of temporary priority regulatory signs at intersections within one hour of a report of an inoperable traffic control signal.' Council Members Shahana K. Hanif (primary), Julie Won, Kalman Yeger, and Justin L. Brannan sponsored the bill. The session ended with the bill filed, not enacted. Analysts noted that faster signage could 'significantly reduce the risk of pedestrian injury and vehicular collisions at intersections with malfunctioning traffic control signals.' The city’s current two-hour response and 24-hour wait for alternative controls left vulnerable road users exposed. This bill tried to close that deadly gap.

      Res 0091-2022
      Yeger sponsors bill raising penalties for out-of-state vehicle registration.
      ▸

      Council calls for stiffer penalties on drivers dodging New York registration. Out-of-state cars, often tied to deadly crashes, skirt city rules and costs. The measure aims to crack down on scofflaws and close loopholes that endanger New Yorkers.

      Resolution 0091-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on March 24, 2022, and closed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023. The resolution, titled 'Resolution calling upon the New York State Legislature to introduce and pass, and the Governor to sign, legislation increasing penalties on drivers who improperly register vehicles in another state,' was sponsored by Council Members Robert F. Holden, Kalman Yeger (primary), and Justin L. Brannan. The bill cites data showing out-of-state cars are involved in a growing share of city crashes, including 11,000 injuries and 62 deaths in 2021 alone. The council urges Albany to act, targeting drivers who dodge taxes, fees, and insurance by registering elsewhere. The measure seeks to close enforcement gaps that let dangerous drivers slip through, shifting the burden and risk onto city streets.

      Res 0090-2022
      Yeger sponsors resolution urging stricter fitness rules for older drivers.
      ▸

      Council calls for tougher checks on aging drivers. S.5004 would force a medical exam after seven license renewals. The move follows a teen’s death by a senior driver. New York lags behind other states on older driver safety rules.

      Resolution 0090-2022, filed at session’s end, came from the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced March 24, 2022, it urges Albany to pass S.5004. The resolution’s title: 'calling upon the New York State Legislature to pass, and the Governor to sign, S.5004, to enhance the fitness and safety requirements for certain licensed individuals.' Council Members Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor, District 44) and Robert F. Holden (co-sponsor, District 30) led the push. The bill responds to the death of Madeline Sershen, 17, killed by an 88-year-old driver who ran a red. S.5004 would require drivers to prove medical fitness with a physician’s exam after their seventh license renewal. New York’s current rules only demand a vision test every eight years, far less strict than many states. The council’s action highlights a gap in protections for pedestrians and other vulnerable road users.

      Int 0075-2022
      Yeger co-sponsors moving billboard ban, boosting street safety for all.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban moving billboards. The bill would have stopped cars from carrying giant ads through city streets. Sponsors wanted to codify an existing rule. The session ended. The bill died. The danger rolls on.

      Int 0075-2022 was introduced on March 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to ban moving billboards, echoing the matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to banning moving billboards.' Council Members Gale A. Brewer (primary), Justin L. Brannan, Erik D. Bottcher, Kalman Yeger, and Lincoln Restler sponsored the measure. The bill sought to codify an existing city rule (34 RCNY 4-12(j)), making it illegal to operate, stand, or park vehicles for commercial advertising. Violators would face a $500 penalty. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Int 0053-2022
      Yeger sponsors bill to restrict overnight commercial parking, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban commercial trucks from sleeping on quiet streets. The bill died. Utility and oil trucks kept their curb. Pedestrians and families still dodge hulking vehicles at dawn. The city blinked. The danger stayed.

      Int 0053-2022, introduced on February 24, 2022, sought to amend city law to bar commercial vehicles from parking overnight on residential streets, between 9 p.m. and 5 a.m. The Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure handled the bill. The measure removed exemptions for gas, oil, and utility trucks. The matter summary reads: 'This bill would prohibit the parking of commercial vehicles owned or operated by gas or oil heat suppliers, gas or oil heat systems maintenance companies or any public utility in residential streets overnight.' Council Members Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor), Robert F. Holden, Vickie Paladino, and Kevin C. Riley backed the bill. The council filed it at session’s end, on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst weighed in, but the bill’s failure means residential streets remain lined with large trucks, leaving vulnerable road users exposed.
