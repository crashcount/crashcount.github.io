      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn’s Streets Bleed While Leaders Look Away

      Written July 7, 2025

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Brooklyn CB12, the street is a wound that never heals. Twenty people have died here since 2022. Seventeen more have been left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. In the last year alone, five lives ended. Two were children. One was a man in his prime. The numbers do not flinch: 663 injured, 7 seriously, in just twelve months (NYC Open Data).

      Last November, a van changed lanes on Bay Parkway and McDonald Avenue. The driver died. His passenger, a woman, survived with broken bones. In August, a sixteen-year-old on a bike was crushed by a turning truck at Coney Island Avenue and Ditmas. He never made it home. The dead do not speak. The living remember.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars did most of the damage. They killed seven. They injured more than five hundred. Trucks and buses killed two, injured thirty. Motorcycles and mopeds, twenty-one injuries, one death. Bikes, twenty-five injuries, no deaths. The street is not fair. The bigger machine wins (NYC Open Data).

      Leaders: Action and Absence

      The council talks. Sometimes it acts. Council Member Kalman Yeger co-sponsored bills to audit crossings and add speed reducers. But he also voted no on safer truck routes, no on daylighting, no on greenways, no on ending jaywalking tickets. He opposed the city’s greenway plan, even as others called it a chance to “make a profound impact.”

      When the state moved to renew speed cameras—proven to cut deaths, Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein voted no. Yeger did too. The silence after each vote is louder than the debate.

      The Voices Left Behind

      A son watched his father die on New Utrecht Avenue. “He didn’t stop. He just kept going,” said Henry Tziquin. The driver fled. The family waited for a miracle that never came.

      What Now

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings. Demand leaders who do not look away.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      SUVs and Cars: 7 deaths, 526 injuries. Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 47 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 21 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 25 injuries. Most deaths and injuries come from cars, SUVs, and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These are not random accidents. The pattern is clear: most deaths and injuries come from cars and trucks. Proven safety measures—like lower speed limits, protected crossings, and better enforcement—can prevent them. Every crash is a policy choice.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, redesign dangerous crossings, expand protected bike lanes, and support automated enforcement. They can vote for—not against—proven safety measures. They can listen to residents who demand safer streets.

      Yeger co-sponsors bill that could delay safer neighborhood loading zones.
      ▸

      Council Member Yeger filed a bill to force DOT to warn neighbors before new loading zones. The bill demands posted notices, alerts to local leaders, and a month for public comment. The measure died in committee. No change for street safety.

      Int 1274-2023, introduced by Council Member Kalman Yeger, was filed with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on December 6, 2023. The bill sought to 'expand notice requirements and require a comment period prior to the installation of a neighborhood loading zone.' Yeger sponsored the measure. It would have required DOT to post physical notices, notify council members, community boards, property owners, and allow a 30-day public comment period before installing loading zones. DOT would also have to publish new zone locations online within five days. The bill was filed at the end of session and did not advance.       Int 0417-2022
      Yeger votes no on notice requirements bill, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to streamline how the city notifies communities about big street changes. Bike lanes now follow the same rules as other major projects. The city can act faster after feedback. The old bike lane notice law is gone.

      Int 0417-2022, now enacted, amends the city code to create a single, uniform process for notifying council members and community boards about major transportation projects—including bike lanes. The bill, handled by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on May 19, 2022, and passed on January 6, 2024. The official title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to notice requirements for certain transportation projects and the repeal of section 19-187 in relation thereto.' Council Member Shekar Krishnan led as primary sponsor, joined by dozens of colleagues. The law repeals the old, separate bike lane notice process and folds it into the broader project notice rules. Now, after a set notice period and community feedback, the Department of Transportation can move ahead without delay. This change aims to cut red tape and speed up street redesigns that affect vulnerable road users.

      Int 0708-2022
      Yeger votes no, opposing safer truck routes and improved street visibility.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to overhaul city truck routes. The bill orders daylighting at intersections and new signage. It aims to cut crashes, boost visibility, and shrink truck miles. Sponsors demand safer streets for people on foot and bike. Change is now law.

      Int 0708-2022, now Local Law 171 of 2023, was enacted by the City Council on December 16, 2023, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The law, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to redesigning the city truck route network," requires the Department of Transportation to redesign truck routes to "improve safety, increase visibility, reduce traffic congestion, and reduce vehicle miles traveled." Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by dozens of co-sponsors. The law mandates daylighting—removing parking near intersections—to clear sight lines for drivers and protect people crossing. DOT must consult with safety groups, residents, and businesses, and replace truck route signage for better clarity. The law sets deadlines: initial report by November 2023, implementation by June 2024, and new signage by July 2024. This overhaul targets the deadly risk trucks pose to vulnerable road users at city intersections.
