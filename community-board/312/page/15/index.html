      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn Bleeds While Politicians Look Away

      Written June 29, 2025

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      Another Year, More Blood on the Street

      In Brooklyn CB12, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people have died on these streets since 2022. More than 2,100 have been injured. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers—no one is spared. In the last twelve months alone, five lives lost, 677 injured, seven left with wounds that will not heal (NYC Open Data).

      Last week, a 17-year-old boy died after his moped collided with a car on East 8th Street. He was thrown from the seat. His passenger, just 14, left with broken bones. Police said, “The teenager was riding a gas moped with a passenger on the back, traveling north on East 8th St. in Midwood when it slammed into a Genesis G80 vehicle operated by a 71-year-old man making a left turn off East 8th St.” (NY Daily News)

      Days before, a drunk, unlicensed driver killed a moped rider in Bay Ridge. The man died less than half a mile from home. Police said, “Moreno was driving west on 67th Street while Mota was driving south on Third Avenue, and he hit her passenger-side door.” (Gothamist)

      Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and cars do the most harm. In three years, they killed seven, injured 374, and left five with serious wounds. Trucks and buses killed two, injured 30. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, sons, mothers.

      A child crossing with his sister. A cyclist crushed by a turning truck. A pedestrian struck in the crosswalk. The stories repeat. The pain does not fade.

      Leadership: Votes, Silence, and Missed Chances

      Local leaders have failed to act with urgency. Council Member Simcha Felder has a record of opposing speed cameras and safer street redesigns. After a speeding driver killed three people, Felder made no statement and skipped the funeral. As reported, “Felder has a long record of opposing street safety measures, including Vision Zero, speed cameras, speed limits, and street redesigns.” (Streetsblog NYC)

      Assembly Member Michael Novakhov stood at a funeral and said, “six red-light or speed violations in one year [is] too little,” and claimed, “any driver can get much more than six.” He called for fewer cameras, not more (Streetsblog NYC).

      Some bills have been filed—audits, daylighting, raised speed reducers—but most die in committee or are watered down. The streets remain the same. The bodies pile up.

      What Now? Stand Up or Stand By

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph limit. Demand speed cameras where children cross. Demand street redesigns that put people first. Do not wait for another funeral.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      Cars and Trucks: SUVs and cars killed 7, injured 374, and caused 5 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 2, injured 30, and caused 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: No deaths, but 14 minor injuries, 6 moderate injuries, and 1 serious injury. Bikes: No deaths, but 17 minor injuries and 8 moderate injuries (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The same patterns repeat: left turns, speeding, unlicensed drivers, dangerous intersections. These deaths and injuries are preventable with better street design, enforcement, and policy.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign dangerous intersections, and support bills that protect people walking and cycling. They can stop blocking proven safety measures.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1164-2023
      Yeger votes no, opposing a bill that improves street safety.
      ▸

      Council passed Int 1164-2023. The law forces DOT to map out where street safety money goes. It tracks past investment, crash rates, and who gets left behind. The city must show its work, district by district. No more hiding the gaps.

      Int 1164-2023, now enacted, amends the city code to require an 'investment roadmap' in every Streets Master Plan starting December 1, 2026. The bill moved through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passing on October 19, 2023, and became law on November 19, 2023. The law states: 'The department shall prioritize and promote...the safety of all street users...and improving equity in infrastructure investment.' Sponsored by Kevin C. Riley (primary), with Brooks-Powers, Narcisse, Hanif, Brewer, Restler, and Rivera as co-sponsors, the bill demands DOT report, by neighborhood, on safety infrastructure investment, crash rates, and demographics. It shines a light on which districts get safety upgrades and which are left exposed. The roadmap aims to close the gap for underserved areas, forcing transparency and accountability for every dollar spent on street safety.

      Int 0712-2022
      Yeger votes yes on license plate reporting bill with no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to report cars with unreadable plates dodging cameras. The bill targets drivers hiding their plates from red light and speed cameras. Reports must show where, when, and how plates go unseen. Data goes public, every quarter.

      Int 0712-2022, now enacted as Local Law 155 of 2023, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and the full Council on October 19, 2023. The law, sponsored by Gale A. Brewer with co-sponsors including Erik D. Bottcher, Lincoln Restler, and others, amends the city code to require the Department of Transportation to report on vehicles with license plates unreadable by photo violation monitoring systems. The law’s title states it is 'in relation to reporting on motor vehicles with license plates that are unreadable by photo violation monitoring systems.' DOT must post quarterly reports showing the time, location, and reasons plates evade cameras—whether by concealment, distortion, or missing tags. The law aims to expose drivers who dodge automated enforcement, shining light on a loophole that puts pedestrians and cyclists at risk. The reports must be public and sent to the Mayor and Council Speaker.
