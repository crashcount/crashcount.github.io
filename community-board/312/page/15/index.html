      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn’s Body Count Grows—Who’s Brave Enough to Stop the Killing?

      Written July 18, 2025

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Brooklyn CB12, the bodies keep piling up. Twenty-one people have died on these streets since 2022. Two were children. Seventeen more suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In the last twelve months alone, six people died and seven were seriously hurt. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the sound of sirens at night, the hush after a crash.

      Just last month, a 17-year-old was killed and a 14-year-old injured when a moped collided with a parked SUV on Avenue N. Days later, a 70-year-old man was struck and killed crossing East 5th Street. The driver was distracted. The man never made it home. The dead do not get second chances.

      The Vehicles That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. Since 2022, they have taken eight lives and left 379 people injured in CB12. Trucks and buses killed two more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll. The street does not care what you drive. But the bigger the vehicle, the harder the blow.

      Leaders: Action or Excuses?

      Local leaders have had chances to act. Some have chosen delay or denial. Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein voted against renewing the speed camera program that protects children near schools. He also voted no on bills to make school speed zones safer. Streetsblog NYC named him among those who “voted against speed camera program.” The cost is measured in funerals, not fines.

      When the state considered a bill to force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the law, local Senator Sam Sutton missed the vote. The silence is loud. The streets stay deadly.

      The Voices Left Behind

      After a BMW driver killed two men in a hit-and-run, a neighbor said “drivers speed on that stretch of roadway”. The driver admitted, “I had a six pack of Modelos and two drinks. I was driving the car by myself last night. It’s my fault. After I hit them, I came home”. The dead cannot answer.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Thoughts and Prayers

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Tell them to back speed cameras, lower speed limits, and support laws that stop repeat offenders. Every day they wait, another family waits for a knock at the door.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      Most deaths and injuries come from cars and SUVs (8 deaths, 379 injuries), followed by trucks and buses (2 deaths, 45 injuries), motorcycles and mopeds (no deaths, 21 injuries), and bikes (no deaths, 25 injuries). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These deaths and injuries are not random. Policies like speed cameras, lower speed limits, and better street design can prevent them. When leaders delay or oppose these measures, people die.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can support speed cameras, lower speed limits, and back laws that stop repeat dangerous drivers. They can vote for bills that protect people outside cars, not just drivers.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      A 8471
      Eichenstein sponsors bill on licensing and training for low-speed e-vehicles.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 8471 demands licenses, training, and insurance for low-speed electric vehicles. It targets city streets. The bill aims to regulate who drives, how they learn, and who pays when crashes come.

      Assembly bill A 8471 was proposed on December 29, 2023, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits with the Assembly, sponsored by Simcha Eichenstein of District 48. The measure 'relates to the licensing, registration, purchase, insurance and training for low velocity electric vehicles.' It requires new license classes, a safety manual, operator testing, registration, and liability insurance for these vehicles in cities over one million. Eichenstein leads the push for tighter controls. No safety analyst has yet assessed the impact on vulnerable road users.
