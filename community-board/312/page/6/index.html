      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn’s Body Count Grows—Politicians Shrug, Streets Stay Deadly

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, ensured all quotes have inline links, deduped and sorted citations.)

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Brooklyn CB12, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people killed. Seventeen left with serious injuries. Since 2022, there have been over 3,600 crashes. The dead are not numbers. They are children, elders, neighbors. They are the boy who never made it home, the cyclist crushed at the curb, the mother who will not see another sunrise.

      Just last week, a 17-year-old died on East 8th Street. His moped collided with a car. He was rushed to Maimonides. He did not survive. The NY Daily News reported, “The teenager was riding a gas moped with a passenger on the back, traveling north on East 8th St. in Midwood when it slammed into a Genesis G80 vehicle operated by a 71-year-old man making a left turn off East 8th St., cops said.” The driver stayed. The street stayed the same. [Teen Killed, Man Critical In Brooklyn Crash]

      SUVs and trucks do the most damage. Six deaths and hundreds of injuries from SUVs alone. Trucks and buses killed two. Cars, motorcycles, mopeds, bikes—all take their share. But the biggest vehicles leave the deepest scars.

      Leaders Talk, Streets Stay Deadly

      The council votes. The bills pile up. Some sound good. Most move slow. Council Member Kalman Yeger co-sponsored bills for speed reducers and safer crossings, but also voted against safer truck routes and greenways. He was absent for key votes on daylighting and pedestrian safety. Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein voted against renewing the speed camera program that saves lives. Senator Simcha Felder has a record of blocking speed cameras and safer street designs.

      After a crash, the street is washed clean. The pain stays. “He was a boy who loved life,” said a neighbor after another child was killed.

      What Now? No More Waiting

      Every crash is preventable. Lower the speed limit. Build real protection for people walking and biking. Install raised crosswalks. Keep speed cameras running. Hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable. Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Demand action.

      The blood on the street is not an act of God. It is a failure of will. The dead cannot speak. You can.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      SUVs and Cars: 7 deaths, 374 minor injuries, 139 moderate injuries, 5 serious injuries (SUVs/cars); Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 30 minor injuries, 13 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: No deaths, 14 minor injuries, 6 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Bikes: No deaths, 17 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Every crash is preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and enforcement against repeat dangerous drivers can save lives. The numbers show the cost of delay.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, keep speed cameras running, and pass laws to hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable. They can show up after a crash and fight for real change, not just words.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
