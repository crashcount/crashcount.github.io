      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn Bleeds While Politicians Look Away

      Written July 4, 2025

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 4, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Brooklyn CB12, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people are dead. Seventeen more are maimed for life. Since 2022, there have been 3,637 crashes. The wounds are not just numbers. They are children, elders, neighbors. They are the sound of sirens at midnight, the blood on the crosswalk, the empty seat at dinner.

      Just this spring, a 61-year-old man was killed crossing Bay Parkway. The SUV kept going straight. The man did not. The city called it an ‘apparent death’. No law, no sign, no redesign saved him.

      Fourteen people were hurt when a van slammed into a tree on a Tuesday morning. The van was full of adults with special needs. “It’s sad because they definitely need a lot of attention more than anyone else. I hope that they’re all OK at the end of the day,” said a witness. The tree stood. The people inside did not.

      The Record of Local Leaders

      Local politicians have not stopped the bleeding. Council Member Simcha Felder has a record of opposing speed cameras, lower speed limits, and street redesigns. After a speeding driver killed three people this spring, Felder made no statement. He did not show up at the funeral. Streetsblog wrote, “Felder has a long record of opposing street safety measures, including Vision Zero, speed cameras, speed limits, and street redesigns.” Streetsblog reported.

      Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein voted against renewing the city’s school zone speed camera program. He stood with those who called cameras a burden, not a lifesaver. See the vote.

      Some bills have been filed to audit crossings, add speed humps, or improve signage. Most have stalled or been watered down. The dead remain dead. The injured do not heal.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras on every deadly block. Demand streets that put people first. Do not wait for another siren.

      The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a decision.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians here are caused by cars and trucks (9 deaths, 7 serious injuries, 526 total incidents), followed by motorcycles and mopeds (no deaths, 1 serious injury, 21 incidents), and bikes (no deaths, no serious injuries, 25 incidents). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Speed cameras, lower speed limits, and safer street design have all been shown to reduce deaths and injuries. Every crash is a policy failure, not just bad luck.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, expand speed camera coverage, redesign dangerous streets, and support bills that protect people walking and biking. They can also listen to residents and act after every crash, not just after public outcry.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
