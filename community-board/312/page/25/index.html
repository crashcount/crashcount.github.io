      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn’s Blood Streets: Leaders Let Cars Kill

      Written July 14, 2025

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 14, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Brooklyn CB12, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-one people killed on the streets since 2022. Seventeen more left with serious injuries. The dead include children, elders, and those just trying to cross the road. In the last year alone, six lives ended and seven more were shattered by injuries that will not heal. The disaster moves slow, but it does not stop.

      Just days ago, a BMW ran a red light at Third Avenue and 52nd Street. Two men, Kex Un Chen, 80, and Faqiu Lin, 59, were crossing. The car did not brake. Both men died in the street. The driver sped away. Police found him later. The charges: manslaughter, criminally negligent homicide, reckless driving, leaving the scene. “The vehicle was traveling south on Third Avenue when it struck the pedestrians and sped away.” EMS pronounced both men dead at the scene.

      Neighbors know the danger. “Drivers speed on that stretch of roadway,” one said. The line for the food pantry was long that morning. The men were just trying to get by.

      The Pattern: No End in Sight

      The carnage is not new. In the last twelve months, 686 people were hurt in crashes here. Children, elders, workers. SUVs and cars did most of the harm—eight deaths, 379 minor injuries, 143 moderate, five serious. Trucks and buses killed two, hurt dozens more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes added to the toll, but the deadliest threat is always the car.

      The numbers are steady. The grief is not. Each crash leaves a hole. Each name becomes a number. The street stays the same.

      Leadership: Votes Against Safety

      Local leaders have not done enough. Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein voted against renewing the speed camera program that protects children near schools. He opposed safer school speed zones for children. He also voted against reauthorizing the city’s speed camera program, a proven tool to slow drivers and save lives. He opposed the safety-boosting speed camera program reauthorization.

      Some bills were filed to study or improve crossings, but most died in committee. The streets remain as they were. The bodies keep coming.

      What Now: Demand Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras on every deadly stretch. Demand that repeat offenders lose their right to drive. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      Most deaths and injuries to pedestrians here were caused by SUVs and cars (8 deaths, 379 minor injuries, 143 moderate, 5 serious), followed by trucks and buses (2 deaths, 30 minor injuries, 15 moderate, 2 serious). Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths but 14 minor and 6 moderate injuries, 1 serious. Bikes caused no deaths but 17 minor and 8 moderate injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These are not accidents. The same deadly patterns repeat: speeding, red light running, reckless driving. Proven tools—lower speed limits, speed cameras, safer street design—can prevent these deaths. Policy, not fate, decides who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, expand speed camera coverage, and support bills that take repeat dangerous drivers off the road. They can fund safer street designs and hold city agencies accountable for slow action. Every vote and every delay has a cost.

      Int 0578-2022
      Yeger co-sponsors bill boosting pedestrian crossing safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force DOT to study deadly intersections and add signals that give walkers a head start or full crossing time. The bill died at session’s end. Streets stay risky. Pedestrians wait for action.

      Bill Int 0578-2022, introduced July 14, 2022, aimed to amend city law to improve safety at pedestrian crossings. The Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure reviewed it, but the bill was filed without passage at the end of session on December 31, 2023. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to improving safety at pedestrian crossings in the city.' Council Members Robert F. Holden (primary sponsor), Ari Kagan, Justin L. Brannan, Jennifer Gutiérrez, Sandy Nurse, Kalman Yeger, Rita C. Joseph, and Tiffany Cabán sponsored the bill. It would have required DOT to audit the city’s deadliest intersections and consider installing leading pedestrian intervals and exclusive pedestrian phases—signals proven to protect people on foot. The bill’s failure leaves dangerous crossings unchanged.
