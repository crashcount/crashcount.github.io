      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      No More Bodies for Broken Streets—Make Them Answer for Every Death

      Written July 8, 2025

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 8, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Blood

      In Brooklyn CB12, the street does not forgive. Twenty people have died here since 2022. Over 2,100 have been injured. Seventeen were left with wounds so deep they are called serious. The dead include children, elders, workers. They are gone. Their families wait for justice that does not come.

      Just last year, a 67-year-old man was killed crossing 15th Avenue at 54th Street. The truck kept going straight. The man did not go home. The data says he was “killed”—no more, no less. The street stays the same (NYC Open Data crash record).

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and the Young

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. They account for 7 deaths and over 500 injuries. Trucks and buses killed 2, injured 45. Motorcycles and mopeds hurt 21. Bikes injured 25. The numbers do not lie. The danger is heavy, fast, and metal.

      Children are not spared. In the last year, two under 18 have died. One was a cyclist, crushed by a turning truck. Another was a passenger, thrown from a moped. The city counts them. Their parents remember them.

      Leadership: Votes, Silence, and Missed Chances

      The council votes. Sometimes for safety, sometimes against. Council Member Kalman Yeger voted against safer truck routes and daylighting intersections—measures proven to save lives. He voted no on the bill that would have made streets safer for people crossing on foot (NYC Council voting record). He voted no on the greenway plan (NYC Greenway plan). He was absent for votes on bills to protect seniors and daylight crossings. He did co-sponsor a bill to audit dangerous intersections, but it never passed (NYC Council voting record).

      When the council voted to expand 24/7 speed cameras, Yeger supported it (NYC Council speed camera vote). But when it came time to reauthorize the school zone speed camera program in Albany, Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein voted no (Streetsblog NYC report). The cameras work. The votes do not always follow the bodies.

      The Words That Remain

      A son stood on the street and watched his father die. “He didn’t stop. He just kept going,” said Henry Tziquin. The driver fled. The family waited by the hospital bed. “He never moved. Never. never.” Henry said.

      What Now: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings. Demand that the next child is not counted in silence.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      Cars and SUVs: 7 deaths, 515 injuries. Trucks and buses: 2 deaths, 47 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 0 deaths, 21 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 25 injuries. The greatest harm comes from cars, SUVs, and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The numbers show a pattern. Most deaths and injuries come from cars and trucks. These are preventable with better policies, safer streets, and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, protected crossings, daylighting, and real enforcement. They can support bills that redesign streets for people, not just cars. They can listen to families who have lost loved ones.

      Eichenstein votes no, opposing a bill that increases school zone safety.
      ▸

      Assembly passes A 7652. The bill launches speed cameras near Schenectady schools. Cameras catch drivers who speed. The program ends in 2028 unless renewed. Most members vote yes. Some vote no. Streets near schools remain risky for children on foot.

      Bill A 7652, titled "Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the city of Schenectady," advanced with a committee vote in the Assembly on June 7, 2024. The measure creates a pilot program to install speed cameras near schools, aiming to catch drivers who exceed posted limits. The authorization for the program expires December 31, 2028. Assemblymembers Phil Steck (District 110, primary sponsor) and Angelo Santabarbara (District 111, co-sponsor) led the push. The vote passed with strong support but faced opposition from several members. The bill’s summary reads: "Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the city of Schenectady; repeals authorization of program December 31, 2028." No formal safety analyst note was provided, but the measure targets driver speed in zones where children walk, bike, and cross.
