      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      She Drove Through Red. They Died. City Hall Watched.

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      Blood on the Crosswalks

      A mother and her two daughters stepped into the light on Ocean Parkway. They never made it across. The driver hit them at 68 miles per hour. She ran two red lights. She never braked. The mother and girls died. The boy is still in a coma. The driver’s license was suspended. Her car had 21 speed camera and five red light tickets. She owed $10,000 in fines. She drove anyway. The District Attorney called it “one of the worst collisions I’ve ever seen on a New York City street”.

      A 19-year-old man tried to cross McDonald Avenue. An EMT, lights flashing, struck him. His head hit the pavement. He left in an ambulance, fighting for his life. Police are still investigating.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the last twelve months, five people died on these streets.

      Children, elders, and working men and women.

      There were 679 injuries. Seven were serious. One child dead. One elder dead. One cyclist dead. The rest are numbers, but every number is a name, a face, a family. NYC Open Data

      SUVs led the killing. Trucks, sedans, bikes, and buses followed. The dead do not care about the make or model.

      Promises, Delays, and the Cost of Waiting

      Local leaders talk about Vision Zero. They talk about safer streets. But the blood dries faster than the laws change.

      After the Ocean Parkway crash, the District Attorney said: “What is very clear is that she should not have been on the road that day.”

      But nothing stopped her. Nothing stopped the next one. The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has the power to keep repeat offenders off the road. It has the power to harden crosswalks and protect the people who walk and ride. Power means nothing if it is not used.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. Tell them to protect speed cameras. Tell them to build streets for people, not for cars. Do not wait for another mother, another child, another name on a cold slab. Take action now.

      Int 0758-2022
      Yeger co-sponsors e-bike licensing bill, likely reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council tried to force plates on e-bikes and scooters. The bill died in committee. Riders faced new rules, more scrutiny. The city’s cars kept killing. The council moved on. Vulnerable road users stayed exposed. The system failed to shift blame from drivers.

      Int 0758-2022 was introduced in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on October 12, 2022. The bill aimed to require every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter, and other legal motorized vehicle to be registered with the Department of Transportation and display a visible license plate. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered.' Council Member Inna Vernikov sponsored the bill, joined by Robert F. Holden and 29 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. No safety analyst assessed the impact on vulnerable road users. The bill’s failure left the city’s most exposed—cyclists, pedestrians, passengers—without new protections. The streets stayed dangerous. The cars kept their edge.

      Int 0627-2022
      Yeger sponsors bill that slows street redesigns, risking reduced pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0627-2022 would force city agencies to warn and consult local leaders before cutting traffic lanes or parking for long stretches. The bill died in committee. No safety review for pedestrians or cyclists was made public.

      Int 0627-2022 was introduced on August 11, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill required agencies to notify council members, community boards, and business districts before removing a traffic lane or parking for 500 feet or three blocks for at least a week. The matter summary reads: 'providing notice and an opportunity for comment before implementing a major traffic change.' Council Members Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor), Francisco P. Moya, Sandra Ung, and Pierina Ana Sanchez backed the bill. It was filed at the end of session, never enacted. The bill would have given local officials ten days to respond to proposed changes, but exempted urgent safety actions. No safety analyst assessed the impact on vulnerable road users. The bill’s fate leaves the status quo: agencies can still act without local input.
