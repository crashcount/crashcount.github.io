      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn’s Blood Road: Speed Kills, Leaders Stall, Families Grieve

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 16, 2025 – Fixed link formatting, tightened prose, and cleaned citations.)

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Brooklyn CB12, the road does not forgive. Twenty-one people have died here since 2022. Two were children. Seventeen more suffered injuries so grave they may never heal. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the empty seat at dinner, the shoes left at the door.

      Just last week, two men—ages 80 and 59—were killed crossing Third Avenue at 52nd Street. The driver ran the red light and kept going. Police said the car was “speeding southbound” and did not brake before impact. Both men died where they fell. City Harvest expressed deep sadness over the tragic deaths, noting the victims “may have been on their way to our Mobile Market simply trying to access food like so many New Yorkers in need.”

      A week before, a 70-year-old man was killed crossing Avenue I at East 5th Street. The driver was distracted. The man died at the intersection. No warning, no second chance.

      The Vehicles That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the damage. Since 2022, they have killed eight people and injured 522 more on these streets. Trucks and buses killed two. Motorcycles and mopeds, none. Bikes, none. The pattern is clear. The danger is not hidden.

      Leaders: Action and Inaction

      Local leaders have had chances to act. Some have. Some have not. Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein voted against renewing the speed camera program that protects children near schools. He was one of nine city lawmakers to oppose it.

      Senator Sam Sutton missed key votes on bills that would have made school zones safer and forced repeat speeders to slow down. The bills passed anyway, but not with his help. The silence is loud.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made upstream. Every delay is a risk pushed onto the most vulnerable. The road will not change itself.

      Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras at every school. Demand that repeat speeders lose their right to endanger your family.

      No more waiting. No more names added to the list. The time to act is now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      Cars and Trucks: 8 deaths, 522 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 21 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 25 injuries. Cars and trucks are the main killers. Data source
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. The pattern is clear. Most deaths and injuries come from cars and trucks. Speeding, distraction, and reckless driving are choices. These crashes are preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, and support laws that target repeat dangerous drivers. They can fund safer street designs and hold reckless drivers accountable.

      S 5602
      Eichenstein votes no, opposing expanded speed camera hours and safer streets.
      ▸

      Senate passed S 5602 to keep school zone speed cameras running longer. More eyes on reckless drivers. Lawmakers push back against speeding near kids. The vote was clear. The danger remains.

      Bill S 5602, titled 'Relates to the hours of operation of a school zone speed camera demonstration program,' passed the Senate on May 25, 2022, and the Assembly on June 2, 2022. The bill extends the hours that speed cameras operate in New York City school zones. Primary sponsor: Senator Gounardes, with co-sponsors Biaggi, Cleare, Gianaris, Hoylman, Jackson, Kavanagh, Krueger, Myrie, Persaud, Ramos, Rivera, and Sepulveda. The measure saw strong support in both chambers, despite some opposition. The bill aims to keep cameras watching when children are at risk. More enforcement, more accountability. The fight for safer streets continues.

      A 8936
      Eichenstein votes yes to require safer complete street designs, improving safety.
      ▸

      Albany passed A 8936. Cities get more state cash if they build complete streets. Lawmakers want safer roads. The bill sailed through both chambers. Money now follows safety.

      Bill A 8936, titled 'Relates to complete street design features and funding of construction and improvements at a municipalities' expense,' passed the Assembly on May 23, 2022, and the Senate on June 1, 2022. The bill boosts state funding for transportation projects when municipalities add complete street features. Assemblymember Fahy led as primary sponsor, joined by Hunter, Seawright, Woerner, and others. The Assembly and Senate both voted yes, with broad support. The law aims to push cities to design streets for everyone—pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers—by tying state dollars to safety upgrades.
