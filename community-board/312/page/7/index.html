      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn Bleeds While Leaders Stall

      Written July 3, 2025

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 3, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      In Brooklyn CB12, the street does not forgive. Twenty people are dead since 2022. Seventeen more are marked by serious injury. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old. Two children under 18 are gone. Four more children were seriously hurt. The old are not spared. Four people over 65 are dead.

      Last year alone, three people died and 361 were injured in crashes in this district. The year before, three more died. The bodies change, the count does not.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      Just last week, fourteen people were hurt when a van slammed into a tree in Brooklyn. The van carried adults with special needs. The driver swerved, the van hit the tree, and the passengers paid the price. “It’s sad because they definitely need a lot of attention more than anyone else. I hope that they’re all OK at the end of the day,” said a witness. The cause is under investigation. The pain is not.

      A few weeks back, a moped rider, just 17, died on Avenue N. The crash data says only this: “Apparent Death.” The street does not care for names.

      Leadership: Votes, Silence, and Missed Chances

      Local leaders have not done enough. Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein voted against renewing the city’s speed camera program—one of the few proven ways to slow cars and save lives. Nine city lawmakers stood in the way. The votes signal disregard for those outside cars. “The event text does not describe a specific policy or legislative action, so no direct safety impact on pedestrians and cyclists can be assessed,” wrote a safety analyst.

      Council Member Simcha Felder has a record of blocking speed cameras, fighting street redesigns, and blaming the dead. When three people were killed by a speeder, Felder said nothing. He did not show up at the funeral. The silence is loud.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every delay, every vote against cameras or safer streets, is a choice. Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand more speed cameras. Demand street redesigns that protect people, not cars.

      The street will not change itself. Only you can force their hand.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      Cars and Trucks: 7 deaths, 5 serious injuries, 514 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, 21 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 25 injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or can they be prevented?
      These are not random accidents. Policies like speed cameras, lower speed limits, and safer street design have been proven to prevent deaths and injuries. Every delay is a choice.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can vote for more speed cameras, lower speed limits to 20 mph, redesign dangerous streets, and support bills that protect people walking and biking. They can also show up and speak out after crashes, not stay silent.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
