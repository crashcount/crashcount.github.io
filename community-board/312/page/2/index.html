      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn Bleeds While City Hall Shrugs

      Written July 9, 2025

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      In Brooklyn CB12, the street does not forgive. Twenty people are dead since 2022. Seventeen more are marked with serious injuries. These are not just numbers. They are fathers, daughters, neighbors—the sound of sirens at night, the hush after impact.

      Just weeks ago, a 17-year-old was killed on Avenue N. A 14-year-old boy riding behind him was thrown from the moped and left broken in the street. The crash report lists the cause: “Passing or Lane Usage Improper. Unsafe Speed.” No comfort in the words. Only the fact of a life ended, another changed forever, as shown in NYC Open Data.

      A 70-year-old man, Dov Broyde, was crossing Avenue I. The driver did not stop in time. Police said, “The driver plowed into him”. Broyde died at the hospital. The driver stayed. No charges were filed.

      Five deaths in the last year. Two were children.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the harm: seven deaths, five serious injuries, 527 total crashes. Trucks and buses: two deaths, two serious injuries, 47 crashes. Motorcycles and mopeds: one serious injury, 21 crashes. Bikes: none dead, but the wounds are real—25 crashes with injuries. The street is not equal. The bigger the machine, the more it takes.

      Leaders: Votes and Vacuums

      The council talks. Sometimes it acts. Council Member Kalman Yeger voted no on the bill to end jaywalking tickets—a law meant to stop blaming the dead for their own deaths. He voted no on safer truck routes and on the greenway plan. He was absent for votes on daylighting and senior safety. He did co-sponsor a bill for raised speed reducers at camera locations, but the bill died. The silence is louder than the words.

      The Voices Left Behind

      “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open,” an eyewitness said after a 90-year-old was struck by a moped and left to die. “He hit the guy and he left.”

      What Now?

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand that the council stop blaming the dead and start protecting the living. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      Cars and Trucks: 7 deaths, 5 serious injuries, 527 crashes. Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 2 serious injuries, 47 crashes. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 serious injury, 21 crashes. Bikes: No deaths, 25 crashes with injuries. The biggest machines do the most harm, as shown in NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. The numbers and stories show a pattern. Most deaths and injuries could be prevented with better street design, lower speeds, and stronger enforcement. These are not random. They are the result of choices.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, daylight intersections, redesign dangerous streets, and support bills that protect people outside cars. They can stop blaming victims and start demanding action from city agencies.

      ▸

      City eyes a center bus lane on Flatbush. Concrete islands promise safer crossings. Details remain thin. Cars may still block buses. The street could change. Pedestrians and riders wait. The city holds its breath.

      DOT Plans Protected Bike Lane Court Street
      ▸

      Court Street bleeds from double parking and swerving cars. Cyclists and walkers dodge danger daily. DOT will cut a lane, add a protected bike path, and shrink space for reckless driving. Fewer lanes, fewer crashes. Safety, not speed, takes the street.

      Streetsblog NYC (2025-06-06) reports the Department of Transportation will install a protected bike lane on Brooklyn’s Court Street, a corridor plagued by double parking and sideswipe crashes. DOT’s Chris Brunson said, “The narrower street width for vehicles will de-incentivize double parking on the corridor.” The redesign removes a travel lane, adds a protected bike lane, and aims to curb driver behavior that leads to crashes. Between 2022 and 2024, 165 crashes on this mile-long stretch caused one death and injured 15 cyclists and 23 pedestrians. Most pedestrian injuries happened while crossing with the light, showing drivers failed to yield. The plan targets excess road capacity and prioritizes vulnerable users on a Vision Zero Priority Corridor.

      City Orders 15 MPH Limit For E-Bikes

The move highlights rising e-bike use and the city’s struggle to manage micromobility safety.

      Flatbush Avenue Bus Lanes, Pedestrian Islands Planned
      ▸

      Flatbush Avenue will lose car lanes. Bus lanes and pedestrian islands will take their place. Fifty-five killed or badly hurt since 2019. Buses crawl. Pedestrians dodge traffic. The city moves to fix a deadly, clogged corridor.

      Gothamist reported on June 4, 2025, that New York City's Department of Transportation plans to overhaul Flatbush Avenue between Livingston Street and Grand Army Plaza. The redesign replaces two car lanes with 24/7 bus-only lanes and adds pedestrian islands. DOT officials said, 'almost 70,000 daily bus riders are stuck waiting too long for slow buses, drivers are caught in a mess of traffic and pedestrians are left crossing intersections clogged with vehicles.' Since 2019, 55 people have been killed or severely injured in crashes along this stretch. The plan removes curbside parking and bans cars from bus lanes, aiming to speed up twelve bus routes and protect people on foot. Most residents in the area do not own cars and depend on slow buses. The proposal reflects a shift toward prioritizing vulnerable road users and addressing systemic danger on one of Brooklyn's busiest corridors.
