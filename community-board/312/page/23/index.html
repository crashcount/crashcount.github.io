      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?

      Brooklyn’s Blood Price: Policy Kills, Leaders Hide

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed duplicate headline in body and cleaned up citations.)

      Brooklyn CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      In Brooklyn CB12, the street is a gauntlet. Since 2022, 20 people have died. 2,181 have been injured. 17 were left with wounds so deep they are called serious. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The living carry scars you cannot see.

      Just last week, a 17-year-old boy died on a moped in Midwood. He was thrown from the seat. His passenger was left fighting for life. Police said, “Jhoan later died at the hospital” (NY Daily News).

      A year ago, a 16-year-old cyclist was crushed by a turning truck at Coney Island Avenue and Ditmas. The city counted the body. The street stayed the same.

      SUVs and trucks do most of the killing. In three years, SUVs and cars took 7 lives and left 517 injured. Trucks and buses killed 2, injured 45. Bikes and mopeds are not blameless, but their toll is a fraction.

      Leadership: Votes, Silence, and Delay

      The numbers rise. The policies crawl. Council Member Simcha Felder has a record of fighting safety: he blocked speed cameras, opposed lower speed limits, and skipped the funeral for three killed by a speeder. Streetsblog wrote, “Felder has a long record of opposing street safety measures, including Vision Zero, speed cameras, speed limits, and street redesigns” (Streetsblog NYC).

      Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein voted against renewing the city’s speed camera program. He stood with those who called cameras a burden, not a shield. The votes are on the record. The bodies are in the ground.

      Some bills have been filed. Some died in committee. Some passed, but the street does not change overnight. Every day of delay is another risk.

      The Call: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      This is not fate. It is policy.

      Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Tell them: Lower the speed limit. Build real protection. Stop blaming the dead.

      The street remembers every name. Do not let your leaders forget.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 44, assembly district AD 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB12?
      It includes the Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Borough Park, Kensington, and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, District 43, and District 44, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 45, AD 48, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 21, and SD 22.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB12?
      SUVs and Cars: 7 deaths, 374 injuries. Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 30 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 14 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 17 injuries. Most deaths and injuries come from SUVs, cars, and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These are not random accidents. The numbers rise because of choices—speed limits, street design, and enforcement. Every delay in action means more risk.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign streets, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking. They can stop blaming victims and start building real safety.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0627-2022
      Yeger sponsors bill that slows street redesigns, risking reduced pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0627-2022 would force city agencies to warn and consult local leaders before cutting traffic lanes or parking for long stretches. The bill died in committee. No safety review for pedestrians or cyclists was made public.

      Int 0627-2022 was introduced on August 11, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill required agencies to notify council members, community boards, and business districts before removing a traffic lane or parking for 500 feet or three blocks for at least a week. The matter summary reads: 'providing notice and an opportunity for comment before implementing a major traffic change.' Council Members Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor), Francisco P. Moya, Sandra Ung, and Pierina Ana Sanchez backed the bill. It was filed at the end of session, never enacted. The bill would have given local officials ten days to respond to proposed changes, but exempted urgent safety actions. No safety analyst assessed the impact on vulnerable road users. The bill’s fate leaves the status quo: agencies can still act without local input.

      Int 0578-2022
      Yeger co-sponsors bill boosting pedestrian crossing safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force DOT to study deadly intersections and add signals that give walkers a head start or full crossing time. The bill died at session’s end. Streets stay risky. Pedestrians wait for action.

      Bill Int 0578-2022, introduced July 14, 2022, aimed to amend city law to improve safety at pedestrian crossings. The Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure reviewed it, but the bill was filed without passage at the end of session on December 31, 2023. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to improving safety at pedestrian crossings in the city.' Council Members Robert F. Holden (primary sponsor), Ari Kagan, Justin L. Brannan, Jennifer Gutiérrez, Sandy Nurse, Kalman Yeger, Rita C. Joseph, and Tiffany Cabán sponsored the bill. It would have required DOT to audit the city’s deadliest intersections and consider installing leading pedestrian intervals and exclusive pedestrian phases—signals proven to protect people on foot. The bill’s failure leaves dangerous crossings unchanged.

      Int 0596-2022
      Yeger co-sponsors bill to boost pedestrian and cyclist safety via curb repairs.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0596-2022 would force DOT to repair broken curbs when streets get resurfaced. Curbs shape the border between sidewalk and street. Broken curbs trip walkers, let cars mount sidewalks, and endanger the frail. The bill died in committee.

      Int 0596-2022 was introduced on July 14, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill required the Department of Transportation to repair any curb deemed a safety hazard during street resurfacing. The matter summary reads: 'requiring that the department of transportation repair broken curbs as part of resurfacing projects.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers sponsored the bill, joined by Schulman, Brewer, Dinowitz, Krishnan, Narcisse, Menin, Yeger, Avilés, Nurse, Gutiérrez, Riley, Brannan, Sanchez, and Louis. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. Broken curbs are a daily threat to pedestrians, especially the elderly and disabled. The bill would have forced the city to address these hazards as part of routine work, but the effort stalled.
