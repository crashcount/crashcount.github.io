      Speed Kills. Inaction Kills More.

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 4, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, clarified vehicle injury breakdown, and cleaned citations.)

      Manhattan CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Human Cost on Downtown Streets

      A man steps off the curb. A car comes fast. The street is wide. The light is red or green or nothing at all. It does not matter. In Manhattan CB1, the numbers pile up. One person killed. Twelve left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In three years, 701 people hurt in crashes. The youngest was a child. The oldest, eighty-eight. The pain does not care about age.

      Cars and SUVs hit hardest. They killed one, left sixty-four with wounds that cut deep. Buses and trucks, bikes and mopeds, all played their part. But it is the weight of steel and speed that breaks bodies most often.

      Leadership: Promises and the Waiting Game

      The city says it is fighting back. “In order to make New York City the best place to raise a family, we need to be safer at every level — including on our streets,” said Mayor Eric Adams. The city passed Sammy’s Law. It can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit has not dropped yet. The law sits. The streets wait.

      Speed cameras work. They cut speeding by more than half. But the law that keeps them running is always about to expire. Albany debates. The clock ticks. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the last year, no one died in a crash in CB1. But 242 were hurt. Seven were left with injuries that will not heal. The year before, one person died. Three were maimed. The trend is down, but the danger is not gone. Every number is a name. Every name is a family.

      What Comes Next: The Call for Action

      Lower the speed limit. Make it real. Keep the cameras on. Redesign the streets so people come first, not cars. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call until they act. Do not wait for another body on the pavement.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 1, assembly district AD 65 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB1?
      It includes the Financial District-Battery Park City, Tribeca-Civic Center, and The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council District District 1, Assembly Districts AD 61, AD 65, and AD 66, and State Senate Districts SD 26 and SD 27.

      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?

      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, keep speed cameras running, and redesign streets to protect people. They can act now, not wait.
