      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Left Turn at Junction and Corona: Three on Foot, One Mistake

      Queens CB4: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      Just after 8 PM on Sep 10, at Junction Blvd and Corona Ave, a driver turned left and hit a 27‑year‑old woman, a 9‑year‑old girl, and a 3‑year‑old. Police recorded failure to yield by the driver; two were crossing with the signal. NYC Open Data

      This Week

      • Sep 9: A driver going west on 55 Ave hit a 19‑year‑old on a bike at Justice Ave. NYC Open Data
      • Sep 5: A left‑turning SUV driver hit a 42‑year‑old woman crossing with the signal at 43 Ave and 104 St. NYC Open Data
      • Aug 31: A truck driver turning left hit a 46‑year‑old on an e‑bike at Roosevelt Ave and 93 St. NYC Open Data

      The toll on these few blocks

      Since Jan 1, 2022, in Queens CB4 there have been 18 deaths and 2,552 injuries in 5,108 crashes. People walking account for 9 deaths and 678 injuries; people on bikes for 1 death and 319 injuries. NYC Open Data

      Police reports show patterns you can touch at the curb. Disregarded signals by drivers are tied to 2 deaths here. Driver inattention is tied to 60 injuries and 2 serious injuries. NYC Open Data

      Harm clusters on known corridors: the Long Island Expressway area tops injuries and deaths; Roosevelt Avenue follows. NYC Open Data filtered – L.I.E., Roosevelt Ave

      The clock doesn’t spare mornings

      Police logged 3 deaths at the 8 AM hour here, with other deaths spread through early morning and night. The violence does not wait for dark. NYC Open Data – hourly rollup

      On Sep 10 at Junction and Corona, officers cited failure to yield. On Sep 5 at 43 Ave and 104 St, the same story: a turning driver, a person with the walk, a body in the crosswalk. Crash 4841856, Crash 4839979

      Fix the turns. Slow the cars.

      Daylight the corners. Harden the turns. Give leading pedestrian intervals. These tools fit this map of left‑turn crashes and failure‑to‑yield. Target Roosevelt Avenue and 57 Avenue, where deaths and injuries pile up. NYC Open Data – Roosevelt Ave, 57 Avenue

      Council Member Shekar Krishnan put it plainly about slow capital fixes: “The infrastructure projects, the transportation and green space projects, need to be progressing at a much much faster rate.” Streetsblog NYC

      Albany’s lever for the worst drivers

      A small group of drivers does the most harm. The state has a bill to stop them. The Stop Super Speeders Act — S 4045 / A 2299 — would require repeat violators to use speed‑limiting tech. Senator Jessica Ramos co‑sponsored S 4045 and voted yes in committee on May 20; she missed a later June 13 committee vote. Open States

      Assembly Member Catalina Cruz voted yes to renew 24‑hour school‑zone speed cameras (S 8344). Open States

      The street tells us what to do next

      The corner at Junction and Corona tells the story: turns too fast, sightlines blocked, people on foot paying the price. The Assembly and Senate can curb the worst speeders now. City Hall can slow the default speed and harden these turns. Start here. Act now. /take_action/

      Res 1024-2025 Steven Raga Backs Safety‑Boosting Owner Liability Camera Enforcement
      ▸

      Aug 14 - Illegally parked cars endanger people on foot and bike. Res 1024-2025 urges Albany to pass A.5440. Cameras ticket owners who flout posted rules. Fines escalate. Goal: clear lanes and crosswalks. Make streets less hostile to people, not cars.

      Res 1024-2025 sits in Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced and referred August 14, 2025. The resolution urges passage of State bill A.5440, which, in the Council’s words, "imposes owner liability for failure of an operator to comply with traffic control indicators within the city of New York." Sponsor: Council Member Lincoln Restler. A.5440, by Assemblymember Steven Raga, authorizes a six-year camera pilot to ticket owners for posted parking-rule violations caught by street or vehicle-mounted cameras. Fines start at $50 and rise to $250 for repeaters, with a $25 late penalty. DOT must publish a two-year report. The aim: fewer illegal blockers, safer space for people outside cars.

      Res 1024-2025 Steven Raga Backs Safety‑Boosting Owner Liability Camera Pilot
