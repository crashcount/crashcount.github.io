      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB4?

      Queens Streets Run Red. Lower the Limit—Or Count More Dead.

      Written June 6, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Queens CB4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 6, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Queens CB4, the numbers do not lie. Eleven dead. Seventeen with injuries so serious they may never walk the same. In just the last twelve months, two more lives ended, five more left with wounds that will not heal. See NYC Open Data.

      Children are not spared. Fifty-four injured, one seriously, under eighteen. The old are not spared. Thirteen injuries for those over seventy-five. These are not just numbers. They are families waiting for a voice at the door, a phone that will not ring.

      What Hits, Hurts, and Kills

      Cars and SUVs are the main weapon. They killed two, left sixty-seven with moderate injuries, eight with wounds called serious. Trucks and buses killed one, left five more broken. Motorcycles and mopeds killed one, hurt four. Bikes killed one, left six with injuries. The street does not care who you are. It only cares that you are in the way.

      Leadership: Words and Waiting

      The city talks of Vision Zero. The city talks of cameras, of lower speed limits, of new laws. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can set 20 mph limits. But the limit is not yet set. The cameras that catch speeders may go dark if Albany does not act. The city says it wants zero deaths. The city waits.

      In Queens CB4, the waiting costs lives. The council can lower the speed limit now. The mayor can push. The state can keep the cameras running. Every day of delay is another day for a child to bleed in the crosswalk.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand the cameras stay on. Join the groups that fight for the dead and the living. Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Int 0606-2024
      Moya co-sponsors e-bike and scooter registration bill, reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council wants plates on every e-bike and scooter. The bill sits in committee. Riders face new rules. Lawmakers push for registration. Streets wait. No change yet. The city debates control. Vulnerable users watch. The system holds its breath.

      Int 0606-2024 was introduced on March 7, 2024, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered," would force all e-bikes, e-scooters, and similar vehicles not already covered by DMV rules to register with the Department of Transportation and display a visible plate. Council Member Inna Vernikov led as primary sponsor, joined by Holden, Farías, Riley, Brannan, and many others. The bill was laid over in committee on December 11, 2024. The measure aims to track these vehicles, but its impact on vulnerable road users remains unassessed. The city’s debate continues, with no law yet in force.

      ▸

      Lawmakers push to triple fare-free bus lines. Fifteen routes, more buses, faster rides. Focus on low-income neighborhoods. The move aims to shift New Yorkers from cars to transit. Congestion pricing looms. Riders want speed, reliability, and access.

      Int 0322-2024
      Francisco P Moya Supports Misguided Notice Before Traffic Lane Cuts
      ▸

      Council Member Moya pushes a bill to force city agencies to warn and consult before cutting traffic lanes or parking. The measure targets changes over 500 feet or three blocks. It sits in committee. No safety review for walkers or riders yet.

      Int 0322-2024, sponsored by Council Member Francisco P. Moya, was introduced on February 28, 2024, and referred to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill states: 'providing notice and an opportunity for comment before implementing a major traffic change.' It requires the Department of Transportation, or any relevant agency, to notify and allow comments from council members, community boards, and business improvement districts before removing a vehicular lane or parking for 500 feet or three blocks for at least a week. The bill remains in committee. Moya leads the effort, but no safety analyst has weighed in on the impact for pedestrians or cyclists. The bill’s effect on vulnerable road users is unknown.
