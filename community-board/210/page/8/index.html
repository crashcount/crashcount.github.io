      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx CB10?

      Left for Dead: Bronx Drivers Kill, Leaders Look Away

      Written June 22, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Bronx CB10: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Hurt That Never Ends

      A woman lies in the road at dawn. Eighteen years old. Thrown from a scooter on the Hutchinson River Parkway. The driver flees. She is left behind, fighting for her life. Seven are hurt, one is critical. The police say, “An 18-year-old was in critical condition after being thrown from a moped in the hit-and-run” CBS New York. The cars keep moving.

      In the last twelve months, one person died and 459 were injured in traffic crashes in Bronx CB10. Five were left with serious injuries. The numbers do not stop. They do not rest.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. In three years, they killed two people and hurt 177 more. Trucks and buses killed one, hurt 17. Motorcycles and mopeds, six injuries. Bikes, two. The street is not safe for the softest bodies.

      The Leaders and Their Silence

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They say, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many” Mayor Adams. But the speed limits stay high. The cameras are not everywhere. The laws are slow. The drivers are fast.

      Local leaders have the power to lower speed limits. They have the power to demand more cameras, more safe crossings, more enforcement. They have the power to act. But the blood on the asphalt says they have not done enough.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand more speed cameras. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Every day you wait, another name becomes a number. Do not let them look away. Take action now.

      Int 0606-2024
      Marmorato co-sponsors e-bike and scooter registration bill, reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council wants plates on every e-bike and scooter. The bill sits in committee. Riders face new rules. Lawmakers push for registration. Streets wait. No change yet. The city debates control. Vulnerable users watch. The system holds its breath.

      Int 0606-2024 was introduced on March 7, 2024, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered," would force all e-bikes, e-scooters, and similar vehicles not already covered by DMV rules to register with the Department of Transportation and display a visible plate. Council Member Inna Vernikov led as primary sponsor, joined by Holden, Farías, Riley, Brannan, and many others. The bill was laid over in committee on December 11, 2024. The measure aims to track these vehicles, but its impact on vulnerable road users remains unassessed. The city’s debate continues, with no law yet in force.

      Int 0079-2024
      Marmorato co-sponsors bill to boost pedestrian lighting, improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council members want 500 corridors lit for walkers each year. The bill demands bright sidewalks—no less than 1 footcandle. Most corridors must connect, forming safer, well-lit routes. The measure sits in committee, waiting for action. Darkness remains a threat.

      Int 0079-2024, introduced on February 8, 2024, sits 'Laid Over in Committee' with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installation of pedestrian lighting fixtures,' would require the transportation commissioner to install sidewalk lighting in at least 500 commercial corridors per year, each lit to a minimum of 1 footcandle (11 lux). At least 450 corridors must be contiguous to others with new or existing lighting. Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Krishnan, Bottcher, and many others. The bill aims to cut through the city’s darkness, demanding light for those on foot. It remains stalled in committee, its promise unrealized.

      Marmorato Supports Safety Boosting East Bronx Metro-North Stations
      ▸

      Council hears from Bronx riders. Four new Metro-North stations promise faster, safer commutes. Councilmembers Marmorato and Farias back the plan. The public weighs in. The city moves closer to breaking car dependence in transit deserts.

      On January 5, 2024, the City Council held a public input session on the plan to open four new Metro-North stations in East Bronx. The proposal, nearing the Uniform Land Use Review Procedure (ULURP) certification, aims to serve Co-op City, Morris Park, Parkchester/Van Nest, and Hunts Point. The session, supported by Councilmember Kristy Marmorato (District 13) and Councilmember Amanda Farias, highlighted the need for faster, more reliable transit. Marmorato said, 'the Metro-North will provide an alternative for drivers dealing with congestion pricing, and for those who might be reluctant to take the subway.' Farias called it part of a 'better transit future.' The Department of City Planning updated residents, reflecting ongoing community feedback. The plan has bipartisan support and promises to reshape transit for neighborhoods long underserved by safe, fast options.
