      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx CB10?

      Left for Dead: Bronx Drivers Kill, Leaders Look Away

      Written June 22, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Bronx CB10: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Hurt That Never Ends

      A woman lies in the road at dawn. Eighteen years old. Thrown from a scooter on the Hutchinson River Parkway. The driver flees. She is left behind, fighting for her life. Seven are hurt, one is critical. The police say, “An 18-year-old was in critical condition after being thrown from a moped in the hit-and-run” CBS New York. The cars keep moving.

      In the last twelve months, one person died and 459 were injured in traffic crashes in Bronx CB10. Five were left with serious injuries. The numbers do not stop. They do not rest.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. In three years, they killed two people and hurt 177 more. Trucks and buses killed one, hurt 17. Motorcycles and mopeds, six injuries. Bikes, two. The street is not safe for the softest bodies.

      The Leaders and Their Silence

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They say, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many” Mayor Adams. But the speed limits stay high. The cameras are not everywhere. The laws are slow. The drivers are fast.

      Local leaders have the power to lower speed limits. They have the power to demand more cameras, more safe crossings, more enforcement. They have the power to act. But the blood on the asphalt says they have not done enough.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand more speed cameras. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Every day you wait, another name becomes a number. Do not let them look away. Take action now.

      A 116
      Benedetto co-sponsors e-scooter bill that likely reduces street safety.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 116 would force e-scooter riders in New York City to get a license, read a safety manual, and buy insurance. The bill blocks sales to unlicensed buyers. It aims to regulate e-scooters but leaves questions for those on foot and bike.

      Assembly Bill 116, now in sponsorship, sits in the Assembly. Filed January 1, 2025, it seeks to 'establish a bicycle and electric scooter operator's safety manual; provide for the issuance of a license for residents who successfully read and comprehend the operator's safety manual; require residents who operate an electric scooter in New York City to have a license to operate such devices; require instruction in electric scooter safety as part of the drivers pre-licensing course; require liability insurance for electric scooters in cities having a population of one million or more; prohibit the sale of an electric scooter to a purchaser unless the purchaser has a license to operate the electric scooter and has insurance in a city having a population of one million or more.' Assemblymember John Zaccaro Jr. leads sponsorship, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Michael Benedetto, Deborah Glick, David McDonough, and Michael Novakhov. The bill targets e-scooter operators, not drivers of larger vehicles. No safety analyst has assessed its impact on pedestrians or cyclists.

      Int 1142-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 19, 2024. The bill is titled, "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing child with autism warning plaques on streets." Council Member Kevin C. The city continues to rely on signage, not street redesign, to address systemic danger.
