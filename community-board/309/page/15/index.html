      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB9?

      101 Years Old. Walk Signal. Still Killed. What Will It Take?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Brooklyn CB9: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      A woman, 101 years old, stepped into the crosswalk at Brooklyn Avenue and Montgomery Street. She had the walk signal. An unlicensed driver in a GMC SUV turned left and struck her. She died twelve days later. Her name was Taibel Brod. Her son called it a tragedy. Her grandson said, “She was extremely independent till her last day.”

      In the last twelve months, 388 people were injured and 6 suffered serious injuries in Brooklyn CB9. One person died. Most were walking, biking, or just trying to cross the street. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      Patterns of Harm

      SUVs and cars did the most damage. In three years, they killed two pedestrians and left ten with serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused three serious injuries. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes added to the toll. No one is spared. Children, elders, workers, neighbors—each one a story cut short or changed forever.

      The dead do not get second chances. The injured carry pain that does not heal. The street stays the same.

      Leadership: Progress and Silence

      Local leaders have tools. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. Cameras catch speeders and red-light runners, but the law that keeps them running is always at risk. In the last year, no new redesigns or safety measures were announced for these blocks. The driver who killed Taibel Brod was charged, but the street did not change.

      The silence is loud. Every day without action is another day someone does not come home.

      What Comes Next

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand safer speeds, more cameras, and streets built for people, not just cars.

      Do not wait for another tragedy.

      Take action now.

      Int 1261-2023
      Hudson co-sponsors Brooklyn residential parking permits, modestly improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council Member Hudson pushed a bill for residential parking permits in Northwestern Brooklyn. The plan set aside spaces for locals and non-residents. The bill died at session’s end. Streets near shops and offices would have stayed open to all.

      Int 1261-2023 was introduced by Council Member Crystal Hudson on December 6, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill called for 'the creation of a residential parking permit system in Northwestern Brooklyn.' It required the Department of Transportation to set permit zones, charge fees, and reserve at least 20% of spaces for non-residents. Streets next to commercial, office, or retail zones would be exempt. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. Hudson sponsored the measure, aiming to control curb space and manage parking demand in a dense, contested area.

      Res 0817-2023
      Hudson co-sponsors unlimited transfers bill, boosting transit safety and equity.
      ▸

      Council called for unlimited subway and bus transfers within two hours. Riders now pay extra for complex trips. The bill aimed to cut costs for those who need more than one transfer. It stalled at session’s end. The fight continues.

      Resolution 0817-2023 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It called on the State Legislature and Governor to create unlimited transfers within two hours for MTA pay-per-ride users. The resolution was introduced October 19, 2023, and filed at session’s end on December 31, 2023. The matter title reads: 'Resolution calling on the New York State Legislature to pass, and the New York State Governor to sign, legislation, which would create unlimited transfers within the two-hour period of paying the Metropolitan Transportation Authority subway or bus fare for pay-per-ride users.' Council Member Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Jennifer Gutiérrez, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, and Kevin C. Riley. The bill targeted fare burdens on riders with long, complex trips, who now pay more than those with direct routes. The Council’s push aimed to level the field for all transit users.
