      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB2?

      Eight Dead, Hundreds Hurt—City Hall Lets the Blood Run

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Manhattan CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      Eight dead. Twenty seriously hurt. In three years, the streets of Manhattan CB2—Soho, Greenwich Village, West Village—have not let up. Cyclists, pedestrians, elders, children. The numbers are not just numbers. They are bodies on Broome Street, sirens on Cornelia, a life ended at Bleecker and Sixth.

      In the last 12 months alone: 2 killed, 10 seriously injured, 334 hurt. The dead include a 44-year-old cyclist thrown under a truck after a van door swung open on Broome Street. The driver admitted, “I opened the door. I didn’t even see the guy. I only saw the accident.” The man on the bike was Georgios Smaragdis. He never made it home.

      A few days later, a cyclist was crushed in a hit-and-run at 5th Avenue and 13th. Myung Jin Chung woke up in an ambulance. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” Chung said. He needed sixteen hours of surgery. The driver ran two red lights and vanished.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      City Hall talks of Vision Zero. Lower speed limits. More cameras. Safer intersections. But in CB2, the carnage continues. The city has the power to set a 20 mph speed limit. It has not used it. Speed cameras cut speeding by more than half, but their future is always in doubt. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program lapsed. No word on renewal.

      No press conferences on Broome Street. No new protected lanes. No public reckoning for the dead. The drivers who stayed at the scene walk free. The ones who fled are still gone.

      What Comes Next

      The disaster is slow, but it is not silent. Residents and advocates have pushed for change. Some leaders listen. Most wait. The city can act now—lower the speed limit, harden the lanes, keep the cameras running. But every day of delay is another day of risk.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. The blood on the street is not an act of God. It is a choice. Until leaders act, the disaster will grind on.

      Bottcher Supports Safety Boosting Curbside Trash Enclosures
      ▸

      Times Square Alliance launched curbside trash bins at 43rd and Eighth. The bins clear sidewalks for heavy foot traffic. Council member Bottcher backs the Clean Curbs program. The move shifts trash from walkways to the street, freeing space for people.

      On April 19, 2022, the Times Square Alliance, one of New York’s largest business improvement districts, installed curbside trash enclosures at 43rd Street and Eighth Avenue. This pilot, part of the Clean Curbs program, is a joint effort by the Departments of Sanitation and Transportation, with support from Small Business Services and Council member Erik Bottcher. Bottcher, a Sanitation Committee member, said, “On-street containerized trash is an idea whose time has come in New York City.” The Alliance funded the $9,000 bins, aiming to clear sidewalks in one of the city’s busiest pedestrian zones. The program marks a shift in public-realm management, prioritizing pedestrian movement and sanitation in dense areas. No formal safety analysis was provided, but the move reclaims space from cars for people.

      Res 0108-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors Atlantic Ticket expansion, boosting transit use and street safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to expand and lock in the Atlantic Ticket. They want Far Rockaway and more LIRR stations in the fare program. The move aims to cut commute times and boost transit use. The bill is filed, not enacted.

      Res 0108-2022 was introduced on April 14, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution urges the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to 'expand and make permanent the Atlantic Ticket pilot in order to include Far Rockaway, and other stations along the LIRR corridor, in the fare affordability program.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Yeger, Stevens, Hanif, Bottcher, Krishnan, and many others. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. The Atlantic Ticket pilot, launched in 2018, gave Southeast Queens and Brooklyn riders cheaper LIRR fares. Advocates say expanding it citywide could mean faster, safer commutes and more transit riders. The council’s action highlights the need for affordable, accessible transit—especially for neighborhoods with long, punishing commutes.

      Res 0109-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors Interborough Express study, boosting transit and street safety.
      ▸

      Council members push for more study of the Interborough Express. The plan aims to link Brooklyn and Queens by rail or bus. The resolution supports more review, not construction. No direct safety impact for pedestrians or cyclists yet. The bill is filed.

      Resolution 0109-2022, filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, urges continued exploration and feasibility evaluation of the proposed Interborough Express (IBX). Introduced April 14, 2022, and filed at session's end, the measure states: "Resolution supporting the continued exploration and feasibility evaluation of the proposed Interborough Express." Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Bottcher, Krishnan, and others.       Res 0110-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors resolution boosting subway service, improving overall transit safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to run more peak-hour trains from distant subway terminals. Riders in transit deserts face long waits, missed trains, and crowded platforms. The resolution highlights gaps in service that strand working New Yorkers far from Manhattan.

      Res 0110-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution, introduced April 14, 2022, and filed at session's end on December 31, 2023, urges the MTA to 'adjust schedules for distant subway terminal lines to include more peak-direction rush hour trains for commuters to travel to central economic hubs.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Bottcher, and others. The bill responds to service gaps hitting outer boroughs, end-of-line stations, and transit deserts—areas where 600,000 New Yorkers live with poor transit access. The council points to pandemic-era staffing shortages and train cancellations that left riders stranded. The measure aims to push the MTA to close these gaps and connect more New Yorkers to jobs and opportunity.

      Bottcher Highlights Harmful Placard Abuse Enforcement Failures
      ▸

      City investigators stall on placard abuse. Council members press for action. Streets stay blocked. Curb access lost. Police and city workers park with impunity. Laws ignored. Promises broken. Vulnerable New Yorkers pay the price.

      On April 12, 2022, the City Council held an oversight hearing on placard abuse and enforcement. The new Department of Investigation (DOI) boss, Jocelyn Strauber, hesitated to confirm if placard abuse is a problem, despite a 2019 law requiring DOI and NYPD to investigate and report on the issue. Council Member Erik Bottcher pressed Strauber, citing resident complaints about fake placards and blocked curbs. Bottcher said, 'NYPD traffic enforcement agents are not issuing tickets to many people using counterfeit placards.' Council Member Lincoln Restler criticized the lack of a digital placard system and is drafting new enforcement legislation. Local Law 6 mandates weekly sweeps and monthly reports, but agencies have failed to comply. The Adams administration has not advanced citizen enforcement. The result: curb space remains dangerous and inaccessible for pedestrians, cyclists, and those who need it most.

      Bottcher Opposes Enforcement Delays in Misguided Placard Abuse Crackdown
      ▸

      City agencies stalled on a law to curb parking placard abuse. Enforcement never began. Bureaucrats blamed COVID. Streets stayed clogged. Bike lanes, bus lanes, and sidewalks stayed blocked. Councilmember Bottcher demanded action. Advocates called the delay dangerous. The city did nothing.

      On April 11, 2022, the City Council held an oversight hearing on enforcement delays for Local Law 6. The law ordered NYPD to inspect 25 blocks weekly for placard abuse and another 25 for blocked bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and crosswalks. The Department of Investigation (DOI) was to review and report by September 30, 2020. Agencies missed deadlines, citing pandemic and staffing shortages. Councilmember Erik Bottcher, representing District 3, pressed for action, saying businesses suffered from fake placards. The hearing highlighted the matter: 'Standing still zone: Two-year-old parking placard abuse probe stuck in bureaucratic time warp, NYC investigator blames COVID.' Advocates and @placardabuse called the delays corrupt and dangerous. Danny Pearlstein of Riders Alliance said placard abuse is no victimless crime, making streets more dangerous and slowing buses. No timeline for enforcement was given.

      Christopher Marte Backs Safety Boosting Bus Lane Expansion
      ▸

      CUNY students and Councilman Marte rallied at City Hall. They called out slow buses and high fares. They demanded more bus lanes, safer streets, and affordable rides. Marte said 30 miles of new lanes is not enough. The city must do more.

The event targeted New York City’s sluggish bus service and the high cost of MTA fares. The rally demanded Mayor Adams speed up bus commutes, install at least 30 miles of new bus lanes in 2022, and expand the Fair Fares program for low-income riders. No formal bill was under consideration, but the council and advocates pushed for $3.1 billion in funding for these changes. The action highlights the urgent need for safer, faster, and more affordable transit for vulnerable New Yorkers.
