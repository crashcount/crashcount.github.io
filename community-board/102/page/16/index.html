      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB2?

      Eight Dead, Hundreds Hurt—City Hall Lets the Blood Run

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Manhattan CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      Eight dead. Twenty seriously hurt. In three years, the streets of Manhattan CB2—Soho, Greenwich Village, West Village—have not let up. Cyclists, pedestrians, elders, children. The numbers are not just numbers. They are bodies on Broome Street, sirens on Cornelia, a life ended at Bleecker and Sixth.

      In the last 12 months alone: 2 killed, 10 seriously injured, 334 hurt. The dead include a 44-year-old cyclist thrown under a truck after a van door swung open on Broome Street. The driver admitted, “I opened the door. I didn’t even see the guy. I only saw the accident.” The man on the bike was Georgios Smaragdis. He never made it home.

      A few days later, a cyclist was crushed in a hit-and-run at 5th Avenue and 13th. Myung Jin Chung woke up in an ambulance. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” Chung said. He needed sixteen hours of surgery. The driver ran two red lights and vanished.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      City Hall talks of Vision Zero. Lower speed limits. More cameras. Safer intersections. But in CB2, the carnage continues. The city has the power to set a 20 mph speed limit. It has not used it. Speed cameras cut speeding by more than half, but their future is always in doubt. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program lapsed. No word on renewal.

      No press conferences on Broome Street. No new protected lanes. No public reckoning for the dead. The drivers who stayed at the scene walk free. The ones who fled are still gone.

      What Comes Next

      The disaster is slow, but it is not silent. Residents and advocates have pushed for change. Some leaders listen. Most wait. The city can act now—lower the speed limit, harden the lanes, keep the cameras running. But every day of delay is another day of risk.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. The blood on the street is not an act of God. It is a choice. Until leaders act, the disaster will grind on.

      Int 1011-2023
      Bottcher votes yes on fare evasion info bill, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing police to hand out flyers on reduced fare programs to anyone ticketed or arrested for subway fare evasion. The flyers list eligibility, discounts, and how to apply. The law aims to inform, not punish. It is now in effect.

      Int 1011-2023, now enacted as Local Law 30 of 2024, moved through the Committee on Public Safety and passed the City Council on January 20, 2024. The bill requires police officers patrolling subway stations to carry and distribute flyers about city-run reduced fare programs to anyone arrested or summoned for fare evasion. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to providing information on the reduced fare programs to persons issued a summons for fare evasion.' Council Member Farah N. Louis sponsored the bill, joined by Brooks-Powers, Brewer, Dinowitz, Barron, Krishnan, and others. The flyers must explain eligibility, discount details, and application steps. The law took effect 120 days after passage. No direct safety impact for vulnerable road users was noted.

      Int 0781-2022
      Bottcher votes yes on vehicle stop reporting bill, safety impact neutral.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing NYPD to spell out why they stop cars. Each stop now gets logged with the reason, time, and outcome. No names. Just facts. The city posts these reports online. A move for sunlight. No more secrets.

      Int 0781-2022, now law, came through the Committee on Public Safety. The Council passed it on January 20, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the police department to report on the justification for the stop in vehicle encounter reports," demands the NYPD document every vehicle stop. Reports must include the reason for the stop, the level of offense, and demographic details, but no personal identifiers. Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Lincoln Restler (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, and others led the charge. The law aims to expose patterns in police stops and holds officers to account. Reports go online, open to all. This is a step toward transparency, not just for drivers, but for every New Yorker who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.

      Int 0417-2022
      Rivera votes yes on bill clarifying transportation project notification rules, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to streamline how the city notifies communities about big street changes. Bike lanes now follow the same rules as other major projects. The city can act faster after feedback. The old bike lane notice law is gone.

      Int 0417-2022, now enacted, amends the city code to create a single, uniform process for notifying council members and community boards about major transportation projects—including bike lanes. The bill, handled by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on May 19, 2022, and passed on January 6, 2024. The official title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to notice requirements for certain transportation projects and the repeal of section 19-187 in relation thereto.' Council Member Shekar Krishnan led as primary sponsor, joined by dozens of colleagues. The law repeals the old, separate bike lane notice process and folds it into the broader project notice rules. Now, after a set notice period and community feedback, the Department of Transportation can move ahead without delay. This change aims to cut red tape and speed up street redesigns that affect vulnerable road users.
