      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB6?

      BQE, Atlantic, and the Cost of Waiting

      Written August 31, 2025 (Revised August 31, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Brooklyn CB6: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 31, 2025

      Just before 9 AM on Aug 27, 2025, a box truck hit a motorcyclist on the BQE near the Atlantic Ave exit. The rider died; police later arrested the truck driver for leaving the scene, according to ABC7 and the Daily News.

      He was one of 8 people killed on Brooklyn CB6 streets since Jan 1, 2022, with 1,739 injured and 20 seriously hurt, city data show (NYC Open Data).

      The map keeps pointing at the same places

      The BQE through CB6 is a wound. It accounts for a hotspot with 1 death and 248 injuries in this period. Atlantic Avenue shows 1 death and 41 injuries. Both are listed among the top danger sites in this area (NYC Open Data).

      Injuries stack up midday into the evening. From noon through 6 PM, hourly counts stay high, peaking around the late afternoon commute (NYC Open Data).

      Trucks hit hard. Since 2022, pedestrians struck by trucks here include 2 deaths in the dataset’s breakdown by vehicle type (NYC Open Data).

      The pattern isn’t easing

      This year isn’t kinder. Through Aug 31, crashes in CB6 are up 25.1% over last year’s pace; injuries are up 32.4%. Serious injuries are down, but only to 2 so far—two lives changed forever all the same (NYC Open Data).

      Some causes we can name are in the file: failure to yield and driver distraction appear in injury cases in this zone (with documented serious injuries tied to each) (NYC Open Data).

      Leaders know what saves lives

      “This doesn’t have to be this way,” State Sen. Andrew Gounardes said at a Brooklyn safety rally this summer. “Here we are, once again gathering to mourn another preventable tragedy on our streets. But it doesn’t have to be this way.” (BKReader).

      Albany just extended New York City’s 24/7 school‑zone speed cameras through 2030, a program touted for cutting speeding where installed (AMNY; Streetsblog). Gounardes sponsored the Senate bill to reauthorize cameras and has also sponsored a bill to require intelligent speed assistance for repeat speeders (Open States: S 8344; Open States: S 4045).

      In the Assembly, Jo Anne Simon voted yes on the speed‑camera extension and co‑sponsored the companion “speed limiter” bill (A 2299). She also co‑sponsored A 7997 to crack down on plates that dodge camera enforcement (A 2299 via context; A 7997).

      Citywide, advocates are pressing the Council and the Mayor to use Sammy’s Law authority to drop default speeds to 20 MPH on local streets. The case is laid out here.

      Fix the corners, slow the straightaways

      The files point to practical work. Daylight every corner on Atlantic and 4th. Add hardened turns and leading pedestrian intervals at the BQE exit at Atlantic. Use truck‑calming design and routing where heavy vehicles mix with walkers. Target failure‑to‑yield and distraction where injuries stack up midday. These steps match the harms on the record (NYC Open Data).

      City Hall can make the whole grid calmer. Lower the default speed. Albany can yank the worst offenders out of the red by mandating speed limiters for repeat speeders. Both tools exist in front of our officials now (Streetsblog; Open States: S 4045; A 2299; Take Action).

      Back on the BQE, near Atlantic, the pavement still bears the scars. The count keeps growing until someone turns the wheel. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What area does this cover?
      Brooklyn Community Board 6, which includes Carroll Gardens–Cobble Hill–Gowanus–Red Hook and Park Slope. It overlaps parts of Council Districts 38 and 39, Assembly Districts 44, 51, and 52, and State Senate Districts 20 and 26.
      ▸ How many people were killed and injured here since 2022?
      From Jan 1, 2022 to Aug 31, 2025, 8 people were killed and 1,739 were injured in traffic crashes in Brooklyn CB6, with 20 listed as seriously injured, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Where are the worst hotspots?
      The Brooklyn‑Queens Expressway corridor within CB6 and Atlantic Avenue both appear in the top danger locations in the data, with documented deaths and dozens to hundreds of injuries.
      ▸ Which officials can act, and what have they done?
      State Sen. Andrew Gounardes sponsored the speed‑camera reauthorization and the speed‑limiter bill S 4045. Assembly Member Jo Anne Simon voted yes on extending school‑zone speed cameras and co‑sponsored A 2299 (speed limiters) and A 7997 (plate enforcement). Council Member Shahana Hanif represents District 39; the city now has the tools to lower speeds and expand street‑safety design. The question is when they will use them.
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We pulled counts from NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes: h9gi‑nx95; Persons: f55k‑p6yu) for Jan 1, 2022–Aug 31, 2025, filtered to Brooklyn Community Board 6. We used fields for date/time, location, injury severity, person type, and vehicle type to total deaths, injuries, serious injuries, top locations, hourly patterns, and pedestrian impacts by vehicle class. Data were accessed Aug 31, 2025. You can start from the source tables here and here.

      Adams Removes Bedford Avenue Bike Lane
      ▸

      Jun 13 - City will rip out a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. The lane calmed a deadly stretch. Drivers still parked illegally. Children darted into traffic. Now, cyclists and pedestrians face more danger. Policy shifts, safety slips. Streets stay lethal.

      Streetsblog NYC reported on June 13, 2025, that Mayor Adams will remove the protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue, a corridor known for high crash rates. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'before this bike lane from 2018 to 2022 this is the area that had the second largest numbers of pedestrian fatalities and crashes in the city.' The lane faced opposition from some Hasidic leaders, citing children running into the lane from illegally parked cars and buses. Despite tweaks—school bus zones, daylighted corners, no standing areas—drivers kept parking illegally, blocking sightlines and endangering children. The city rarely removes protected lanes, but Adams acted after political pressure. Moving the lane to Classon Avenue, as some demand, would force cyclists onto a chaotic BQE ramp. The decision highlights persistent driver violations and policy gaps that leave vulnerable road users exposed.

      Gounardes Supports Safety Boosting Speed Limiting Devices
      ▸

      Jun 13 - Senate passes bill forcing delivery apps to insure workers and crash victims. Lawmakers tout support for the injured. But insurance comes after the hit. Speeders keep driving. Danger stays on the street. Prevention takes a back seat.

      On June 13, 2025, the New York State Senate passed a bill requiring food delivery apps to provide insurance for delivery workers, pedestrians, and cyclists. Lawmakers back insurance, but leave the streets risky.

      S 4045 Gounardes votes yes in committee, boosting street safety by curbing repeat speeders.
      ▸

      Jun 12 - Senate backs S 4045. Repeat speeders face forced installation of speed assistance tech. Eleven points or six camera tickets triggers action. Law targets reckless drivers. Streets may get safer for those outside the car.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on June 11 and June 12, 2025.       Gounardes Supports Safety‑Boosting Speed Limiter Program Expansion
      ▸

      Jun 11 - Senate gutted the Super Speeder Bill at midnight. Over 130,000 reckless drivers dodge speed-limiter rules. Only the worst offenders face consequences. Pedestrians and cyclists stay exposed. Lawmakers chose drivers over safety. The city’s deadliest keep rolling. Streets stay dangerous.

      On June 11, 2025, the New York State Senate amended the Super Speeder Bill, sponsored by Sen. Andrew Gounardes (D-Bay Ridge). The bill originally required drivers with six or more speed or red-light camera tickets in a year to install speed-limiting devices. The Senate raised the threshold to sixteen speed-camera tickets, and removed red-light violations from the count. Streetsblog NYC reports, 'the state Senate moved to help more than 130,000 reckless drivers avoid accountability.' Gounardes defended the change, saying it would allow the program to grow slowly. Assembly Member Michaell Novakhov (R-Midwood) argued the threshold was too low. The amendment means only 17,000 drivers, not 150,000, will face restrictions. According to safety analysts, this move undermines deterrence, increases risk to pedestrians and cyclists, and signals that dangerous driving is tolerated. Vulnerable road users remain at risk. The bill’s status now sets a weaker standard for accountability.
