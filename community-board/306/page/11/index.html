      Flatbush Bleeds: Lives Lost While City Delays

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 7, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB6: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Death on Familiar Streets

      A woman, 83, was killed by a truck turning right on Bond Street. She was crossing. She did not make it to the other side. A 72-year-old woman died at Flatbush and 5th, struck by a taxi. An 18-year-old, riding in a sedan on Court Street, never got out of the car alive. In the last twelve months, two people died and six suffered serious injuries in Brooklyn CB6. The names fade, but the pain does not. In the same period, 495 people were hurt. The streets do not forgive.

      The Numbers: Relentless and Rising

      953 crashes. 495 injuries. 2 deaths. In just one year. The old, the young, the in-between. No one is spared. Cars and trucks do most of the damage: 1 killed, 3 seriously hurt by cars and SUVs; 1 killed by a truck. Bikes and motorcycles wound, but the deadliest machines are still the biggest. See the NYC Open Data crash records.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Flatbush Avenue is a scar through the heart of Brooklyn. The city promises to heal it. “Right now, Flatbush Avenue above Prospect Park doesn’t work for anyone. Almost 70,000 daily bus riders are stuck waiting too long for slow buses, drivers are caught in a mess of traffic, and pedestrians are left crossing intersections clogged with vehicles,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. The plan: center-running bus lanes, pedestrian islands, fewer car lanes. The city calls it a “world-class, welcoming corridor for everyone.”

      But the work is not done. DOT did not share a block-by-block plan. Parking may stay. Bus lanes may not be protected. “The goal is to make Flatbush Avenue ‘a high performing transit priority street that connects various Brooklyn neighborhoods’ rather than a road that merely moves cars between Brooklyn and Manhattan,” said DOT Assistant Director Dustin Khuu.

      55 people have been killed or severely injured on Flatbush since 2019. The city says it will finish the project in 2025. The dead cannot wait.

      What You Can Do: Demand More, Demand Now

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bus lanes and crosswalks that put people first. Do not wait for another name to become a number. Take action now.

      Int 1039-2024
      Hanif co-sponsors bike share fee cap, boosting cycling safety and access.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1039-2024 would stop bike share operators from charging members more than a subway fare for short rides. The cap covers e-bikes up to one hour and regular bikes up to two hours. The bill sits in committee.

      Int 1039-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on September 12, 2024. The bill reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to setting maximum amounts for certain member usage fees charged by a bike share operator.' Council Member Chi A. Ossé leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Marte, Hudson, Cabán, Brannan, Hanif, Brewer, and Abreu. The bill would prohibit bike share companies from charging members more than the base subway fare for e-bike rides of one hour or less, or regular bike rides of two hours or less. The law would take effect 120 days after passage and apply to new or renewed contracts. This measure aims to keep bike share affordable for New Yorkers, making cycling a more accessible option.

      Int 0746-2024
      Hanif votes yes, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety with greener medians.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to turn bare medians green. The city must plant trees or use medians for stormwater. Agencies must report progress. The law targets high-risk areas. Votes showed strong support. The mayor returned it unsigned. Streets will change.

      Int 0746-2024, now Local Law 94 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 12, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the improvement and maintenance of medians through the planting of vegetation or use in stormwater management," requires the city to improve at least one mile of paved medians every two years until 2046. The Department of Transportation, Parks, and Environmental Protection must add planted medians, tree beds, or stormwater infrastructure, focusing on high-priority investment areas. Council Member Shekar Krishnan sponsored the bill, joined by Feliz, Avilés, Nurse, Banks, Restler, Ung, Schulman, Holden, Hudson, and Yeger. The Council passed the bill with broad support, though the mayor returned it unsigned. The law mandates annual progress reports and public tracking of improved medians and responsible agencies.
