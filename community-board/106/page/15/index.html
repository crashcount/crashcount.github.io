      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB6?

      Blood on the Asphalt: Vision Zero, Zero Results

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB6: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      One death. Thirteen serious injuries. Five hundred seventy-one wounded. That is the cost of traffic violence in Manhattan Community Board 6 in the last year alone (NYC Open Data). The numbers do not bleed, but people do. An 81-year-old woman, struck by an SUV on East 59th, did not make it home (Drunk Driver Kills Immigrant Pedestrian in Manhattan). A cyclist, 46, killed on First Avenue, his body thrown from the bike (Cyclist Sues NYPD Over Red Light Tickets). The dead do not get a second chance. The wounded carry scars.

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Trucks, SUVs, taxis, bikes—each leaves its mark. In the last three years, cars and SUVs killed one, left three with serious wounds, and caused dozens more injuries (NYC Open Data). Trucks and buses, three serious injuries. Bikes, one serious injury. The city counts the bodies. The city counts the broken bones. The city does not count the dreams lost.

      Leadership: Promises and Gaps

      The city talks of Vision Zero. The mayor calls traffic violence a crime. “In order to make New York City the best place to raise a family, we need to be safer at every level — including on our streets,” said Mayor Adams. But the pace is slow. The law lets cyclists cross with the walk signal, but police still ticket them. “It’s ridiculous that cyclists are being ticketed for riding legally, safely, and defensively,” said Alexa Sledge of Transportation Alternatives. The city says one thing. The street says another.

      What Comes Next

      Speed kills. Delay kills. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has the power to protect speed cameras. It has the power to stop the bleeding. But power unused is no power at all.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. Every day of delay is another day of blood on the street. Take action now.

      Bores Supports Safety Boosting License and Registration Bill
      ▸

      Council backs state bills to force moped buyers to show a valid license and register at the point of sale. Unregistered mopeds menace streets. Pedestrians and cyclists pay the price. The measure aims to close loopholes and cut illegal, dangerous riding.

      Resolution 0024-2024 sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced February 8, 2024, it urges Albany to pass A.8052 and S.7703. The resolution calls for 'purchasers of limited use motorcycles present a driver’s license appropriate for the legal operation... and register such limited use motorcycles, prior to completing a purchase.' Council Member Gale A. Brewer leads, joined by Rivera, Schulman, Krishnan, Won, Bottcher, and others. The bills would require dealers to check licenses and register mopeds at sale. The Council notes that illegal, unregistered mopeds endanger pedestrians, cyclists, and riders. NYPD seized over 8,600 illegal mopeds by September 2023. The resolution presses for safeguards to keep unlicensed, unregistered vehicles off city streets.

      Int 0094-2024
      Powers sponsors bill restricting low bass sound permits, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council bill targets deep bass. No more permits for sound devices thumping below 40 hertz. Police commissioner loses power to approve these. Schulman and Powers push the change. Action sits with Public Safety Committee. No direct impact on street safety for walkers or riders.

      Int 0094-2024, introduced February 8, 2024, sits with the Committee on Public Safety. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting the issuance of permits for the use of sound apparatuses to produce low bass sounds,' would stop the police commissioner from granting permits for devices that emit bass at 40 hertz or below for 10 seconds or more. Council Members Keith Powers (primary sponsor, District 4) and Lynn C. Schulman (co-sponsor, District 29) lead the effort. The bill was referred to committee on introduction. The measure amends existing sound permit rules but does not address traffic violence or the safety of pedestrians and cyclists. No safety analyst has found a direct effect on vulnerable road users.

      Bores Supports Stricter Enforcement and Registration for Mopeds
      ▸

      Upper West Side’s board voted 8-1 to demand crackdowns on unregistered mopeds. The resolution urges city and state to punish illegal dealers, enforce laws, and back Albany’s registration bill. Lawmakers say loopholes fuel chaos. Immigrants often misled. Streets stay dangerous.

      On December 14, 2023, Community Board 7’s transportation committee passed a resolution, 8-1, demanding action against unregistered mopeds. The board called on the Department of Motor Vehicles to impose civil penalties on unregistered dealers and urged city and state officials to enforce laws against selling non-street-legal mopeds. The resolution also asks the city to consider a buy-back program for gas-powered mopeds. The matter, titled 'Upper West Side Panel Backs Resolution to Rein in Dangerous Mopeds,' supports Albany legislation requiring sellers to register mopeds with the DMV before sale. State Senator Brad Hoylman-Sigal and Assembly Member Alex Bores sponsor the bill. Bores said, 'We need to cut off the flow of these vehicles before they get onto these streets.' The board’s move aims to close loopholes, protect vulnerable road users, and address the chaos caused by unregistered mopeds.
