      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB6?

      Blood on the Asphalt: Vision Zero, Zero Results

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB6: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      One death. Thirteen serious injuries. Five hundred seventy-one wounded. That is the cost of traffic violence in Manhattan Community Board 6 in the last year alone (NYC Open Data). The numbers do not bleed, but people do. An 81-year-old woman, struck by an SUV on East 59th, did not make it home (Drunk Driver Kills Immigrant Pedestrian in Manhattan). A cyclist, 46, killed on First Avenue, his body thrown from the bike (Cyclist Sues NYPD Over Red Light Tickets). The dead do not get a second chance. The wounded carry scars.

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Trucks, SUVs, taxis, bikes—each leaves its mark. In the last three years, cars and SUVs killed one, left three with serious wounds, and caused dozens more injuries (NYC Open Data). Trucks and buses, three serious injuries. Bikes, one serious injury. The city counts the bodies. The city counts the broken bones. The city does not count the dreams lost.

      Leadership: Promises and Gaps

      The city talks of Vision Zero. The mayor calls traffic violence a crime. “In order to make New York City the best place to raise a family, we need to be safer at every level — including on our streets,” said Mayor Adams. But the pace is slow. The law lets cyclists cross with the walk signal, but police still ticket them. “It’s ridiculous that cyclists are being ticketed for riding legally, safely, and defensively,” said Alexa Sledge of Transportation Alternatives. The city says one thing. The street says another.

      What Comes Next

      Speed kills. Delay kills. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has the power to protect speed cameras. It has the power to stop the bleeding. But power unused is no power at all.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. Every day of delay is another day of blood on the street. Take action now.

      Liz Krueger Opposes Misguided Congestion Pricing Cancellation Plan
      ▸

      Governor Hochul scrapped congestion pricing. The MTA lost a billion a year. Lawmakers scrambled. Senator Liz Krueger rejected a payroll tax hike. The MTA faces a funding cliff. Subway upgrades, elevators, and clean buses now hang in the balance.

      On June 6, 2024, Governor Hochul’s sudden reversal on Manhattan congestion pricing sparked chaos in Albany. The plan, set to start June 30, would have charged drivers $15 to enter below 60th Street, raising $1 billion yearly for transit. Hochul proposed a payroll tax hike instead, but State Senator Liz Krueger opposed it, saying, "I do not think we have an appetite for that in the Senate." The bill’s fate is uncertain as the legislative session ends. The MTA board, city officials, and business groups all voiced concern. Projects like the Second Avenue Subway, subway accessibility, and zero-emission buses now face cancellation. No alternative funding plan exists. Vulnerable riders—those who rely on transit—stand to lose the most.

      S 9718
      Gonzalez votes yes, boosting street safety with complete street design.
      ▸

      Senate Bill S 9718 passed. It pushes for complete street design. The aim: safer roads for everyone. Pedestrians, cyclists, and passengers stand to gain. The vote split. Some senators said no. But the bill moved forward. Streets may change.

      Senate Bill S 9718, titled "Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles," advanced through the Senate with committee votes on May 28 and June 3, 2024. The bill, sponsored by Sean Ryan (District 61) and co-sponsored by Liz Krueger, Monica Martinez, Jack M. Martins, Anthony H. Palumbo, and Julia Salazar, passed both votes despite opposition. The measure calls for redesigning streets to protect all users, not just drivers. The vote was not unanimous—several senators voted no, but the majority carried it through. The bill’s focus is clear: safer streets for people on foot, on bikes, and in vehicles. No safety analyst note was provided, but the intent is to cut danger at the curb and crosswalk.

      Int 0928-2024
      Powers co-sponsors cool pavement pilot bill with no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council wants DOT to test cool pavement in four heat-vulnerable, disadvantaged neighborhoods. The pilot aims to fight the city’s heat island effect. The bill sits in committee. No direct impact on pedestrian or cyclist safety. Streets stay hot. Danger remains.

      Int 0928-2024, introduced May 23, 2024, sits 'Laid Over in Committee' at the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill orders the Department of Transportation, with Health and Mental Hygiene, to run a pilot project testing cool pavement—materials that lower street temperatures—across at least four neighborhoods facing heat and disadvantage. The official summary reads: 'A Local Law in relation to requiring the department of transportation to conduct a pilot project on the use of cool pavement.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by 29 co-sponsors. The bill requires DOT to report findings to the Mayor and Council. Safety analysts note no direct effect on vulnerable road users: the measure targets heat, not traffic violence. The city’s most dangerous streets remain unchanged.
