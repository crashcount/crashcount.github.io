      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB6?

      Blood on the Asphalt: Vision Zero, Zero Results

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 1, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB6: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      One death. Thirteen serious injuries. Five hundred seventy-one wounded. That is the cost of traffic violence in Manhattan Community Board 6 in the last year alone (NYC Open Data). The numbers do not bleed, but people do. An 81-year-old woman, struck by an SUV on East 59th, did not make it home (Drunk Driver Kills Immigrant Pedestrian in Manhattan). A cyclist, 46, killed on First Avenue, his body thrown from the bike (Cyclist Sues NYPD Over Red Light Tickets). The dead do not get a second chance. The wounded carry scars.

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Trucks, SUVs, taxis, bikes—each leaves its mark. In the last three years, cars and SUVs killed one, left three with serious wounds, and caused dozens more injuries (NYC Open Data). Trucks and buses, three serious injuries. Bikes, one serious injury. The city counts the bodies. The city counts the broken bones. The city does not count the dreams lost.

      Leadership: Promises and Gaps

      The city talks of Vision Zero. The mayor calls traffic violence a crime. “In order to make New York City the best place to raise a family, we need to be safer at every level — including on our streets,” said Mayor Adams. But the pace is slow. The law lets cyclists cross with the walk signal, but police still ticket them. “It’s ridiculous that cyclists are being ticketed for riding legally, safely, and defensively,” said Alexa Sledge of Transportation Alternatives. The city says one thing. The street says another.

      What Comes Next

      Speed kills. Delay kills. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has the power to protect speed cameras. It has the power to stop the bleeding. But power unused is no power at all.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. Every day of delay is another day of blood on the street. Take action now.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Manhattan Community Board 6

      Int 1050-2024
      Powers co-sponsors bill capping TLC insurance, potentially reducing street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1050-2024 blocks the Taxi and Limousine Commission from demanding more injury coverage than state law. The measure, laid over in committee, shields cab companies from higher insurance costs. Passengers and crash victims face tighter limits on payouts.

      Int 1050-2024 was introduced on September 26, 2024, and is now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to limiting the amount of liability coverage that the taxi and limousine commission may require for vehicles it licenses," bars the TLC from requiring more personal injury protection than state law demands. Council Member Diana I. Ayala is the primary sponsor, joined by over two dozen co-sponsors including De La Rosa, Banks, Ossé, Marte, and others. The bill was laid over by committee on February 10, 2025. The measure would take effect immediately if passed. By capping required coverage, the bill may leave crash victims with less compensation after collisions involving TLC-licensed vehicles.

      Int 0346-2024
      Powers votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.

      Int 0746-2024
      Powers votes yes, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety with greener medians.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to turn bare medians green. The city must plant trees or use medians for stormwater. Agencies must report progress. The law targets high-risk areas. Votes showed strong support. The mayor returned it unsigned. Streets will change.

      Int 0746-2024, now Local Law 94 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 12, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the improvement and maintenance of medians through the planting of vegetation or use in stormwater management," requires the city to improve at least one mile of paved medians every two years until 2046. The Department of Transportation, Parks, and Environmental Protection must add planted medians, tree beds, or stormwater infrastructure, focusing on high-priority investment areas. Council Member Shekar Krishnan sponsored the bill, joined by Feliz, Avilés, Nurse, Banks, Restler, Ung, Schulman, Holden, Hudson, and Yeger. The Council passed the bill with broad support, though the mayor returned it unsigned. The law mandates annual progress reports and public tracking of improved medians and responsible agencies.

      Gonzalez Criticizes Lack of Dedicated Bus Lanes
      ▸

      G train rolls again. After months of silence, trains run from Queens to Brooklyn. Riders endured shuttle buses, slow streets, no dedicated lanes. Council Member Restler praises upgrades, slams city for missing bus lanes. Modern signals promise speed, but funding future hangs in balance.

      On September 3, 2024, the G train returned to full service after a summer shutdown for signal modernization. The $368 million project, led by the MTA, replaced century-old signals with Communication-Based Train Control (CBTC), aiming for faster, more reliable service. Council Member Lincoln Restler (District 33) praised the MTA’s handling and shuttle bus communication but criticized the city for not creating dedicated bus lanes during the closure, saying, 'They expressed dismay, however, that the city had not constructed a dedicated lane so shuttle buses could move faster.' Restler called for renewed investment in the MTA, including the resumption of congestion pricing, to fund future upgrades. The project continues through 2027, but no more full shutdowns are planned. The lack of bus lanes during the outage left vulnerable riders exposed to slow, crowded streets.
