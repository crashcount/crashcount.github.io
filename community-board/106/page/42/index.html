      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB6?

      Blood on the Asphalt: New York's Leaders Stall, Neighbors Die

      Written August 4, 2025 (Revised August 11, 2025 – Fixed link formatting, removed duplicate headline, cleaned citations.)

      Manhattan CB6: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 4, 2025

      The Slow Grind of Loss

      In Manhattan CB6, the numbers do not lie. Six people killed. Thirty more left with serious injuries. Since 2022, there have been over 3,400 crashes. The wounded are not just numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. A 49-year-old man crushed by a bus at 3rd Avenue and East 28th Street. An 81-year-old woman killed by an SUV on East 59th. A 23-year-old cyclist, dead on East 21st, struck by a sedan. The blood dries, but the ache stays.

      The Vehicles That Harm

      Cars and SUVs did the most damage. They killed two, left hundreds hurt. Trucks and buses killed one, left dozens more with broken bodies. Bikes and e-bikes, too, are in the tally—one dead, scores injured. The streets are not safe for anyone on foot or on two wheels. The risk is everywhere, at every hour.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Some leaders have acted. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit. She also backed the renewal of school speed zones. But the city has the power to do more. The Council can lower the speed limit to 20 mph right now. They have not. The silence is loud. The delay is deadly.

      Council Member Keith Powers called for action: “[The state] certainly should take advantage of this very expensive infrastructure in Midtown,” he said, after congestion pricing was paused and enforcement tools sat idle. The tools to save lives gather dust while bodies gather in the morgue.

      The Voices on the Street

      Residents see the danger. After a crash, a woman named Nita said, “No one stops at these stop signs. We see people go through these red lights all the time.” she told CBS New York. The city knows. The state knows. Still, the carnage continues.

      What You Must Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand enforcement against repeat speeders. Demand streets where a child can cross without fear. Every day of delay is another day of blood on the asphalt.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB6 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4, assembly district AD 74 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB6?
      It includes the Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Gramercy, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, and United Nations neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2 and District 4, Assembly Districts AD 73 and AD 74, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB6?
      SUVs and Cars: 2 deaths, 273 minor injuries, 92 moderate injuries, 4 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 1 death, 26 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries, 3 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 29 minor injuries, 17 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 6 minor injuries, 5 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The pattern is clear. Crashes follow from speed, enforcement gaps, and street design. They are preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the speed limit to 20 mph, pass and enforce laws against repeat speeders, and redesign streets to protect people walking and cycling.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      S 3897
      Krueger votes yes to require safer complete street designs, improving safety.
      ▸

      Senate passed S 3897. More state cash flows to cities that build complete streets. Lawmakers push for safer roads. Pedestrians and cyclists get a shot at survival.

      Bill S 3897 cleared the Senate committee on March 2, 2022, with a final vote on May 25, 2022. The bill, titled 'Relates to complete street design features and funding of construction and improvements at a municipalities' expense,' boosts state funding for transportation projects when cities add complete street features. Senator Kennedy sponsored the bill. Senators including Cooney, Gallivan, Kaminsky, Kaplan, Liu, and others voted yes. The measure aims to tie state money to safer street design, pushing municipalities to build roads that protect people outside cars.

      Powers Supports Safety Boosting Park Avenue Car Space Reclamation
      ▸

      DOT will cut a car lane on Park Avenue. The median will grow, swallowing asphalt. Advocates want bike lanes and car-free stretches. Council Member Powers calls it a shift in city priorities. Private money backs the plan. Final design still unknown.

      On January 25, 2022, the city Department of Transportation advanced a proposal to reclaim car space on Park Avenue. The plan, discussed in committee and supported by Council Member Keith Powers, aims to expand the median from 20 to 48 feet by removing one of three vehicle lanes. The project, described as a 'once-in-a-lifetime opportunity' by Alfred Cerullo of the Grand Central Partnership, seeks to create more open, accessible public space for people and businesses in East Midtown. Advocates like Jon Orcutt (Bike New York) and Danny Harris (Transportation Alternatives) urge the city to maximize space for people, calling for fully car-free stretches and a bike lane, since Park Avenue has no bus routes. The plan aligns with the NYC 25x25 initiative to convert 25% of car space to people space by 2025. Private commercial interests, including the local Business Improvement District and Fisher Brothers, are supporting and funding the project. The final design will be set by a landscape architect’s master plan.

      Keith Powers Urges Immediate Action on Safety Boosting Fifth Avenue Bike Lane
      ▸

      Fifth Avenue stays dangerous. DOT stalls on promised redesign. Bike lane, busway, and pedestrian fixes remain on paper. Local business pressure blocks progress. Community Board 5 approved. Crashes and injuries mount. Council Member Powers urges action. Vulnerable road users wait.

      The Fifth Avenue redesign, announced in 2020 under former Mayor de Blasio, remains stalled as of January 10, 2022. The project, once a bold car-free busway, was scaled back to a protected bike lane and minor pedestrian upgrades. No new bus lane. Community Board 5 approved the plan, but DOT has not started work. Transportation Alternatives’ Cory Epstein says, 'Busways and bike lanes are good for the economy, good for our climate, and good for our Vision Zero goals.' Since the project’s announcement, crashes and injuries continue on the corridor. Vulnerable road users remain at risk as the city delays.
