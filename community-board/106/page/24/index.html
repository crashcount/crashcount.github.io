      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB6?

      Blood on the Asphalt: Vision Zero, Zero Results

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB6: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      One death. Thirteen serious injuries. Five hundred seventy-one wounded. That is the cost of traffic violence in Manhattan Community Board 6 in the last year alone (NYC Open Data). The numbers do not bleed, but people do. An 81-year-old woman, struck by an SUV on East 59th, did not make it home (Drunk Driver Kills Immigrant Pedestrian in Manhattan). A cyclist, 46, killed on First Avenue, his body thrown from the bike (Cyclist Sues NYPD Over Red Light Tickets). The dead do not get a second chance. The wounded carry scars.

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Trucks, SUVs, taxis, bikes—each leaves its mark. In the last three years, cars and SUVs killed one, left three with serious wounds, and caused dozens more injuries (NYC Open Data). Trucks and buses, three serious injuries. Bikes, one serious injury. The city counts the bodies. The city counts the broken bones. The city does not count the dreams lost.

      Leadership: Promises and Gaps

      The city talks of Vision Zero. The mayor calls traffic violence a crime. “In order to make New York City the best place to raise a family, we need to be safer at every level — including on our streets,” said Mayor Adams. But the pace is slow. The law lets cyclists cross with the walk signal, but police still ticket them. “It’s ridiculous that cyclists are being ticketed for riding legally, safely, and defensively,” said Alexa Sledge of Transportation Alternatives. The city says one thing. The street says another.

      What Comes Next

      Speed kills. Delay kills. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has the power to protect speed cameras. It has the power to stop the bleeding. But power unused is no power at all.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. Every day of delay is another day of blood on the street. Take action now.

      Gonzalez Supports Safety Boosting State Participation in BQE Planning
      ▸

      Eighteen Brooklyn officials demand state DOT address BQE’s full deadly stretch. They reject piecemeal fixes. They call out decades of harm. The state’s refusal leaves neighborhoods exposed. The city’s hands are tied. The highway’s danger remains. Vulnerable lives hang in the balance.

      On February 10, 2023, eighteen Brooklyn lawmakers issued a joint letter demanding New York State Department of Transportation (DOT) participate in comprehensive planning for the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway (BQE). The statement, prompted by a Streetsblog report, reads: "We ask that the NYS DOT remember the daily harms caused by the BQE in its current state—not just the crumbling cantilever section—and join us at the table in search of a solution." The group includes Reps. Nydia Velázquez, Dan Goldman, Borough President Antonio Reynoso, state Sens. Jabari Brisport, Julia Salazar, Kristen Gonzalez, Andrew Gounardes, Assembly Members Bobby Carroll, Maritza Davila, Phara Souffrant Forrest, Emily Gallagher, Jo Anne Simon, Marcela Mitaynes, and Council Members Alexa Avilés, Shahana Hanif, Crystal Hudson, Jennifer Gutiérrez, and Lincoln Restler. Senator Gounardes called the state’s refusal to join the city’s visioning process “unacceptable and irresponsible.” The lawmakers stress that the BQE’s harms—crashes, pollution, division—stretch from Bay Ridge to Greenpoint. Without state action, only city-owned segments see attention. The city’s efforts are limited. The state’s inaction leaves systemic danger unaddressed.

      A 3180
      Bores co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 3180 demands every state- or federally-funded road project weigh complete street design. It pushes agencies to publish clear guidance. The bill aims to force planners to see people, not just cars. Streets must serve all. No more blind spots.

      Assembly Bill A 3180 was introduced on February 3, 2023, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in committee, awaiting further action. Its matter title reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding.' Didi Barrett leads as primary sponsor, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Amy Paulin, Carrie Woerner, and many others as co-sponsors. The bill compels agencies to consider complete street design—meaning streets built for pedestrians, cyclists, and transit users, not just drivers. It also requires the department to publish guidance and reference materials for public use. This measure, if enforced, could reshape how New York builds and rebuilds its roads, putting vulnerable road users at the center of every project.

      Int 0905-2023
      Powers co-sponsors bill to speed up street resurfacing, boosting safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force street resurfacing jobs to finish in two weeks. Delays must be explained to neighbors. Streets torn up for months endanger walkers and riders. The bill died at session’s end. The danger remains. The clock keeps ticking.

      Int 0905-2023 was introduced on February 2, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to amend city law to require the Department of Transportation to finish all street resurfacing work within two weeks. If more time was needed, the department would have to notify the community, stating the reason and new timeline. The matter summary reads: 'ensure that all work associated with the resurfacing of any street is finalized within 2 weeks from the start of work.' Council Member Diana I. Ayala sponsored the bill, joined by thirty other members. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. No safety analyst reviewed the impact, but torn-up streets are a known threat to pedestrians and cyclists. The bill is dead. The hazard lives on.

      A 1280
      Bores co-sponsors bill boosting street safety for all users.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 1280 pushes for safe streets. It calls for complete street design. Dozens of lawmakers back it. The bill aims to protect everyone—walkers, riders, and drivers. The fight for safer roads moves forward. The danger is real. The need is urgent.

      Assembly Bill A 1280, now in the sponsorship stage, sits before the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 13, 2023, the bill 'enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles.' Jonathan Rivera leads as primary sponsor, joined by over 70 co-sponsors including Patrick Burke, Kenny Burgos, and Jessica Gonzalez-Rojas. The bill has not yet advanced to committee or vote. Its language centers the safety of all road users—pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers—by demanding infrastructure that reduces risk and prioritizes vulnerable people. The measure reflects growing legislative momentum for street design that saves lives and prevents injury. The urgency is clear: New York streets remain dangerous. Lawmakers are under pressure to act.
