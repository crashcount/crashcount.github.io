      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB4?

      Bushwick Bleeds While Leaders Stall: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll on Bushwick Streets

      A man stands in a crosswalk at Wyckoff and Himrod. A car hits him. He flies ten feet, lands hard, and the driver vanishes. Ruari Fay-Handebeaux wakes in the hospital with a broken ankle, a fractured skull, and a pelvis split open. He says, “Broken ankle, broken humorous, bunch of fractures in my skull” (ABC7).

      In the last twelve months, five people died on Brooklyn CB4 streets. Ten more were seriously injured. Four hundred forty-seven were hurt. The dead include a 59-year-old man, struck while crossing with the signal. A 26-year-old cyclist, crushed by a car. A 65-year-old moped rider, ejected and killed. The numbers do not slow. Eight deaths, eighteen serious injuries, and over 1,600 hurt since 2022 (NYC Open Data).

      Repeat Offenders, Repeated Loss

      Hayden Wallace was 29. He died in a Bushwick hit-and-run. The man charged with killing him had fled crashes before. Hayden’s aunt said, “He lived life to the fullest. He was only 29 years old and lit up every room he entered. He was loved by so many” (NY Daily News).

      The driver was out on bail for another crash (NY Daily News). He ran again. The law let him back behind the wheel. The street did not forgive.

      What Has Been Done—And What Hasn’t

      Local leaders talk of Vision Zero. They cite new speed cameras, intersection redesigns, and the power to lower speed limits. But the streets in Bushwick do not change fast. The intersection where Ruari was hit is still the same. The law that could lower speeds to 20 mph sits unused. The camera program faces expiration. No new protections for the most vulnerable.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. Every death, every broken body, is preventable. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Demand streets that put people first. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Reynoso Supports Boroughwide Summer Streets Expansion and Connection
      ▸

      Mayor Adams expands Summer Streets to every borough. Five Saturdays. Seventeen miles. Cars banned from 7 a.m. to 1 p.m. Brooklyn and Queens get major corridors. Advocates want longer hours and connected routes. City cites costs and safety. Streets open, but only for a while.

      On June 12, 2023, Mayor Adams announced the expansion of New York City’s Summer Streets program to all five boroughs. The program, managed by the Department of Transportation, will run on five Saturdays in July and August, from 7 a.m. to 1 p.m., covering 17 miles. Borough presidents Antonio Reynoso (Brooklyn) and Donovan Richards (Queens) pushed for this expansion, with Reynoso praising the use of major corridors like Eastern Parkway. Mayor Adams said, “This is a five-borough city and we are now saying the Summer Streets program is going be a five-borough initiative. Everyone deserves to enjoy that.” Adams expressed interest in longer hours but cited public safety and NYPD staffing costs as barriers. Advocates want more hours and connected car-free routes. The city will spend $1.5 million, not including staffing. The expansion marks a step forward, but the early end time leaves advocates wanting more.

      Int 1063-2023
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors bill for EV charging info app, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to create a city app showing electric vehicle charging stations. The app would map locations, show voltage, connector types, and real-time status. It would include e-bike charging info. Strict privacy rules would limit data collection and retention.

      Bill Int 1063-2023 was introduced on June 8, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill called for the Department of Information Technology and Telecommunications to build a mobile app displaying all public electric vehicle charging stations in New York City. The app would show locations, voltage, charging level, connector types, e-bike compatibility, and real-time availability. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the creation of a mobile application that provides information about electric vehicle charging stations.' Council Member Keith Powers sponsored the bill, joined by Avilés, Cabán, Farías, Riley, Richardson Jordan, Ossé, Ung, Hanif, Restler, and Gutiérrez. The bill set strict privacy: no device data, no tracking, no forced consent. The bill was filed at session’s end, not enacted.

      Int 1071-2023
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors bill speeding removal of hazardous utility poles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force quick removal of abandoned or dangerous utility poles and wires. Owners would have sixty days to clear junk, or act at once if hazards loom. The bill died in committee. Streets stay cluttered. Danger lingers.

      Int 1071-2023 was introduced on June 8, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to require owners to remove disused or abandoned utility poles, wires, and appurtenances within 60 days, and to act immediately if conditions were dangerous. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the timelines for the removal of abandoned or unsafe utility poles, wires, and appurtenances, and the transfer of appurtenances to newly erected poles.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán sponsored the bill, joined by Kevin C. Riley, Jennifer Gutiérrez, Lincoln Restler, Kristin Richardson Jordan, Amanda Farías, and Robert F. Holden. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The bill’s failure leaves hazardous poles and wires on city streets, a threat to those on foot and bike.
