      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB7?

      Brooklyn Streets Kill—City Stalls. Demand Action Now.

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB7: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In Brooklyn CB7, the street does not forgive. Since 2022, 13 people have died and 2,148 have been injured in crashes. Twenty of those injuries were serious—lives changed in a blink, families left with empty chairs and hospital bills. In the last year alone, 2 people died and 664 were hurt. The dead are not numbers. A 70-year-old woman, crossing with the signal, struck by a turning sedan on 7th Avenue. A 30-year-old man, crushed by a truck on 60th Street. A 49-year-old cyclist, thrown and killed by a pickup at 53rd and 4th. The street is a wound that never closes.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists bleed first. Trucks, sedans, SUVs—they do the killing. In the last three years, cars and trucks took 5 lives, motorcycles and mopeds 1, bikes none. The rest are left with broken bones, crushed limbs, and memories that do not heal. The numbers are steady. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They promise safer streets, lower speed limits, more cameras. But in CB7, the pace is slow. The deaths keep coming. The city says, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” The words are right. The action is not enough. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not done it here. Cameras catch speeders, but the law that keeps them running is always at risk. Every delay is another risk, another family waiting for a call that never should come.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand more cameras, more street redesigns, more action. Do not wait for another obituary.

      The street will not change itself. You have to make it happen.

      Take action now.

      SUVs Collide on Gowanus Expressway, Two Injured
      ▸

      Two male drivers suffered whiplash and eye and neck injuries in a multi-SUV crash on the Gowanus Expressway. The collision involved lane changing and driver inattention. Both vehicles sustained left-side damage in the northbound lanes.

      According to the police report, the crash occurred on the Gowanus Expressway at 13:43 involving three SUVs traveling northbound. One SUV, driven by a 58-year-old male with a learner's permit, was changing lanes when it collided with another SUV going straight ahead. The report cites 'Driver Inattention/Distraction' and 'Driver Inexperience' as contributing factors for the 58-year-old driver. Both drivers were injured: the older driver suffered an eye injury and whiplash, while the younger driver, 18, also experienced whiplash and neck injury. The point of impact was the left front bumper of the lane-changing SUV and the left rear bumper of the struck vehicle. Vehicle damage was concentrated on the left front quarter panel and left rear bumper, confirming a side-impact collision. The report does not list any victim behaviors as contributing factors.

      Avilés Condemns Misguided Weakening of School Idling Rule
      ▸

      DEP wants to shrink anti-idling zones near schools. Advocates push back. They say it puts children at risk. Council Member Avilés calls the move a betrayal. No one supports the change. The fight is over air, lungs, and truth.

      On March 8, 2024, the Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) faced public outrage at a hearing over its proposal to weaken school-zone vehicle idling regulations. The matter, described as a move to 'loosen vehicle idling regulations around schools,' would let drivers idle engines by narrowing the definition of 'adjacent'—allowing them to move just down the block or across the street to evade the one-minute rule. Council Member Alexa Avilés, who sponsored a bill to expand these protections, condemned DEP’s plan, stating it 'flies in the face' of efforts to protect children, especially those with asthma. Every public speaker opposed the change. Ernest Welde accused DEP of misleading the public, calling them the 'Department of Enabling Polluters.' DEP said it would consider feedback before finalizing the rule. The proposal remains under review, with no support from the public or council.

      Alexa Avilés Supports Safety Boosting Cruise Ship Pollution Bill
      ▸

      Councilmember Avilés pushes a bill to force cruise ships to plug into shore power and cut diesel fumes at city docks. The law demands traffic plans to curb congestion and noise in Red Hook and Hell’s Kitchen. The fight for clean air begins.

      On March 7, 2024, the City Council voted on a bill requiring cruise ships docking in New York City to use shore power when available, instead of burning diesel. The bill, known as the 'Our Water, Our Air Act,' also mandates traffic mitigation plans to reduce congestion, noise, and pollution near terminals, especially in Red Hook and Hell’s Kitchen. Councilmember Alexa Avilés, representing District 38 and Red Hook, sponsored the bill after years of local organizing. She said, 'The health and wellness of my community members are first and foremost.' The bill alters city contracts to enforce shore power use and directs the Economic Development Corporation to expand infrastructure. Local business owner Susan Povich called it 'the first step to address traffic and emissions.' The measure aims to cut deadly air and street hazards for neighborhoods long battered by cruise traffic.
