      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB7?

      Brooklyn Streets Kill—City Stalls. Demand Action Now.

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB7: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In Brooklyn CB7, the street does not forgive. Since 2022, 13 people have died and 2,148 have been injured in crashes. Twenty of those injuries were serious—lives changed in a blink, families left with empty chairs and hospital bills. In the last year alone, 2 people died and 664 were hurt. The dead are not numbers. A 70-year-old woman, crossing with the signal, struck by a turning sedan on 7th Avenue. A 30-year-old man, crushed by a truck on 60th Street. A 49-year-old cyclist, thrown and killed by a pickup at 53rd and 4th. The street is a wound that never closes.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists bleed first. Trucks, sedans, SUVs—they do the killing. In the last three years, cars and trucks took 5 lives, motorcycles and mopeds 1, bikes none. The rest are left with broken bones, crushed limbs, and memories that do not heal. The numbers are steady. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They promise safer streets, lower speed limits, more cameras. But in CB7, the pace is slow. The deaths keep coming. The city says, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” The words are right. The action is not enough. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not done it here. Cameras catch speeders, but the law that keeps them running is always at risk. Every delay is another risk, another family waiting for a call that never should come.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand more cameras, more street redesigns, more action. Do not wait for another obituary.

      The street will not change itself. You have to make it happen.

      Take action now.

      Gounardes Supports Safety Boosting Penalties for Obstructed Plates
      ▸

      Council backs tougher fines for drivers who hide plates. Obscured tags let reckless motorists dodge cameras and tickets. The problem grows. Lawmakers want stiffer penalties to stop the evasion and protect people on city streets.

      Resolution 0467-2024 sits in the Committee on Public Safety. Introduced June 20, 2024, it urges Albany to pass S.2447/A.5234. The measure’s title: 'increase the penalties for purposefully obstructed license plates.' Council Members Justin L. Brannan (primary sponsor, District 47) and Robert F. The resolution calls for higher fines, confiscation of plate coverings, and registration suspensions. Lawmakers say this crackdown is needed to keep dangerous drivers from slipping through the cracks and to make streets safer for everyone.

      Int 0928-2024
      Avilés co-sponsors cool pavement pilot bill with no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council wants DOT to test cool pavement in four heat-vulnerable, disadvantaged neighborhoods. The pilot aims to fight the city’s heat island effect. The bill sits in committee. No direct impact on pedestrian or cyclist safety. Streets stay hot. Danger remains.

      Int 0928-2024, introduced May 23, 2024, sits 'Laid Over in Committee' at the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill orders the Department of Transportation, with Health and Mental Hygiene, to run a pilot project testing cool pavement—materials that lower street temperatures—across at least four neighborhoods facing heat and disadvantage. The official summary reads: 'A Local Law in relation to requiring the department of transportation to conduct a pilot project on the use of cool pavement.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by 29 co-sponsors. The bill requires DOT to report findings to the Mayor and Council. Safety analysts note no direct effect on vulnerable road users: the measure targets heat, not traffic violence. The city’s most dangerous streets remain unchanged.
