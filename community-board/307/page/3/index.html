      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB7?

      Third Avenue Doesn't Bleed by Accident

      Written July 13, 2025

      Brooklyn CB7: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      Blood on Third Avenue

      Friday morning, July 11th. Two men stepped into the crosswalk at 52nd Street and Third Avenue. A BMW ran the red. The car did not slow. Kex Un Chen, 80, and Faqiu Lin, 59, were hit and left in the street. Both died where they fell. The driver sped away. Police caught him later. He told them, “I had a six pack of Modelos and two drinks. I was driving the car by myself last night. It’s my fault. After I hit them, I came home.” told police.

      The men were trying to reach a food pantry. “We are deeply saddened by the tragic deaths of two individuals in Sunset Park this morning. Our thoughts are with their families and loved ones. We understand they may have been on their way to our Mobile Market simply trying to access food like so many New Yorkers in need,” said City Harvest.

      This is not rare. In the last 12 months, Brooklyn CB7 saw 2 killed, 6 seriously hurt, and over 700 injured in 1,031 crashes. In the first half of 2025, 1 person is dead, 4 are seriously hurt, and 420 are injured. The dead are mostly people on foot or bike. The old, the young, the ones with nowhere else to go.

      The Same Streets, The Same Danger

      Neighbors say drivers speed on that stretch. The city’s own data shows 59 crashes and 80 injuries in three blocks around the site in five years. reported CBS New York. The blood dries, but the danger stays.

      What Has Been Done—And What Hasn’t

      Local leaders have taken steps. Council Member Alexa Avilés co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act and a bill to ban parking near crosswalks. Senator Andrew Gounardes voted to renew speed cameras and backed the Stop Super Speeders Act. Assembly Member Marcela Mitaynes voted yes to extend school speed zones and co-sponsored a bill for speed limiters. But the citywide speed limit is still above 20 mph. Enforcement is slow. Cameras catch speeders, but repeat offenders keep driving. The laws are not enough. The dead keep coming.

      Call to Action: Make Them Hear You

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed limiters for repeat offenders. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand streets where the old and the hungry can cross and live. Do not wait for the next body in the road.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB7 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38, assembly district AD 51 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB7?
      It includes the Windsor Terrace-South Slope, Sunset Park (West), Sunset Park (Central), and Green-Wood Cemetery neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, and District 43, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 20, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB7?
      In Brooklyn CB7, SUVs and cars were involved in 212 pedestrian injuries and 3 deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 15 injuries and 1 death. Bikes were involved in 32 injuries, with no deaths. (Source: NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      They are preventable. Speeding, red light running, and poor street design are choices and policy failures, not fate. The deaths and injuries in Brooklyn CB7 are the result of decisions that can be changed.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      Local politicians can lower the speed limit to 20 mph, expand daylighting at intersections, support speed limiters for repeat offenders, and fund protected crossings and bike lanes. They can also push for stronger enforcement against reckless drivers.

      Flatbush Avenue Bus Lanes, Pedestrian Islands Planned
      ▸

      Flatbush Avenue will lose car lanes. Bus lanes and pedestrian islands will take their place. Fifty-five killed or badly hurt since 2019. Buses crawl. Pedestrians dodge traffic. The city moves to fix a deadly, clogged corridor.

      Gothamist reported on June 4, 2025, that New York City's Department of Transportation plans to overhaul Flatbush Avenue between Livingston Street and Grand Army Plaza. The redesign replaces two car lanes with 24/7 bus-only lanes and adds pedestrian islands. DOT officials said, 'almost 70,000 daily bus riders are stuck waiting too long for slow buses, drivers are caught in a mess of traffic and pedestrians are left crossing intersections clogged with vehicles.' Since 2019, 55 people have been killed or severely injured in crashes along this stretch. The plan removes curbside parking and bans cars from bus lanes, aiming to speed up twelve bus routes and protect people on foot. Most residents in the area do not own cars and depend on slow buses. The proposal reflects a shift toward prioritizing vulnerable road users and addressing systemic danger on one of Brooklyn's busiest corridors.

The bill, sponsored by Sen. Andrew Gounardes, aims to curb reckless driving. Initially, Sen. After discussion with Gounardes, Palumbo shifted, supporting the bill if it meets its goal. Palumbo now signals willingness to take strong action against reckless drivers.
