      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB7?

      Third Avenue Kills—City Stalls, Bodies Fall

      Written July 12, 2025

      Brooklyn CB7: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      The Bodies in the Crosswalk

      On July 11, two men tried to cross Third Avenue in Sunset Park. A BMW came fast. It did not stop. The car hit them. Both men died in the street. The driver ran. Police later arrested him and charged him with manslaughter and leaving the scene. The men were named Kex Un Chen and Faqiu Lin. They were walking to a food pantry. “We’re very heartbroken. He was a good person,” said Lin’s sister.

      This was not the first time. It will not be the last. In the last 12 months, 2 people died and 706 were injured in traffic crashes in Brooklyn CB7. Six were seriously hurt. The dead are mostly on foot or bike. The numbers do not stop. CBS New York reported the car “drove through a Brooklyn intersection, hitting and killing them as they crossed the street.”

      The Corridor of Broken Promises

      Third Avenue is wide. It is fast. It is deadly. The city had a plan to fix it. The plan would have narrowed lanes and added protection. The community board supported it. The city paused the plan after business owners complained. Since then, 96 crashes have injured 80 people. “It was only a matter of time before somebody else died on this corridor and here we are,” said Julio Peña.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Member Alexa Avilés co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and to legalize crossing mid-block, ending jaywalking tickets. These are steps forward. But the city has not finished the job. The mayor and DOT have the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not done it. The redesign for Third Avenue is still on hold. The dead wait for action.

      The Numbers That Do Not Lie

      Since 2022, 13 people have died and 2,243 have been injured in traffic crashes in Brooklyn CB7. Cars and trucks killed or hurt 212 people on foot. Motorcycles and mopeds, 15. Bikes, 32. The toll is steady. The pain is not spread evenly. The most vulnerable pay the price.

      Call to Action: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city finish the job. Lower the speed limit. Build the safety projects. Do not let another body hit the street while leaders wait.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB7 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38, assembly district AD 51 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB7?
      It includes the Windsor Terrace-South Slope, Sunset Park (West), Sunset Park (Central), and Green-Wood Cemetery neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 39, and District 43, Assembly Districts AD 44, AD 49, and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 20, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB7?
      Cars and Trucks: 212 killed or injured. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 15. Bikes: 32. These numbers come from city crash data as of July 2025. Source
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Crashes are not random. Streets can be made safer. Speed limits, street design, and enforcement all change the odds. Every crash is preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign deadly streets, ban parking near crosswalks, and push for enforcement that targets dangerous driving—not people walking or biking. They can finish projects that save lives instead of delaying them.

      Gounardes Champions Safety Boosting Stop Super Speeders Bill
      ▸

      Senator Palumbo, once wary, now backs the Stop Super Speeders bill. The measure targets repeat speeders with court-ordered limiters. Twenty-one percent of fatal pedestrian crashes involve such drivers. Speed limiters cut deaths by over a third. Action, not talk.

      On May 16, 2025, the New York State Senate debated the Stop Super Speeders bill (S4045/A2029), which requires speed-limiting devices for drivers with six or more speed-camera violations in a year. The bill, sponsored by Sen. Andrew Gounardes, aims to curb reckless driving. Initially, Sen. Anthony Palumbo (R-Suffolk) opposed the measure, citing concerns about government overreach and enforcement. He compared it to ignition locks for drunk drivers, which he supports. After discussion with Gounardes, Palumbo shifted, supporting the bill if it meets its goal. The bill’s summary states it targets repeat speeders with court orders and misdemeanor charges for non-compliance. Data shows 21 percent of fatal pedestrian crashes in NYC involve vehicles with six or more camera tickets in the prior year. Speed limiters have been shown to reduce traffic deaths by 37 percent. Palumbo now signals willingness to take strong action against reckless drivers.
