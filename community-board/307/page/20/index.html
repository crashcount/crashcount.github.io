      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB7?

      Brooklyn Streets Kill—City Stalls. Demand Action Now.

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 2, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB7: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In Brooklyn CB7, the street does not forgive. Since 2022, 13 people have died and 2,148 have been injured in crashes. Twenty of those injuries were serious—lives changed in a blink, families left with empty chairs and hospital bills. In the last year alone, 2 people died and 664 were hurt. The dead are not numbers. A 70-year-old woman, crossing with the signal, struck by a turning sedan on 7th Avenue. A 30-year-old man, crushed by a truck on 60th Street. A 49-year-old cyclist, thrown and killed by a pickup at 53rd and 4th. The street is a wound that never closes.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists bleed first. Trucks, sedans, SUVs—they do the killing. In the last three years, cars and trucks took 5 lives, motorcycles and mopeds 1, bikes none. The rest are left with broken bones, crushed limbs, and memories that do not heal. The numbers are steady. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They promise safer streets, lower speed limits, more cameras. But in CB7, the pace is slow. The deaths keep coming. The city says, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” The words are right. The action is not enough. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not done it here. Cameras catch speeders, but the law that keeps them running is always at risk. Every delay is another risk, another family waiting for a call that never should come.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand more cameras, more street redesigns, more action. Do not wait for another obituary.

      The street will not change itself. You have to make it happen.

      Take action now.

      Gounardes Opposes BQE Expansion Doubling Down On Past
      ▸

All concepts include a buffered or protected bike lane on Atlantic Avenue, but the impact on vulnerable road users remains uncertain.

      Gounardes Criticizes Public Costs of Oversized Vehicles
      ▸

      SUVs kill. Their bulk crushes bodies and streets. Lawmakers want heavier vehicles to pay more. Revenue would fund safer roads. The bill follows a grim rise in deaths, especially among children. The city bleeds. The council moves. The fight is on.

      Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani and Senator Andrew Gounardes introduced a bill to increase registration fees for heavy vehicles and SUVs in New York. The proposal, announced June 23, 2023, aims to direct new revenue toward street safety projects. The report behind the bill states: 'Injuries from crashes involving large vehicles increased by 91 percent and fatalities by 75 percent between 2016 and 2019.' Mamdani said, 'This is an initiative to make our streets safer for our children.' Gounardes called out the public cost of 'mini-tanks.' The bill responds to data showing nearly half of children killed on city streets were struck by drivers of large vehicles, rising to 80 percent in 2022. Lawmakers call this common-sense action to address the deadly toll of oversized cars.

      Avilés co-sponsors bill for EV charging info app, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to create a city app showing electric vehicle charging stations. The app would map locations, show voltage, connector types, and real-time status. It would include e-bike charging info. Strict privacy rules would limit data collection and retention.

      Bill Int 1063-2023 was introduced on June 8, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill called for the Department of Information Technology and Telecommunications to build a mobile app displaying all public electric vehicle charging stations in New York City. The app would show locations, voltage, charging level, connector types, e-bike compatibility, and real-time availability. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the creation of a mobile application that provides information about electric vehicle charging stations.' Council Member Keith Powers sponsored the bill, joined by Avilés, Cabán, Farías, Riley, Richardson Jordan, Ossé, Ung, Hanif, Restler, and Gutiérrez. The bill set strict privacy: no device data, no tracking, no forced consent. The bill was filed at session’s end, not enacted.

      Gounardes Supports Safety Boosting Sammy’s Law for Lower Speeds
      ▸

      Sammy’s Law would let New York City drop speed limits to 20 mph on deadly streets. Council Member Gutierrez led the charge. Brooks-Powers joined. Streets scarred by crashes and deaths. Lower speeds mean fewer bodies broken. The city waits. Lives hang in the balance.

      Sammy’s Law, debated by the NYC Council and State Legislature, seeks home rule for New York City to lower speed limits from 25 to 20 mph on dangerous streets. Council Member Jen Gutierrez carried the resolution; Selvena Brooks-Powers later signed on. The bill targets corridors like Bushwick Avenue, Laurelton Parkway, and East Gun Hill Road—sites of hundreds of crashes and dozens killed or maimed since 2019. The matter summary states: 'Because people are dying and being maimed on New York's most-dangerous 25-mile-per-hour streets.' Supporters, including State Sen. Andrew Gounardes, cite the science: lower speeds mean less force, fewer deaths. Research shows 20 mph zones cut crashes by 60 percent. The bill does not mandate, but allows, lower limits where carnage is highest. The council’s action centers the lives of pedestrians and cyclists, demanding the city act before more are lost.
