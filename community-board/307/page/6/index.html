      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB7?

      Brooklyn Streets Kill—City Stalls. Demand Action Now.

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 7, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB7: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In Brooklyn CB7, the street does not forgive. Since 2022, 13 people have died and 2,148 have been injured in crashes. Twenty of those injuries were serious—lives changed in a blink, families left with empty chairs and hospital bills. In the last year alone, 2 people died and 664 were hurt. The dead are not numbers. A 70-year-old woman, crossing with the signal, struck by a turning sedan on 7th Avenue. A 30-year-old man, crushed by a truck on 60th Street. A 49-year-old cyclist, thrown and killed by a pickup at 53rd and 4th. The street is a wound that never closes.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists bleed first. Trucks, sedans, SUVs—they do the killing. In the last three years, cars and trucks took 5 lives, motorcycles and mopeds 1, bikes none. The rest are left with broken bones, crushed limbs, and memories that do not heal. The numbers are steady. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They promise safer streets, lower speed limits, more cameras. But in CB7, the pace is slow. The deaths keep coming. The city says, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” The words are right. The action is not enough. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not done it here. Cameras catch speeders, but the law that keeps them running is always at risk. Every delay is another risk, another family waiting for a call that never should come.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand more cameras, more street redesigns, more action. Do not wait for another obituary.

      The street will not change itself. You have to make it happen.

      Take action now.

      On February 19, 2025, the Trump administration withdrew federal approval for New York City's congestion pricing program. State Senators Andrew Gounardes and Zellnor Myrie condemned the action, warning, 'Trump will be directly responsible for more traffic, more crashes, more polluted air, slower buses and less funding for our transit system.' Local officials and advocates say ending congestion pricing will harm vulnerable road users and worsen street danger.

      Assembly Bill A 5440, introduced on February 14, 2025, is now in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits with the New York State Assembly, awaiting committee action. Primary sponsor Steven Raga (District 30) leads, joined by co-sponsors Rebecca Seawright, Tony Simone, Yudelka Tapia, Karines Reyes, and Marcela Mitaynes.       S 3387
      Gounardes sponsors bill mandating complete street design, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 3387 orders complete street design for all DOT projects using state or federal funds. The law aims to force safer roads for walkers, cyclists, and riders. Sponsors demand the state stop building deadly streets.

      Senate bill S 3387 was introduced on January 27, 2025, and is now in the sponsorship stage. The bill, titled 'Relates to requiring inclusion of complete street design for state and local transportation projects subject to oversight by the department of transportation,' requires that all DOT projects—state or local, with federal or state funding—must include complete street design. Senator Andrew Gounardes leads as primary sponsor, joined by Patricia Fahy, Brad Hoylman-Sigal, and Liz Krueger as co-sponsors. The measure targets every project under DOT oversight. The bill’s summary is blunt: 'Requires inclusion of complete street design for state and local transportation projects undertaken by DOT or which receive both federal and state funding and are subject to DOT oversight.' No safety analyst note was provided, but the bill’s intent is clear—force safer, more inclusive streets for all.
