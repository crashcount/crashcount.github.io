      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB9?

      Queens Streets: Blood on Their Hands Until Speeds Drop

      Written June 19, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Queens CB9: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Two people dead. Over 550 injured. Three left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. That is the toll of traffic violence in Queens Community Board 9 in the last year alone, according to NYC Open Data. The names are not here. But the numbers are. They do not flinch.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      A man in his 50s lies in a hospital bed, fighting for his life after a black SUV struck him and kept going. It happened just before midnight on 101st Avenue in Ozone Park. The driver did not stop. The man was found unresponsive on the road. He is not a statistic to his family. But to the city, he is one more number in a long line, as reported by ABC7.

      On Atlantic Avenue, a van driver lost control and killed Zhihong Shi, 31, as he exited a double-parked car. Two others were hurt. The van driver had a medical episode. The street did not forgive the mistake. “Shi and the other victims, ages 67 and 48, were all rushed to Jamaica Hospital, but Shi could not be saved.”

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      Local leaders have tools. They have power. They have words. Mayor Adams calls traffic violence “the serious crime that it is” and says, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” Speed cameras now run 24/7. The city can set speed limits lower, thanks to Sammy’s Law. But the pace is slow. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program has lapsed. Cameras need reauthorization. The city can lower speeds to 20 mph but has not done so citywide.

      What Comes Next

      Every day of delay is another risk. The city can act. The council can act. Albany can act. But the dead cannot wait. The injured cannot wait. The families cannot wait.

      Call to Action

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Schulman co-sponsors ban on low bass sound permits, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council bill targets deep bass. No more permits for sound devices thumping below 40 hertz. Police commissioner loses power to approve these. Schulman and Powers push the change. Action sits with Public Safety Committee. No direct impact on street safety for walkers or riders.

      Int 0094-2024, introduced February 8, 2024, sits with the Committee on Public Safety. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting the issuance of permits for the use of sound apparatuses to produce low bass sounds,' would stop the police commissioner from granting permits for devices that emit bass at 40 hertz or below for 10 seconds or more. Council Members Keith Powers (primary sponsor, District 4) and Lynn C. Schulman (co-sponsor, District 29) lead the effort. The bill was referred to committee on introduction. The measure amends existing sound permit rules but does not address traffic violence or the safety of pedestrians and cyclists. No safety analyst has found a direct effect on vulnerable road users.

      Int 0781-2022
      Ariola votes no on vehicle stop reporting bill with neutral safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing NYPD to spell out why they stop cars. Each stop now gets logged with the reason, time, and outcome. No names. Just facts. The city posts these reports online. A move for sunlight. No more secrets.

      Int 0781-2022, now law, came through the Committee on Public Safety. The Council passed it on January 20, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the police department to report on the justification for the stop in vehicle encounter reports," demands the NYPD document every vehicle stop. Reports must include the reason for the stop, the level of offense, and demographic details, but no personal identifiers. Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Lincoln Restler (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, and others led the charge. The law aims to expose patterns in police stops and holds officers to account. Reports go online, open to all. This is a step toward transparency, not just for drivers, but for every New Yorker who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.

      Int 0417-2022
      Ariola votes no on notice requirements bill, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to streamline how the city notifies communities about big street changes. Bike lanes now follow the same rules as other major projects. The city can act faster after feedback. The old bike lane notice law is gone.

      Int 0417-2022, now enacted, amends the city code to create a single, uniform process for notifying council members and community boards about major transportation projects—including bike lanes. This change aims to cut red tape and speed up street redesigns that affect vulnerable road users.

      Int 0708-2022
      Ariola votes yes to redesign truck routes, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to overhaul city truck routes. The bill orders daylighting at intersections and new signage. It aims to cut crashes, boost visibility, and shrink truck miles. Sponsors demand safer streets for people on foot and bike. Change is now law.

      Int 0708-2022, now Local Law 171 of 2023, was enacted by the City Council on December 16, 2023, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The law, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to redesigning the city truck route network," requires the Department of Transportation to redesign truck routes to "improve safety, increase visibility, reduce traffic congestion, and reduce vehicle miles traveled." Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by dozens of co-sponsors. The law mandates daylighting—removing parking near intersections—to clear sight lines for drivers and protect people crossing. DOT must consult with safety groups, residents, and businesses, and replace truck route signage for better clarity. The law sets deadlines: initial report by November 2023, implementation by June 2024, and new signage by July 2024. This overhaul targets the deadly risk trucks pose to vulnerable road users at city intersections.
