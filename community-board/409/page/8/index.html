      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB9?

      Queens Streets: Blood on Their Hands Until Speeds Drop

      Written June 19, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Queens CB9: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Two people dead. Over 550 injured. Three left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. That is the toll of traffic violence in Queens Community Board 9 in the last year alone, according to NYC Open Data. The names are not here. But the numbers are. They do not flinch.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      A man in his 50s lies in a hospital bed, fighting for his life after a black SUV struck him and kept going. It happened just before midnight on 101st Avenue in Ozone Park. The driver did not stop. The man was found unresponsive on the road. He is not a statistic to his family. But to the city, he is one more number in a long line, as reported by ABC7.

      On Atlantic Avenue, a van driver lost control and killed Zhihong Shi, 31, as he exited a double-parked car. Two others were hurt. The van driver had a medical episode. The street did not forgive the mistake. “Shi and the other victims, ages 67 and 48, were all rushed to Jamaica Hospital, but Shi could not be saved.”

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      Local leaders have tools. They have power. They have words. Mayor Adams calls traffic violence “the serious crime that it is” and says, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” Speed cameras now run 24/7. The city can set speed limits lower, thanks to Sammy’s Law. But the pace is slow. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program has lapsed. Cameras need reauthorization. The city can lower speeds to 20 mph but has not done so citywide.

      What Comes Next

      Every day of delay is another risk. The city can act. The council can act. Albany can act. But the dead cannot wait. The injured cannot wait. The families cannot wait.

      Call to Action

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Schulman Calls for Accountability in Delivery App Safety Compliance
      ▸

      Council grilled delivery apps on June 21. Seven bills target reckless e-bike riding, battery fires, and low pay. Lawmakers want apps to answer for worker traffic violations and vehicle safety. Pedestrians and cyclists face daily danger. Councilmember Schulman called for accountability.

      On June 21, 2024, the NYC Council's Committee on Consumer and Worker Protection held a hearing on seven bills regulating delivery apps. The bills aim to address 'myriad health and safety concerns' from e-bike crashes, battery fires, and low pay. Councilmember Lynn Schulman, District 29, spoke out: 'Right now, delivery workers are essentially incentivized for speed... Other bills target pay transparency and tip protections. Councilmembers Shaun Abreu and Rafael Salamanca also spoke in support. The measures seek to hold companies accountable for dangers faced by vulnerable road users and delivery workers alike.

      Weprin Considers Payroll Mobility Tax for MTA Funding
      ▸

      Governor Hochul scrapped congestion pricing. The MTA lost a billion a year. Lawmakers scrambled. Senator Liz Krueger rejected a payroll tax hike. The MTA faces a funding cliff. Subway upgrades, elevators, and clean buses now hang in the balance.

      On June 6, 2024, Governor Hochul’s sudden reversal on Manhattan congestion pricing sparked chaos in Albany. The plan, set to start June 30, would have charged drivers $15 to enter below 60th Street, raising $1 billion yearly for transit. Hochul proposed a payroll tax hike instead, but State Senator Liz Krueger opposed it, saying, "I do not think we have an appetite for that in the Senate." The bill’s fate is uncertain as the legislative session ends. The MTA board, city officials, and business groups all voiced concern. Projects like the Second Avenue Subway, subway accessibility, and zero-emission buses now face cancellation. No alternative funding plan exists. Vulnerable riders—those who rely on transit—stand to lose the most.

      Int 0928-2024
      Schulman co-sponsors cool pavement pilot bill with no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council wants DOT to test cool pavement in four heat-vulnerable, disadvantaged neighborhoods. The pilot aims to fight the city’s heat island effect. The bill sits in committee. No direct impact on pedestrian or cyclist safety. Streets stay hot. Danger remains.

      Int 0928-2024, introduced May 23, 2024, sits 'Laid Over in Committee' at the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill orders the Department of Transportation, with Health and Mental Hygiene, to run a pilot project testing cool pavement—materials that lower street temperatures—across at least four neighborhoods facing heat and disadvantage. The official summary reads: 'A Local Law in relation to requiring the department of transportation to conduct a pilot project on the use of cool pavement.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by 29 co-sponsors. The bill requires DOT to report findings to the Mayor and Council. Safety analysts note no direct effect on vulnerable road users: the measure targets heat, not traffic violence. The city’s most dangerous streets remain unchanged.
