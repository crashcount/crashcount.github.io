      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB9?

      Queens Streets: Blood on Their Hands Until Speeds Drop

      Written June 19, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Queens CB9: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 19, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Two people dead. Over 550 injured. Three left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. That is the toll of traffic violence in Queens Community Board 9 in the last year alone, according to NYC Open Data. The names are not here. But the numbers are. They do not flinch.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      A man in his 50s lies in a hospital bed, fighting for his life after a black SUV struck him and kept going. It happened just before midnight on 101st Avenue in Ozone Park. The driver did not stop. The man was found unresponsive on the road. He is not a statistic to his family. But to the city, he is one more number in a long line, as reported by ABC7.

      On Atlantic Avenue, a van driver lost control and killed Zhihong Shi, 31, as he exited a double-parked car. Two others were hurt. The van driver had a medical episode. The street did not forgive the mistake. “Shi and the other victims, ages 67 and 48, were all rushed to Jamaica Hospital, but Shi could not be saved.”

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      Local leaders have tools. They have power. They have words. Mayor Adams calls traffic violence “the serious crime that it is” and says, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” Speed cameras now run 24/7. The city can set speed limits lower, thanks to Sammy’s Law. But the pace is slow. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program has lapsed. Cameras need reauthorization. The city can lower speeds to 20 mph but has not done so citywide.

      What Comes Next

      Every day of delay is another risk. The city can act. The council can act. Albany can act. But the dead cannot wait. The injured cannot wait. The families cannot wait.

      Call to Action

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Int 0329-2022
      Ariola co-sponsors hit-and-run reward bill with no overall safety impact.
      ▸

      Council tried to pay tipsters for catching hit-and-run drivers who maim or kill. The bill died. No reward. No justice for victims. Streets stay dangerous. Drivers flee. Pedestrians and cyclists pay the price. Lawmakers failed to act.

      Int 0329-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Public Safety on May 5, 2022. The bill aimed to amend the city code to 'establish a reward for individuals who provide information leading to the apprehension, prosecution or conviction of a person who seriously injures or kills another individual in a hit-and-run accident.' Council Member Rita C. Joseph sponsored the bill, joined by sixteen co-sponsors including Brooks-Powers, Narcisse, Vernikov, and others. The bill would have authorized up to $1,000 for information leading to the capture of hit-and-run drivers, but excluded law enforcement and city employees from eligibility. The measure was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023. With the bill's failure, the city offers no extra incentive to help catch drivers who leave victims bleeding in the street.

      Schulman co-sponsors bill raising fines for excessive vehicle noise.
      ▸

The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to increasing certain penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles.' Council Member Sandra Ung sponsored the bill, joined by 26 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. There is no evidence the bill would have improved safety for vulnerable road users. The city’s noise problem, and its threat to livable streets, remains unresolved.

      Int 0253-2022
      Ariola co-sponsors school bus parking ban, boosting street safety and visibility.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban school buses from parking on city streets overnight and weekends. The bill died in committee. Buses still line curbs after dark. Streets remain cluttered. Danger for walkers and riders lingers. No change. Status quo wins.

      Int 0253-2022 was introduced in the New York City Council on April 28, 2022. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting school bus parking on city streets overnight and on weekends,' aimed to stop school buses from parking on city streets between 5:00 p.m. and 5:00 a.m. on weekdays and all weekend. The measure was referred to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Sponsors included Justin L. Brannan (primary), Robert F. Holden, Joann Ariola, Sandy Nurse, and Marjorie Velázquez. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, without a vote or enactment. School buses continue to occupy curb space overnight, leaving visibility and access issues for pedestrians and cyclists unresolved.

      Schulman co-sponsors bill creating active transportation office, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Res 0108-2022
      Schulman co-sponsors Atlantic Ticket expansion, boosting transit use and street safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to expand and lock in the Atlantic Ticket. They want Far Rockaway and more LIRR stations in the fare program. The move aims to cut commute times and boost transit use. The bill is filed, not enacted.

      Res 0108-2022 was introduced on April 14, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution urges the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to 'expand and make permanent the Atlantic Ticket pilot in order to include Far Rockaway, and other stations along the LIRR corridor, in the fare affordability program.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Yeger, Stevens, Hanif, Bottcher, Krishnan, and many others. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. The Atlantic Ticket pilot, launched in 2018, gave Southeast Queens and Brooklyn riders cheaper LIRR fares. Advocates say expanding it citywide could mean faster, safer commutes and more transit riders. The council’s action highlights the need for affordable, accessible transit—especially for neighborhoods with long, punishing commutes.

      Res 0110-2022
      Schulman co-sponsors resolution boosting subway service, improving overall transit safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to run more peak-hour trains from distant subway terminals. Riders in transit deserts face long waits, missed trains, and crowded platforms. The resolution highlights gaps in service that strand working New Yorkers far from Manhattan.

      Res 0110-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution, introduced April 14, 2022, and filed at session's end on December 31, 2023, urges the MTA to 'adjust schedules for distant subway terminal lines to include more peak-direction rush hour trains for commuters to travel to central economic hubs.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Bottcher, and others. The bill responds to service gaps hitting outer boroughs, end-of-line stations, and transit deserts—areas where 600,000 New Yorkers live with poor transit access. The council points to pandemic-era staffing shortages and train cancellations that left riders stranded. The measure aims to push the MTA to close these gaps and connect more New Yorkers to jobs and opportunity.
