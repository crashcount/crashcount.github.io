      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB9?

      Queens Streets: Blood on Their Hands Until Speeds Drop

      Written June 19, 2025 (Revised June 29, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Queens CB9: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 19, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Two people dead. Over 550 injured. Three left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. That is the toll of traffic violence in Queens Community Board 9 in the last year alone, according to NYC Open Data. The names are not here. But the numbers are. They do not flinch.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      A man in his 50s lies in a hospital bed, fighting for his life after a black SUV struck him and kept going. It happened just before midnight on 101st Avenue in Ozone Park. The driver did not stop. The man was found unresponsive on the road. He is not a statistic to his family. But to the city, he is one more number in a long line, as reported by ABC7.

      On Atlantic Avenue, a van driver lost control and killed Zhihong Shi, 31, as he exited a double-parked car. Two others were hurt. The van driver had a medical episode. The street did not forgive the mistake. “Shi and the other victims, ages 67 and 48, were all rushed to Jamaica Hospital, but Shi could not be saved.”

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      Local leaders have tools. They have power. They have words. Mayor Adams calls traffic violence “the serious crime that it is” and says, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” Speed cameras now run 24/7. The city can set speed limits lower, thanks to Sammy’s Law. But the pace is slow. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program has lapsed. Cameras need reauthorization. The city can lower speeds to 20 mph but has not done so citywide.

      What Comes Next

      Every day of delay is another risk. The city can act. The council can act. Albany can act. But the dead cannot wait. The injured cannot wait. The families cannot wait.

      Call to Action

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Queens Community Board 9

      Int 0880-2024
      Ariola sponsors bill that slows or blocks key street safety improvements.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0880-2024 would force DOT to notify and present plans for any bus or bike lane changes to local boards. Boards get sixty days to respond. Sponsors want quarterly updates online. The bill sits in committee. No safety review yet.

      Int 0880-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on May 16, 2024, by Council Members Joann Ariola (District 32, primary sponsor) and Robert F. Holden (District 30, co-sponsor). The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to community notification of proposed major transportation projects,' redefines any bus lane, busway, or bike lane construction or removal as a major project, regardless of length. It requires DOT to offer presentations to affected community boards and gives those boards sixty days to comment. DOT must also post quarterly progress reports online. The bill was referred to committee and has not yet been voted on. No safety analyst has assessed its impact on vulnerable road users.

      Int 0875-2024
      Ariola sponsors bill to require step street lighting, boosting pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council wants lights on city step streets. The bill orders the DOT to brighten at least 25 stairways a year. Dark stairs mean danger. Light means fewer falls, fewer hidden threats. The push comes from a broad coalition. The goal is simple: safer steps.

      Int 0875-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced May 16, 2024, it commands the Department of Transportation to install pedestrian lighting on at least 25 step streets each year. The bill’s matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the installation of pedestrian lighting on step streets.' Prime sponsor Joann Ariola leads, joined by Council Members Hanks, Ayala, Salaam, Brooks-Powers, Banks, Restler, Brannan, Ung, Gutiérrez, Louis, Hudson, Schulman, Ossé, Krishnan, Nurse, Moya, and Marmorato. Step streets are public staircases linking streets at different heights. The bill aims to ensure these stairs are well-lit, reducing risk for walkers. Once all step streets are lit, the mandate ends. The measure targets a clear hazard: darkness on city stairs.

      Int 0857-2024
      Schulman co-sponsors bill to tow abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Abandoned cars choke sidewalks and crossings. Int 0857-2024 orders city crews to tow vehicles that block streets or hide their plates. Council pushes for swift removal—seventy-two hours after notice. Streets clear. Danger drops for those on foot and bike.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced April 18, 2024. The bill reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to towing vehicles that are an encumbrance on the street.' The bill targets vehicles that endanger pedestrians and cyclists by blocking sightlines and crossings. It aims to clear hazards fast, making streets safer for all vulnerable road users.

      ▸

      Int 0606-2024
      Ariola co-sponsors e-bike and scooter registration bill, reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council wants plates on every e-bike and scooter. The bill sits in committee. Riders face new rules. Lawmakers push for registration. Streets wait. No change yet. The city debates control. Vulnerable users watch. The system holds its breath.

      Int 0606-2024 was introduced on March 7, 2024, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered," would force all e-bikes, e-scooters, and similar vehicles not already covered by DMV rules to register with the Department of Transportation and display a visible plate. Council Member Inna Vernikov led as primary sponsor, joined by Holden, Farías, Riley, Brannan, and many others. The bill was laid over in committee on December 11, 2024. The measure aims to track these vehicles, but its impact on vulnerable road users remains unassessed. The city’s debate continues, with no law yet in force.
