      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB18?

      Brooklyn CB18: Flatlands speed, Seaview blood, and a clock that won’t stop

      Written August 25, 2025 (Revised August 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB18: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      Flatlands Avenue runs long. The sirens run longer. In Brooklyn CB18, drivers maim and kill, and the street keeps moving.

      Seaview and Rockaway: a man down in the crosswalk

      A 66-year-old man was struck and killed at Seaview Avenue and Rockaway Parkway. The SUV kept straight. The death was recorded at 7:25 p.m. The city log lists the pedestrian “crossing against signal.” The only fact that matters is he is dead. The driver is not. NYC’s crash file shows the SUV’s left front hit (CrashID 4768224).

      Avenue L and East 88th: failure to yield, a life erased

      On Avenue L at E 88th Street, early evening, a driver making a left turn hit a person in the crosswalk and killed her. The record says “failure to yield.” The victim was a pedestrian at the intersection. Time: 6:46 p.m. Date: January 10, 2025. The city file carries one sharp line: “Apparent Death.” (CrashID 4785035).

      Flatbush at Hendrickson: night strike

      Flatbush Avenue. Hendrickson Place. 3:46 a.m. A 60-year-old pedestrian died. The SUV was going straight. Another New Yorker, gone in the dark. (CrashID 4771642).

      Kings Highway burn, Avenue U crash

      A December night on Kings Highway and Avenue M. A sedan at unsafe speed. Fire. The 57-year-old driver died with the belt on. The car was “demolished.” (CrashID 4776867).

      Memorial Day weekend at Flatbush and Avenue U. A 30-year-old motorcyclist, “unsafe speed,” collided with a 2014 fire apparatus. He was ejected. He died. (CrashID 4815725).

      This district bleeds at dusk

      CB18’s toll since 2022: 12 pedestrians, 1 cyclist, 5 vehicle occupants dead. Pedestrians suffer 612 reported injuries; cyclists, 177. The heaviest hours stack up when families are out: deaths peak at 6 p.m., 7 p.m., 9–11 p.m. The 6 p.m. hour shows four deaths, the 7 p.m. and 9 p.m. hours add more. Injury counts swell after school and into the night. These are the clock’s hard edges, not opinions. District stats are drawn from the same city feed used above.

      On corridors, the names repeat: Flatlands Avenue (2 deaths, 193 injuries), Utica Avenue (1 death, 114 injuries), Avenue L (1 death, 101 injuries). These are not abstractions. These are blocks. (Open data rollups).

      Why people die here

      “Unsafe speed” appears again and again in the fatal files. In the district rollup, speed and other driver actions lumped as “other” coincide with the most deaths and the widest harm. “Failure to yield” shows in the Avenue L killing. “Disregarded traffic control” appears in another fatal. The city’s own breakdown lists pedestrians as the largest share of deaths. The small-geo analysis gives the counts.

      What the clock demands now

      • Fix the corners that maim: daylight the crosswalks at Flatlands, Utica, and Avenue L; add hardened lefts and leading pedestrian intervals at Seaview and Rockaway; close passing lanes at Kings Highway. These are standard tools the city deploys where people die.
      • Target the late hours. The curve from 6 p.m. to 11 p.m. carries the highest death load. Put enforcement and calming where the bodies fall.
      • Protect walkers first. Pedestrians take most of the deaths here. Treat their space as the priority, not the leftover.

      The law can slow the blood

      Albany passed power to drop speeds citywide. The city can set 20 mph on local streets. Slower streets spare lives. Our own site explains how to press the officials who hold the pen. See our guide to act now: Take Action.

      At the state level, senators voted in committee for a bill to force speed limiters on repeat violators. One line says why: “Relates to requiring the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices for repeated violation of maximum speed limits.” Senate file S 4045. Senator Kevin Parker voted yes in committee on June 11–12, 2025, and Senator Roxanne Persaud did too, according to the bill record. (Open States).

      Names the map won’t forget

      Flatlands Avenue. Utica Avenue. Avenue L. Seaview and Rockaway. Kings Highway and Avenue M. Avenue U and Flatbush. These are the places where the sheet goes over the face and the traffic moves on.

      What will change the next siren

      • Daylight and harden turning movements at CB18’s worst intersections: Flatlands, Utica, Avenue L.
      • Night-focused enforcement and calming from 6–11 p.m. where deaths cluster.
      • Citywide slower defaults and state speed limiters for repeat speeders.

      Lower the speed. Cut the turns. Make the night safer. If you want that done, call them. Start here: Take Action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where are the worst crash spots in Brooklyn CB18?
      Flatlands Avenue leads with 2 deaths and 193 injuries. Utica Avenue has 1 death and 114 injuries. Avenue L shows 1 death and 101 injuries. These figures come from NYC Open Data rollups for CB18.
      ▸ When are crashes most deadly here?
      Deaths cluster between 6 p.m. and 11 p.m., with a spike at 6 p.m. Night hours also show high injury counts in the district’s hourly breakdown.
      ▸ Who is most at risk in CB18?
      Pedestrians. Since 2022, 12 pedestrians have been killed and 612 injured, compared with 1 bicyclist killed and 177 injured.
      ▸ What policies could cut the toll now?
      Lower NYC’s default speed limit on local streets and pass state rules to install speed limiters on repeat violators, as advanced in Senate bill S 4045 in committee votes.

      City Targets Overweight Trucks On BQE
      ▸

      Heavy trucks pound the BQE. The city will ticket Staten Island-bound rigs. Sensors catch the violators. The road crumbles under weight it cannot bear. Warnings are over. Fines begin. The system aims to cut danger and slow decay.

      Gothamist (2025-06-21) reports that New York City will start ticketing overweight Staten Island-bound trucks on the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway. The Department of Transportation will fine violators $650 per incident, using in-road sensors to detect excess weight. Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, "Overweight trucks cause wear and tear on our roadways and we all pay the price through expensive repairs to our infrastructure." The city issued over 3,000 warnings during a 90-day grace period. Similar enforcement on Queens-bound trucks led to a 60% drop in overweight vehicles. Albany has approved expanding this automated system to more city roadways. The BQE’s aging structure faces risk from trucks exceeding its design limits, raising safety and infrastructure concerns.

      Narcisse Opposes Harmful Crackdown Targets Vulnerable Cyclists
      ▸

      Mayor Adams and Commissioner Tisch push their crackdown citywide. Police target cyclists. Riders face more stops, more tickets. Streets grow tense. Enforcement rises. Vulnerable road users bear the brunt. Safety slips further from reach.

      On June 17, 2025, Mayor Eric Adams and Police Commissioner Jessica Tisch announced the expansion of their 'quality-of-life' pilot program at a press conference. The program, which turns routine traffic violations by cyclists into criminal summonses, will go citywide by the end of August. The matter, reported by Streetsblog NYC as 'The Crackdown This Time Edition,' drew sharp criticism from advocates and riders. Council Member Mercedes Narcisse acknowledged drivers as the main threat to pedestrians but called for consideration of drivers in transit deserts. Mayor Adams promised to consult with communities before redesigning streets. Safety analysts warn: expanding this enforcement targets vulnerable road users, discourages cycling, and shifts blame from drivers and infrastructure. The move undermines mode shift and street safety, putting cyclists and pedestrians at greater risk.
