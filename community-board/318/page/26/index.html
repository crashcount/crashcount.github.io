      Seventeen Dead, Silence Reigns: Brooklyn’s Streets Demand Action

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned and deduped citations, ensured proper inline links.)

      Brooklyn CB18: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Steel

      A man steps into the crosswalk. A car turns left. The man does not make it home. In Brooklyn CB18, the story repeats. Seventeen people have died on these streets since 2022. Forty-four more have been left with serious injuries. The numbers are blunt, but the pain is sharp. In the last twelve months alone, five families lost someone forever. Over 1,200 people were hurt. NYC Open Data

      A child is struck at Avenue L and East 88th. A 66-year-old man is killed at Rockaway Parkway and Seaview Avenue. The dead do not speak. The city moves on. But the wreckage remains.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      The headlines do not stop. A motorcyclist is killed by a fire truck at Flatbush and Avenue U. The FDNY says only, “The incident is under investigation.” A Jeep chased by police crashes onto the subway tracks. A witness describes the chaos: “Next thing I know, he was into the fence. Absolutely crazy.” Each crash is a wound that does not heal.

      Cars and SUVs do most of the killing. Eleven deaths, thirteen serious injuries, ninety-five moderate injuries since 2022. Trucks and buses add more. Motorcycles, mopeds, bikes—smaller numbers, but the damage is real.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      The city talks about Vision Zero. Speed cameras. Lower limits. But the blood keeps flowing. The law now allows New York City to set safer speed limits, but the limit remains high. Cameras catch speeders, but only where they are installed. The city redesigns some intersections, but not enough. The work is slow. The silence from some leaders is louder than sirens.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand more cameras, more street redesigns, more urgency. Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The patterns are clear. Speed, street design, and enforcement shape the outcome. These deaths and injuries are preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign dangerous streets, and fund enforcement. They can act now, not after another death.
