      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB4?

      No More Bodies in the Road: Drop the Speed, Save a Life

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Manhattan CB4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      A man sits in the street at West 40th and 9th. A box truck moves south. The driver is 75. The truck does not stop. The man dies where he sits. Police say, “A man was fatally struck by a box truck in Manhattan over the weekend” reported Patch.

      A year before, a woman, age 86, crosses 8th Avenue at West 25th. An SUV turns left. She is hit and killed. The driver is unlicensed. The city records her death. The street forgets her name.

      In three years, 12 people have died in Manhattan CB4. 34 more suffered serious injuries. 1,501 were hurt. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      The Machines That Kill

      Trucks and SUVs crush. Cars maim. Bikes and mopeds injure, but they do not kill here. In the last year, trucks killed two. SUVs killed one. The city counts the bodies. The city moves on.

      The Leaders and Their Words

      The city says it is fighting. “NYPD officers will be highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action,” said Police Commissioner Edward Caban. Cameras catch speeders. Laws pass. But the deaths do not stop.

      The mayor says, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many” said the mayor. But the speed limit is still 25. The city has the power to lower it to 20. It has not. The cameras that save lives are at risk of going dark. The law to keep them on waits in Albany. The city waits. The bodies do not.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city lower the speed limit to 20 mph. Demand the cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Int 0758-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors e-bike licensing bill, likely reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council tried to force plates on e-bikes and scooters. The bill died in committee. Riders faced new rules, more scrutiny. The city’s cars kept killing. The council moved on. Vulnerable road users stayed exposed. The system failed to shift blame from drivers.

      Int 0758-2022 was introduced in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on October 12, 2022. The bill aimed to require every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter, and other legal motorized vehicle to be registered with the Department of Transportation and display a visible license plate. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered.' Council Member Inna Vernikov sponsored the bill, joined by Robert F. Holden and 29 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. No safety analyst assessed the impact on vulnerable road users. The bill’s failure left the city’s most exposed—cyclists, pedestrians, passengers—without new protections. The streets stayed dangerous. The cars kept their edge.

      Bottcher Backs Safety Boosting Stricter Apartment Sanitation Rules
      ▸

      Manhattan’s Community Board 4 wants tougher trash rules. They demand buildings keep garbage off sidewalks. Rats and trash block the way for pedestrians. The board voted 46-0. Council member Bottcher backs the push. The city’s trash piles threaten everyone on foot.

      On October 3, 2022, Manhattan Community Board 4 (CB4) passed a resolution demanding stricter sanitation rules for apartment buildings. The board, covering Chelsea and Hell’s Kitchen, voted 46-0 to send recommendations to Sanitation Commissioner Jessica Tisch and Council member Erik Bottcher. The matter calls for buildings to 'take greater responsibility for the garbage their residents create.' CB4 leaders Jeffrey LeFrancois, Christine Berthet, and Dale Corvino led the charge. Key recommendations include rodent-proof containers, waste compacting, interior trash storage, and moving trash off sidewalks into curb lane corrals. Council member Bottcher supported the effort, stating, 'The status quo isn’t working.' The Department of Sanitation echoed these goals. The resolution targets the city’s notorious sidewalk trash piles, which block and endanger pedestrians.
