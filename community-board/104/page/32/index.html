      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB4?

      No More Bodies in the Road: Drop the Speed, Save a Life

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Manhattan CB4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      A man sits in the street at West 40th and 9th. A box truck moves south. The driver is 75. The truck does not stop. The man dies where he sits. Police say, “A man was fatally struck by a box truck in Manhattan over the weekend” reported Patch.

      A year before, a woman, age 86, crosses 8th Avenue at West 25th. An SUV turns left. She is hit and killed. The driver is unlicensed. The city records her death. The street forgets her name.

      In three years, 12 people have died in Manhattan CB4. 34 more suffered serious injuries. 1,501 were hurt. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      The Machines That Kill

      Trucks and SUVs crush. Cars maim. Bikes and mopeds injure, but they do not kill here. In the last year, trucks killed two. SUVs killed one. The city counts the bodies. The city moves on.

      The Leaders and Their Words

      The city says it is fighting. “NYPD officers will be highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action,” said Police Commissioner Edward Caban. Cameras catch speeders. Laws pass. But the deaths do not stop.

      The mayor says, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many” said the mayor. But the speed limit is still 25. The city has the power to lower it to 20. It has not. The cameras that save lives are at risk of going dark. The law to keep them on waits in Albany. The city waits. The bodies do not.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city lower the speed limit to 20 mph. Demand the cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Int 0501-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill boosting civilian reporting to improve street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants near schools. Civilians can report violators. Each offense draws a $175 fine. The city pays whistleblowers a cut. The bill stalled. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0501-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on June 2, 2022. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations,' aimed to create a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 1,320 feet of a school. The penalty: $175 per violation, enforced through the Office of Administrative Trials and Hearings. Civilians, except city employees, could file complaints and receive 25% of collected fines. The Department of Transportation would support this with a phased-in reporting program and annual public reports. Council Member Carlina Rivera led as primary sponsor, joined by over two dozen co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Int 0500-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill to revoke private parking permits, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0500-2022 would kill private vehicle parking permits. All existing permits get revoked. Only disabled drivers and union contracts are spared. The bill targets abuse and privilege. It died in committee. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0500-2022 was introduced on June 2, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill sought to "prohibit the issuance of private vehicle parking permits and revoke such existing permits," with exceptions for people with disabilities and collective bargaining agreements. Council Member Christopher Marte led as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Brewer, Joseph, Nurse, Cabán, Avilés, Bottcher, Won, Ossé, and Richardson Jordan, plus the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill aimed to end a system that let private cars claim curb space, fueling illegal parking and danger for those on foot or bike. It was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. The status quo remains: permits still shield drivers, while vulnerable road users face the risk.

      Bottcher Opposes Small Clean Curbs Pilot Supports Containerization
      ▸

      The city’s $1.3-million Clean Curbs pilot puts trash in curbside bins, not on sidewalks. Advocates say it’s too small. Only two enclosures in Times Square. Just one block for residents. They want more bins, more blocks, more ambition. City defends slow rollout.

      On May 10, 2022, advocates criticized the Clean Curbs pilot, a $1.3-million initiative announced in 2020 and led by the Department of Sanitation (DSNY). The pilot aims to reduce sidewalk trash piles by testing curbside enclosures. The commercial phase launched with two, four-bin units in Times Square, funded by the Times Square Alliance. The residential phase will cover a single Manhattan block, using nine enclosures and removing six parking spaces. Advocates argue, as the matter states, 'the city's 'Clean Curbs' pilot is too small and unambitious to be scalable citywide.' Council Member Erik Bottcher, a leader on trash issues, is linked to the pilot’s district but declined comment. Local group CHEKPEDS also declined. Advocates want a broader test, more bins, and better truck equipment. DSNY spokesman Vincent Gragnani says, 'The $1.3-million Clean Curbs pilot is exactly that, a pilot that will allow us to see what works and what does not work.'

      • Advocates: The City’s ‘Clean Curbs’ Pilot is Too Small, Streetsblog NYC, Published 2022-05-10
      Bottcher Silent on Small Scale Clean Curbs Pilot
      ▸

      The city’s $1.3-million ‘Clean Curbs’ pilot puts trash bins in Times Square and one Manhattan block. Advocates say it is too small, too slow. Council Member Erik Bottcher, a leader on trash, stays silent. The pilot leaves sidewalks crowded and danger unchecked.

      On May 10, 2022, the City Council debated the ‘Clean Curbs’ pilot, a $1.3-million program aiming to reduce sidewalk trash by testing bin enclosures. The pilot, announced in 2020, launched with two enclosures in Times Square and plans for nine more on a single Manhattan block. The matter summary warns, 'the city's 'Clean Curbs' pilot is too small and unambitious to be scalable citywide.' Council Member Erik Bottcher, District 3, known for trash reform, was mentioned but declined comment. Advocates and local groups like CHEKPEDS also withheld statements. Critics argue the pilot’s limited scope cannot yield meaningful data or real change. They call for a broader, comprehensive approach to keep sidewalks clear and safe for pedestrians. The Department of Sanitation defends the pilot as a test run. For now, the city’s most vulnerable remain at risk amid crowded curbs.
