      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB4?

      No More Bodies in the Road: Drop the Speed, Save a Life

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Manhattan CB4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      A man sits in the street at West 40th and 9th. A box truck moves south. The driver is 75. The truck does not stop. The man dies where he sits. Police say, “A man was fatally struck by a box truck in Manhattan over the weekend” reported Patch.

      A year before, a woman, age 86, crosses 8th Avenue at West 25th. An SUV turns left. She is hit and killed. The driver is unlicensed. The city records her death. The street forgets her name.

      In three years, 12 people have died in Manhattan CB4. 34 more suffered serious injuries. 1,501 were hurt. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      The Machines That Kill

      Trucks and SUVs crush. Cars maim. Bikes and mopeds injure, but they do not kill here. In the last year, trucks killed two. SUVs killed one. The city counts the bodies. The city moves on.

      The Leaders and Their Words

      The city says it is fighting. “NYPD officers will be highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action,” said Police Commissioner Edward Caban. Cameras catch speeders. Laws pass. But the deaths do not stop.

      The mayor says, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many” said the mayor. But the speed limit is still 25. The city has the power to lower it to 20. It has not. The cameras that save lives are at risk of going dark. The law to keep them on waits in Albany. The city waits. The bodies do not.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city lower the speed limit to 20 mph. Demand the cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Res 0108-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors Atlantic Ticket expansion, boosting transit use and street safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to expand and lock in the Atlantic Ticket. They want Far Rockaway and more LIRR stations in the fare program. The move aims to cut commute times and boost transit use. The bill is filed, not enacted.

      Res 0108-2022 was introduced on April 14, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution urges the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to 'expand and make permanent the Atlantic Ticket pilot in order to include Far Rockaway, and other stations along the LIRR corridor, in the fare affordability program.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Yeger, Stevens, Hanif, Bottcher, Krishnan, and many others. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. The Atlantic Ticket pilot, launched in 2018, gave Southeast Queens and Brooklyn riders cheaper LIRR fares. Advocates say expanding it citywide could mean faster, safer commutes and more transit riders. The council’s action highlights the need for affordable, accessible transit—especially for neighborhoods with long, punishing commutes.

      Res 0109-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors Interborough Express study, boosting transit and street safety.
      ▸

      Council members push for more study of the Interborough Express. The plan aims to link Brooklyn and Queens by rail or bus. The resolution supports more review, not construction. No direct safety impact for pedestrians or cyclists yet. The bill is filed.

      Resolution 0109-2022, filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, urges continued exploration and feasibility evaluation of the proposed Interborough Express (IBX). Introduced April 14, 2022, and filed at session's end, the measure states: "Resolution supporting the continued exploration and feasibility evaluation of the proposed Interborough Express." Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Bottcher, Krishnan, and others. The IBX would run along a 14-mile corridor from Bay Ridge to Jackson Heights, connecting 17 subway lines and the LIRR. The resolution does not authorize construction, only further study. No safety analyst has assessed the impact on vulnerable road users. The bill's status is 'Filed (End of Session)'.

      Res 0110-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors resolution boosting subway service, improving overall transit safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to run more peak-hour trains from distant subway terminals. Riders in transit deserts face long waits, missed trains, and crowded platforms. The resolution highlights gaps in service that strand working New Yorkers far from Manhattan.

      Res 0110-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution, introduced April 14, 2022, and filed at session's end on December 31, 2023, urges the MTA to 'adjust schedules for distant subway terminal lines to include more peak-direction rush hour trains for commuters to travel to central economic hubs.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Bottcher, and others. The bill responds to service gaps hitting outer boroughs, end-of-line stations, and transit deserts—areas where 600,000 New Yorkers live with poor transit access. The council points to pandemic-era staffing shortages and train cancellations that left riders stranded. The measure aims to push the MTA to close these gaps and connect more New Yorkers to jobs and opportunity.

      Bottcher Opposes Enforcement Delays in Misguided Placard Abuse Crackdown
      ▸

      City agencies stalled on a law to curb parking placard abuse. Enforcement never began. Bureaucrats blamed COVID. Streets stayed clogged. Bike lanes, bus lanes, and sidewalks stayed blocked. Councilmember Bottcher demanded action. Advocates called the delay dangerous. The city did nothing.

      On April 11, 2022, the City Council held an oversight hearing on enforcement delays for Local Law 6. The law ordered NYPD to inspect 25 blocks weekly for placard abuse and another 25 for blocked bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and crosswalks. The Department of Investigation (DOI) was to review and report by September 30, 2020. Agencies missed deadlines, citing pandemic and staffing shortages. Councilmember Erik Bottcher, representing District 3, pressed for action, saying businesses suffered from fake placards. The hearing highlighted the matter: 'Standing still zone: Two-year-old parking placard abuse probe stuck in bureaucratic time warp, NYC investigator blames COVID.' Advocates and @placardabuse called the delays corrupt and dangerous. Danny Pearlstein of Riders Alliance said placard abuse is no victimless crime, making streets more dangerous and slowing buses. No timeline for enforcement was given.

      Int 0075-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors moving billboard ban, boosting street safety for all.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban moving billboards. The bill would have stopped cars from carrying giant ads through city streets. Sponsors wanted to codify an existing rule. The session ended. The bill died. The danger rolls on.

      Int 0075-2022 was introduced on March 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to ban moving billboards, echoing the matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to banning moving billboards.' Council Members Gale A. Brewer (primary), Justin L. Brannan, Erik D. Bottcher, Kalman Yeger, and Lincoln Restler sponsored the measure. The bill sought to codify an existing city rule (34 RCNY 4-12(j)), making it illegal to operate, stand, or park vehicles for commercial advertising. Violators would face a $500 penalty. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.
