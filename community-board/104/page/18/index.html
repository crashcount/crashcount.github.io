      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB4?

      No More Bodies in the Road: Drop the Speed, Save a Life

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Manhattan CB4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      A man sits in the street at West 40th and 9th. A box truck moves south. The driver is 75. The truck does not stop. The man dies where he sits. Police say, “A man was fatally struck by a box truck in Manhattan over the weekend” reported Patch.

      A year before, a woman, age 86, crosses 8th Avenue at West 25th. An SUV turns left. She is hit and killed. The driver is unlicensed. The city records her death. The street forgets her name.

      In three years, 12 people have died in Manhattan CB4. 34 more suffered serious injuries. 1,501 were hurt. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      The Machines That Kill

      Trucks and SUVs crush. Cars maim. Bikes and mopeds injure, but they do not kill here. In the last year, trucks killed two. SUVs killed one. The city counts the bodies. The city moves on.

      The Leaders and Their Words

      The city says it is fighting. “NYPD officers will be highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action,” said Police Commissioner Edward Caban. Cameras catch speeders. Laws pass. But the deaths do not stop.

      The mayor says, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many” said the mayor. But the speed limit is still 25. The city has the power to lower it to 20. It has not. The cameras that save lives are at risk of going dark. The law to keep them on waits in Albany. The city waits. The bodies do not.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city lower the speed limit to 20 mph. Demand the cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Simone Supports Delivery App Accountability and Worker Safety
      ▸

      Council Member Bottcher and colleagues launched ‘Slow Your Roll, Respect The Stroll’ to urge cyclists to respect pedestrians. They admit cars are the main killers. Five died in Bottcher’s district last year—three were pedestrians hit by drivers. The campaign runs citywide.

      On January 19, 2024, Council Member Erik Bottcher unveiled the ‘Slow Your Roll, Respect The Stroll’ campaign. The effort, joined by Sen. Brad Hoylman-Sigal and Assembly Member Tony Simone, aims to remind cyclists to stop at red lights and avoid sidewalks. Bottcher’s district saw 2,215 crashes last year, injuring 267 pedestrians and 218 cyclists; three of five fatalities were pedestrians killed by drivers.       Int 1011-2023
      Bottcher votes yes on fare evasion info bill, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing police to hand out flyers on reduced fare programs to anyone ticketed or arrested for subway fare evasion. The flyers list eligibility, discounts, and how to apply. The law aims to inform, not punish. It is now in effect.

      Int 1011-2023, now enacted as Local Law 30 of 2024, moved through the Committee on Public Safety and passed the City Council on January 20, 2024. The bill requires police officers patrolling subway stations to carry and distribute flyers about city-run reduced fare programs to anyone arrested or summoned for fare evasion. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to providing information on the reduced fare programs to persons issued a summons for fare evasion.' Council Member Farah N. Louis sponsored the bill, joined by Brooks-Powers, Brewer, Dinowitz, Barron, Krishnan, and others. The flyers must explain eligibility, discount details, and application steps. The law took effect 120 days after passage. No direct safety impact for vulnerable road users was noted.

      Int 0781-2022
      Bottcher votes yes on vehicle stop reporting bill, safety impact neutral.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing NYPD to spell out why they stop cars. Each stop now gets logged with the reason, time, and outcome. No names. Just facts. The city posts these reports online. A move for sunlight. No more secrets.

      Int 0781-2022, now law, came through the Committee on Public Safety. The Council passed it on January 20, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the police department to report on the justification for the stop in vehicle encounter reports," demands the NYPD document every vehicle stop. Reports must include the reason for the stop, the level of offense, and demographic details, but no personal identifiers. Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Lincoln Restler (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, and others led the charge. The law aims to expose patterns in police stops and holds officers to account. Reports go online, open to all. This is a step toward transparency, not just for drivers, but for every New Yorker who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.

      Upper West Side’s board voted 8-1 to demand crackdowns on unregistered mopeds. The resolution urges city and state to punish illegal dealers, enforce laws, and back Albany’s registration bill. Lawmakers say loopholes fuel chaos. Immigrants often misled. Streets stay dangerous.

      On December 14, 2023, Community Board 7’s transportation committee passed a resolution, 8-1, demanding action against unregistered mopeds. The board called on the Department of Motor Vehicles to impose civil penalties on unregistered dealers and urged city and state officials to enforce laws against selling non-street-legal mopeds. The board’s move aims to close loopholes, protect vulnerable road users, and address the chaos caused by unregistered mopeds.
