      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB4?

      Manhattan Bleeds While City Hall Dawdles—Demand Safety Now

      Written July 3, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 3, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      One death. Two dozen injured. Hundreds of crashes. That’s just this year in Manhattan CB4. The numbers are cold, but the pain is not. In the last twelve months, 935 crashes tore through these blocks. One person died. Eight suffered injuries so grave they may never walk the same. Four hundred fifty-three were hurt. The dead do not speak. The wounded limp on.

      Last year, an 86-year-old woman was killed crossing with the light at 8th Avenue and West 25th. The driver turned left, did not yield, and struck her head-on. She died there, in the crosswalk. The driver was unlicensed. The cause: failure to yield and inattention (NYC Open Data).

      A 23-year-old cyclist died on West 36th. A truck crushed a man on West 40th. The list runs long. Each name, a life cut short or broken.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and automate bike lane enforcement. He voted to legalize jaywalking, shifting blame away from pedestrians and toward those behind the wheel. But the pace is slow. The bill to ban parking near crosswalks—daylighting—still sits in committee. The dead wait for no one.

      Bottcher praised the new protected bike lane on Sixth Avenue, saying, “By completing the Sixth Avenue bike lane, we are not only improving the safety of cyclists but also enhancing the overall experience for pedestrians and drivers alike.” But the lanes come one by one, while crashes come every day.

      The Cost of Delay

      Every delay is a risk. Every unprotected crossing is a dare. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has the power to daylight every intersection. It has the power to build real protection for people on foot and bike. But power unused is no comfort to the grieving.

      As the city drags its feet, the toll mounts. As one FDNY chief said after a bus crash at Port Authority, “One with moderate injuries that required extrication, 10 patients with minor injuries all transported to local area hospitals.” The words are dry. The pain is not.

      Act Now—Or Count the Dead

      Call your council member. Demand daylighting at every corner. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes on every avenue. The city will not move until you push. The dead cannot wait.

      Int 1011-2023
      Bottcher votes yes on fare evasion info bill, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing police to hand out flyers on reduced fare programs to anyone ticketed or arrested for subway fare evasion. The flyers list eligibility, discounts, and how to apply. The law aims to inform, not punish. It is now in effect.

      Int 1011-2023, now enacted as Local Law 30 of 2024, moved through the Committee on Public Safety and passed the City Council on January 20, 2024. The bill requires police officers patrolling subway stations to carry and distribute flyers about city-run reduced fare programs to anyone arrested or summoned for fare evasion. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to providing information on the reduced fare programs to persons issued a summons for fare evasion.' Council Member Farah N. Louis sponsored the bill, joined by Brooks-Powers, Brewer, Dinowitz, Barron, Krishnan, and others. The flyers must explain eligibility, discount details, and application steps. The law took effect 120 days after passage. No direct safety impact for vulnerable road users was noted.

      Int 0781-2022
      Bottcher votes yes on vehicle stop reporting bill, safety impact neutral.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing NYPD to spell out why they stop cars. Each stop now gets logged with the reason, time, and outcome. No names. Just facts. The city posts these reports online. A move for sunlight. No more secrets.

      Int 0781-2022, now law, came through the Committee on Public Safety. The Council passed it on January 20, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the police department to report on the justification for the stop in vehicle encounter reports," demands the NYPD document every vehicle stop. Reports must include the reason for the stop, the level of offense, and demographic details, but no personal identifiers. Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Lincoln Restler (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, and others led the charge. The law aims to expose patterns in police stops and holds officers to account. Reports go online, open to all. This is a step toward transparency, not just for drivers, but for every New Yorker who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.

      Hoylman-Sigal Supports Safety-Boosting Moped Registration Legislation
      ▸

      Upper West Side’s board voted 8-1 to demand crackdowns on unregistered mopeds. The resolution urges city and state to punish illegal dealers, enforce laws, and back Albany’s registration bill. Lawmakers say loopholes fuel chaos. Immigrants often misled. Streets stay dangerous.

      On December 14, 2023, Community Board 7’s transportation committee passed a resolution, 8-1, demanding action against unregistered mopeds. The board called on the Department of Motor Vehicles to impose civil penalties on unregistered dealers and urged city and state officials to enforce laws against selling non-street-legal mopeds. The resolution also asks the city to consider a buy-back program for gas-powered mopeds. The matter, titled 'Upper West Side Panel Backs Resolution to Rein in Dangerous Mopeds,' supports Albany legislation requiring sellers to register mopeds with the DMV before sale. State Senator Brad Hoylman-Sigal and Assembly Member Alex Bores sponsor the bill. Bores said, 'We need to cut off the flow of these vehicles before they get onto these streets.' The board’s move aims to close loopholes, protect vulnerable road users, and address the chaos caused by unregistered mopeds.

      Int 1260-2023
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill increasing street safety by targeting obscured license plates.
      ▸

      Council tried to stop drivers from hiding plates. The bill set steep fines and jail for obscured tags. It died in committee. The streets stay the same. Scofflaws keep rolling, plates unreadable, danger unchecked.

      Int 1260-2023 was introduced on December 6, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to prohibit parking, standing, stopping, or operating a motor vehicle with obscured or defaced license plates. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting the parking, standing, stopping, or operation of a motor vehicle with obscured or defaced license plates.' Council Members Oswald Feliz (primary), Gale A. Brewer, Erik D. Bottcher, and Robert F. Holden sponsored the measure. It would have imposed fines up to $1,000 and jail time for repeat offenders. The bill was filed at session’s end, leaving enforcement unchanged. Vulnerable road users remain exposed to drivers who hide from accountability.
