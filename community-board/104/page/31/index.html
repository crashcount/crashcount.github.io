      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB4?

      No More Bodies in the Road: Drop the Speed, Save a Life

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Manhattan CB4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      A man sits in the street at West 40th and 9th. A box truck moves south. The driver is 75. The truck does not stop. The man dies where he sits. Police say, “A man was fatally struck by a box truck in Manhattan over the weekend” reported Patch.

      A year before, a woman, age 86, crosses 8th Avenue at West 25th. An SUV turns left. She is hit and killed. The driver is unlicensed. The city records her death. The street forgets her name.

      In three years, 12 people have died in Manhattan CB4. 34 more suffered serious injuries. 1,501 were hurt. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      The Machines That Kill

      Trucks and SUVs crush. Cars maim. Bikes and mopeds injure, but they do not kill here. In the last year, trucks killed two. SUVs killed one. The city counts the bodies. The city moves on.

      The Leaders and Their Words

      The city says it is fighting. “NYPD officers will be highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action,” said Police Commissioner Edward Caban. Cameras catch speeders. Laws pass. But the deaths do not stop.

      The mayor says, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many” said the mayor. But the speed limit is still 25. The city has the power to lower it to 20. It has not. The cameras that save lives are at risk of going dark. The law to keep them on waits in Albany. The city waits. The bodies do not.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city lower the speed limit to 20 mph. Demand the cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Bottcher Opposes Small Clean Curbs Pilot Supports Containerization
      ▸

      The city’s $1.3-million Clean Curbs pilot puts trash in curbside bins, not on sidewalks. Advocates say it’s too small. Only two enclosures in Times Square. Just one block for residents. They want more bins, more blocks, more ambition. City defends slow rollout.

      On May 10, 2022, advocates criticized the Clean Curbs pilot, a $1.3-million initiative announced in 2020 and led by the Department of Sanitation (DSNY). The pilot aims to reduce sidewalk trash piles by testing curbside enclosures. The commercial phase launched with two, four-bin units in Times Square, funded by the Times Square Alliance. The residential phase will cover a single Manhattan block, using nine enclosures and removing six parking spaces. Advocates argue, as the matter states, 'the city's 'Clean Curbs' pilot is too small and unambitious to be scalable citywide.' Council Member Erik Bottcher, a leader on trash issues, is linked to the pilot’s district but declined comment. Local group CHEKPEDS also declined. Advocates want a broader test, more bins, and better truck equipment. DSNY spokesman Vincent Gragnani says, 'The $1.3-million Clean Curbs pilot is exactly that, a pilot that will allow us to see what works and what does not work.'

      Bottcher Supports Safety Boosting Curbside Trash Enclosures
      ▸

      Times Square Alliance launched curbside trash bins at 43rd and Eighth. The bins clear sidewalks for heavy foot traffic. Council member Bottcher backs the Clean Curbs program. The move shifts trash from walkways to the street, freeing space for people.

      On April 19, 2022, the Times Square Alliance, one of New York’s largest business improvement districts, installed curbside trash enclosures at 43rd Street and Eighth Avenue. This pilot, part of the Clean Curbs program, is a joint effort by the Departments of Sanitation and Transportation, with support from Small Business Services and Council member Erik Bottcher. The program marks a shift in public-realm management, prioritizing pedestrian movement and sanitation in dense areas. No formal safety analysis was provided, but the move reclaims space from cars for people.
