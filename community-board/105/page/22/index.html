      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?

      Seven Dead, Still Waiting: NYC Streets Demand Action

      Written July 3, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Manhattan CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 3, 2025

      The Toll in Plain Sight

      The bodies keep coming. In the last twelve months, seven people died on the streets of Manhattan CB5. Twenty more were left with serious injuries. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about the weather, the time of day, or the dreams that ended on the pavement (NYC Open Data).

      A man, 34, was killed crossing 7th Avenue and West 36th Street. The driver was distracted. The city called it inattention. The man is still dead (NYC Open Data).

      Fourteen people were hurt when buses collided at Port Authority. The terminal closed. The city kept moving. “Fourteen people are being treated for non-life-threatening injuries,” said authorities. The ramp reopened. The pain lingers.

      Leadership: Steps and Stalls

      Local leaders have moved. Council Member Erik Bottcher and Keith Powers voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable for crossing the street. They co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and speed up protected bike lanes. These are steps. But the pace is slow. The danger is not.

      Council Member Powers called for using idle congestion pricing cameras for speed and red-light enforcement. The machines sit unused. The streets stay the same.

      “By completing the Sixth Avenue bike lane, we are not only improving the safety of cyclists but also enhancing the overall experience for pedestrians and drivers alike,” said Bottcher. The lane is open. The city is not yet safe.

      The Disaster Is Slow, But It Is Here

      Every death is preventable. The city knows what works: slower speeds, daylighted corners, protected lanes. The laws are written. The votes are cast. But the blood dries slow on the crosswalks.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand every crosswalk be clear. Demand action before the next name is carved into stone.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB5 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4, assembly district AD 75 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB5?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square and Midtown-Times Square neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, District 4, and District 6, Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?
      Cars and Trucks were responsible for 6 deaths, 287 minor injuries, 159 moderate injuries, and 13 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 0 deaths, 25 minor injuries, 19 moderate injuries, and 2 serious injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths, 65 minor injuries, 41 moderate injuries, and 7 serious injuries.[NYC Open Data]
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. The city knows what works: slower speeds, clear crosswalks, protected lanes. Every death is a failure to act.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, clear crosswalks, protected bike lanes, and automated enforcement. They can fund and prioritize street redesigns that protect people, not cars.

      Int 1011-2023
      Bottcher votes yes on fare evasion info bill, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing police to hand out flyers on reduced fare programs to anyone ticketed or arrested for subway fare evasion. The flyers list eligibility, discounts, and how to apply. The law aims to inform, not punish. It is now in effect.

      Int 1011-2023, now enacted as Local Law 30 of 2024, moved through the Committee on Public Safety and passed the City Council on January 20, 2024. The bill requires police officers patrolling subway stations to carry and distribute flyers about city-run reduced fare programs to anyone arrested or summoned for fare evasion. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to providing information on the reduced fare programs to persons issued a summons for fare evasion.' Council Member Farah N. Louis sponsored the bill, joined by Brooks-Powers, Brewer, Dinowitz, Barron, Krishnan, and others. The flyers must explain eligibility, discount details, and application steps. The law took effect 120 days after passage. No direct safety impact for vulnerable road users was noted.

      Int 0781-2022
      Bottcher votes yes on vehicle stop reporting bill, safety impact neutral.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing NYPD to spell out why they stop cars. Each stop now gets logged with the reason, time, and outcome. No names. Just facts. The city posts these reports online. A move for sunlight. No more secrets.

      Int 0781-2022, now law, came through the Committee on Public Safety. The Council passed it on January 20, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the police department to report on the justification for the stop in vehicle encounter reports," demands the NYPD document every vehicle stop. Reports must include the reason for the stop, the level of offense, and demographic details, but no personal identifiers. Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Lincoln Restler (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, and others led the charge. The law aims to expose patterns in police stops and holds officers to account. Reports go online, open to all. This is a step toward transparency, not just for drivers, but for every New Yorker who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.
