      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?

      No More Excuses: Manhattan Streets Are Killing Us

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 20, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Manhattan CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt

      A man walks to work. A woman crosses with her child. A cyclist waits for the light. In Manhattan CB5, the street does not forgive. Fourteen people have died here since 2022. Over 2,100 have been hurt. Fifty-six were left with wounds that do not heal—broken bones, broken minds, lives split in two. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie.

      Last year, a 34-year-old man was killed in the crosswalk at 7th Avenue and West 36th Street. The driver kept going straight. The pedestrian did not go home. NYC Open Data.

      Just days ago, a DOT worker was slashed by an e-bike rider after a near miss on Broadway and Cedar. The city called it an “abhorrent assault of a NYC DOT employee who performs critical work to keep our city moving”. The worker bled on the sidewalk. The rider fled. No one was arrested.

      Hazards on Every Corner

      The danger is not only in speed. It is in what we allow. A van crashed in Midtown. Police found 76 propane tanks and 75 gallons of gasoline inside. A food cart broke loose and struck a parked car with a woman and child inside. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment. Firefighters forced entry and pulled out the fuel. The air stank of gas. The city was lucky.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      Some leaders act. Assembly Member Tony Simone and State Senator Liz Krueger have both voted to extend school speed zones and back bills to curb repeat speeders. Krueger voted yes on a bill to force speed limiters on the worst drivers. But the city drags its feet. The 20 mph citywide limit is still not law.

      Council Member Keith Powers called for using idle congestion pricing cameras to catch speeders and reckless drivers, but the equipment sits unused. The streets stay the same. The bodies pile up.

      “Our thoughts are with our employee and their loved ones, and we hope they make a quick recovery. We will be coordinating with NYPD to bring the perpetrator to justice.” DOT spokesperson

      The Call

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand speed limiters for repeat offenders. Demand streets that do not kill. The next life lost could be yours, or someone you love.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB5 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4, assembly district AD 75 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB5?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square and Midtown-Times Square neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, District 4, and District 6, Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians here were caused by SUVs and Cars (7 deaths, 481 injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (2 deaths, 65 injuries), Bikes (0 deaths, 119 injuries), and Motorcycles and Mopeds (0 deaths, 46 injuries).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speeding, distraction, reckless driving, hazardous cargo. These are preventable with better laws, enforcement, and street design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce a 20 mph citywide speed limit, support speed limiters for repeat offenders, expand automated enforcement, and redesign streets to protect people walking and biking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1138-2024
      Bottcher sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill bars cars from blocking crosswalks. No standing or parking within 20 feet. City must install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections yearly. Streets clear. Sightlines open. Danger cut.

      Int 1138-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 5, 2024. The bill states: “prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.” Council Member Erik D. Bottcher leads, joined by Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and over two dozen co-sponsors. The law orders the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections each year, up from 100. The city must also run outreach and education. The bill aims to keep crosswalks clear, improve visibility, and protect people on foot and bike. No more hiding behind parked cars. The committee laid the bill over on April 21, 2025.
