      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?

      Manhattan CB5: Sixteen dead since 2022. The streets keep taking.

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Manhattan CB5 is small on a map and big in pain. Since 2022, 16 people are dead here. Eight were walking. Four were riding bikes. The rest were inside the machines. The city kept moving.

      • Source: NYC Open Data rollups for this area.

      Sixth Avenue bleeds

      A left‑turning SUV hit an 83‑year‑old woman at W 58th and Sixth. She died. It was 10:22 p.m. The record shows a licensed driver, a left turn, and a head injury. The woman had the signal, per the data field. The details are plain, final (crash 4792095).

      On West 36th and Seventh, at 11:31 p.m., a Toyota SUV going straight struck a 34‑year‑old man in the intersection. The dataset lists “Driver Inattention/Distraction.” He died there (crash 4728673).

      The area’s worst corridors are written in the logs: Avenue of the Americas leads the board with 1 death and 101 injuries. W 51st Street shows three deaths.

      Bikes, buses, bodies

      Two 67‑year‑old e‑bike riders died together at Fifth and West 51st at 3:05 p.m. The record shows head injuries and death. An SUV was parked. The bikes were going straight. There is no why in the file. Only time, place, and two lives gone (crash 4796322).

      At East 51st and Fifth, a southbound bus hit a 39‑year‑old man. The entry says “Not in Roadway.” He died. The bus shows right‑front bumper damage. A parked boom lift sits in the notes like a dumb witness (crash 4788709).

      Here, bikes take hits, again and again: 612 bicyclist injuries and 18 serious injuries since 2022. Pedestrians: 783 injuries, 32 serious, and 8 deaths. Trucks and buses leave heavier marks: 70 pedestrian injuries tied to large vehicles, with one death in this tally. The numbers are bare and heavy (NYC Open Data rollup).

      When the harm hits hardest

      Pain spikes mid‑afternoon into evening. Three deaths around 3 p.m. One at 4 p.m. Another at 5 p.m. Nights are not quiet. Deaths at 10 a.m., noon, 1 a.m., 6 a.m., 8 a.m., 9 p.m., 10 p.m., 11 p.m. The hours mark the body count (hourly distribution, NYC Open Data).

      The top listed factors read like a shrug: “other” leads with 13 deaths. “Vulnerable road user error” is tagged on two deaths. “Driver inattention/distraction” appears on injuries. “Unsafe speed” barely registers in the local coding, but speed still kills; when it shows up, people break (small‑area factors, NYC Open Data).

      Promises uptown, funerals midtown

      On Canal Street, the city moved only after two more deaths. A stolen car flew off the bridge at 109 mph and killed Kevin Cruickshank and May Kwok at Bowery and Canal. “We are taking immediate steps to fortify this intersection,” said the transportation chief (Gothamist, NY1). Advocates warned most of the corridor stays deadly (Gothamist). The pattern is the policy.

      Fourteen blocks north, in CB5, people keep dying at everyday speeds, at ordinary turns, under green lights. The file does not explain. It only counts.

      Fix the worst corners now

      Start with the killers we can name. Harden left turns at Sixth Avenue. Give longer LPIs at W 51st. Daylight corners on Avenue of the Americas and W 51st Street. Build truck‑safe turns and bus cameras where buses hit. These are standard tools. They work when installed. Install them here.

      Citywide brakes

      Albany gave New York the tool to lower speeds. The city can use it. A 20 mph default slows the blow. Fewer funerals follow. The case is simple. The ask is old. The lives are new each time. Act now (see our action guide).

      Repeat speeders keep killing. The Legislature moved a bill to fit speed limiters to the worst drivers. It passed key Senate votes with support from Sen. Krueger. The bill targets drivers with patterns of tickets and points (Open States S 4045).

      Lower speeds. Lock the throttle on the repeat offenders. Do it before the next timestamp.

      One call

      This district has 16 dead since 2022. Names reduced to rows. Files don’t mourn. People do. Make the call. Ask for slower streets and real limits that hold.

      Take one step today: demand action to slow cars and stop repeat speeders. Start here: Take Action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How many people have died in Manhattan CB5 since 2022?
      Sixteen. Eight were pedestrians, four were cyclists, and four were vehicle occupants, per NYC Open Data rollups for this area.
      ▸ Where are the worst trouble spots?
      Avenue of the Americas and W 51st Street top the list in injuries and deaths. Both appear in NYC Open Data’s top intersection records for this district.
      ▸ When do severe crashes cluster?
      Deaths are logged across the day, with multiple fatalities in the 3–5 p.m. window and several late at night, according to the hourly distribution in the dataset.
      ▸ Which vehicles hurt pedestrians most here?
      SUVs and cars account for the largest share of pedestrian injuries overall; trucks and buses also appear in serious cases, including a bus-involved death in 2025.

      Powers Urges Safety Boosting E-Bike Battery Buyback Program
      ▸

      Four dead in Chinatown. Unsafe e-bike batteries sparked the blaze. Council Member Powers leads a bill to swap out dangerous batteries. Mayor Adams hesitates, citing costs. Delivery workers face risk. The Council demands action. Lives hang in the balance.

      Intro 949, a City Council bill, seeks to buy back unsafe e-bike batteries and provide safe replacements at low or no cost. The bill, introduced by Council Member Keith Powers, has support from 33 Council members. It follows a deadly Chinatown fire on June 21, 2023, that killed four people. The bill is in committee, with Powers urging Mayor Adams and the FDNY to support it, stating, 'There is a clear consensus that a battery swap program will save lives.' Mayor Adams questioned the program's cost and called for federal, state, and Council funding. FDNY Commissioner Laura Kavanagh backed the need for safe batteries for delivery workers. The Council's push comes as battery fires rise, endangering workers and residents. The bill has broad coalition support, but City Hall has not fully committed.

      S 6808
      Krueger votes yes to create safety zones, improving street safety for all.
      ▸

      Senate passes S 6808. The bill creates first responder safety zones. It sets speed limits in these zones. Lawmakers act after crashes and close calls. The vote is strong. The danger is real. The streets demand change.

      Senate bill S 6808, titled 'Relates to establishing first responder safety zones and setting speed limits in such safety zones,' moved through committee and passed multiple Senate votes between May 2023 and March 2024. Primary sponsor John Mannion led the push, joined by Joseph P. Addabbo Jr., Iwen Chu, and Pamela Helming. The bill aims to carve out protected zones for first responders and lower speed limits in those areas. The measure passed with broad support, reflecting urgency after repeated crashes near emergency scenes. The bill’s text and votes show lawmakers responding to the deadly toll of reckless driving near first responders. Vulnerable road users—pedestrians, passengers, and responders—stand in harm’s way. The bill targets that risk.

      S 2714
      Krueger votes yes, boosting street safety and access for everyone.
      ▸

      Senate passes S 2714. Bill pushes complete street design. Aim: safer roads for all. Pedestrians, cyclists, and riders get space. Car dominance challenged. Lawmakers move to cut street carnage.

      Senate bill S 2714, titled 'Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles,' advanced through committee and passed several Senate votes, most recently on March 27, 2024. Sponsored by Timothy M. Kennedy with support from Jake Ashby, Jamaal Bailey, and others, the bill mandates street designs that protect everyone—not just drivers. The measure saw strong support but faced opposition from some senators. By requiring complete street principles, S 2714 aims to reduce danger for pedestrians, cyclists, and transit users. The bill marks a shift away from car-first planning, forcing cities to build streets for people, not just traffic.
