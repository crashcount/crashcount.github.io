      Seven Dead, Still Waiting: NYC Streets Demand Action

      Written July 3, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 3, 2025

      The Toll in Plain Sight

      The bodies keep coming. In the last twelve months, seven people died on the streets of Manhattan CB5. Twenty more were left with serious injuries. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about the weather, the time of day, or the dreams that ended on the pavement (NYC Open Data).

      A man, 34, was killed crossing 7th Avenue and West 36th Street. The driver was distracted. The city called it inattention. The man is still dead (NYC Open Data).

      Fourteen people were hurt when buses collided at Port Authority. The terminal closed. The city kept moving. “Fourteen people are being treated for non-life-threatening injuries,” said authorities. The ramp reopened. The pain lingers.

      Leadership: Steps and Stalls

      Local leaders have moved. Council Member Erik Bottcher and Keith Powers voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable for crossing the street. They co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and speed up protected bike lanes. These are steps. But the pace is slow. The danger is not.

      Council Member Powers called for using idle congestion pricing cameras for speed and red-light enforcement. The machines sit unused. The streets stay the same.

      “By completing the Sixth Avenue bike lane, we are not only improving the safety of cyclists but also enhancing the overall experience for pedestrians and drivers alike,” said Bottcher. The lane is open. The city is not yet safe.

      The Disaster Is Slow, But It Is Here

      Every death is preventable. The city knows what works: slower speeds, daylighted corners, protected lanes. The laws are written. The votes are cast. But the blood dries slow on the crosswalks.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand every crosswalk be clear. Demand action before the next name is carved into stone.

      Krueger Supports State Funding to Avoid Harmful Fare Hikes
      ▸

      Mayor Adams told Albany: the city pays enough for the MTA. State lawmakers pushed back. Hochul wants the city to cover more. The fight over who funds transit leaves riders in limbo. No new money means service cuts or fare hikes loom.

      On February 16, 2023, Mayor Eric Adams traveled to Albany to oppose Governor Hochul’s proposal for New York City to contribute an extra $500 million annually to the MTA. Adams argued, “MTA is a statewide obligation. We’re doing our share.” State lawmakers, including Assembly Member Ed Braunstein and State Senator Liz Krueger, countered that the city should help. Hochul’s budget would shift more costs—like Access-A-Ride and student Metrocards—to the city. Progressive lawmakers suggested taxing the wealthy and freezing fares. Senate Majority Leader Andrea Stewart-Cousins warned, “To increase fares would be detrimental.” The debate continues as the legislature drafts its own budget. No direct safety analysis was provided, but funding gaps threaten transit service, putting vulnerable riders at risk.

      A 602
      Krueger votes yes in committee, boosting funding for safer street designs.
      ▸

      Assembly and Senate passed A 602. The bill sets state funding rules for federally assisted and municipal complete street projects. Lawmakers moved fast. Streets shaped by budgets, not safety.

      Bill A 602, titled 'Relates to the percentage responsibility of the state for federally assisted projects,' passed committee votes in the Assembly on January 24, 2023, and in the Senate on February 13, 2023. Sponsored by Patricia Fahy, the bill addresses how much the state pays for federally assisted projects and for municipal projects with complete street designs.
