      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?

      Seven Dead, Still Waiting: NYC Streets Demand Action

      Written July 3, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 3, 2025

      The Toll in Plain Sight

      The bodies keep coming. In the last twelve months, seven people died on the streets of Manhattan CB5. Twenty more were left with serious injuries. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about the weather, the time of day, or the dreams that ended on the pavement (NYC Open Data).

      A man, 34, was killed crossing 7th Avenue and West 36th Street. The driver was distracted. The city called it inattention. The man is still dead (NYC Open Data).

      Fourteen people were hurt when buses collided at Port Authority. The terminal closed. The city kept moving. “Fourteen people are being treated for non-life-threatening injuries,” said authorities. The ramp reopened. The pain lingers.

      Leadership: Steps and Stalls

      Local leaders have moved. Council Member Erik Bottcher and Keith Powers voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable for crossing the street. They co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and speed up protected bike lanes. These are steps. But the pace is slow. The danger is not.

      Council Member Powers called for using idle congestion pricing cameras for speed and red-light enforcement. The machines sit unused. The streets stay the same.

      “By completing the Sixth Avenue bike lane, we are not only improving the safety of cyclists but also enhancing the overall experience for pedestrians and drivers alike,” said Bottcher. The lane is open. The city is not yet safe.

      The Disaster Is Slow, But It Is Here

      Every death is preventable. The city knows what works: slower speeds, daylighted corners, protected lanes. The laws are written. The votes are cast. But the blood dries slow on the crosswalks.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand every crosswalk be clear. Demand action before the next name is carved into stone.

      Int 0075-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors moving billboard ban, boosting street safety for all.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban moving billboards. The bill would have stopped cars from carrying giant ads through city streets. Sponsors wanted to codify an existing rule. The session ended. The bill died. The danger rolls on.

      Int 0075-2022 was introduced on March 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to ban moving billboards, echoing the matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to banning moving billboards.' Council Members Gale A. Brewer (primary), Justin L. Brannan, Erik D. Bottcher, Kalman Yeger, and Lincoln Restler sponsored the measure. The bill sought to codify an existing city rule (34 RCNY 4-12(j)), making it illegal to operate, stand, or park vehicles for commercial advertising. Violators would face a $500 penalty. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Int 0088-2022
      Powers sponsors bill requiring package delivery report, no direct safety impact.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force city agencies to count package deliveries and their harm. The law called for annual reports on delivery volume, traffic, and air quality. It demanded a plan to cut the damage. The bill died at session’s end.

      Int 0088-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on March 10, 2022. The bill required the Department of Transportation, with other agencies, to file annual reports on package delivery volumes, traffic, and environmental impacts. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring a report on package deliveries.' Council Member Keith Powers sponsored the bill, joined by Selvena N. Brooks-Powers, Gale A. Brewer, Tiffany Cabán, and Public Advocate Jumaane Williams. The bill demanded a plan to reduce the harm caused by delivery vehicles. It was filed without passage on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided.
