      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?

      Blood on the Asphalt: City Hall Stalls, Pedestrians Die

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 18, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      Seven dead. Twenty-two seriously hurt. In the past year, Manhattan CB5 has seen 1,200 crashes. The numbers are blunt. The pain is not. A 34-year-old man was killed crossing 7th Avenue and West 36th Street. The driver was distracted. The man is gone. The street is the same. NYC crash data

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the worst of it. In the last twelve months, three people over 65 died. Two more over 75. One was crossing with the light. One was not in the road. The cars kept moving.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      City leaders talk about safety. They pass laws. They hold meetings. But the streets do not change fast enough. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not used it. Cameras catch speeders, but Albany lets the law expire and then debates bringing it back. Each delay is a risk. Each risk is a life.

      On 34th Street, the city wants a busway. Some cheer. Some worry about cars clogging side streets. “It’s time to prioritize pedestrians and transit users, and not the minority of residents who use their personal cars to get around,” said Charles Todd. Others call for more studies. More waiting. “He argued that even if cars largely ‘disappear’ from 34th Street, they could then ‘impact’ the surrounding neighborhood, rather than ‘bus riders or bike riders.’ He called for a ’traffic study’ and an ‘action plan.’” said Richard Hart.

      The Blunt Edge of Policy

      SUVs and cars killed six. Trucks and buses killed two. Bikes and mopeds hurt dozens, but they did not kill. The city redesigns intersections. It adds bike lanes. Still, the deaths keep coming. The most vulnerable—pedestrians, the old, the young—pay the price for every delay, every watered-down law, every inch given back to traffic.

      Act: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras that never go dark. Demand streets for people, not just for cars.

      Every day of delay is another name lost. Do not wait for the next siren. Take action now.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Manhattan Community Board 5

      Res 0109-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors Interborough Express study, boosting transit and street safety.
      ▸

      Council members push for more study of the Interborough Express. The plan aims to link Brooklyn and Queens by rail or bus. The resolution supports more review, not construction. No direct safety impact for pedestrians or cyclists yet. The bill is filed.

      Resolution 0109-2022, filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, urges continued exploration and feasibility evaluation of the proposed Interborough Express (IBX). Introduced April 14, 2022, and filed at session's end, the measure states: "Resolution supporting the continued exploration and feasibility evaluation of the proposed Interborough Express." Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Bottcher, Krishnan, and others. The IBX would run along a 14-mile corridor from Bay Ridge to Jackson Heights, connecting 17 subway lines and the LIRR. The resolution does not authorize construction, only further study. No safety analyst has assessed the impact on vulnerable road users. The bill's status is 'Filed (End of Session)'.

      Res 0110-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors resolution boosting subway service, improving overall transit safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to run more peak-hour trains from distant subway terminals. Riders in transit deserts face long waits, missed trains, and crowded platforms. The resolution highlights gaps in service that strand working New Yorkers far from Manhattan.

      Res 0110-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution, introduced April 14, 2022, and filed at session's end on December 31, 2023, urges the MTA to 'adjust schedules for distant subway terminal lines to include more peak-direction rush hour trains for commuters to travel to central economic hubs.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Bottcher, and others. The bill responds to service gaps hitting outer boroughs, end-of-line stations, and transit deserts—areas where 600,000 New Yorkers live with poor transit access. The council points to pandemic-era staffing shortages and train cancellations that left riders stranded. The measure aims to push the MTA to close these gaps and connect more New Yorkers to jobs and opportunity.

      Bottcher Highlights Harmful Placard Abuse Enforcement Failures
      ▸

      City investigators stall on placard abuse. Council members press for action. Streets stay blocked. Curb access lost. Police and city workers park with impunity. Laws ignored. Promises broken. Vulnerable New Yorkers pay the price.

      On April 12, 2022, the City Council held an oversight hearing on placard abuse and enforcement. The new Department of Investigation (DOI) boss, Jocelyn Strauber, hesitated to confirm if placard abuse is a problem, despite a 2019 law requiring DOI and NYPD to investigate and report on the issue. Council Member Erik Bottcher pressed Strauber, citing resident complaints about fake placards and blocked curbs. Bottcher said, 'NYPD traffic enforcement agents are not issuing tickets to many people using counterfeit placards.' Council Member Lincoln Restler criticized the lack of a digital placard system and is drafting new enforcement legislation. Local Law 6 mandates weekly sweeps and monthly reports, but agencies have failed to comply. The Adams administration has not advanced citizen enforcement. The result: curb space remains dangerous and inaccessible for pedestrians, cyclists, and those who need it most.

      Bottcher Opposes Enforcement Delays in Misguided Placard Abuse Crackdown
      ▸

      City agencies stalled on a law to curb parking placard abuse. Enforcement never began. Bureaucrats blamed COVID. Streets stayed clogged. Bike lanes, bus lanes, and sidewalks stayed blocked. Councilmember Bottcher demanded action. Advocates called the delay dangerous. The city did nothing.

      On April 11, 2022, the City Council held an oversight hearing on enforcement delays for Local Law 6. The law ordered NYPD to inspect 25 blocks weekly for placard abuse and another 25 for blocked bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and crosswalks. The Department of Investigation (DOI) was to review and report by September 30, 2020. Agencies missed deadlines, citing pandemic and staffing shortages. Councilmember Erik Bottcher, representing District 3, pressed for action, saying businesses suffered from fake placards. The hearing highlighted the matter: 'Standing still zone: Two-year-old parking placard abuse probe stuck in bureaucratic time warp, NYC investigator blames COVID.' Advocates and @placardabuse called the delays corrupt and dangerous. Danny Pearlstein of Riders Alliance said placard abuse is no victimless crime, making streets more dangerous and slowing buses. No timeline for enforcement was given.

      Powers Presses DOT for Update on Delayed Busway
      ▸

      The Fifth Avenue busway remains stuck. Promised in 2020, it faces business pushback and city silence. Riders wait. Speeds drop. Advocates fume. DOT offers no answers. The city drags its feet while vulnerable road users pay the price.

      Bill: Fifth Avenue busway implementation. Status: Delayed. Committee: Not specified. Key dates: Announced 2020, stalled as of April 8, 2022. The project, described as a 'car-free busway between 57th and 34th streets,' aimed to speed up commutes for 110,000 bus riders. Council Member Keith Powers pressed DOT for updates, saying, 'we shouldn’t miss a big opportunity to transform Fifth Avenue.' Danny Pearlstein of Riders Alliance called it 'one of the most important busways in the City.' Despite proven success elsewhere, business interests stalled progress. The current plan offers a protected bike lane and pedestrian improvements, but no dedicated bus lane. DOT has not provided a timeline. Advocates and local officials remain frustrated. Vulnerable road users—bus riders, cyclists, pedestrians—are left exposed as the city delays.
