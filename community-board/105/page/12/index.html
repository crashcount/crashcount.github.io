      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?

      Seven Dead, Still Waiting: NYC Streets Demand Action

      Written July 3, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Manhattan CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 3, 2025

      The Toll in Plain Sight

      The bodies keep coming. In the last twelve months, seven people died on the streets of Manhattan CB5. Twenty more were left with serious injuries. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about the weather, the time of day, or the dreams that ended on the pavement (NYC Open Data).

      A man, 34, was killed crossing 7th Avenue and West 36th Street. The driver was distracted. The city called it inattention. The man is still dead (NYC Open Data).

      Fourteen people were hurt when buses collided at Port Authority. The terminal closed. The city kept moving. “Fourteen people are being treated for non-life-threatening injuries,” said authorities. The ramp reopened. The pain lingers.

      Leadership: Steps and Stalls

      Local leaders have moved. Council Member Erik Bottcher and Keith Powers voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable for crossing the street. They co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and speed up protected bike lanes. These are steps. But the pace is slow. The danger is not.

      Council Member Powers called for using idle congestion pricing cameras for speed and red-light enforcement. The machines sit unused. The streets stay the same.

      “By completing the Sixth Avenue bike lane, we are not only improving the safety of cyclists but also enhancing the overall experience for pedestrians and drivers alike,” said Bottcher. The lane is open. The city is not yet safe.

      The Disaster Is Slow, But It Is Here

      Every death is preventable. The city knows what works: slower speeds, daylighted corners, protected lanes. The laws are written. The votes are cast. But the blood dries slow on the crosswalks.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand every crosswalk be clear. Demand action before the next name is carved into stone.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB5 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4, assembly district AD 75 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB5?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square and Midtown-Times Square neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, District 4, and District 6, Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?
      Cars and Trucks were responsible for 6 deaths, 287 minor injuries, 159 moderate injuries, and 13 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 0 deaths, 25 minor injuries, 19 moderate injuries, and 2 serious injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths, 65 minor injuries, 41 moderate injuries, and 7 serious injuries.[NYC Open Data]
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. The city knows what works: slower speeds, clear crosswalks, protected lanes. Every death is a failure to act.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, clear crosswalks, protected bike lanes, and automated enforcement. They can fund and prioritize street redesigns that protect people, not cars.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1050-2024
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill capping TLC insurance, potentially reducing street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1050-2024 blocks the Taxi and Limousine Commission from demanding more injury coverage than state law. The measure, laid over in committee, shields cab companies from higher insurance costs. Passengers and crash victims face tighter limits on payouts.

      Int 1050-2024 was introduced on September 26, 2024, and is now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to limiting the amount of liability coverage that the taxi and limousine commission may require for vehicles it licenses," bars the TLC from requiring more personal injury protection than state law demands. Council Member Diana I. Ayala is the primary sponsor, joined by over two dozen co-sponsors including De La Rosa, Banks, Ossé, Marte, and others. The bill was laid over by committee on February 10, 2025. The measure would take effect immediately if passed. By capping required coverage, the bill may leave crash victims with less compensation after collisions involving TLC-licensed vehicles.

      Int 0346-2024
      Bottcher votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.

      Int 0746-2024
      Bottcher votes yes, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety with greener medians.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to turn bare medians green. The city must plant trees or use medians for stormwater. Agencies must report progress. The law targets high-risk areas. Votes showed strong support. The mayor returned it unsigned. Streets will change.

      Int 0746-2024, now Local Law 94 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 12, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the improvement and maintenance of medians through the planting of vegetation or use in stormwater management," requires the city to improve at least one mile of paved medians every two years until 2046. The Department of Transportation, Parks, and Environmental Protection must add planted medians, tree beds, or stormwater infrastructure, focusing on high-priority investment areas. Council Member Shekar Krishnan sponsored the bill, joined by Feliz, Avilés, Nurse, Banks, Restler, Ung, Schulman, Holden, Hudson, and Yeger. The Council passed the bill with broad support, though the mayor returned it unsigned. The law mandates annual progress reports and public tracking of improved medians and responsible agencies.
