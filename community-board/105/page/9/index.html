      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?

      Blood on the Asphalt: City Hall Stalls, Pedestrians Die

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 29, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 18, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      Seven dead. Twenty-two seriously hurt. In the past year, Manhattan CB5 has seen 1,200 crashes. The numbers are blunt. The pain is not. A 34-year-old man was killed crossing 7th Avenue and West 36th Street. The driver was distracted. The man is gone. The street is the same. NYC crash data

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the worst of it. In the last twelve months, three people over 65 died. Two more over 75. One was crossing with the light. One was not in the road. The cars kept moving.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      City leaders talk about safety. They pass laws. They hold meetings. But the streets do not change fast enough. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not used it. Cameras catch speeders, but Albany lets the law expire and then debates bringing it back. Each delay is a risk. Each risk is a life.

      On 34th Street, the city wants a busway. Some cheer. Some worry about cars clogging side streets. “It’s time to prioritize pedestrians and transit users, and not the minority of residents who use their personal cars to get around,” said Charles Todd. Others call for more studies. More waiting. “He argued that even if cars largely ‘disappear’ from 34th Street, they could then ‘impact’ the surrounding neighborhood, rather than ‘bus riders or bike riders.’ He called for a ’traffic study’ and an ‘action plan.’” said Richard Hart.

      The Blunt Edge of Policy

      SUVs and cars killed six. Trucks and buses killed two. Bikes and mopeds hurt dozens, but they did not kill. The city redesigns intersections. It adds bike lanes. Still, the deaths keep coming. The most vulnerable—pedestrians, the old, the young—pay the price for every delay, every watered-down law, every inch given back to traffic.

      Act: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras that never go dark. Demand streets for people, not just for cars.

      Every day of delay is another name lost. Do not wait for the next siren. Take action now.

      A 803
      Simone co-sponsors bill boosting cyclist safety through automated bike lane enforcement.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 803 lands in Albany. It targets drivers who block bike lanes. Cameras will catch violators. Streets choke with cars. Cyclists swerve, risk death. Lawmakers push for clear lanes. The city’s riders wait for action.

      Assembly Bill 803 was introduced on January 6, 2025, in the New York State Assembly. The bill sits in the sponsorship stage. It aims to 'establish in the city of New York a bicycle lane safety program to enforce certain restrictions on the use of bicycle lanes and/or protected bicycle lanes by means of bicycle lane photo devices.' Primary sponsor Zohran Mamdani leads a coalition of fourteen co-sponsors, including Brian Cunningham, Jessica Gonzalez-Rojas, and Harvey Epstein. The bill has not yet advanced to committee or vote. If passed, it would allow automated cameras to ticket drivers who block bike lanes, a move designed to keep lanes clear for cyclists and prevent deadly crashes. Lawmakers back the measure, but the city’s vulnerable road users still face daily danger until enforcement begins.

      A 324
      Simone co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 324 demands every state- or federally-funded road project weigh complete street design. The bill pushes agencies to publish clear guidance. Streets built for cars alone kill. This bill aims to force change at the blueprint.

      Assembly Bill 324 was introduced on January 1, 2025, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in the New York State Assembly, with Didi Barrett as the primary sponsor and a long list of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Amy Paulin, and others. The bill summary reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding; authorizes the department to compile and make publicly available guidance and reference material related to consideration of complete street design.' This measure would require state and federally funded transportation projects to consider designs that protect all road users, not just drivers. The bill’s broad sponsorship signals growing support for safer, more inclusive streets. No safety analyst note was provided.
