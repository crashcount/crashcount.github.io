      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?

      Blood on the Asphalt: City Hall Stalls, Pedestrians Die

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised July 1, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 18, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      Seven dead. Twenty-two seriously hurt. In the past year, Manhattan CB5 has seen 1,200 crashes. The numbers are blunt. The pain is not. A 34-year-old man was killed crossing 7th Avenue and West 36th Street. The driver was distracted. The man is gone. The street is the same. NYC crash data

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the worst of it. In the last twelve months, three people over 65 died. Two more over 75. One was crossing with the light. One was not in the road. The cars kept moving.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      City leaders talk about safety. They pass laws. They hold meetings. But the streets do not change fast enough. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not used it. Cameras catch speeders, but Albany lets the law expire and then debates bringing it back. Each delay is a risk. Each risk is a life.

      On 34th Street, the city wants a busway. Some cheer. Some worry about cars clogging side streets. “It’s time to prioritize pedestrians and transit users, and not the minority of residents who use their personal cars to get around,” said Charles Todd. Others call for more studies. More waiting. “He argued that even if cars largely ‘disappear’ from 34th Street, they could then ‘impact’ the surrounding neighborhood, rather than ‘bus riders or bike riders.’ He called for a ’traffic study’ and an ‘action plan.’” said Richard Hart.

      The Blunt Edge of Policy

      SUVs and cars killed six. Trucks and buses killed two. Bikes and mopeds hurt dozens, but they did not kill. The city redesigns intersections. It adds bike lanes. Still, the deaths keep coming. The most vulnerable—pedestrians, the old, the young—pay the price for every delay, every watered-down law, every inch given back to traffic.

      Act: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras that never go dark. Demand streets for people, not just for cars.

      Every day of delay is another name lost. Do not wait for the next siren. Take action now.

      Int 0905-2023
      Powers co-sponsors bill to speed up street resurfacing, boosting safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force street resurfacing jobs to finish in two weeks. Delays must be explained to neighbors. Streets torn up for months endanger walkers and riders. The bill died at session’s end. The danger remains. The clock keeps ticking.

      Int 0905-2023 was introduced on February 2, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to amend city law to require the Department of Transportation to finish all street resurfacing work within two weeks. If more time was needed, the department would have to notify the community, stating the reason and new timeline. The matter summary reads: 'ensure that all work associated with the resurfacing of any street is finalized within 2 weeks from the start of work.' Council Member Diana I. Ayala sponsored the bill, joined by thirty other members. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. No safety analyst reviewed the impact, but torn-up streets are a known threat to pedestrians and cyclists. The bill is dead. The hazard lives on.

      A 1280
      Simone co-sponsors bill boosting street safety for all users.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 1280 pushes for safe streets. It calls for complete street design. Dozens of lawmakers back it. The bill aims to protect everyone—walkers, riders, and drivers. The fight for safer roads moves forward. The danger is real. The need is urgent.

      Assembly Bill A 1280, now in the sponsorship stage, sits before the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 13, 2023, the bill 'enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles.' Jonathan Rivera leads as primary sponsor, joined by over 70 co-sponsors including Patrick Burke, Kenny Burgos, and Jessica Gonzalez-Rojas. The bill has not yet advanced to committee or vote. Its language centers the safety of all road users—pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers—by demanding infrastructure that reduces risk and prioritizes vulnerable people. The measure reflects growing legislative momentum for street design that saves lives and prevents injury. The urgency is clear: New York streets remain dangerous. Lawmakers are under pressure to act.

      Krueger Supports Safety Boosting E Bike Battery Regulations
      ▸

      Councilman Oswald Feliz backs a bill forcing landlords to post FDNY fire warnings about e-bike batteries. Six dead, 140 injured this year. Fires spark in homes, endanger tenants and delivery workers. The city moves to curb uncertified batteries and inform the public.

      Bill requires NYC landlords to display FDNY safety bulletins on e-bike battery fire risks. Introduced by Councilman Oswald Feliz (District 15) on November 27, 2022. The measure responds to at least 140 e-bike battery fires in 2022, with six deaths and 140 injuries. Feliz said, 'We must ensure that products sold in our city are safe for New Yorkers.' The bill restricts uncertified batteries and supports FDNY’s education campaign. The bulletin warns tenants about the dangers of charging and storing e-bike batteries at home. State Senator Liz Krueger also pushes for tighter battery regulations. The council’s action targets a growing threat to vulnerable residents—delivery workers and tenants—who face deadly fires in their homes and workplaces.
