      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?

      Blood on the Crosswalk: Leaders Stall, Pedestrians Die

      Written August 5, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Manhattan CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 5, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      Seven dead. Twenty seriously hurt. That’s just the last twelve months in Manhattan CB5. The numbers are cold, but the pain is not. Each body broken on the street is a life cut short, a family left waiting for a voice that will not answer.

      Just this spring, a 34-year-old man was killed crossing 7th Avenue. The driver kept going straight. The pedestrian never got up. The cause: driver inattention. The city calls it a crash. The family calls it a loss that does not heal.

      Patterns That Do Not Break

      Crashes keep coming. In the last year, CB5 saw 1,504 crashes, 822 injuries, and 20 serious injuries. The old, the young, the ones just trying to cross the street. Three people over 65 killed. Two more over 75. No one is spared. Cars, SUVs, trucks, taxis—they all take their share. SUVs alone killed six pedestrians in this district since 2022. Buses and trucks add to the toll. The city’s data does not show mercy.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      Some leaders have acted. Assembly Member Tony Simone co-sponsored a bill to expand speed camera enforcement. State Senator Liz Krueger voted yes on a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed limiters. But the streets remain deadly. Council Member Keith Powers called for using idle congestion pricing cameras for enforcement, saying, “[The state] certainly should take advantage of this very expensive infrastructure in Midtown” (nypost.com).

      Residents are not fooled by promises. “No one stops at these stop signs. We see people go through these red lights all the time,” said Nita. The city’s pace is slow. The street does not wait.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by leaders who delay, by systems that favor speed over safety. The dead cannot call for change. The living must do it for them.

      Call your council member. Call your state senator. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed limiters for repeat offenders. Demand streets where a walk does not end in blood.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB5 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 4, assembly district AD 75 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB5?
      It includes the Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square and Midtown-Times Square neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 2, District 3, District 4, and District 6, Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB5?
      Cars and Trucks: SUVs and cars caused the most deaths (6 from SUVs) and hundreds of injuries. Trucks and buses also killed and injured dozens. Motorcycles and Mopeds: Fewer in number, but still responsible for injuries. Bikes: Caused injuries but no deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These are not random accidents. They are the result of policy choices—speed limits, enforcement, street design. Every crash could have been prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed camera enforcement, require speed limiters for repeat offenders, and redesign streets to protect people walking and biking.
      ▸ How many people have been killed or seriously injured in Manhattan CB5 recently?
      In the last twelve months, seven people were killed and twenty more suffered serious injuries in traffic crashes. NYC Open Data

      Int 1160-2025
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill to speed up pavement markings, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly crossings for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, forces the Department of Transportation to install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. The bill, sponsored by Farah N. Louis (primary) and co-sponsored by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, Ariola, and others, passed on March 15, 2025. The law demands annual reporting on compliance and reasons for any delay. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Quick, visible lines cut confusion and protect people crossing or riding. The law took effect immediately.
