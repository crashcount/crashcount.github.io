      Twelve Dead on CB12 Streets: Delay Is a Death Sentence

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations)

      Manhattan CB12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Manhattan CB12, the numbers do not bleed, but the people do. Twelve dead. Thirty-eight left with serious wounds since 2022. The dead include a 15-year-old thrown from a moped on Saint Nicholas Avenue, a cyclist crushed on West 181st, a woman burned alive in a car on 10th Avenue. The living carry scars, broken bones, and memories that do not heal.

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm. They killed three pedestrians and left over a hundred with moderate or serious injuries. Trucks, motorcycles, and bikes—none are blameless, but the weight of steel and speed falls hardest from behind the wheel of a car.

      Leadership: Words, Delays, and the Sound of Sirens

      The city talks of Vision Zero. The mayor says, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many” said Mayor Adams. But the streets do not listen to words. In April, two NYPD officers chased a stolen car into Inwood. The car crashed and burned. The officers left the scene. The man inside died alone. The NYPD suspended the officers and launched an investigation. Commissioner Tisch said, “The NYPD’s enforcement efforts must never put the public or the police at undue risk, and pursuits for violations and low-level crimes can be both potentially dangerous and unnecessary” Commissioner Tisch, NY Post.

      The city has added protected bike lanes and lowered speed limits in some places. But the pace is slow. The deaths do not wait.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every delay is a choice. Every injury is a warning. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It can redesign streets, install more cameras, and hold reckless drivers to account. But it will not act unless you force it to.

      Call your council member. Demand action. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a failure. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 10, assembly district AD 72 and state senate district SD 31.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB12?
      It includes the Washington Heights (South), Washington Heights (North), Inwood, Highbridge Park, and Inwood Hill Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 7 and District 10, Assembly Districts AD 71 and AD 72, and State Senate District SD 31.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB12?
      Cars and SUVs: 3 deaths, 113 moderate injuries, 11 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 8 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 0 deaths, 6 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. The data shows patterns—speed, inattention, unsafe turns. These are preventable with better street design, lower speed limits, and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, expand protected bike lanes, install more speed cameras, and push for stronger enforcement against reckless drivers. They can act now, or answer for every life lost.
