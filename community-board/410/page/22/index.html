      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB10?

      Twelve Dead, Forty Broken: Queens Streets Bleed While Leaders Stand Silent

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB10: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Queens CB10, the numbers do not lie. Twelve dead. Forty seriously hurt. In three and a half years, 4,082 crashes tore through these streets. The wounded are not just numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. A cyclist left bleeding and alone on 115th Avenue, the driver gone before help arrived. Police said the victim was found “unconscious” and are “now looking for evidence to help them track down the driver” (ABC7).

      A moped rider, Antonio Smith-Ortiz, was killed by a wrong-way driver on 149th Avenue. The driver sped off. No arrest. No closure. “The driver, who was going against traffic in the eastbound lane, then struck an unoccupied parked 2015 Ford Transit 350 Courier van before speeding off” (NY Daily News).

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and the Rest

      Cars and SUVs killed three. Trucks and buses killed one. Motorcycles and mopeds killed two. Even a bus and a taxi took lives. The rest—hundreds—are left with broken bodies, some never to walk again. The Belt Parkway, Nassau Expressway, Lefferts Boulevard. The names change, the outcome does not.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They talk about speed cameras, about lowering limits. But in Queens CB10, the carnage continues. No local leader here has stood up with a plan that matches the scale of the blood on the street. The laws that matter—like Sammy’s Law, which lets the city lower speed limits—are on the books. But the limit is not lowered. The cameras that catch speeders are at risk of going dark unless Albany acts. The silence is loud.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every delay is another body. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Demand streets that put people first, not cars. Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Adams Clarifies Bills Target Sellers Not Delivery Workers
      ▸

      City Council pushed forward a battery buy-back and tough lithium-ion rules. Delivery workers’ safety and livelihoods hung in the balance. Lawmakers targeted sellers, not riders. The bills aimed to stop deadly fires without punishing those who rely on e-bikes to survive.

      On March 2, 2023, the New York City Council advanced a package of lithium-ion battery safety bills. Council Member Keith Powers introduced the buy-back bill, which creates a citywide program for defective or uncertified batteries. Powers said, "We don't want to penalize businesses and workers without thinking about this strategically." The Council also moved bills from Oswald Feliz, Gale Brewer, Alexa Avilés, and Robert Holden. These measures ban uncertified batteries, require fireproof charging containers, mandate public education, and order FDNY reporting on battery fires. Speaker Adrienne Adams clarified the bills target commercial sellers, not workers. Shahana Hanif urged that policy must "keep workers" at the center. The Council and delivery workers’ union stressed that tech companies, not workers, should bear costs. The FDNY opposed the reporting bill over resource concerns. The legislation aims to curb battery fires while protecting delivery workers who depend on e-bikes.

      Adams Supports Seasonal Outdoor Dining with DOT Oversight
      ▸

      Council moves to make outdoor dining permanent, but curbside setups will be seasonal. Sidewalk dining stays year-round. Lawmakers cite new rules and design standards. Advocates push for year-round use. Noise, rats, and lost parking fuel debate. Streets change, but danger lingers.

      Bill details emerged February 27, 2023, as the City Council advanced legislation for a permanent, seasonal outdoor dining program. The measure, expected before lawmakers next month, keeps sidewalk dining year-round but limits curbside 'streeteries' to warmer months. Council Member Justin Brannan said, 'The plan is not to make permanent the current outdoor dining sheds you see on our streets ... but to create a better program with new rules, new standards, clear design guidelines and operating requirements.' Speaker Adrienne Adams leads the Council in fine-tuning the bill, while Mayor Adams supports Department of Transportation oversight. Council Member Marjorie Velázquez’s original bill would have shifted control to the Department of Consumer and Worker Protection. Advocates like Jackson Chabot of Open Plans argue, 'A year-round option is an absolute must.' The bill responds to complaints about noise, rats, and lost parking, but also credits the program with saving 100,000 jobs. The Council’s move reshapes public space, but the safety impact for pedestrians and cyclists remains unaddressed.
