      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      South Conduit, two crashes, one hour of pain

      Queens CB10: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 21, 2025

      Just after noon on Sep 6, at South Conduit Avenue and 131 Street, the driver of a moped hit a truck and went down hard. Police logged severe injuries to his legs from the city’s crash log.

      This Week

      • The same morning at 122 Street and 109 Avenue, a crash involving a bike, a motorcycle, and two SUVs left a 28‑year‑old driver unconscious with head wounds, police said in the crash file (NYC Open Data).
      • On Aug 27 at Rockaway Boulevard and 97 Street, a sedan driver hit a 10‑year‑old in the crosswalk; police cited driver inexperience and following too closely (crash record).
      • On Aug 25 at 109 Avenue and 107 Street, the driver of an SUV turned right and hit a 21‑year‑old crossing with the signal; police recorded failure to yield by the driver (crash record).

      The toll in this corner of Queens

      Since Jan 1, 2022, Queens Community Board 10 has recorded 22 deaths, 3,970 injuries, and 56 serious injuries in 5,824 crashes (NYC Open Data). People walking account for 7 deaths and 528 injuries in that span, with SUVs and sedans often involved (NYC Open Data – rollups).

      Year to date, 8 people have been killed here, up from 2 at this point last year; crashes and injuries are also higher than a year ago (NYC Open Data – YoY).

      Night brings the worst of it. The hours around 8 PM, 10 PM, and 11 PM stack the dead, according to the city’s logs for this area (NYC Open Data – hourly).

      Corners that don’t forgive

      Belt Parkway is a wound that doesn’t close: 5 deaths and 511 injuries tied to it in these records. North Conduit Avenue tells its own story: 2 deaths and 255 injuries (NYC Open Data – locations).

      The causes are plain in the files. A 23‑year‑old man walking was killed on 101 Street near 103 Avenue late on Jul 31; police cited unsafe speed by the driver of a 2021 sedan (case 4832080). Two days in August, police again wrote up failure to yield at a crosswalk on 109 Avenue and a driver following too closely at Rockaway Boulevard (crash records).

      This month, a 44‑year‑old woman directing traffic at a Nassau Expressway work site was run down and left to die; police later arrested a driver according to local reports. “The safety of pedestrians and all street users remains a top priority,” City Council Speaker Adrienne Adams’ office said of a visibility bill now moving through the council (AMNY).

      Simple fixes, delayed

      On these corners, the work is basic: daylight every approach so drivers can see people at the crosswalks; give walkers a head start with leading pedestrian intervals; harden turns so drivers slow; put real night‑time enforcement where the deaths stack up; and keep trucks in lanes designed for them on South and North Conduit. These measures target the patterns police already record: failure to yield, unsafe turning, unsafe speed (NYC Open Data).

      At City Hall, a bill to ban parking within 20 feet of crosswalks is under review. Speaker Adams’ office calls it a safety measure and says the process is “deliberative” (AMNY). The council has the power to clear sightlines. Streets will not do it themselves.

      Stop the worst repeat offenders

      In Albany, the Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045) would force drivers with a record of violations to install speed‑limiting tech. State Senator James Sanders voted yes in committee this June (NYS Senate bill page). Assembly Member Stacey Pheffer Amato voted no on a separate bill to extend school speed zones (Senate vote log). Council Speaker Adrienne Adams says visibility at corners is a priority, but the daylighting bill is still stuck in process (AMNY).

      The record in CB10 is not abstract. It is names, ages, and corners. It is a child hit at Rockaway Boulevard. It is a worker on Nassau Expressway who never made it home. If you want this to change, tell the people with the pen to use it. Start here.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What area does this story cover?
      Queens Community Board 10, which includes South Ozone Park, Ozone Park, Howard Beach–Lindenwood, and Spring Creek Park.
      ▸ How many crashes and victims are we talking about?
      From Jan 1, 2022 to Sep 21, 2025, NYC’s crash data logs 5,824 crashes in Queens CB10, causing 22 deaths, 56 serious injuries, and 3,970 total injuries. People walking account for 7 deaths and 528 injuries. Source: NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets.
      ▸ What are the worst locations?
      Belt Parkway (5 deaths, 511 injuries) and North Conduit Avenue (2 deaths, 255 injuries) appear most often in severe outcomes in this area’s records. Source: NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Who are the officials here and what have they done?
      Council Speaker Adrienne Adams represents this area at City Hall and has called crosswalk visibility a safety priority as a daylighting bill moves through the Council (AMNY). In Albany, State Senator James Sanders voted yes on S4045 to rein in repeat speeders, while Assembly Member Stacey Pheffer Amato voted no on extending school speed zones (NYS Senate records).
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We analyzed NYC’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4), filtering for incidents within Queens Community Board 10 between 2022-01-01 and 2025-09-21. We counted deaths, injuries, and serious injuries from the Persons table and matched them to crash locations. Data was accessed Sep 21, 2025. You can explore the base datasets here.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
