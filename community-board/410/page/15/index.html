      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB10?

      Twelve Dead in Queens—Who Will Stop the Next One?

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 8, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB10: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Slow Grind of Loss

      Twelve dead. Forty-one left with injuries that will not heal. In Queens CB10, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, the streets have claimed lives and broken bodies. The dead do not get a second chance. The injured carry the weight. In the last year alone, two more people died. Four were seriously hurt. The pain is not spread evenly. It falls hardest on those outside the car—walkers, cyclists, children.

      Just days ago, a BMW vaulted the median on the Belt Parkway and slammed into oncoming cars. Six people were hurt, three left fighting for life. Police closed the road. The NYPD’s Collision Investigation Squad came to count the damage. “It was unclear what caused the driver to lose control but police said speed may have been an issue,” the Post reported. The cause is always the same: speed, steel, and the city’s refusal to change.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Cars and SUVs do most of the harm. They killed three. They left hundreds with broken bones and worse. Trucks and buses took another life. Motorcycles and mopeds killed one more. Bikes injured two. The street is a battleground, but only some are armed.

      The stories repeat. A woman crossing the street, struck by a bus. A child hit by a turning car. A moped rider ejected and left dead on the expressway. The names change. The grief does not.

      Leaders: Action and Inaction

      Local leaders have voted both ways. Council Member Joann Ariola voted against legalizing jaywalking, a law that would have shifted blame away from pedestrians and toward drivers. She also voted no on daylighting, a proven fix that clears sightlines at corners. But she has backed bills for better lighting and safer truck routes. The record is mixed. The results are not. “Our leaders are failing New Yorkers,” said Transportation Alternatives.

      The city has the power to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not done so. Every day of delay is another day for the next crash.

      Call to Action: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every corner. Demand action, not words. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Find your council member and take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB10 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 28, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB10?
      It includes the South Ozone Park, Ozone Park, Howard Beach-Lindenwood, and Spring Creek Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 28 and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, and AD 31, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 15, and SD 19.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB10?
      Cars and SUVs: 3 deaths, 8 serious injuries, 291 minor injuries. Trucks and buses: 1 death, 4 serious injuries, 18 minor injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 death, 2 serious injuries, 3 minor injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 1 minor injury. Most deaths and injuries come from cars and SUVs.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These are not accidents. Most crashes in Queens CB10 involve speed, failure to yield, or poor street design. Proven fixes like lower speed limits and daylighting corners can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, daylighting, protected bike lanes, and better enforcement. They can support bills that shift responsibility to drivers and redesign dangerous streets. Every vote and delay matters.

      Int 1164-2023
      Ariola absent as committee advances bill improving street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council passed Int 1164-2023. The law forces DOT to map out where street safety money goes. It tracks past investment, crash rates, and who gets left behind. The city must show its work, district by district. No more hiding the gaps.

      Int 1164-2023, now enacted, amends the city code to require an 'investment roadmap' in every Streets Master Plan starting December 1, 2026. The bill moved through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passing on October 19, 2023, and became law on November 19, 2023. The law states: 'The department shall prioritize and promote...the safety of all street users...and improving equity in infrastructure investment.' Sponsored by Kevin C. Riley (primary), with Brooks-Powers, Narcisse, Hanif, Brewer, Restler, and Rivera as co-sponsors, the bill demands DOT report, by neighborhood, on safety infrastructure investment, crash rates, and demographics. It shines a light on which districts get safety upgrades and which are left exposed. The roadmap aims to close the gap for underserved areas, forcing transparency and accountability for every dollar spent on street safety.

      Int 0712-2022
      Ariola absent as committee advances bill with no direct safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to report cars with unreadable plates dodging cameras. The bill targets drivers hiding their plates from red light and speed cameras. Reports must show where, when, and how plates go unseen. Data goes public, every quarter.

      Int 0712-2022, now enacted as Local Law 155 of 2023, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and the full Council on October 19, 2023. The law, sponsored by Gale A. Brewer with co-sponsors including Erik D. Bottcher, Lincoln Restler, and others, amends the city code to require the Department of Transportation to report on vehicles with license plates unreadable by photo violation monitoring systems. The law’s title states it is 'in relation to reporting on motor vehicles with license plates that are unreadable by photo violation monitoring systems.' DOT must post quarterly reports showing the time, location, and reasons plates evade cameras—whether by concealment, distortion, or missing tags. The law aims to expose drivers who dodge automated enforcement, shining light on a loophole that puts pedestrians and cyclists at risk. The reports must be public and sent to the Mayor and Council Speaker.

      Int 0906-2023
      Ariola votes yes to move trucks off residential streets, boosting safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to force off-street parking for tractor trailers. No more big rigs clogging residential blocks. By 2025, each borough must have a site. The city must act or explain delays. Streets may breathe. Danger shifts from curbs.

      Int 0906-2023, now Local Law 142 of 2023, was enacted by the City Council on October 29, 2023, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill’s title: 'A Local Law in relation to parking for tractor trailers and other commercial trucks.' It requires the city to identify at least one feasible off-street parking location for tractor trailers in each borough by the end of 2023, and to establish those sites by December 31, 2025. No location can be on a residential street. Council Member Kamillah Hanks sponsored the bill, joined by Brannan, Riley, Dinowitz, Ung, Brooks-Powers, and others. The law aims to clear residential streets of oversized trucks, shifting their presence to designated lots. The council’s action targets the chronic hazard of trucks parked where people walk, bike, and live.
