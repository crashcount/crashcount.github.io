      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB10?

      Twelve Dead in Queens—Who Will Stop the Next One?

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB10: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Slow Grind of Loss

      Twelve dead. Forty-one left with injuries that will not heal. In Queens CB10, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, the streets have claimed lives and broken bodies. The dead do not get a second chance. The injured carry the weight. In the last year alone, two more people died. Four were seriously hurt. The pain is not spread evenly. It falls hardest on those outside the car—walkers, cyclists, children.

      Just days ago, a BMW vaulted the median on the Belt Parkway and slammed into oncoming cars. Six people were hurt, three left fighting for life. Police closed the road. The NYPD’s Collision Investigation Squad came to count the damage. “It was unclear what caused the driver to lose control but police said speed may have been an issue,” the Post reported. The cause is always the same: speed, steel, and the city’s refusal to change.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Cars and SUVs do most of the harm. They killed three. They left hundreds with broken bones and worse. Trucks and buses took another life. Motorcycles and mopeds killed one more. Bikes injured two. The street is a battleground, but only some are armed.

      The stories repeat. A woman crossing the street, struck by a bus. A child hit by a turning car. A moped rider ejected and left dead on the expressway. The names change. The grief does not.

      Leaders: Action and Inaction

      Local leaders have voted both ways. Council Member Joann Ariola voted against legalizing jaywalking, a law that would have shifted blame away from pedestrians and toward drivers. She also voted no on daylighting, a proven fix that clears sightlines at corners. But she has backed bills for better lighting and safer truck routes. The record is mixed. The results are not. “Our leaders are failing New Yorkers,” said Transportation Alternatives.

      The city has the power to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not done so. Every day of delay is another day for the next crash.

      Call to Action: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every corner. Demand action, not words. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Find your council member and take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB10 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 28, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB10?
      It includes the South Ozone Park, Ozone Park, Howard Beach-Lindenwood, and Spring Creek Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 28 and District 32, Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, and AD 31, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 15, and SD 19.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB10?
      Cars and SUVs: 3 deaths, 8 serious injuries, 291 minor injuries. Trucks and buses: 1 death, 4 serious injuries, 18 minor injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 death, 2 serious injuries, 3 minor injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 1 minor injury. Most deaths and injuries come from cars and SUVs.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These are not accidents. Most crashes in Queens CB10 involve speed, failure to yield, or poor street design. Proven fixes like lower speed limits and daylighting corners can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, daylighting, protected bike lanes, and better enforcement. They can support bills that shift responsibility to drivers and redesign dangerous streets. Every vote and delay matters.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0606-2024
      Ariola co-sponsors e-bike and scooter registration bill, reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council wants plates on every e-bike and scooter. The bill sits in committee. Riders face new rules. Lawmakers push for registration. Streets wait. No change yet. The city debates control. Vulnerable users watch. The system holds its breath.

      Int 0606-2024 was introduced on March 7, 2024, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered," would force all e-bikes, e-scooters, and similar vehicles not already covered by DMV rules to register with the Department of Transportation and display a visible plate. Council Member Inna Vernikov led as primary sponsor, joined by Holden, Farías, Riley, Brannan, and many others. The bill was laid over in committee on December 11, 2024. The measure aims to track these vehicles, but its impact on vulnerable road users remains unassessed. The city’s debate continues, with no law yet in force.

      Int 0603-2024
      Ariola sponsors bill that could reduce street safety for pedestrians.
      ▸

      Council bill demands DOT keep curbs high after street work. Low curbs mean water floods sidewalks. Pedestrians wade through puddles. Property owners get fined. Ariola leads, Holden, Paladino, Vernikov back her. Committee reviews. No action yet. Streets stay risky.

      Int 0603-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure since March 7, 2024. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that the department of transportation maintain curb heights following street construction,' targets a simple failure: after street work, curbs often vanish, water pools, and property owners get fined for city mistakes. Council Member Joann Ariola sponsors, joined by Holden, Paladino, and Vernikov. The bill orders DOT to restore curbs to proper height after any street, sidewalk, or gutter work. The committee has not voted. The bill aims to stop flooding and fines, but leaves the daily hazard for walkers and wheelchair users unaddressed until passed.
