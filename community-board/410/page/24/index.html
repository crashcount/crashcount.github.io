      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB10?

      Twelve Dead, Forty Broken: Queens Streets Bleed While Leaders Stand Silent

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB10: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Queens CB10, the numbers do not lie. Twelve dead. Forty seriously hurt. In three and a half years, 4,082 crashes tore through these streets. The wounded are not just numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. A cyclist left bleeding and alone on 115th Avenue, the driver gone before help arrived. Police said the victim was found “unconscious” and are “now looking for evidence to help them track down the driver” (ABC7).

      A moped rider, Antonio Smith-Ortiz, was killed by a wrong-way driver on 149th Avenue. The driver sped off. No arrest. No closure. “The driver, who was going against traffic in the eastbound lane, then struck an unoccupied parked 2015 Ford Transit 350 Courier van before speeding off” (NY Daily News).

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and the Rest

      Cars and SUVs killed three. Trucks and buses killed one. Motorcycles and mopeds killed two. Even a bus and a taxi took lives. The rest—hundreds—are left with broken bodies, some never to walk again. The Belt Parkway, Nassau Expressway, Lefferts Boulevard. The names change, the outcome does not.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They talk about speed cameras, about lowering limits. But in Queens CB10, the carnage continues. No local leader here has stood up with a plan that matches the scale of the blood on the street. The laws that matter—like Sammy’s Law, which lets the city lower speed limits—are on the books. But the limit is not lowered. The cameras that catch speeders are at risk of going dark unless Albany acts. The silence is loud.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every delay is another body. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Demand streets that put people first, not cars. Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Adams Backs Safety Boosting Bike Lanes and Intersections
      ▸

      2022 battered New York’s streets. The city pledged safer intersections, stronger bike lanes, and more speed cameras. Council Speaker Adrienne Adams favored parking over outdoor dining. The MTA hiked fares. Riders, walkers, and cyclists faced old dangers and new promises.

      This year-in-review covers New York City’s major transportation events and policies in 2022. Council Speaker Adrienne Adams (District 28) featured prominently, voicing support for parking over curbside sheds during the outdoor dining debate. The summary, published December 27, 2022, highlights pledges to fortify 100 miles of bike lanes and make 1,000 intersections safer. The MTA settled lawsuits to make 95% of subway stations ADA compliant by 2055. The city exceeded its intersection safety goal, and the state approved 24/7 speed cameras. Adams’s stance on parking signals a preference for car storage over expanded public space, a move that can limit safety gains for pedestrians and cyclists. The review underscores a year of mixed progress: new safety measures, but persistent threats for vulnerable road users.

      Adams Supports Busway Hour Cuts Despite Safety Concerns
      ▸

      City made Jamaica and Archer Avenue busways permanent. Jamaica Avenue hours slashed. Archer stays 24/7. Northern Boulevard bus lanes started after months of delay. Local politicians fought restrictions. Bus riders still face slow trips. City bows to business pressure.

      On November 15, 2022, the Adams administration made busways on Jamaica and Archer avenues permanent after a one-year pilot. The Department of Transportation cut Jamaica Avenue’s busway hours from 24/7 to 6 a.m. to 8 p.m. daily, while Archer Avenue remains round-the-clock. The Northern Boulevard bus lane project, stalled for months after Council Member Francisco Moya lobbied against it, finally began installation. Council Members Nantasha Williams and Selvena Brooks-Powers opposed the busways, calling for their elimination and citing business concerns. Queens Borough President Donovan Richards and other local officials pushed for even shorter hours. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez defended the busways, saying, 'strong bus infrastructure is a necessity.' Despite opposition, the city kept some busway hours intact, but caved to business interests, reducing protection for bus riders and vulnerable road users.

      Ariola Calls for Safe Charging Infrastructure and Global Solutions
      ▸

      Council pushes bills to curb deadly lithium-ion battery fires. FDNY supports bans on unsafe batteries and calls for more safety info. Advocates warn bans hit low-income delivery workers. Debate rages as fires, injuries, and deaths mount. City eyes safer charging.

      On November 14, 2022, the City Council held a hearing on a package of bills addressing lithium-ion battery fires. The FDNY supported four out of five bills, including bans on second-use and non-certified batteries, a public safety campaign, and safety information for delivery workers. The FDNY expressed concern about resource demands for detailed annual fire reporting. The matter summary states the bills aim to address the rise of deadly fires from faulty batteries, often used by delivery workers. Council Member Joann Ariola urged the FDNY to consider international solutions and invest in safe charging infrastructure. Hildalyn Colón Hernández of Los Deliveristas Unidos opposed the bans, arguing they punish low-income workers and miss root causes. The city recently abandoned a proposal to ban e-bikes on NYCHA property and is working on new charging stations. The debate highlights the struggle to protect vulnerable workers while preventing more deaths and injuries.
