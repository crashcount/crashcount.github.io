      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB10?

      Twelve Dead, Forty Broken: Queens Streets Bleed While Leaders Stand Silent

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB10: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Queens CB10, the numbers do not lie. Twelve dead. Forty seriously hurt. In three and a half years, 4,082 crashes tore through these streets. The wounded are not just numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. A cyclist left bleeding and alone on 115th Avenue, the driver gone before help arrived. Police said the victim was found “unconscious” and are “now looking for evidence to help them track down the driver” (ABC7).

      A moped rider, Antonio Smith-Ortiz, was killed by a wrong-way driver on 149th Avenue. The driver sped off. No arrest. No closure. “The driver, who was going against traffic in the eastbound lane, then struck an unoccupied parked 2015 Ford Transit 350 Courier van before speeding off” (NY Daily News).

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and the Rest

      Cars and SUVs killed three. Trucks and buses killed one. Motorcycles and mopeds killed two. Even a bus and a taxi took lives. The rest—hundreds—are left with broken bodies, some never to walk again. The Belt Parkway, Nassau Expressway, Lefferts Boulevard. The names change, the outcome does not.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They talk about speed cameras, about lowering limits. But in Queens CB10, the carnage continues. No local leader here has stood up with a plan that matches the scale of the blood on the street. The laws that matter—like Sammy’s Law, which lets the city lower speed limits—are on the books. But the limit is not lowered. The cameras that catch speeders are at risk of going dark unless Albany acts. The silence is loud.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every delay is another body. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Demand streets that put people first, not cars. Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Adrienne E Adams Opposes Safety Boosting Sammy’s Law Reform
      ▸

      Pedestrian deaths surged 27 percent in 2024. The Council sits idle on Sammy’s Law, blocking a citywide 20 mph limit. Speeding rages. DOT lowers limits piecemeal. Streets stay deadly. Vision Zero fades. Vulnerable New Yorkers pay the price.

      City Council has not acted on Sammy’s Law, which would let New York City lower its speed limit to 20 mph. The bill remains stalled, with Speaker Adrienne Adams showing little interest. The matter: 'Sammy's Law authorized the Council to lower the citywide speed limit to 20 miles per hour.' Inaction comes as 127 people died in crashes through June 30, 2024—more than any year since Vision Zero began. Pedestrian deaths rose 27 percent from 2023. DOT, led by rep Nick Benson, uses its limited authority to lower speed limits at 201 locations by year’s end. Adams says, 'Each Council member is going to have to weigh in on how they feel it should be enacted or should not be enacted in their district.' Meanwhile, injuries and deaths climb. The Council’s delay leaves vulnerable road users exposed.

      Ariola Opposes Increased Housing Density Near Transit
      ▸

      Low-density districts block City of Yes. Most community boards vote no. High-density areas back the plan. The fight centers on housing, parking, and who bears the city’s growth. Vulnerable New Yorkers wait as politics stall change.

      Bill: City of Yes housing proposal. Status: Community board votes and public testimony as of July 22, 2024. Of 57 boards, 38 opposed the plan, 20 supported it, mostly in denser neighborhoods. The proposal, described as aiming to build 'a little more housing in every neighborhood,' faces resistance in low-density areas. Council Member Joann Ariola (R-Woodhaven) opposed more housing in her district, calling it 'suburbs.' Brooklyn Borough President Antonio Reynoso criticized neighborhoods that refuse new housing, saying it increases pressure on poorer areas. Arlene Schlesinger, a Queens resident, voiced strong opposition, especially to lifting parking mandates and adding accessory dwelling units. Community boards, often older and whiter, resist change, leaving the city’s most vulnerable to bear the brunt of housing and transportation inequity.

      Adams Defends Compromise Policy Reducing Roadway Dining Safety
      ▸

      Outdoor dining in New York City’s roadways is fading fast. New rules force restaurants to pack up curbside setups for four winter months. Only 23 have hearings to join the permanent program. The city keeps most curb space for cars. Sidewalk dining survives.

      The policy, shaped by the City Council and Mayor Adams, phases out pandemic-era roadway dining structures. As of July 12, 2024, only 23 restaurants have public hearings scheduled to join the permanent program, with hundreds more applications pending, according to the Department of Transportation. The matter summary states: 'New York City is likely to see a steep drop in restaurants offering outdoor dining in roadways after its permanent al fresco program phases out the pandemic-era structures next month.' Council Speaker Adrienne Adams and her office defend the compromise, saying it 'strikes the right balance.' Restaurant owners like Thiru Rajamani and Sam say the seasonal pause and costs are too high. Advocates argue the city is missing a chance to reclaim curb space from cars. Sidewalk dining will remain year-round.

      Adams Backs Fair Fares Expansion Boosting Transit Safety
      ▸

      City Council raised Fair Fares eligibility. Now, more low-income New Yorkers get half-price MetroCards. The move adds $10 million to the program. Over one million people now qualify. Councilmember Brannan calls it a step for equity. Advocates want more.

      On June 28, 2024, the City Council expanded the Fair Fares MetroCard program, raising eligibility from 120% to 145% of the federal poverty line. The change, part of the Fiscal Year 2025 budget, was championed by Council Finance Chair Justin Brannan. The bill increases access for individuals earning up to $21,837 and families of four earning $45,240. The council added $10 million in funding, bringing the total to over $100 million annually. Brannan and economist James Parrott wrote, 'By expanding the reach of Fair Fares, we can ensure that all New Yorkers—particularly those who are already struggling during a crisis of affordability—can fully participate in the economic, social, and cultural opportunities of our city.' The expansion boosts eligibility from 932,000 to just over 1 million people. Advocates and Speaker Adrienne Adams say it is a win, but still short of the 200% threshold they seek.

      On June 23, 2024, Council Member Justin Brannan (District 47) published an op-ed urging expansion of the Fair Fares program. The piece, titled 'Why expanding Fair Fares eligibility is a matter of racial equity,' highlights the push to raise eligibility from 120% to 200% of the Federal Poverty Level. He points out that subway and bus riders, mostly people of color and low-income workers, get the least public subsidy. The op-ed calls for urgent investment in affordable transit, especially for outer-borough and underinvested communities. Brannan’s action centers the needs of those who rely on safe, accessible transit to move through the city.

      Amato votes yes, advancing school speed cameras that improve pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Assembly passes A 7652. The bill launches speed cameras near Schenectady schools. Cameras catch drivers who speed. The program ends in 2028 unless renewed. Most members vote yes. Some vote no. Streets near schools remain risky for children on foot.

      Bill A 7652, titled "Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the city of Schenectady," advanced with a committee vote in the Assembly on June 7, 2024. The measure creates a pilot program to install speed cameras near schools, aiming to catch drivers who exceed posted limits. The authorization for the program expires December 31, 2028. Assemblymembers Phil Steck (District 110, primary sponsor) and Angelo Santabarbara (District 111, co-sponsor) led the push. The vote passed with strong support but faced opposition from several members. The bill’s summary reads: "Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the city of Schenectady; repeals authorization of program December 31, 2028." No formal safety analyst note was provided, but the measure targets driver speed in zones where children walk, bike, and cross.
