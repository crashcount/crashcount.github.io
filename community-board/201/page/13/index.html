      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx CB1?

      Bronx CB1: East 149th keeps taking lives

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Bronx CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      East 149th Street does not forgive. Since 2022, Bronx CB1 logged 15 deaths and 2,387 injuries across 3,763 crashes. The toll skews to people outside cars: six pedestrians and three cyclists dead. The count is from city data.

      East 149th: the line that keeps breaking

      A taxi going east on East 149th struck a man late at night. He never woke up, the city file says June 25, 2024. Three months later, a 52‑year‑old man died near E 149 St and Morris Ave in a chain of SUVs in the afternoon, also on E 149 St April 2, 2025. A cyclist riding east at Brook Ave was hit by a left‑turning MTA bus and killed at night on E 149 St Feb. 25, 2025.

      The city’s hotspot list puts East 149 Street among the worst in CB1. So is Bruckner Blvd and the Bruckner Expressway—corridors built for speed, not people NYC Open Data top intersections.

      When the clock turns, the bodies rise

      The hours tell a pattern. Injuries climb after dark. From 8 p.m. to 11 p.m., injuries stay high, with a spike at 8 p.m. and a grim rise in deaths at 9–11 p.m. and again at 1–2 a.m. In this board, nights cut deep: deaths stack at 1 a.m., 2 a.m., 9–11 p.m., and 7 p.m. hourly distribution.

      Trucks, buses, taxis — heavy metal, heavy harm

      Pedestrians here are most often struck by SUVs and sedans. But trucks and buses are in the mix and deadly: trucks/buses caused a share of pedestrian deaths and serious injuries, including the bus that killed a cyclist on E 149 St at Brook Ave vehicle rollup; crash 4795059. Bruckner is a truck route. The bodies are local.

      What’s causing it

      The city tags “other” as the lead factor category for deaths and injuries in CB1. After that, errors by people outside cars show up. Distraction appears. Red‑light running shows in fatal records too contributing factors.

      Two minutes on Bruckner, a life gone

      A moped and a tanker truck met at Bruckner Blvd and Southern Blvd just after 7 a.m. A 22‑year‑old passenger died. The record notes a left turn and a traffic control disregarded June 13, 2025.

      The parkway teaches the same lesson

      Outside the board, the Bronx River Parkway showed what speed and passing do to flesh. A 21‑year‑old driver was charged after two riders died. One sister said, “Two people were killed. He was drunk.” The DA charged vehicular manslaughter and DWI, Gothamist reported, naming the dead: Manuel Amarantepenalo, 19, and Enrique Martinez, 21 Gothamist. The mother asked, “How could they let him go?”

      What to fix now on our blocks

      • Daylight the crossings on East 149th and along Bruckner. Hard posts. No curbside parking at corners. Left turns need steel, not paint.
      • Left‑turn signals and hardened turns at E 149 St and Brook Ave, Morris Ave, and other repeat corners. A bus killed a cyclist there at night. Protect the line of travel crash 4795059.
      • Truck routing and targeted night enforcement on Bruckner Blvd/Southern Blvd where the moped passenger died. Keep heavy vehicles out of conflict where possible crash 4820106.

      Citywide tools that cut speed and repeat harm

      Albany renewed 24‑hour school‑zone cameras through 2030, but speed is still the knife. The Council and mayor can set safer limits. Lower the default speed. The city has the power, advocates say. Then go after the worst repeat offenders. A tiny slice of drivers do outsized harm. As one analysis put it, “1.5% of motorists cause 21% of pedestrian deaths,” and 30+ camera tickets multiplies the risk fifty‑fold. The Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045/A2299) would require speed‑limiting tech for drivers with repeat points or tickets. State Sen. Jose Serrano backed it in committee S 4045. Assembly Member Amanda Septimo co‑sponsors the Assembly version A 2299.

      One more thing. The city’s own numbers show nights here are deadly. Slow the streets. Stop the repeat speeders. Do it before the next body hits the asphalt.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where is Bronx CB1?
      It covers Mott Haven–Port Morris and Melrose. It overlaps Council Districts 8 and 17, Assembly Districts 79 and 84, and State Senate Districts 29 and 32.
      ▸ What are the worst corridors in CB1?
      City data flags East 149 Street, Bruckner Blvd, and the Bruckner Expressway as top hotspots for injuries and deaths.
      ▸ When are crashes worst?
      Injuries surge after dark, with deaths clustering around 7 p.m., 9–11 p.m., and 1–2 a.m., according to the hourly distribution.
      ▸ What policies could help now?
      Lower the default city speed limit and require speed‑limiting tech for repeat violators (S4045/A2299). Senator Serrano voted yes in committee; Assembly Member Septimo co‑sponsors the Assembly bill.

      Int 0346-2024
      Ayala votes yes to decriminalize jaywalking, boosting pedestrian safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians now cross anywhere, any time. No summons. Law strips drivers of excuses. Streets shift. Power tilts to people on foot.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, rewrites New York’s pedestrian rules. The bill, heard by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passed on September 26, 2024, and became law on October 26, 2024. Its title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way.” Sponsored by Council Member Tiffany Cabán and co-sponsored by Narcisse, Won, Hanif, Hudson, and Restler, the law lets pedestrians cross streets anywhere, even against signals. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks. The Department of Transportation must educate all street users on new rights and responsibilities. The law removes a tool police used to target walkers. It shifts blame from people on foot to the system and those behind the wheel.
