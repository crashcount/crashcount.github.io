      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx CB1?

      Bronx Streets Bleed While Leaders Stall—How Many More Must Die?

      Written July 21, 2025

      Bronx CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 21, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Five people are dead. Seven hundred forty-two are hurt. In the last twelve months, Bronx CB1 has seen 1,067 crashes. Fifteen of those injuries were serious. These are not numbers. They are lives ended, bodies broken, families left with empty chairs.

      Just this spring, a 52-year-old man was crushed and killed by an SUV on Morris Avenue. He was not at an intersection. He never made it home. NYC Open Data records the loss. The street remembers.

      A 57-year-old cyclist died on East 149th, thrown from his e-bike and struck by a turning bus. There was no second chance. The city moves on. The families do not.

      The Pattern: Young, Old, No One Spared

      Children, elders, workers, riders, walkers. The violence does not discriminate. In the last year, 78 children under 18 were injured. Two people aged 18–24 died. Two more in their fifties. The old and the young, all at risk.

      The killers are not strangers. SUVs and cars caused the most harm—four deaths, 211 minor injuries, 96 moderate, 13 serious. Trucks and buses killed one, hurt dozens. Motorcycles, mopeds, bikes—they all draw blood, but the biggest toll comes from the biggest machines.

      Leadership: Votes, Silence, and the Need for More

      Some leaders have acted. State Senator Jose Serrano voted yes to curb repeat speeders—a bill to force speed limiters on the worst offenders. He voted yes in committee. Assembly Member Amanda Septimo co-sponsored the same bill in the Assembly. She put her name on it. Council Member Diana Ayala backed a bill to ban parking near crosswalks. She co-sponsored it.

      But the pace is slow. The deaths keep coming. The streets do not wait for another committee meeting.

      The Call: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home. Call your council member. Call your state reps. Demand they finish the job.

      No more studies. No more excuses. Lower the speed limits. Build the barriers. End the carnage.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Bronx CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 8, assembly district AD 84 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in Bronx CB1?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris and Melrose neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8 and District 17, Assembly Districts AD 79 and AD 84, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 32.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Bronx CB1?
      Cars and SUVs: 4 deaths, 211 minor injuries, 96 moderate injuries, 13 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 1 death, 20 minor injuries, 11 moderate injuries, 5 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 17 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 8 minor injuries, 3 moderate injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The pattern is clear. Most deaths and injuries come from cars, trucks, and unsafe streets. These are preventable tragedies, not random accidents.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, build safer crosswalks and bike lanes, and require speed limiters for repeat dangerous drivers. They can act now, not after another death.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up-to-date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Septimo votes yes to extend school speed cameras, boosting pedestrian safety.

      Senate passed S 5602 to keep school zone speed cameras running longer. More eyes on reckless drivers. Lawmakers push back against speeding near kids. The vote was clear. The danger remains.

      Bill S 5602, titled 'Relates to the hours of operation of a school zone speed camera demonstration program,' passed the Senate on May 25, 2022, and the Assembly on June 2, 2022. The bill extends the hours that speed cameras operate in New York City school zones. Primary sponsor: Senator Gounardes, with co-sponsors Biaggi, Cleare, Gianaris, Hoylman, Jackson, Kavanagh, Krueger, Myrie, Persaud, Ramos, Rivera, and Sepulveda. The measure saw strong support in both chambers, despite some opposition. The bill aims to keep cameras watching when children are at risk. More enforcement, more accountability. The fight for safer streets continues.
