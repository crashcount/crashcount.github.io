      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Another Name, Another Body: Brooklyn Streets Still Kill

      Written July 8, 2025

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 8, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Brooklyn CB2, the numbers do not lie. Twelve dead. Forty-two seriously injured. More than 2,400 hurt since 2022. The streets do not forgive. Each number is a body, a family, a life split open. The latest: a 55-year-old woman, crushed by an SUV at Fulton and Washington. A 45-year-old passenger, killed at Flatbush and State. A 74-year-old man, thrown from his e-bike, never to stand again. The list does not end.

      The Human Cost

      There is no poetry in the aftermath. Only the sound of sirens and the silence that follows. “He didn’t stop. He just kept going,” said Henry Tziquin, whose father was killed by a hit-and-run driver in Brooklyn. The pain is not abstract. It is a son searching for his father on the ground, eyes closed, never to open again.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. In CB2, they are behind the majority of deaths and serious injuries to people on foot and bike. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, and bikes add to the toll, but the pattern is clear. The most vulnerable—walkers, cyclists, children—pay the price for someone else’s speed, someone else’s mistake.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      Local leaders have moved, but not fast enough. Council Member Crystal Hudson has co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and speed up protected bike lanes. The city is daylighting intersections and adding bike lanes, but the pace is slow. Speed cameras are up, but loopholes remain. The state renewed the speed camera program, and Senator Andrew Gounardes said, “speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone” during the reauthorization. But the dead keep coming. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every delay is another body. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, clear crosswalks, and enforcement that targets the real danger—drivers who kill and flee. Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB2 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35, assembly district AD 57 and state senate district SD 25.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB2?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, and Brooklyn Navy Yard neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33 and District 35, Assembly Districts AD 52 and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 25 and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?
      Cars and SUVs: 3 deaths, 13 serious injuries, 339 total injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 2 serious injuries, 13 total injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, 40 total injuries. Cars and SUVs are the main killers.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speed, failure to yield, dangerous streets. These deaths and injuries are preventable with better policy and design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, build protected bike lanes, daylight intersections, and pass laws that hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can act now, not later.

      Gounardes Supports Safety‑Boosting Speed Limiter Program Expansion
      ▸

      Senate gutted the Super Speeder Bill at midnight. Over 130,000 reckless drivers dodge speed-limiter rules. Only the worst offenders face consequences. Pedestrians and cyclists stay exposed. Lawmakers chose drivers over safety. The city’s deadliest keep rolling. Streets stay dangerous.

      On June 11, 2025, the New York State Senate amended the Super Speeder Bill, sponsored by Sen. Andrew Gounardes (D-Bay Ridge). The bill originally required drivers with six or more speed or red-light camera tickets in a year to install speed-limiting devices. The Senate raised the threshold to sixteen speed-camera tickets, and removed red-light violations from the count. Streetsblog NYC reports, 'the state Senate moved to help more than 130,000 reckless drivers avoid accountability.' Gounardes defended the change, saying it would allow the program to grow slowly. Assembly Member Michaell Novakhov (R-Midwood) argued the threshold was too low. The amendment means only 17,000 drivers, not 150,000, will face restrictions. According to safety analysts, this move undermines deterrence, increases risk to pedestrians and cyclists, and signals that dangerous driving is tolerated. Vulnerable road users remain at risk. The bill’s status now sets a weaker standard for accountability.
