      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Brooklyn’s Streets Are Killing Us—Who Will Stop the Bloodshed?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Brooklyn CB2, the numbers do not lie. Eleven people killed. Forty-one left with serious injuries. More than 2,300 hurt since 2022. The dead include a 55-year-old woman crushed by an SUV on Washington Avenue, a 74-year-old man thrown from his e-bike and killed by a turning bus at Jay and Tillary, and a 45-year-old woman who died in the back seat of a car on Flatbush Avenue. Each crash is a life ended or broken. Each is a story cut short.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. They killed two people and injured more than 300 on foot. Trucks and buses added to the toll. Bikes and e-bikes hurt dozens, but the deadliest threat rolled on four wheels. The numbers are plain: the street is not safe for the unprotected.

      Leadership: Promises and Pauses

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Members Crystal Hudson and Lincoln Restler have backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and require daylighting at intersections. But the bills sit in committee. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph, but has not used it. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      The Human Cost

      The pain is not abstract. “Tiffany was literally the center of our world. She made me a better person, a better man,” said James Cifuni after his pregnant wife was run down and killed in Brooklyn. “I lost my whole family tonight and I don’t think I’ll ever be the same.”

      On another street, a bus pinned an 87-year-old man under its wheels. He was just standing near the corner when the driver turned and hit him. Police said officers who responded to the scene discovered an 87-year-old man pinned under the city bus at East 12th Street and Avenue Z in Sheepshead Bay around 6:40 p.m.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every stalled bill, every unprotected crosswalk, every ignored speed limit is a choice. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every intersection. Demand protected bike lanes that do not disappear.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Council moves to form a task force on out-of-state vehicle registrations. DOT, NYPD, and Finance join forces. One year to study, then report. Aim: expose the scale, recommend fixes. Vulnerable road users wait for action. Systemic risk remains unchecked.

      Bill Int 0765-2024 sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after introduction on April 11, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law in relation to establishing a task force on vehicles registered out-of-state," calls for an interagency group—DOT, NYPD, Finance, plus mayoral and council appointees—to study the prevalence of out-of-state vehicle registrations in New York City. Sponsors include Council Members Farah N. Louis (primary), Robert F. Holden, Gale A. Brewer, Amanda Farías, James F. Gennaro, and Lincoln Restler. The task force has one year to investigate and report to the Mayor and Council Speaker. The bill aims to expose registration loopholes and recommend solutions, but for now, the city’s most vulnerable—pedestrians, cyclists, passengers—remain at risk as enforcement and accountability lag.

      Restler Calls Placard Abuse a Harmful Public Safety Risk
      ▸

Council Member Lincoln Restler calls placards a public safety risk. The report urges digital permits and real accountability. Danger remains for those on foot.

      On April 3, 2024, the Department of Investigation released an oversight report exposing widespread NYPD placard abuse and enforcement failures. The report, mandated by Local Law 6 of 2020, found 'widespread misuse by the NYPD of city-issued parking placards, failure by the city to monitor who has placards, and a complete abrogation of enforcement against cops.' Council Member Lincoln Restler, District 33, has long opposed placards, calling them 'not just an abuse of power [but] a public safety risk and nuisance.' The report recommends a uniform digital permit system, elimination of 'self-enforcement' zones, and stricter 311 complaint handling. The NYPD failed to complete required evaluations and often ignored complaints. The DOI calls for better officer training and for traffic enforcement agents to handle placard abuse. The system, as it stands, leaves vulnerable road users exposed to illegal parking and unchecked driver privilege.
