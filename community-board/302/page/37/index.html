      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Brooklyn’s Streets Are Killing Us—Who Will Stop the Bloodshed?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Brooklyn CB2, the numbers do not lie. Eleven people killed. Forty-one left with serious injuries. More than 2,300 hurt since 2022. The dead include a 55-year-old woman crushed by an SUV on Washington Avenue, a 74-year-old man thrown from his e-bike and killed by a turning bus at Jay and Tillary, and a 45-year-old woman who died in the back seat of a car on Flatbush Avenue. Each crash is a life ended or broken. Each is a story cut short.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. They killed two people and injured more than 300 on foot. Trucks and buses added to the toll. Bikes and e-bikes hurt dozens, but the deadliest threat rolled on four wheels. The numbers are plain: the street is not safe for the unprotected.

      Leadership: Promises and Pauses

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Members Crystal Hudson and Lincoln Restler have backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and require daylighting at intersections. But the bills sit in committee. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph, but has not used it. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      The Human Cost

      The pain is not abstract. “Tiffany was literally the center of our world. She made me a better person, a better man,” said James Cifuni after his pregnant wife was run down and killed in Brooklyn. “I lost my whole family tonight and I don’t think I’ll ever be the same.”

      On another street, a bus pinned an 87-year-old man under its wheels. He was just standing near the corner when the driver turned and hit him. Police said officers who responded to the scene discovered an 87-year-old man pinned under the city bus at East 12th Street and Avenue Z in Sheepshead Bay around 6:40 p.m.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every stalled bill, every unprotected crosswalk, every ignored speed limit is a choice. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every intersection. Demand protected bike lanes that do not disappear.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Int 1151-2023
      Hudson co-sponsors solar crosswalk bill, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council pushed for 500 solar-lit crosswalks. The bill demanded action—100 new devices each year. It called for a hard look at results. But the session ended. The bill died. Streets wait. Pedestrians keep crossing in the dark.

      Int 1151-2023, introduced August 3, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to require the Department of Transportation to install at least 100 illuminated, solar-powered traffic control devices at crosswalks each year for five years—a total of 500. The bill also ordered a study comparing these devices to unlit signs, probing their power to deter traffic violations and mapping out logistical hurdles. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the installation of solar-powered crosswalks.' Council Member Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Menin, Brooks-Powers, Rivera, and over thirty others. The bill was filed at session’s end on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The city’s crosswalks remain unchanged. Vulnerable road users remain exposed.

      Int 1142-2023
      Restler co-sponsors bill boosting tow pound capacity, improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force NYPD tow pounds to match enforcement needs. The law demanded public reports on towing and storage. The session ended before action. Streets stay clogged. Danger remains for those on foot and bike.

      Int 1142-2023 was introduced in the Committee on Public Safety on August 3, 2023. The bill required the NYPD to run tow pound facilities with enough capacity to deter illegal parking and traffic violations. It also mandated annual public reports on tow pound use and enforcement, disaggregated by precinct. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to police department tow pound capacity.' Council Members Kamillah Hanks (primary sponsor), Oswald Feliz, Sandra Ung, Lincoln Restler, Shaun Abreu, Farah N. Louis, Mercedes Narcisse, and Amanda Farías sponsored the bill. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, with no further action. No safety analyst assessed the impact for vulnerable road users. The city’s failure to expand towing leaves illegal cars on sidewalks and crosswalks. Pedestrians and cyclists pay the price.
