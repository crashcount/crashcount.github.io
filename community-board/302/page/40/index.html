      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Twelve Dead in Brooklyn—Lower the Speed, Stop the Killing

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 21, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, cleaned citations, and improved link formatting and quote attribution.)

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll, Measured in Flesh

      Twelve dead. Forty-two seriously hurt. That is the cost on Brooklyn CB2 streets since 2022. The numbers do not bleed, but people do. In the last year alone, five lives ended and ten more were shattered by injuries that will not heal. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, mothers, sons, and daughters.

      Just weeks ago, a woman was crushed at the corner of Fulton and Washington. She was 55. She died in the street, struck by an SUV. The record says only: “Crush injuries. Apparent death.” NYC Open Data.

      On State Street, a sedan slammed into a stopped SUV. A passenger died. No warning. No time to run. The record is cold: “Chest. Apparent death.” NYC Open Data.

      The Drivers: Cars, Trucks, and the Rest

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. Out of all pedestrian injuries, sedans and SUVs are the main culprits. Trucks and buses add to the toll. Bikes and motorcycles injure, but they do not kill like the cars do. The street is not safe for the slow or the small.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have voted to extend school speed zones and back bills to curb repeat speeders. Senator Jabari Brisport and Assembly Member Phara Souffrant Forrest both voted yes to extend speed cameras in school zones. The law is clear: “improving child pedestrian safety.”

      But the carnage continues. The cameras catch speeders, but the city has not lowered the default speed limit to 20 mph. The most dangerous drivers—those with a record of tickets—still roam free. The bills are on the table. The bodies are on the street.

      The Words That Remain

      A neighbor, after a hit-and-run, said it plain: “Drivers speed on that stretch of roadway.”

      A relative, after a woman was killed by a car, said: “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter.”

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      Act Now: Demand Action

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real penalties for repeat speeders. The dead cannot speak. You can. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB2 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35, assembly district AD 57 and state senate district SD 25.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB2?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, and Brooklyn Navy Yard neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33 and District 35, Assembly Districts AD 52 and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 25 and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?
      Cars and Trucks: Sedans and SUVs caused the most pedestrian injuries and deaths. Trucks and buses also contributed. Motorcycles and Mopeds: These caused fewer injuries and one death. Bikes: Bicycles injured pedestrians but did not kill. The greatest risk comes from cars and trucks. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The patterns are clear. Speed, reckless driving, and policy failures make these deaths predictable—and preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower the speed limit to 20 mph, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws to keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road. They can act now, or answer for the next death.

      S 7099
      Gounardes sponsors bill mandating complete streets, boosting safety for all users.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 7099 orders complete street design in all DOT projects with state or federal funds. Streets must serve walkers, cyclists, and riders. No more car-first blueprints. Gounardes leads. Hoylman-Sigal, Krueger back him.

      Senate bill S 7099, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York Senate. Filed May 18, 2023, the bill 'requires inclusion of complete street design for state and local transportation projects undertaken by DOT or which receive both federal and state funding and are subject to DOT oversight.' Senator Andrew Gounardes sponsors the measure. Senators Brad Hoylman-Sigal and Liz Krueger co-sponsor. The bill demands every street project under DOT control put people first—pedestrians, cyclists, and transit riders. No safety analyst note is available yet. The bill aims to end car-centric planning.

      Gounardes Urges Urgent Action to Save Lives
      ▸

      Sammy’s Law, which lets New York City set speed limits below 25 mph, failed to make the state budget. The Council now holds the power. Lawmakers stall. Streets stay deadly. Victims’ families and advocates demand action. Lives hang in the balance.

      Sammy’s Law, named for 12-year-old Sammy Cohen Eckstein, would let New York City lower speed limits below 25 mph. The bill failed to enter the 2023 state budget, shifting responsibility to the City Council. In 2021, the Council backed a home rule message 42-6, but the Assembly blocked it. Last year, the Council reversed course and failed to pass the message. This session, Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez introduced a resolution urging passage, with 10 sponsors. Transportation Committee Chair Selvena Brooks-Powers must hold a hearing before a vote. Speaker Adrienne Adams and Brooks-Powers have not signed on, citing negotiations. The bill’s summary states: 'Life-saving legislation that would allow New York City to set speed limits below 25 miles per hour failed to make it into this year's state budget.' Data show slower speeds save lives. Advocates and victims’ families keep pushing. The Council’s inaction keeps streets dangerous.
