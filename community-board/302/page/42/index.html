      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Twelve Dead, Forty-Two Broken: Brooklyn’s Streets Need Justice, Not More Excuses

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A woman steps off the curb at Fulton and Washington. An SUV crushes her. She dies on the street. Her name is not in the headlines. She is one of twelve killed in Brooklyn CB2 since 2022. Forty-two more have been left with serious injuries. Some will never walk again. Some will never speak. The numbers are not just numbers. They are lives cut short, families broken, futures erased.

      The Pattern Is Relentless

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. They killed two, left 12 with serious injuries, and hurt hundreds more. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes all add to the toll. No one is safe. Not the child on a bike. Not the old man crossing the street. Not the woman waiting for the light. The disaster moves slow, but it never stops.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      Local leaders have talked about safety. They have sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, to speed up protected bike lanes, to require daylighting at intersections. But the pace is slow. The bills sit in committee. The danger does not wait.

      “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open,” said an eyewitness after a recent hit-and-run. The driver fled. The city keeps counting the dead.

      Council Member Crystal Hudson and Assembly Member Phara Souffrant Forrest have backed safety bills. But the streets are still deadly. The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has not done so. “We want to make sure what happened doesn’t happen again,” said a neighbor. The city has not listened.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand daylight at every corner. Do not wait for another body in the street. Do not let them wait, either.

      Andrew Gounardes Supports Misguided Bill Rewarding Illegal Plate Reporting
      ▸

      Drivers hide plates. Cameras miss them. Streets stay dangerous. Senator Gounardes pushes a bill to pay citizens for reporting illegal plates. Police claim action, but advocates see little change. The bill sits in committee. Ghost cars keep rolling.

      State Senator Andrew Gounardes introduced a bill in 2022 to reward citizens who report illegal license plates. The bill remains in committee. The measure would give whistleblowers 25% of the ticket, up to $75. The matter targets drivers who obscure or remove plates to dodge automated enforcement. As city and state agencies rely more on cameras to catch speeders and red-light runners, more drivers hide their plates. The Department of Transportation says cameras failed to ticket over 4% of violators last fall, up from 1% in 2019. Advocates accuse police of lax enforcement and even breaking the law themselves. Gounardes’s bill, still stalled, aims to close this loophole and put power in the hands of citizens. Vulnerable road users remain at risk while enforcement lags.

      On December 1, 2022, Brooklyn Council Member Lincoln Restler renewed his push for Int. 501, a bill to fine drivers $175 for blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants near schools, and to reward civilians with 25% of the fine for reporting violations.       Res 0390-2022
      Restler co-sponsors QueensLink study, boosting future transit and street safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to study QueensLink. The project would reconnect Queens by rail. Sponsors say it could move thousands, cut car use, and open new parkland. The resolution died at session’s end. No action. No relief for transit deserts.

      Resolution 0390-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It called on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to conduct a comprehensive Environmental Impact Study on the viability of the proposed QueensLink project. The matter was introduced November 22, 2022, and marked as 'Filed (End of Session)' on December 31, 2023. The resolution stated: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to conduct a comprehensive Environmental Impact Study on the viability of the proposed QueensLink project.' Council Members Brooks-Powers, Ariola (primary sponsor), Holden, Restler, and Public Advocate Jumaane Williams sponsored the measure. The bill aimed to address transit deserts in Queens by restoring rail service along the old Rockaway Beach Branch, potentially serving 88,000 daily riders and creating new park space. The resolution expired without action, leaving vulnerable road users in Queens waiting for safer, car-free options.
