      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Brooklyn Streets Still Kill—City Stalls, Families Grieve

      Written July 13, 2025

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Death Toll Grows

      In Brooklyn CB2, the numbers do not lie. Twelve people have died on these streets since 2022. Forty-two more suffered serious injuries. In the last twelve months alone, five families lost someone forever. Seven hundred twenty-seven people were hurt. The wounds are not just numbers. They are broken bodies, empty chairs, and phone calls that never come.

      The Latest Crashes: No End in Sight

      The carnage does not pause. On February 28, a woman died in the back seat of a car on Flatbush Avenue. On May 17, a 55-year-old woman was crushed by an SUV at Fulton and Washington. On November 6, a 74-year-old man was killed by a bus and e-bike at Tillary and Jay. The list goes on. Most victims are people walking, cycling, or just trying to cross the street.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Member Crystal Hudson co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, aiming to clear sightlines for people on foot. She also backed the SAFE Streets Act, which would force the city to redesign dangerous intersections. But these bills are still waiting for a vote. “Completion of the Queens Boulevard redesign has reduced fatalities by 68% since work began in 2015,” the city admits. The lesson is clear: redesign saves lives, delay kills.

      Some progress is real. Speed cameras now run 24/7 in school zones (Streetsblog NYC). But loopholes remain. Drivers with dozens of tickets keep their keys. The bills to stop repeat speeders—like the Stop Super Speeders Act—are still stuck in Albany.

      The Call: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another day of blood on the street. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action on bills that force redesign and stop repeat offenders. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB2 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35, assembly district AD 57 and state senate district SD 25.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB2?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, and Brooklyn Navy Yard neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33 and District 35, Assembly Districts AD 52 and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 25 and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?
      Cars and SUVs: 2 deaths, 224 minor injuries, 106 moderate injuries, 12 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 8 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 0 deaths, 23 minor injuries, 16 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Trucks and Buses: 0 deaths, 44 minor injuries, 15 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. The city admits that redesigning streets cuts deaths by 68%. Delay is a choice, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass bills to redesign streets, ban parking near crosswalks, lower speed limits, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can push for action instead of delay.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Res 0071-2022
      Hudson co-sponsors multilingual transit announcements, boosting public safety for non-English speakers.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to speak to the city. Four languages, not just English. Riders lost in translation miss warnings, miss stops, miss safety. The bill died in committee. The silence continues. Millions still ride in the dark.

      Resolution 0071-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on March 10, 2022. The resolution urged the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make bus and subway announcements in the four most commonly spoken languages in New York City. The official title: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make bus and subway announcements in the four most commonly spoken languages in New York City.' Council Members Shahana K. Hanif (primary sponsor), Sandra Ung, Crystal Hudson, Julie Won, and Tiffany Cabán backed the measure. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided, but the intent was clear: break the language barrier, reach millions of riders, and give all New Yorkers equal warning and access on transit. The MTA still makes most announcements in English only.

      Int 0075-2022
      Restler co-sponsors moving billboard ban, boosting street safety for all.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban moving billboards. The bill would have stopped cars from carrying giant ads through city streets. Sponsors wanted to codify an existing rule. The session ended. The bill died. The danger rolls on.

      Int 0075-2022 was introduced on March 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to ban moving billboards, echoing the matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to banning moving billboards.' Council Members Gale A. Brewer (primary), Justin L. Brannan, Erik D. Bottcher, Kalman Yeger, and Lincoln Restler sponsored the measure. The bill sought to codify an existing city rule (34 RCNY 4-12(j)), making it illegal to operate, stand, or park vehicles for commercial advertising. Violators would face a $500 penalty. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Res 0009-2022
      Hudson co-sponsors resolution for accessible subways, improving safety for vulnerable riders.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to make every renovated subway station fully accessible. Only a fraction of stations have elevators. Lawmakers want no more half-measures. The resolution was filed at session’s end. Riders with disabilities remain stranded underground.

      Resolution 0009-2022 was introduced on February 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It called on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to ensure any subway station undergoing enhancement or renovation becomes fully accessible to people with disabilities. The matter title reads: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make any subway stations undergoing enhancement or renovation fully accessible to people with disabilities.' Council Member Darlene Mealy sponsored the resolution, joined by Brannan, Menin, Louis, Yeger, Hanif, Hudson, Marte, Joseph, Riley, and Brooks-Powers. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. Only 117 out of 493 subway stations are accessible. The Council’s action highlights the city’s failure to guarantee safe, equal passage for all riders. Elevators and upgrades are overdue. The bill’s filing leaves vulnerable New Yorkers waiting.
