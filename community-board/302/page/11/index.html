      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Blood on the Crosswalks: How Many More Will We Bury Before We Act?

      Written July 12, 2025

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      Twelve dead. Forty-two seriously hurt. In just over three years, Brooklyn CB2 has seen 5,209 crashes. The numbers are blunt, but the pain is sharp. A woman, 55, crushed by an SUV at Fulton and Washington. A cyclist, 74, thrown from his e-bike and killed at Tillary and Jay. A child’s knee shattered by a turning car. These are not numbers. These are lives, ended or changed forever.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and SUVs caused the most harm—344 crashes with 2 deaths and 12 serious injuries. Trucks and buses struck 60 people. Bikes, too, are not spared: 40 crashes, 1 serious injury. The street does not care who you are. It only matters who is heavier, who is faster, who walks away.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      Local leaders have pushed for change. Council Member Crystal Hudson co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and speed protected bike lanes. Senator Jabari Brisport and Assembly Member Phara Souffrant Forrest have backed safety bills. But the pace is slow. The city has started daylighting intersections, but too many corners remain blind. Every delay is a risk. Every stalled bill is another family waiting for news at the hospital.

      The Human Cost

      A single quote can cut deeper than any number. “We’re very heartbroken. He was a good person… He wanted to earn things on his own,” said Qin Xiao Chen after her loved one was killed crossing the street. “These two deaths are as horrifying as they were preventable,” said Ben Furnas after another crash. The grief is endless. The cause is not a mystery.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, not promises. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB2 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35, assembly district AD 57 and state senate district SD 25.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB2?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, and Brooklyn Navy Yard neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33 and District 35, Assembly Districts AD 52 and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 25 and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?
      Cars and SUVs: 344 crashes, 2 deaths, 12 serious injuries. Trucks and buses: 60 crashes, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 40 crashes, 1 serious injury. Cars and SUVs are the main killers.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speed, blind corners, unprotected crossings. These are preventable deaths, not random acts.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can pass and enforce laws for daylighting, lower speed limits, and protected bike lanes. They can stop stalling and start building safer streets.

      Gounardes Urges Immediate Safety Boosting Congestion Pricing Implementation
      ▸

      Governor Hochul slashes NYC’s congestion toll to $9. The move aims to beat a federal block but guts traffic reduction. Streets will see less relief. The plan leaves vulnerable road users exposed. The city trades speed and safety for political timing.

      ""It s more important than ever that we start congestion pricing immediately, before Trump can block it," said state Senator Andrew Gounardes (D-Brooklyn). "The time to commit to better public transit, less traffic and cleaner air is now."" -- Andrew Gounardes

      On November 8, 2024, Governor Hochul proposed lowering New York City’s congestion pricing toll from $15 to $9. The plan, a policy proposal to adjust congestion pricing, comes as officials rush to implement it before a new presidential administration can intervene. The original $15 toll, crafted by the Traffic Mobility Review Board and approved by the MTA Board, promised strong traffic reduction and included credits and caps. The $9 version, previously reviewed in environmental assessments, may lack those protections. Economist Charles Komanoff warns, 'You lose other benefits. Most noticeably, you don’t get the immediate traffic speed gain that a $15 toll would give.' State Senator Andrew Gounardes urges swift action, saying, 'The time to commit to better public transit, less traffic and cleaner air is now.' The lower toll is projected to improve traffic speeds by only 6.4 percent, far less than the 17 percent expected from the original plan. With less traffic reduction, streets remain dangerous for pedestrians and cyclists.
