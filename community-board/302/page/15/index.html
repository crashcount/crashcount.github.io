      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Brooklyn’s Streets Are Killing Us—Who Will Stop the Bloodshed?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Brooklyn CB2, the numbers do not lie. Eleven people killed. Forty-one left with serious injuries. More than 2,300 hurt since 2022. The dead include a 55-year-old woman crushed by an SUV on Washington Avenue, a 74-year-old man thrown from his e-bike and killed by a turning bus at Jay and Tillary, and a 45-year-old woman who died in the back seat of a car on Flatbush Avenue. Each crash is a life ended or broken. Each is a story cut short.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. They killed two people and injured more than 300 on foot. Trucks and buses added to the toll. Bikes and e-bikes hurt dozens, but the deadliest threat rolled on four wheels. The numbers are plain: the street is not safe for the unprotected.

      Leadership: Promises and Pauses

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Members Crystal Hudson and Lincoln Restler have backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and require daylighting at intersections. But the bills sit in committee. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph, but has not used it. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      The Human Cost

      The pain is not abstract. “Tiffany was literally the center of our world. She made me a better person, a better man,” said James Cifuni after his pregnant wife was run down and killed in Brooklyn. “I lost my whole family tonight and I don’t think I’ll ever be the same.”

      On another street, a bus pinned an 87-year-old man under its wheels. He was just standing near the corner when the driver turned and hit him. Police said officers who responded to the scene discovered an 87-year-old man pinned under the city bus at East 12th Street and Avenue Z in Sheepshead Bay around 6:40 p.m.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every stalled bill, every unprotected crosswalk, every ignored speed limit is a choice. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every intersection. Demand protected bike lanes that do not disappear.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Gounardes Urges Council to Use Sammy’s Law Authority
      ▸

The matter, discussed in Streetsblog, quotes Adams: “Each Council member is going to have to weigh in on how they feel it should be enacted or should not be enacted in their district.” Adams hesitated to commit to a citywide 20 mph limit, instead suggesting community-by-community decisions. Eric McClure of StreetsPAC called this approach “chaotic and dangerous,” pushing for a uniform 20 mph limit to save lives. Elizabeth Adams of Transportation Alternatives urged a comprehensive, data-driven plan. State Senator Andrew Gounardes and Brad Hoylman-Sigal pressed the Council to use its new powers. The Department of Transportation clarified its limited authority. The Council’s delay leaves vulnerable road users at risk.

      Res 0498-2024
      Restler co-sponsors MTA bus GPS data bill, no direct safety impact.
      ▸

      Council wants the MTA to share real-time GPS from every city bus with first responders. The aim: faster emergency response. Assaults on bus workers keep rising. Passengers and workers face danger. The resolution sits in committee. Action is urgent.

      Resolution 0498-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced July 18, 2024. The measure calls on the MTA to 'create a program that provides New York City’s first responder agencies with real-time Global Positioning System (GPS) data for all MTA buses.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by Amanda Farías, Gale A. Brewer, Justin L. Brannan, and Lincoln Restler. Assaults on bus workers reached 76 in 2022 and 70 in 2023. The Council argues that sharing live GPS data will let police and fire locate buses fast during emergencies. The bill targets the gap between crisis and response, aiming to protect passengers and workers when seconds count.

      Jo Anne Simon Backs Safety Boosting Citywide Daylighting Ban
      ▸

      Assembly Member Jo Anne Simon pushes a bill to ban parking near intersections citywide. The move targets deadly corners where cars block sightlines. Sixteen community boards and dozens of officials back it. DOT drags its feet. Advocates demand action.

      Assembly Member Jo Anne Simon introduced a bill to end New York City's exemption from the state law banning parking within 20 feet of intersections. The bill, now under consideration, has strong support: sixteen community boards and over three dozen elected officials have signed on. The measure aims to remove parked cars from corners, a practice called 'daylighting,' to improve visibility and cut intersection crashes. Simon called it a 'no-brainer,' saying, 'people feel a lot safer crossing those intersections.' DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez has voiced concerns that daylighting could lead to faster, more dangerous turns, but advocates like Sara Lind of Open Plans counter that daylighting is 'proven and widely popular.' The DOT missed a deadline for a daylighting safety study but claims it will daylight 1,000 intersections this year. Advocates urge the city to follow the law and protect vulnerable road users at every intersection.
