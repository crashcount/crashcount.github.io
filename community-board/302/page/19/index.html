      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Brooklyn’s Streets Are Killing Us—Who Will Stop the Bloodshed?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Brooklyn CB2, the numbers do not lie. Eleven people killed. Forty-one left with serious injuries. More than 2,300 hurt since 2022. The dead include a 55-year-old woman crushed by an SUV on Washington Avenue, a 74-year-old man thrown from his e-bike and killed by a turning bus at Jay and Tillary, and a 45-year-old woman who died in the back seat of a car on Flatbush Avenue. Each crash is a life ended or broken. Each is a story cut short.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. They killed two people and injured more than 300 on foot. Trucks and buses added to the toll. Bikes and e-bikes hurt dozens, but the deadliest threat rolled on four wheels. The numbers are plain: the street is not safe for the unprotected.

      Leadership: Promises and Pauses

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Members Crystal Hudson and Lincoln Restler have backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and require daylighting at intersections. But the bills sit in committee. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph, but has not used it. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      The Human Cost

      The pain is not abstract. “Tiffany was literally the center of our world. She made me a better person, a better man,” said James Cifuni after his pregnant wife was run down and killed in Brooklyn. “I lost my whole family tonight and I don’t think I’ll ever be the same.”

      On another street, a bus pinned an 87-year-old man under its wheels. He was just standing near the corner when the driver turned and hit him. Police said officers who responded to the scene discovered an 87-year-old man pinned under the city bus at East 12th Street and Avenue Z in Sheepshead Bay around 6:40 p.m.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every stalled bill, every unprotected crosswalk, every ignored speed limit is a choice. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every intersection. Demand protected bike lanes that do not disappear.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Restler Calls Failure on Sweeper Camera Bill Disappointing
      ▸

      Albany froze congestion pricing. Lawmakers let NYC lower speed limits and expand red light cameras. Some safety bills stalled. Advocates won ground but lost momentum. Streets remain dangerous. The fight for safer roads continues. Progress came, but not enough.

      In June 2024, the New York State legislative session ended with mixed results for street safety. Governor Hochul paused congestion pricing, stalling a major funding plan. Lawmakers passed Sammy’s Law, letting New York City lower speed limits, but exempted some roads. The city can now expand red light cameras from 150 to 600 intersections. Other measures, like parking enforcement cameras on sweepers and the Idaho Stop for cyclists, passed only one chamber. Speed governors for reckless drivers and daylighting did not advance. Advocates like Eric McClure called Sammy’s Law a 'huge victory,' but noted, 'there’s so much more the legislature could be doing.' Council Member Lincoln Restler called the failure to pass sweeper camera enforcement 'super disappointing.' The congestion pricing drama overshadowed progress. Vulnerable road users gained some protections, but systemic danger remains.

      Brisport Backs Safety Boosting Congestion Pricing Plan
      ▸

      Governor Hochul froze New York’s congestion pricing days before launch. Lawmakers and advocates called her move illegal. The MTA faces lost funds, stalled upgrades, and mounting frustration. Transit riders and vulnerable road users are left exposed as car traffic surges unchecked.

      On June 10, 2024, Governor Kathy Hochul abruptly paused New York City’s congestion pricing program, which was set to begin June 30. The 2019 law mandates the state and MTA 'shall' implement congestion pricing, not 'may.' U.S. Rep. Ritchie Torres said, 'When the will of the Executive competes with an Act of the Legislature, the law wins. Or should.' MTA board members, caught off guard, warned that without toll revenue, modernization projects—like electric buses and ADA stations—will be deprioritized. State Sen. Jabari Brisport and Riders Alliance noted a surge in public support for the program after Hochul’s decision. Hochul cited safety and community concerns, but advocates say her move robs New Yorkers of a vital tool to fund transit and cut traffic and pollution. The program’s future remains uncertain, with vulnerable road users facing continued risk from unchecked car traffic.

      A 7652
      Forrest votes yes, advancing school speed cameras that improve pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Assembly passes A 7652. The bill launches speed cameras near Schenectady schools. Cameras catch drivers who speed. The program ends in 2028 unless renewed. Most members vote yes. Some vote no. Streets near schools remain risky for children on foot.

      Bill A 7652, titled "Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the city of Schenectady," advanced with a committee vote in the Assembly on June 7, 2024. The measure creates a pilot program to install speed cameras near schools, aiming to catch drivers who exceed posted limits. The authorization for the program expires December 31, 2028. Assemblymembers Phil Steck (District 110, primary sponsor) and Angelo Santabarbara (District 111, co-sponsor) led the push. The vote passed with strong support but faced opposition from several members. The bill’s summary reads: "Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the city of Schenectady; repeals authorization of program December 31, 2028." No formal safety analyst note was provided, but the measure targets driver speed in zones where children walk, bike, and cross.
