      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Brooklyn Streets Still Kill—City Stalls, Families Grieve

      Written July 13, 2025

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Death Toll Grows

      In Brooklyn CB2, the numbers do not lie. Twelve people have died on these streets since 2022. Forty-two more suffered serious injuries. In the last twelve months alone, five families lost someone forever. Seven hundred twenty-seven people were hurt. The wounds are not just numbers. They are broken bodies, empty chairs, and phone calls that never come.

      The Latest Crashes: No End in Sight

      The carnage does not pause. On February 28, a woman died in the back seat of a car on Flatbush Avenue. On May 17, a 55-year-old woman was crushed by an SUV at Fulton and Washington. On November 6, a 74-year-old man was killed by a bus and e-bike at Tillary and Jay. The list goes on. Most victims are people walking, cycling, or just trying to cross the street.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Member Crystal Hudson co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, aiming to clear sightlines for people on foot. She also backed the SAFE Streets Act, which would force the city to redesign dangerous intersections. But these bills are still waiting for a vote. “Completion of the Queens Boulevard redesign has reduced fatalities by 68% since work began in 2015,” the city admits. The lesson is clear: redesign saves lives, delay kills.

      Some progress is real. Speed cameras now run 24/7 in school zones (Streetsblog NYC). But loopholes remain. Drivers with dozens of tickets keep their keys. The bills to stop repeat speeders—like the Stop Super Speeders Act—are still stuck in Albany.

      The Call: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another day of blood on the street. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action on bills that force redesign and stop repeat offenders. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB2 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35, assembly district AD 57 and state senate district SD 25.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB2?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, and Brooklyn Navy Yard neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33 and District 35, Assembly Districts AD 52 and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 25 and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?
      Cars and SUVs: 2 deaths, 224 minor injuries, 106 moderate injuries, 12 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 8 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 0 deaths, 23 minor injuries, 16 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Trucks and Buses: 0 deaths, 44 minor injuries, 15 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. The city admits that redesigning streets cuts deaths by 68%. Delay is a choice, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass bills to redesign streets, ban parking near crosswalks, lower speed limits, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can push for action instead of delay.

      Int 0492-2024
      Hudson Supports Safety Boosting Crackdown on Illegal Curb Cuts
      ▸

      Council Member Hudson targets rogue curb cuts. The bill forces DOT to probe complaints fast. Illegally carved driveways get painted green. Owners must fix or permit them. Community boards get a heads-up on new curb cut bids. Streets may get safer.

      Int 0492-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced February 28, 2024, by Council Member Crystal Hudson, the bill demands the Department of Transportation investigate illegal curb cut complaints within 30 days. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to illegal curb cuts and requiring local community board notification of curb cut applications.' If DOT finds an illegal curb cut, it paints the curb green for parking and orders owners to legalize or remove it. If owners refuse, DOT fixes it and bills them. Community boards must be notified of all new curb cut applications. Hudson sponsored the bill. The measure aims to curb sidewalk invasions and keep local voices in the loop.
