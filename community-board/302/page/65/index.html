      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Brooklyn’s Streets Are Killing Us—Who Will Stop the Bloodshed?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Brooklyn CB2, the numbers do not lie. Eleven people killed. Forty-one left with serious injuries. More than 2,300 hurt since 2022. The dead include a 55-year-old woman crushed by an SUV on Washington Avenue, a 74-year-old man thrown from his e-bike and killed by a turning bus at Jay and Tillary, and a 45-year-old woman who died in the back seat of a car on Flatbush Avenue. Each crash is a life ended or broken. Each is a story cut short.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. They killed two people and injured more than 300 on foot. Trucks and buses added to the toll. Bikes and e-bikes hurt dozens, but the deadliest threat rolled on four wheels. The numbers are plain: the street is not safe for the unprotected.

      Leadership: Promises and Pauses

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Members Crystal Hudson and Lincoln Restler have backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and require daylighting at intersections. But the bills sit in committee. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph, but has not used it. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      The Human Cost

      The pain is not abstract. “Tiffany was literally the center of our world. She made me a better person, a better man,” said James Cifuni after his pregnant wife was run down and killed in Brooklyn. “I lost my whole family tonight and I don’t think I’ll ever be the same.”

      On another street, a bus pinned an 87-year-old man under its wheels. He was just standing near the corner when the driver turned and hit him. Police said officers who responded to the scene discovered an 87-year-old man pinned under the city bus at East 12th Street and Avenue Z in Sheepshead Bay around 6:40 p.m.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every stalled bill, every unprotected crosswalk, every ignored speed limit is a choice. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every intersection. Demand protected bike lanes that do not disappear.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Gounardes Supports Safety Boosting Mandatory Driver Instruction
      ▸

      Senate Bill 8667 would force new drivers to log six hours with a professional instructor. Gounardes and Carroll say the current system is lax. They want more training. Safety leaders back the move. DOT is reviewing. Streets stay deadly.

      Senate Bill 8667, introduced by Sen. Andrew Gounardes and Assemblymember Bobby Carroll, would require all new drivers in New York State to complete six hours of professional driving instruction before taking the road test. The bill was announced on March 29, 2022. Gounardes slammed the current rules as too lenient, saying, "For some drivers, the first time they take the wheel [as a licensed driver] they are in sole control of their car." Carroll called the requirement "reasonable." Stephen Walling, president of the New York State Association of Professional Driving Schools, said the bill would "go a long way towards keeping all New Yorkers safer on our roads." The NYC Department of Transportation called driver education "crucial" for the safety of pedestrians, cyclists, and other road users, and is reviewing the proposal. The bill awaits committee action.

      Res 0071-2022
      Hudson co-sponsors multilingual transit announcements, boosting public safety for non-English speakers.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to speak to the city. Four languages, not just English. Riders lost in translation miss warnings, miss stops, miss safety. The bill died in committee. The silence continues. Millions still ride in the dark.

      Resolution 0071-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on March 10, 2022. The resolution urged the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make bus and subway announcements in the four most commonly spoken languages in New York City. The official title: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make bus and subway announcements in the four most commonly spoken languages in New York City.' Council Members Shahana K. Hanif (primary sponsor), Sandra Ung, Crystal Hudson, Julie Won, and Tiffany Cabán backed the measure. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided, but the intent was clear: break the language barrier, reach millions of riders, and give all New Yorkers equal warning and access on transit. The MTA still makes most announcements in English only.

      Int 0075-2022
      Restler co-sponsors moving billboard ban, boosting street safety for all.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban moving billboards. The bill would have stopped cars from carrying giant ads through city streets. Sponsors wanted to codify an existing rule. The session ended. The bill died. The danger rolls on.

      Int 0075-2022 was introduced on March 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to ban moving billboards, echoing the matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to banning moving billboards.' Council Members Gale A. Brewer (primary), Justin L. Brannan, Erik D. Bottcher, Kalman Yeger, and Lincoln Restler sponsored the measure. The bill sought to codify an existing city rule (34 RCNY 4-12(j)), making it illegal to operate, stand, or park vehicles for commercial advertising. Violators would face a $500 penalty. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Crystal Hudson Supports Safety Boosting Willoughby Avenue Open Street
      ▸

      Tempers flared as Fort Greene residents clashed over the Willoughby Avenue open street. Barriers vanished without warning, then returned after public outcry. Councilmember Crystal Hudson stood firm. DOT cited rising crash deaths. Most neighbors backed the car-free stretch.

      On March 8, 2022, Councilmember Crystal Hudson hosted a heated debate on the Willoughby Avenue open street in District 35. The meeting followed the abrupt, unannounced removal of barriers from the eight-block stretch, which sparked swift backlash. Hudson, joined by DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodríguez, faced vocal opponents worried about lost parking and emergency access. Hudson said, 'This is about more than just open streets, this is about safe streets.' DOT surveys showed 88 percent support for permanent safety changes. Mayor Eric Adams ordered the street restored after learning Hudson opposed its closure. Rodríguez highlighted a spike in traffic deaths, noting, 'We have a responsibility to think about, first of all, protecting our senior citizens.' According to NYC Crash Mapper, 72 people—mostly cyclists and motorists—were injured on this stretch in the past decade, but no deaths. The open streets program, launched during the pandemic, remains popular and aims to protect vulnerable road users.
