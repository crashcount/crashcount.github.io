      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Brooklyn Bleeds While City Sleeps: Demand 20 MPH Now

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 16, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Twelve people are dead. Forty-two have suffered serious injuries. In just over three years, Brooklyn CB2 has seen 5,209 crashes. The names fade, but the pain does not. A woman, 55, crushed by an SUV at Fulton and Washington. A cyclist, 74, killed by a bus turning at Tillary and Jay. A passenger, 45, gone in a tangle of metal on Flatbush and State. The dead do not get second chances. The living carry scars.

      Most victims are not behind the wheel. Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and SUVs are the main weapons—344 pedestrian injuries and deaths from cars and SUVs alone. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll. The streets are not safe for the old, the young, or anyone on foot.

      Leadership: Progress and Gaps

      Local leaders have acted, but the pace is slow. Senator Jabari Brisport and Senator Andrew Gounardes voted yes to extend school speed zones and to require speed limiters for repeat offenders. These votes matter. They save lives. But the work is not finished. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so. Cameras catch speeders, but the worst repeat offenders still drive. The city can do more. It must.

      “Speed cameras save lives and keep New Yorkers safe,” said Governor Hochul as she signed the renewal into law. But cameras alone are not enough. “Our message to drivers was clear: follow the rules or face the law,” said NYPD Commissioner Jessica Tisch. The message is clear. The streets are not.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by the city, the state, and those who hold power. The dead cannot speak. The living must. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real consequences for repeat offenders. Join the fight. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Act now. The next life lost could be someone you love.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB2 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35, assembly district AD 57 and state senate district SD 25.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB2?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, and Brooklyn Navy Yard neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33 and District 35, Assembly Districts AD 52 and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 25 and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?
      Cars and SUVs: 344 injuries and deaths from cars and SUVs; Trucks and Buses: 60 injuries and deaths. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 13 injuries and deaths. Bikes: 40 injuries and deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speeding, red-light running, repeat offenders. These are preventable crashes, not random accidents.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws that target repeat dangerous drivers. They can redesign streets to protect people walking and biking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Gounardes Backs Safety Boosting Speed Camera Expansion
      ▸

      Speed cameras slash reckless driving. At school zones, speeding drops 94 percent. But the program expires soon. DOT Commissioner Rodriguez urges Albany to act. State Sen. Gounardes backs expansion. Cameras save lives. Delay risks more deaths. Lawmakers hold the key.

      Bill to reauthorize New York City's speed camera program is pending in Albany. The program, covering 750 school zones, needs state approval before June. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez calls speed cameras 'one of the most effective tools' to stop deadly driving. State Sen. Andrew Gounardes, who sponsored the last reauthorization, says, 'my bill to expand the speed camera program has saved lives.' The city wants stronger penalties for repeat offenders and action on license plate fraud, which lets millions of violations go unpunished. The report shows cameras cut speeding by 94 percent and reduce injuries and deaths. The program faces political hurdles, but the evidence is clear: speed cameras protect people on foot and bike. Lawmakers must decide whether to keep this life-saving tool.

      A 2299
      Forrest co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 2299 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets in a year triggers forced speed control tech. Lawmakers move to curb repeat speeders. Streets demand fewer deadly risks.

      Assembly bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 16, 2025, the bill 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' The measure aims to clamp down on repeat speeders with mandatory speed-limiting tech. No safety analyst note was provided.
