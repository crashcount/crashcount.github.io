      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Twelve Dead in Brooklyn—Lower the Speed, Stop the Killing

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, cleaned citations, and improved link formatting and quote attribution.)

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll, Measured in Flesh

      Twelve dead. Forty-two seriously hurt. That is the cost on Brooklyn CB2 streets since 2022. The numbers do not bleed, but people do. In the last year alone, five lives ended and ten more were shattered by injuries that will not heal. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, mothers, sons, and daughters.

      Just weeks ago, a woman was crushed at the corner of Fulton and Washington. She was 55. She died in the street, struck by an SUV. The record says only: “Crush injuries. Apparent death.” NYC Open Data.

      On State Street, a sedan slammed into a stopped SUV. A passenger died. No warning. No time to run. The record is cold: “Chest. Apparent death.” NYC Open Data.

      The Drivers: Cars, Trucks, and the Rest

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. Out of all pedestrian injuries, sedans and SUVs are the main culprits. Trucks and buses add to the toll. Bikes and motorcycles injure, but they do not kill like the cars do. The street is not safe for the slow or the small.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have voted to extend school speed zones and back bills to curb repeat speeders. Senator Jabari Brisport and Assembly Member Phara Souffrant Forrest both voted yes to extend speed cameras in school zones. The law is clear: “improving child pedestrian safety.”

      But the carnage continues. The cameras catch speeders, but the city has not lowered the default speed limit to 20 mph. The most dangerous drivers—those with a record of tickets—still roam free. The bills are on the table. The bodies are on the street.

      The Words That Remain

      A neighbor, after a hit-and-run, said it plain: “Drivers speed on that stretch of roadway.”

      A relative, after a woman was killed by a car, said: “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter.”

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      Act Now: Demand Action

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real penalties for repeat speeders. The dead cannot speak. You can. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB2 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35, assembly district AD 57 and state senate district SD 25.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB2?
      It includes the Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, and Brooklyn Navy Yard neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33 and District 35, Assembly Districts AD 52 and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 25 and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?
      Cars and Trucks: Sedans and SUVs caused the most pedestrian injuries and deaths. Trucks and buses also contributed. Motorcycles and Mopeds: These caused fewer injuries and one death. Bikes: Bicycles injured pedestrians but did not kill. The greatest risk comes from cars and trucks. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The patterns are clear. Speed, reckless driving, and policy failures make these deaths predictable—and preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can lower the speed limit to 20 mph, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws to keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road. They can act now, or answer for the next death.

      Council orders swift removal of abandoned, derelict cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. No plates, no stickers, no excuses. Police and sanitation must act. Safer crossings for all who walk, ride, or wait.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council on June 30, 2025. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to vehicles reported as abandoned to the department of sanitation," requires the Department of Sanitation to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours of report. The NYPD must tow cars lacking valid plates or stickers. Prime sponsor Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led, joined by Crystal Hudson, Amanda Farías, Lincoln Restler, and others. The law targets street hazards, clearing blocked sightlines and crosswalks. It aims to cut risks for pedestrians and cyclists by removing abandoned vehicles fast.

      Gounardes Praises Safety Boosting Speed Camera Renewal
      ▸

      Albany keeps speed cameras rolling. Lawmakers extend the city’s program to 2030. Cameras catch speeders near schools. Streets get safer for walkers and riders. Fewer crashes, fewer deaths. A hard-won victory for the vulnerable. The fight against traffic violence continues.

      ""The verdict came in a long time ago: speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone... At the end of the day, this is about keeping New Yorkers safe. I'm proud to have fought for this program from the beginning, and to renew it now for five years."" -- Andrew Gounardes

      On June 18, 2025, the New York State Legislature reauthorized the city’s speed camera program for five more years, extending it until July 1, 2030. The bill passed both the Senate and Assembly and awaits Governor Hochul’s signature. The measure, described as 'possibly the most important street safety law on the books,' covers 750 school zones. Sen. Andrew Gounardes praised the program, saying, 'speed cameras save lives and make our streets safer for everyone.' DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez credited advocates and lawmakers for the renewal. Amy Sohn supported the move. Safety analysts note that speed cameras reduce vehicle speeds and crashes, improving safety for pedestrians and cyclists without burdening them. The reauthorization marks a rare, clear win for vulnerable road users in the city’s ongoing battle against traffic violence.

      S 7785
      Forrest votes yes to weaken bus rules, increasing pedestrian and cyclist risk.
      ▸

      Senate passed S 7785. The bill carves out large Mitchell-Lama housing from bus traffic rules. Lawmakers voted yes. The carve-out weakens enforcement. Streets grow less safe for people on foot and bike.

      Bill S 7785, titled 'Relates to bus operation-related traffic regulations,' passed the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. The measure, sponsored by Senator Jamaal Bailey, excludes violations in cooperative housing developments with at least 10,000 Mitchell-Lama units from bus operation traffic regulations. The bill advanced through committee and received broad support in both chambers. By exempting these large complexes, the law weakens traffic enforcement where thousands live. This move increases risk to pedestrians and cyclists, stripping away protections that save lives. Vulnerable road users lose another layer of safety in dense city streets.
