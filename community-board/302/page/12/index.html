      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Twelve Dead, Forty-Two Broken: Brooklyn’s Streets Need Justice, Not More Excuses

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A woman steps off the curb at Fulton and Washington. An SUV crushes her. She dies on the street. Her name is not in the headlines. She is one of twelve killed in Brooklyn CB2 since 2022. Forty-two more have been left with serious injuries. Some will never walk again. Some will never speak. The numbers are not just numbers. They are lives cut short, families broken, futures erased.

      The Pattern Is Relentless

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. They killed two, left 12 with serious injuries, and hurt hundreds more. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes all add to the toll. No one is safe. Not the child on a bike. Not the old man crossing the street. Not the woman waiting for the light. The disaster moves slow, but it never stops.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      Local leaders have talked about safety. They have sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, to speed up protected bike lanes, to require daylighting at intersections. But the pace is slow. The bills sit in committee. The danger does not wait.

      “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open,” said an eyewitness after a recent hit-and-run. The driver fled. The city keeps counting the dead.

      Council Member Crystal Hudson and Assembly Member Phara Souffrant Forrest have backed safety bills. But the streets are still deadly. The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has not done so. “We want to make sure what happened doesn’t happen again,” said a neighbor. The city has not listened.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand daylight at every corner. Do not wait for another body in the street. Do not let them wait, either.

      Restler co-sponsors bill to raise SUV fees, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council backs a state bill to hike registration fees for big, heavy vehicles. The move targets SUVs and trucks that kill and injure New Yorkers. Sponsors say the law will cut deaths, push smaller cars, and clear the air. The fight is on.

      Resolution 0601-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced October 10, 2024, it urges Albany to pass S.6657A/A.7978.       Int 1050-2024
      Hudson co-sponsors bill capping TLC insurance, potentially reducing street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1050-2024 blocks the Taxi and Limousine Commission from demanding more injury coverage than state law. The measure, laid over in committee, shields cab companies from higher insurance costs. Passengers and crash victims face tighter limits on payouts.

      Int 1050-2024 was introduced on September 26, 2024, and is now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to limiting the amount of liability coverage that the taxi and limousine commission may require for vehicles it licenses," bars the TLC from requiring more personal injury protection than state law demands. Council Member Diana I. Ayala is the primary sponsor, joined by over two dozen co-sponsors including De La Rosa, Banks, Ossé, Marte, and others. The bill was laid over by committee on February 10, 2025. The measure would take effect immediately if passed. By capping required coverage, the bill may leave crash victims with less compensation after collisions involving TLC-licensed vehicles.

      Int 0346-2024
      Hudson votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.

      Int 1069-2024
      Restler co-sponsors bill reducing commercial parking time, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1069-2024 slashes legal parking time for tractor-trailers and similar trucks to 90 minutes. Commercial vehicles clogging curbs will face tighter limits. The measure targets streets where hulking rigs linger, blocking sightlines and endangering walkers and riders.

      Int 1069-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on September 26, 2024. The bill reads: "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to reducing the maximum time commercial vehicles may park." It limits parking for tractor-trailer combinations, tractors, truck trailers, and semi-trailers to 90 minutes, down from three hours. Council Member Kevin C. Riley leads as primary sponsor, joined by Nantasha M. Williams, Darlene Mealy, Sandy Nurse, and others. The bill was referred to committee the day it was introduced. No safety analyst has yet assessed the direct impact on vulnerable road users, but the measure aims to keep large trucks from blocking curbs and crosswalks, where they threaten pedestrians and cyclists.
