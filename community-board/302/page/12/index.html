      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB2?

      Brooklyn’s Streets Are Killing Us—Who Will Stop the Bloodshed?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Brooklyn CB2, the numbers do not lie. Eleven people killed. Forty-one left with serious injuries. More than 2,300 hurt since 2022. The dead include a 55-year-old woman crushed by an SUV on Washington Avenue, a 74-year-old man thrown from his e-bike and killed by a turning bus at Jay and Tillary, and a 45-year-old woman who died in the back seat of a car on Flatbush Avenue. Each crash is a life ended or broken. Each is a story cut short.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. They killed two people and injured more than 300 on foot. Trucks and buses added to the toll. Bikes and e-bikes hurt dozens, but the deadliest threat rolled on four wheels. The numbers are plain: the street is not safe for the unprotected.

      Leadership: Promises and Pauses

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Members Crystal Hudson and Lincoln Restler have backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and require daylighting at intersections. But the bills sit in committee. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph, but has not used it. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      The Human Cost

      The pain is not abstract. “Tiffany was literally the center of our world. She made me a better person, a better man,” said James Cifuni after his pregnant wife was run down and killed in Brooklyn. “I lost my whole family tonight and I don’t think I’ll ever be the same.”

      On another street, a bus pinned an 87-year-old man under its wheels. He was just standing near the corner when the driver turned and hit him. Police said officers who responded to the scene discovered an 87-year-old man pinned under the city bus at East 12th Street and Avenue Z in Sheepshead Bay around 6:40 p.m.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every stalled bill, every unprotected crosswalk, every ignored speed limit is a choice. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every intersection. Demand protected bike lanes that do not disappear.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Crystal Hudson Urges All Neighborhoods to Share Housing Burden
      ▸

      Council weakened Adams’s zoning plan. Parking mandates stay in low-density zones. Central areas lose mandates. Housing projections drop. Some neighborhoods bear the burden. Car-centric rules persist. Vulnerable road users see little relief. The city’s streets remain dangerous.

      Bill: City of Yes zoning reform. Status: Amended and advanced by City Council on November 22, 2024. The Council’s action, described as 'watering down' the mayor’s plan, keeps mandatory parking in low-density neighborhoods while ending it in central areas. The matter summary states: 'ending mandatory parking in areas with the best transit while keeping the costly mandate in low-density neighborhoods where it most hinders development.' Council Member Crystal Hudson voted yes, urging all neighborhoods to share the housing burden. Council Member Kevin Riley defended single-family zones. Council Member Rafael Salamanca cited balancing concerns and securing $5 billion for affordable housing. Borough President Antonio Reynoso warned of increased housing pressure elsewhere. The Council’s changes preserve car-centric zoning, leaving vulnerable road users exposed and limiting progress on safer, less car-dependent streets.

      Hudson Backs Safety Boosting Curbside Garbage Containerization Bill
      ▸

      Council Member Hudson pushes a bill to charge owners of 10- to 30-unit buildings for curbside trash bins. The law sets a 2032 deadline for citywide containerization. Critics warn the plan moves ahead before pilot results. Streets may change, but risks remain.

      Bill introduced November 20, 2024, by Council Member Crystal Hudson (D-Brooklyn) targets 10- to 30-unit buildings, requiring property owners to pay up to $55 per unit per year for city-installed curbside garbage containers. The bill, now before the City Council, mandates a citywide shift to containerized waste by 2032. The official matter summary: 'Owners of mid-sized buildings would have to pay to maintain and install curbside trash bins as part of the city's push to move trash from the sidewalk to the street.' Hudson claims the law is 'essential' for progress. DSNY Commissioner Jessica Tisch backs the bill, calling it vital for full-scale containerization. Clare Miflin, Center for Zero Waste Design, objects: 'If you’re doing a pilot, evaluate a pilot before you mandate.' The Council will hold a hearing on Hudson’s bill and related measures. No safety analyst has assessed the impact on vulnerable road users.

      Gounardes Urges Immediate Safety Boosting Congestion Pricing Implementation
      ▸

      Governor Hochul cuts the congestion toll to $9. The move aims to beat a Trump block. Experts warn the lower fee will not cut traffic like the original $15 plan. Urgency grows as the MTA stalls projects. Vulnerable road users wait for relief.

      On November 8, 2024, State Senator Andrew Gounardes responded to Governor Hochul’s revised congestion pricing plan. The new proposal drops the peak toll from $15 to $9. The plan’s status is pending, with urgency to implement before a new presidential administration. Gounardes stressed, “It’s more important than ever that we start congestion pricing immediately, before Trump can block it.” The bill aims to fund the MTA’s 2020-2024 capital plan, but economist Charles Komanoff warns, “You don’t get the immediate traffic speed gain that a $15 toll would give.” The measure is meant to reduce traffic and improve transit, but the lower toll means less impact. The MTA has already paused $16 billion in work. The focus is on starting the program, but advocates note the diluted toll will not deliver the same safety and quality-of-life gains for pedestrians and cyclists.
