      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB17?

      Brooklyn’s Blood Price: City Streets Still Kill, Leaders Still Stall

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      Brooklyn CB17: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      The street does not forgive. Since 2022, nine people have died in Brooklyn CB17. Forty more have been seriously injured. The numbers do not show the faces, but the pain is real. In the last twelve months alone, four people died. A woman, 68, was crushed crossing with the signal at Rutland Road and E 95th. A 74-year-old woman was killed on Avenue D. A man, 30, died on Church Avenue. The dead do not get second chances.

      Most victims are on foot or bike. Cars and SUVs did the most harm—over 500 crashes, four deaths, and seven serious injuries to pedestrians. Trucks killed two more. Motorcycles and mopeds left bodies broken. The street is a gauntlet for the old, the young, and everyone in between.

      The Voices: Aftermath and Anger

      After the crash, the street is quiet. But the words remain. “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter,” said Ruiz’s sister-in-law. The grief does not fade. Another voice cuts through: “I have no idea why he was doing donuts in the parking lot,” said Griselda Caraballo, after a driver killed a young woman in a parking lot stunt. The tire marks lingered for days.

      The Response: What Leaders Do—and Don’t

      Local leaders have acted, but not enough. Council Member Farah Louis sponsored bills for safer bike share and senior discounts, but also a bill to cap e-bike speeds at 15 mph—a move that shifts blame to riders, not drivers. State Senator Kevin Parker voted yes to curb repeat speeders and extend school speed zones. But the carnage continues. Every delay is another risk.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand Now

      This is not fate. This is policy. Contact your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against reckless drivers, not just cyclists. Join Families for Safe Streets. Stand up before another family gets the call. The street will not change itself. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB17 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 45, assembly district AD 58 and state senate district SD 21.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB17?
      It includes the East Flatbush-Erasmus, East Flatbush-Farragut, East Flatbush-Rugby, East Flatbush-Remsen Village, and Holy Cross Cemetery neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 40, District 41, District 42, and District 45, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 42, AD 43, and AD 58, and State Senate Districts SD 19, SD 20, and SD 21.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB17?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians: 4 deaths, 403 minor injuries, 137 moderate injuries, and 7 serious injuries. Trucks caused 1 death and 17 minor injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 8 injuries (no deaths). Bikes caused 5 injuries (no deaths).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speeding, failure to yield, reckless driving. These are preventable with better policy and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, crack down on reckless drivers, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking—not just drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
