      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB17?

      Brooklyn’s Streets Are Killing Us—Who Will Stop the Bloodshed?

      Written July 12, 2025

      Brooklyn CB17: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Brooklyn CB17, the numbers do not lie. Nine people are dead. Forty more have been seriously injured. Since 2022, there have been over 5,100 crashes. The bodies pile up, but the engines never stop.

      The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors. A 68-year-old woman, crossing with the signal at Rutland Road and East 95th, was killed by a turning sedan. A 74-year-old woman, walking with the light at Avenue D and New York Avenue, struck down by a driver who failed to yield. A 47-year-old man, crushed by a truck on Linden Boulevard. Each crossing, a risk. Each day, a gamble.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the harm: 4 killed, 7 seriously hurt, hundreds more injured. Trucks and buses killed 2. Motorcycles and mopeds, 0 deaths but 4 serious injuries. Bikes, none. The street is not equal. The bigger the machine, the greater the damage.

      What Leaders Have Done — And Not Done

      Council Member Farah N. Louis has backed some safety bills. She voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the desperate and the poor. She co-sponsored bills for speed humps, raised speed reducers, and better lighting. She voted for protected bike lanes and truck route redesigns. These are steps, but the blood still runs.

      But the city moves slow. The dead do not wait. As one official said after a double fatality on Third Avenue, “These two deaths are as horrifying as they were preventable, and it’s especially gutting when two New Yorkers are killed on a street that the community and City Hall both know is dangerous.”

      A sister mourns: “We’re very heartbroken. He was a good person. In China, he was a vegetable farmer. We don’t know where he was going at that hour. He was very independent and true to his word. He wanted to earn things on his own.” NY Daily News

      What You Can Do Now

      The crisis is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for every crosswalk and bike lane. Join groups like Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name on the list. The street will not change itself. You must force it.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB17 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 45, assembly district AD 58 and state senate district SD 21.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB17?
      It includes the East Flatbush-Erasmus, East Flatbush-Farragut, East Flatbush-Rugby, East Flatbush-Remsen Village, and Holy Cross Cemetery neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 40, District 41, District 42, and District 45, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 42, AD 43, and AD 58, and State Senate Districts SD 19, SD 20, and SD 21.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB17?
      Cars and SUVs: 4 deaths, 7 serious injuries. Trucks and buses: 2 deaths, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 4 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries. Cars and SUVs do the most harm.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data and quotes show these deaths and injuries are preventable. As one official said after a double fatality on Third Avenue, “These two deaths are as horrifying as they were preventable… on a street that the community and City Hall both know is dangerous.”
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, build real protection for crosswalks and bike lanes, and redesign streets to slow cars. They can refuse to delay or water down safety measures.

      Int 0662-2022
      Louis co-sponsors bill to create parking permit enforcement, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to create a parking permit enforcement unit in DOT. The unit would target misuse of city-issued permits. The measure died at session’s end. Streets stay clogged. Danger lingers for those on foot and bike.

      Int 0662-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on September 14, 2022. The bill sought to amend city law by establishing a parking permit enforcement unit within the Department of Transportation. Its summary reads: 'This bill would require the Department of Transportation to create a parking permit enforcement unit that would be dedicated to the enforcement of laws and rules relating to misuse of city-issued parking permits.' The primary sponsor was Kristin Richardson Jordan, joined by Amanda Farías, Selvena N. Brooks-Powers, Julie Won, Rita C. Joseph, Crystal Hudson, Farah N. Louis, and Lincoln Restler. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, with no further action. No safety analyst assessed its impact on vulnerable road users. The city’s permit abuse problem remains unaddressed.
