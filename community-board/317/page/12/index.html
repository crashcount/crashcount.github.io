      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB17?

      Brooklyn Blood on Their Hands: End the Waiting, End the Killing

      Written July 14, 2025

      Brooklyn CB17: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 14, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      Four dead. Over a thousand injured. In the last twelve months, the streets of Brooklyn CB17 have not let up. The dead include a 30-year-old man struck by a sedan on Church Avenue, a 68-year-old woman crossing with the signal on Rutland Road, and a 74-year-old woman killed at Avenue D and New York Avenue. Each was walking. Each was hit by a car. Each is gone.

      The numbers are blunt. 808 crashes so far this year. Four killed. Seven left with injuries so serious they may never recover. The pain is not spread evenly. Children, elders, people just trying to cross the street. The most vulnerable pay the price.

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and the Usual Suspects

      Cars and SUVs did most of the harm. Out of all pedestrian injuries and deaths, sedans and SUVs were behind the wheel in over 550 cases. Trucks and buses killed two. Motorcycles and mopeds injured a handful. Bikes, five. The pattern is old. The outcome is not fate. It is design.

      The Human Cost

      A woman, 68, crossing with the light. A sedan turns left. She is struck, killed. The cause: driver inattention. A 74-year-old, crossing with the signal. Another left turn. Another life ended. A 30-year-old man, hit by a speeding sedan. The crash report lists “unsafe speed”. The street does not forgive.

      Neighbors see it. They know the danger. “Drivers speed on that stretch of roadway,” said one. The numbers back them up. In five years, one three-block stretch saw 59 crashes and 80 injuries. The city counts the dead. The living count the days until the next siren.

      Leadership: Steps and Stalls

      Council Member Farah N. Louis has backed bills for more speed humps, raised crosswalks, and better lighting. She voted to redesign truck routes and daylight intersections. But the pace is slow. The danger is not. Assembly Member Monique Chandler-Waterman missed a key vote to extend school speed zones. The streets wait. The bodies pile up.

      What Now: Demand Action

      This is not an act of God. It is a policy choice. Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Tell them: Lower the speed limit. Build real protection for people walking and biking. Stop waiting.

      Every day they delay, someone else bleeds in the street.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB17 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 45, assembly district AD 58 and state senate district SD 21.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB17?
      It includes the East Flatbush-Erasmus, East Flatbush-Farragut, East Flatbush-Rugby, East Flatbush-Remsen Village, and Holy Cross Cemetery neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 40, District 41, District 42, and District 45, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 42, AD 43, and AD 58, and State Senate Districts SD 19, SD 20, and SD 21.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB17?
      Cars and Trucks: Sedans and SUVs were involved in over 550 pedestrian injuries and deaths. Trucks and buses killed two. Motorcycles and Mopeds: Caused a handful of injuries. Bikes: Five injuries, no deaths. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or just 'accidents'?
      These deaths and injuries are not random. They follow patterns—speed, failure to yield, unsafe streets. They can be prevented with lower speed limits, better design, and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, daylight intersections, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking. They can act now, not after the next tragedy.

      Int 0745-2024
      Louis is excused from voting on neutral-impact micromobility data bill.
      ▸

      The council passed a law forcing DOT to reveal bike and micromobility numbers. Streets and bridges get counted. Protected lanes get tracked. The city must show where riders go and where danger lurks. Data goes public. Riders get seen.

      Int 0745-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...requiring the department of transportation to provide information about bicycle and other micromobility device activity,' was introduced April 11, 2024. It passed committee and full council votes on August 15, 2024, and became law September 14, 2024, as Local Law 88 of 2024. Council Members Selvena N. Brooks-Powers (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, Lynn C. Schulman, Crystal Hudson, Lincoln Restler, and others backed the measure. The law compels DOT to publish monthly and annual data on bike and micromobility use, including counts from automated sensors. DOT must also describe safety projects and analyze crash and usage data to guide improvements. This law forces transparency. It puts the movement and safety of vulnerable riders on record.
