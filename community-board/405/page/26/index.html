      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB5?

      Steel Wins. Flesh Loses. Queens Streets Demand Justice.

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      Queens CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Queens CB5, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-two people killed. Thirty seriously injured. Over 2,600 hurt since 2022 (NYC Open Data). The dead do not get a second chance. The injured carry scars the rest of us cannot see.

      Just this spring, a cyclist was crushed by a truck on Maurice Avenue. The driver failed to yield. The man died from head injuries. He was 47. The data says nothing about his name, only that he is gone (NYC Open Data CrashID 4705063).

      A child cyclist was killed by a turning box truck at Juniper Boulevard North and 80th Street. The record reads: “Crush Injuries. Apparent Death.” (NYC Open Data CrashID 4807280). The street did not forgive a mistake. The truck did not stop in time.

      The System Favors Steel Over Flesh

      Most injuries to pedestrians here come from cars and trucks—over 330 cases, including one death. Trucks and buses killed three. Motorcycles and mopeds killed one. Bikes caused injuries, but not deaths (NYC Open Data). The pattern is clear: the heavier the vehicle, the greater the harm.

      No helmet, no crosswalk, no light will save you when a truck turns and you are in its path. The numbers are not accidents. They are the result of choices made by people and by policy.

      Leadership: Votes and Silence

      Some leaders have moved. Senator Gianaris co-sponsored a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed limiters. Assemblymember Hevesi co-sponsored a matching bill. These are steps. But the carnage continues. Every week brings new names to the list.

      After a bus jumped the curb in Flushing, injuring eight, a passenger described the chaos: “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus.” The MTA pulled the driver from service. The investigation goes on. The pain remains.

      What Now? Demand More

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a warning. Every injury is a call. Contact your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat offenders. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The street will not wait. Neither should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB5 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 30, assembly district AD 28 and state senate district SD 12. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB5?
      It includes the Maspeth, Ridgewood, Glendale, Middle Village, Mount Olivet & All Faiths Cemeteries, Middle Village Cemetery, St. John Cemetery, and Highland Park-Cypress Hills Cemeteries (North) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 30, District 32, and District 34, Assembly Districts AD 28, AD 30, AD 37, AD 38, and AD 39, and State Senate Districts SD 12, SD 15, and SD 18. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB5?
      Most harm comes from Cars and Trucks (over 330 injuries, 1 death), followed by Trucks and Buses (3 deaths), Motorcycles and Mopeds (1 death), and Bikes (injuries, but no deaths). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. The data shows patterns: trucks turning, drivers failing to yield, repeat speeders. These are preventable with better policy and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, support bills that curb repeat dangerous drivers, and redesign streets to protect people walking and cycling.

      Albany gets speed cameras near schools. Lawmakers pass A 7043. Cameras catch drivers who endanger kids. The program runs until 2028. Streets near schools face new watchful eyes.

      Bill A 7043, titled 'Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the city of Albany,' moved forward after committee votes in June 2023. The Assembly passed it on June 6, the Senate on June 8. Patricia Fahy sponsored the bill. The measure allows speed cameras in school zones, aiming to catch drivers who speed near children. The program ends December 31, 2028. Many lawmakers voted yes, some no. The bill targets driver behavior in Albany school zones, putting enforcement where kids walk and cross.
