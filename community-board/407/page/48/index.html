      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB7?

      Queens CB7: speed, turning steel, and the bodies left behind

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB7: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      CB7 is bleeding. Since 2022, at least 23 people died on its streets and highways; drivers injured thousands more. Pedestrians took the worst of it: 10 pedestrians dead; cyclists, 3 dead. SUVs and sedans did most of the harm.

      Where people die

      Main Street at Reeves Avenue: a 68‑year‑old woman crossing outside an intersection was struck and killed by a northbound Toyota. The database lists the factor as “Unsafe Speed.” Open Data.

      On Bowne Street at 41st Avenue, a 63‑year‑old cyclist was hit and killed by a left‑turning Ford SUV. The listed factor: Driver Inattention/Distraction. Open Data.

      College Point Boulevard at Blossom Avenue: a 63‑year‑old moped rider died after a Honda SUV made a left turn. The database says Failure to Yield. Open Data.

      Highways cut through this district and take their toll. The Cross Island Parkway leads CB7’s hotspot list for injuries and deaths; the Whitestone Expressway is next. top locations.

      When the pain hits

      The danger does not sleep. From 5 p.m. to 10 p.m., deaths stack up: 17:00 (1 death), 18:00 (1), 19:00 (3), 20:00 (2), 21:00 (4). hourly pattern. Morning brings its share too: 9 a.m. (2 deaths), 10 a.m. (1), 14:00 (1), 15:00 (1), 16:00 (1). This is not one bad corner. It is the whole day tearing flesh.

      What’s driving the harm

      The data points to drivers. CB7’s leading listed factor is “other,” but named factors tell the story: Failure to Yield killed and injured, “inattention/distraction” injured scores, alcohol appears too. Unsafe speed shows up in a pedestrian death on Main Street. factor rollup.

      Who gets hit: pedestrians and cyclists. Since 2022, pedestrians suffered 807 injuries and 33 serious injuries; cyclists had 244 injuries and 7 serious injuries. Drivers and passengers walk away more often. mode split.

      A city bill to pull lanes back — or pull them out

      While people are dying on Northern Boulevard and College Point Boulevard, Council Member Vickie Paladino sponsored a bill, Int 1362‑2025, that would strip the Streets Master Plan of its protected bike and bus lane targets. The council file says, “This bill would remove the bus lane and bike lane quotas from the Streets Master Plan.” Status: in committee. No vote yet.

      State move against repeat speeders

      Albany moved on people who won’t slow down. The Senate bill S 4045 advanced in June. Senators including John Liu and Toby Stavisky voted yes in committee; Liu also co‑sponsored. The bill would require speed limiters after repeated violations. bill page.

      Local fixes that do not need a new law

      Daylight every corner on Main Street and College Point Boulevard. Harden the left turns that killed a cyclist on 41st Avenue and a moped rider at Blossom Avenue. Add LPIs and leading ped phases at the crash corners. Put concrete where speeding begins on Northern Boulevard. Target the evening peak on Whitestone and Cross Island with speed control. The data tells you when and where. Open Data.

      The ledger

      In the last 12 months, CB7 recorded 3 deaths, 1,348 injuries, and 38 serious injuries. Year‑to‑date injuries are up, with crashes rising about 45% over last year to date. The bodies skew old: two of this year’s dead were 75+; one was 45‑54. period stats.

      What must change citywide

      Slower streets save lives. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can set safer limits. Use it. Demand a 20 mph default and real speed control for repeat offenders. Join others who are fighting. Take action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where are the worst crash locations in Queens CB7?
      The data flags the Cross Island Parkway and the Whitestone Expressway as top hotspots, along with Northern Boulevard and College Point Boulevard.
      ▸ When do the most deadly crashes happen here?
      Evenings are brutal. From 5 p.m. to 10 p.m., the district recorded multiple deaths, peaking at 9–10 p.m. and again at 9 p.m.–10 p.m. per the hourly distribution.
      ▸ What behaviors show up in fatal crashes?
      Failure to Yield, Unsafe Speed, and driver inattention appear in fatal cases, including the Main Street pedestrian death and left-turn kills on Bowne/41st and College Point/Blossom.
      ▸ What policies on the table could change outcomes?
      Council bill Int 1362-2025 would remove protected bike and bus lane quotas. State bill S 4045 would mandate speed limiters for repeat offenders. The city can also set safer speed limits under Sammy’s Law.

      S 3897
      Liu votes yes to require safer complete street designs, improving safety.
      ▸

      Senate passed S 3897. More state cash flows to cities that build complete streets. Lawmakers push for safer roads. Pedestrians and cyclists get a shot at survival.

      Bill S 3897 cleared the Senate committee on March 2, 2022, with a final vote on May 25, 2022. The bill, titled 'Relates to complete street design features and funding of construction and improvements at a municipalities' expense,' boosts state funding for transportation projects when cities add complete street features. Senator Kennedy sponsored the bill. Senators including Cooney, Gallivan, Kaminsky, Kaplan, Liu, and others voted yes. The measure aims to tie state money to safer street design, pushing municipalities to build roads that protect people outside cars.
