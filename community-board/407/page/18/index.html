      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB7?

      Queens Streets Bleed While Leaders Stall

      Written July 9, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB7: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB7

      Two people are dead. Over a thousand are hurt. In the past year alone, Queens CB7 saw 1,022 injuries and 38 serious injuries from 1,715 crashes. Two lives ended. The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do.

      The old and the young are not spared. In the last twelve months, 88 children were injured. Two people over 75 died. The pain is not spread evenly. It finds the vulnerable first.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      Just months ago, a 78-year-old woman was killed by an SUV on Parsons Boulevard. She was not at an intersection. She did not make it home. NYC Open Data

      A year before, a 63-year-old cyclist was struck and killed by a left-turning SUV on Bowne Street. The cause: driver inattention. There was no second chance. NYC Open Data

      Leadership: Small Steps, Slow Change

      Local leaders have moved, but not fast enough. Council Member Sandra Ung co-sponsored bills for speed humps and raised speed reducers. She backed more lighting for step streets and solar crosswalks. These are steps, not leaps.

      But when the Council voted to end jaywalking enforcement—a law that stops blaming the victim—Council Member Vickie Paladino voted no. She stood against a proven safety improvement.

      Assembly Member Ron Kim called out the danger on College Point Boulevard after a toddler was killed: “We’ve always recognized there was a problem on this block.” Streetsblog NYC

      The Road Ahead: Demand More

      The disaster is slow, but it is not fate. Every crash is a policy failure. Every delay is another family broken. Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Int 0766-2024
      Paladino co-sponsors bill boosting safety by targeting obscured license plates.
      ▸

      Council targets hidden plates. Bill slaps fines and jail time on drivers who cover or deface tags. No more hiding from cameras. Law aims to strip shields from reckless motorists. Committee weighs next steps. Streets demand accountability.

      Int 0766-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced April 11, 2024, the bill bans parking, standing, stopping, or operating a vehicle with obscured or defaced license plates. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting the parking, standing, stopping, or operation of a motor vehicle with obscured or defaced license plates.' Council Member Oswald Feliz leads as primary sponsor, joined by Holden, Bottcher, Gennaro, Marte, Restler, Ung, and Paladino. The bill hits violators with up to $1,000 fines, possible jail, and escalating penalties for repeat offenses. The measure aims to end the dodge—drivers hiding plates to evade enforcement. The committee now holds the bill for review.

      Int 0662-2024
      Ung sponsors bill raising fines for vehicle noise, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council members push to hike penalties for drivers who blast horns, rev engines, or use illegal air horns. The bill cracks down on noise from cars and trucks, big and small. It sits in committee. The streets stay loud. Vulnerable users endure.

      Int 0662-2024, now in the Committee on Public Safety, was introduced on March 19, 2024. The bill aims to 'increase certain penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles,' targeting violations like loud engines, illegal claxons, and air horns. Council Member Sandra Ung leads as primary sponsor, joined by Bottcher, Lee, Brannan, Brewer, Vernikov, Zhuang, Gennaro, Won, Louis, Restler, and the Manhattan Borough President. The bill would raise fines for both light and heavy vehicles. It was referred to committee on the day of introduction. No vote has been held. The measure addresses noise but does not directly tackle the physical dangers cars pose to pedestrians and cyclists. Vulnerable road users still face the same risks from traffic violence.
