      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB7?

      Queens Streets Bleed While Leaders Stall

      Written July 9, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB7: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB7

      Two people are dead. Over a thousand are hurt. In the past year alone, Queens CB7 saw 1,022 injuries and 38 serious injuries from 1,715 crashes. Two lives ended. The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do.

      The old and the young are not spared. In the last twelve months, 88 children were injured. Two people over 75 died. The pain is not spread evenly. It finds the vulnerable first.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      Just months ago, a 78-year-old woman was killed by an SUV on Parsons Boulevard. She was not at an intersection. She did not make it home. NYC Open Data

      A year before, a 63-year-old cyclist was struck and killed by a left-turning SUV on Bowne Street. The cause: driver inattention. There was no second chance. NYC Open Data

      Leadership: Small Steps, Slow Change

      Local leaders have moved, but not fast enough. Council Member Sandra Ung co-sponsored bills for speed humps and raised speed reducers. She backed more lighting for step streets and solar crosswalks. These are steps, not leaps.

      But when the Council voted to end jaywalking enforcement—a law that stops blaming the victim—Council Member Vickie Paladino voted no. She stood against a proven safety improvement.

      Assembly Member Ron Kim called out the danger on College Point Boulevard after a toddler was killed: “We’ve always recognized there was a problem on this block.” Streetsblog NYC

      The Road Ahead: Demand More

      The disaster is slow, but it is not fate. Every crash is a policy failure. Every delay is another family broken. Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Res 0449-2024
      Paladino co-sponsors resolution to raise overnight commercial parking fines cap.
      ▸

      Council calls for higher fines on commercial vehicles parked overnight. Current caps let companies treat tickets as business costs. Streets fill with trucks. Residents lose space. The city wants power to set real deterrents. Albany holds the key.

      Resolution 0449-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced June 6, 2024, it urges the State Legislature to let cities raise the cap on commercial overnight parking fines. The matter title reads: "Resolution calling on the State legislature to increase the cap on commercial overnight fines for cities." Council Members Eric Dinowitz (primary sponsor), Vickie Paladino, and Robert F. Holden back the measure. They argue that capped fines are too low—companies absorb them, streets clog with trucks, and residents pay the price. The Council wants authority to set fines that actually deter illegal parking. The resolution points to nearly 27,000 complaints in 2021 and notes that current enforcement tools are weak. The bill is still in committee, awaiting further action.
