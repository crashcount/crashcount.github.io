      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB15?

      Brooklyn Bleeds While Leaders Stall: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written July 4, 2025 (Revised July 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Brooklyn CB15: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 4, 2025

      The Numbers Don’t Lie

      Four people killed. Over 800 injured. That’s just the last twelve months on the streets of Brooklyn CB15. Two of the dead were children. Ten more suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In all, there have been over 1,100 crashes in a single year. The disaster is slow, but it does not stop. NYC Open Data

      Recent Crashes, Fresh Wounds

      Last week, a van slammed into a tree in Bath Beach. Fourteen people were hurt, most of them adults with special needs. The van was headed to a day center. The driver swerved to avoid another car. The crash left a mark on the street and on the families waiting at home. “It’s sad because they definitely need a lot of attention more than anyone else. I hope that they’re all OK at the end of the day,” said a bystander.

      Just days before, a driver fled a traffic stop near Ocean Parkway, dragging an NYPD officer with his car. The officer survived with minor injuries. The suspect sped off, leaving the law and the street behind. Police said the driver “fled the attempted traffic stop, dragging the officer as he drove away.” ABC7

      Leadership: Action, Delay, and Denial

      Council Member Inna Vernikov has a mixed record. She co-sponsored bills for speed humps near parks and rewards for hit-and-run tips, but also voted against the law that decriminalized jaywalking—a measure proven to protect the most vulnerable. She recently intervened to halt a long-promised bike lane project, even after near-unanimous community board support. Safety for people on foot and bike is still up for debate.

      Assembly Member Kalman Yeger voted against renewing the city’s speed camera program, a tool proven to cut speeding and save lives. He has also opposed speed limiter bills and protected bike lanes. Every delay, every vote against proven safety, leaves the next victim’s fate to chance.

      The Call: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by leaders who stall, water down, or block safety. Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand action, not excuses.

      The street does not forgive. Neither should we.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB15 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 48, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB15?
      It includes the Gravesend (East)-Homecrest, Madison, and Sheepshead Bay-Manhattan Beach-Gerritsen Beach neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 44, District 46, and District 48, Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, and AD 59, and State Senate Districts SD 22 and SD 23.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB15?
      Cars and Trucks were responsible for 7 deaths and 490 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 0 deaths and 13 injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths and 7 injuries. The vast majority of harm comes from cars, SUVs, and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by city planners, and by elected officials who delay proven safety measures. Lower speeds, protected bike lanes, and better enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand protected bike lanes, support speed cameras, and redesign dangerous streets. They can stop blocking or delaying safety projects and put people before parking or politics.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0603-2024
      Vernikov co-sponsors bill that may reduce pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council bill demands DOT keep curbs high after street work. Low curbs mean water floods sidewalks. Pedestrians wade through puddles. Property owners get fined. Ariola leads, Holden, Paladino, Vernikov back her. Committee reviews. No action yet. Streets stay risky.

      Int 0603-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure since March 7, 2024. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that the department of transportation maintain curb heights following street construction,' targets a simple failure: after street work, curbs often vanish, water pools, and property owners get fined for city mistakes. Council Member Joann Ariola sponsors, joined by Holden, Paladino, and Vernikov. The bill orders DOT to restore curbs to proper height after any street, sidewalk, or gutter work. The committee has not voted. The bill aims to stop flooding and fines, but leaves the daily hazard for walkers and wheelchair users unaddressed until passed.

      Int 0542-2024
      Vernikov co-sponsors bill to speed up traffic study decisions.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0542-2024 forces DOT to act fast. Traffic study answers must come within 60 days when council members or boards ask for new signals or signs. No more endless waits. The clock starts. Streets can’t wait.

      Int 0542-2024 was introduced on March 7, 2024, and is now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that traffic study determinations be issued no later than 60 days from the date a traffic control device is requested by a city council member or community board.” Council Member Lincoln Restler leads as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Brooks-Powers, and Vernikov. Their bill demands the Department of Transportation deliver traffic study decisions within 60 days of a request. Right now, there’s no set timeline. The bill aims to end delays, forcing the city to move faster when lives are at stake. The measure would take effect 120 days after enactment.

      Int 0606-2024
      Vernikov sponsors e-bike and scooter registration bill, reducing street safety.
      ▸

      Council wants plates on every e-bike and scooter. The bill sits in committee. Riders face new rules. Lawmakers push for registration. Streets wait. No change yet. The city debates control. Vulnerable users watch. The system holds its breath.

      Int 0606-2024 was introduced on March 7, 2024, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered," would force all e-bikes, e-scooters, and similar vehicles not already covered by DMV rules to register with the Department of Transportation and display a visible plate. Council Member Inna Vernikov led as primary sponsor, joined by Holden, Farías, Riley, Brannan, and many others. The bill was laid over in committee on December 11, 2024. The measure aims to track these vehicles, but its impact on vulnerable road users remains unassessed. The city’s debate continues, with no law yet in force.
