      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB15?

      Brooklyn Bleeds: Politicians Stall, Neighbors Die

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB15: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      Four lives lost in the last year. Hundreds more broken. In Brooklyn CB15, the numbers do not lie. Since January 2022, there have been 16 deaths and 2,543 injuries on these streets. Children, elders, mothers. The disaster is slow, but it does not stop.

      Just days ago, a 90-year-old man was struck and killed by a moped at Avenue U and East 14th Street. The rider stopped, looked, and left him dying in the crosswalk. “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open,” said a witness. The driver wore a mask. The police are still searching.

      On Ocean Parkway this spring, a mother and her two daughters were killed crossing with the light. The driver had a record of speed camera violations. The city counted the bodies. The street stayed the same.

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and Silence

      Most deaths come from cars and SUVs. In three years, these vehicles killed seven people and injured nearly 500 more. Trucks and buses killed three. Motorcycles and mopeds, often unregistered, injured and killed with impunity. Bikes caused no deaths, just a handful of minor injuries.

      The numbers are not fate. They are the result of choices. Speed, size, and inaction kill.

      Leaders: Votes, Delays, and Missed Chances

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Member Inna Vernikov co-sponsored bills for speed humps and rewards for hit-and-run tips. But she also voted against ending jaywalking enforcement—a law that would have protected people on foot. She fought to pause new bike lanes, calling them a “woke agenda” while the crashes kept coming. Assembly Member Michael Novakhov opposed speed cameras and lower limits, saying the speed is “too slow” on Ocean Parkway. He called for higher speeds, even as families buried their dead.

      A neighbor spoke for many: “They plow right through red lights, no consideration for people crossing. They just don’t care” said Ryan Rivera.

      What Now: The Only Way Out Is Through

      This is not an accident. It is a policy failure. Every day of delay means another family waits for a call that never should come. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand action, not excuses.

      The blood on the street is not washed away by words. Only by change.

      Int 0905-2023
      Vernikov co-sponsors bill to speed up street resurfacing, boosting safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force street resurfacing jobs to finish in two weeks. Delays must be explained to neighbors. Streets torn up for months endanger walkers and riders. The bill died at session’s end. The danger remains. The clock keeps ticking.

      Int 0905-2023 was introduced on February 2, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to amend city law to require the Department of Transportation to finish all street resurfacing work within two weeks. If more time was needed, the department would have to notify the community, stating the reason and new timeline.       Int 0883-2023
      Vernikov co-sponsors bill increasing penalties, likely reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0883-2023 sought to double fines for e-bike and e-scooter violations. It aimed to restore impoundment powers and repeal recent legal protections. The measure targeted riders, not drivers. The bill died at session’s end. Vulnerable users remain exposed.

      Int 0883-2023 was introduced in the City Council on January 19, 2023, and referred to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill proposed to amend the administrative code by expanding the definition of 'motorized scooter' to include e-bikes and e-scooters, raising civil penalties for violations from $250 to $500, and restoring impoundment authority. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the operation of motorized scooters, and to repeal subdivision e of section 19-176.2 of such code, relating to operators of electric scooters and bicycles with electric assist.' Council Members Althea V. Stevens (primary sponsor), Robert F. Holden, Inna Vernikov, Joann Ariola, Vickie Paladino, Pierina Ana Sanchez, Kalman Yeger, and James F. Gennaro sponsored the bill. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. No safety analyst assessment was provided. The bill’s focus was on enforcement and penalties, not on systemic danger from cars.

      A 1280
      Novakhov co-sponsors bill boosting street safety for all users.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 1280 pushes for safe streets. It calls for complete street design. Dozens of lawmakers back it. The bill aims to protect everyone—walkers, riders, and drivers. The fight for safer roads moves forward. The danger is real. The need is urgent.

      Assembly Bill A 1280, now in the sponsorship stage, sits before the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 13, 2023, the bill 'enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles.' Jonathan Rivera leads as primary sponsor, joined by over 70 co-sponsors including Patrick Burke, Kenny Burgos, and Jessica Gonzalez-Rojas. The bill has not yet advanced to committee or vote. Its language centers the safety of all road users—pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers—by demanding infrastructure that reduces risk and prioritizes vulnerable people. The measure reflects growing legislative momentum for street design that saves lives and prevents injury. The urgency is clear: New York streets remain dangerous. Lawmakers are under pressure to act.
