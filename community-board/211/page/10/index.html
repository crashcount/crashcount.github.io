      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx CB11?

      Two riders dead on the Bronx River Parkway. CB11 keeps counting.

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Bronx CB11: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Two young men died before dawn on the Bronx River Parkway. Police say a 21‑year‑old in a Mercedes tried to pass a Volkswagen, hit it, then struck two motorcycles. The riders, Manuel Amarantepenalo, 19, and Enrique Martinez, 21, were thrown and later pronounced dead. The driver was charged with vehicular manslaughter and DWI. “He was drunk,” the victim’s sister said. “How could they let him go?” Another relative asked in court. The DA is still investigating. Police records note a strong odor of alcohol and a refused test.

      This happened in the footprint of Bronx Community Board 11. It did not happen in a vacuum.

      Where it breaks most

      Since 2022, CB11 has logged five deaths and hundreds of injuries. Top factors include failure to yield and the catch‑all “other,” with alcohol showing up too. City data points to repeat harm at Bronxdale Ave and East Gun Hill Road. Pedestrians took the brunt: three people on foot killed, 289 injured. Riders on bikes and small devices were hit 173 times. Cars and SUVs were involved in most pedestrian injuries; trucks and buses added more. These are not outliers. They are the pattern.

      The clock tells its own story: injuries spike from the school and work hours into the evening, with fatalities scattered at noon, rush, and late night. Speed shows up in the files. So does inattention. The names are gone from most rows. The damage remains.

      Faces behind the numbers

      A 51‑year‑old woman died in a marked crosswalk at Burke and Bouck. The SUV driver was turning left. The file lists “failure to yield.” City data gives no last words.
      A 16‑year‑old girl was struck and killed at Paulding and Neill by a left‑turning box truck. The report again says “failure to yield.” That’s the line.
      A 91‑year‑old man died at Rhinelander and Radcliff. The record is spare. It does not need help to hurt. One more death.

      On Williamsbridge Road at Pierce, a 64‑year‑old man on a bike was killed in a crash involving a pickup and a parked car. The sheet says “unspecified.” The body count does not care what we call it. It’s there.

      What would stop the bleeding here

      The files name turning drivers. Hardened left turns, daylighting at corners, and leading pedestrian intervals at repeat sites like Bronxdale and Gun Hill would cut those hits. Truck routes and enforcement on left‑turn conflicts at Paulding/Neill and Burke/Bouck would protect walkers where trucks and SUVs are doing harm. Nighttime and rush‑hour operations should target passing and speed on the parkway and feeders. These are standard tools. They are missing where the bodies fell.

      Citywide locks for the worst speeders

      A small slice of drivers cause outsized harm. Albany has a bill to force repeat speeders to use speed‑limiting tech. The Senate’s S4045 advanced in June with yes votes from local senators. The measure would require intelligent speed assistance after a pattern of violations. The goal is simple: keep known dangerous drivers from reaching deadly speeds. The bill file is here.

      Slow every street

      Lower speeds save lives. New York now has the power to set safer limits and has extended speed cameras around schools. But the bodies are not waiting. Use the tools. Use them here.

      Bronx CB11 has names on a parkway and at quiet corners. The fixes are known. The choice is the only mystery.

      Take one step that matters. Tell City Hall and Albany to act. Start here: take action.

      Marmorato Opposes Safety‑Boosting Congestion Pricing Cash Grab
      ▸

      As congestion pricing begins, unions and politicians rage. They claim tolls hurt workers and raise costs. Facts show most commuters use transit. Fewer cars mean faster emergency response. The toll funds transit upgrades. The drama masks real safety gains for all.

      On January 3, 2025, public debate erupted as New York City prepared to activate congestion pricing in Manhattan. The measure, set to fund $15 billion in subway and rail improvements, drew fierce opposition from unions and Councilwoman Kristy Marmorato, who called it a 'cash grab' that would 'increase all costs in our daily lives.' The union for FDNY EMTs argued the toll would burden low-wage workers, but data shows 90% of commuters already use public transit. City officials, including mayoral spokesperson Liz Garcia, insisted emergency response would not suffer. The measure's summary notes that reducing car traffic will speed up emergency vehicles and protect passengers. The uproar highlights the tension between entrenched driving privileges and the urgent need to make streets safer for vulnerable road users.
