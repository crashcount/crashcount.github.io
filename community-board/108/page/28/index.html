      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB8?

      York Avenue Bleeds: How Many More Must Die Before City Hall Acts?

      Written June 20, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB8: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      A 90-year-old woman tried to cross York Avenue. A yellow cab made a U-turn and struck her. Moments later, a second car hit her. She died at the hospital. Both drivers stayed. No charges. Frances Rickard was crossing at York Avenue and East 72nd Street around 5:40 p.m. when the 68-year-old man driving the cab made a U-turn and hit her, authorities said.

      A Tesla sped down the FDR, hit the guardrail, flipped, and burned. The driver died. The passenger survived. A witness described the speed and destruction: “At least 120, 130 [mph]. At least. The damage was just so much that it split in half. And that just started going on fire.”

      In three and a half years, 11 people have died on these streets. 44 more suffered serious injuries. The numbers keep rising.

      The Most Vulnerable Pay the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists are the first to bleed. In the last year, two people over 75 died. Children were hurt. The old, the young, the ones who walk—these are the bodies left behind. Cars and SUVs killed four. Trucks killed two. Bikes and mopeds left their mark, but the weight of steel does most of the damage.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      City Hall talks of Vision Zero. They say, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” But the dead keep coming. The city has the power now to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. They have not used it. Speed cameras work, but Albany lets their future hang by a thread. The law could expire. The cameras could go dark. The city builds new bike lanes and claims progress, but the blood on the crosswalks says otherwise.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city use its power. Lower the speed limit. Keep the cameras on. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Take action now.

      Int 0088-2022
      Powers sponsors bill requiring package delivery report, no direct safety impact.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force city agencies to count package deliveries and their harm. The law called for annual reports on delivery volume, traffic, and air quality. It demanded a plan to cut the damage. The bill died at session’s end.

      Int 0088-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on March 10, 2022. The bill required the Department of Transportation, with other agencies, to file annual reports on package delivery volumes, traffic, and environmental impacts. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring a report on package deliveries.' Council Member Keith Powers sponsored the bill, joined by Selvena N. Brooks-Powers, Gale A. Brewer, Tiffany Cabán, and Public Advocate Jumaane Williams. The bill demanded a plan to reduce the harm caused by delivery vehicles. It was filed without passage on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Res 0008-2022
      Menin co-sponsors fare-free holiday transit, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council pushed MTA to drop fares on subways and buses for major holidays. The move aimed to open transit, cut car trips, and ease city movement. The resolution gathered broad support but stalled, filed at session’s end. No action. No change.

      Resolution 0008-2022, filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, called on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority (MTA) to make subways and buses fare-free on major holidays. Introduced February 10, 2022, and filed December 31, 2023, the measure read: "Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority (MTA) to make subways and buses fare-free on major holidays." Council Member Darlene Mealy led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Menin, Louis, Yeger, Hanif, Joseph, Ossé, Nurse, Gutiérrez, Avilés, Cabán, Richardson Jordan, and Brooks-Powers. The bill cited global fare-free transit efforts and past local fare suspensions. Despite wide co-sponsorship, the resolution ended the session without passage or impact. No safety analyst reviewed its effect on vulnerable road users.

      Res 0009-2022
      Menin co-sponsors resolution for accessible subways, improving safety for vulnerable riders.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to make every renovated subway station fully accessible. Only a fraction of stations have elevators. Lawmakers want no more half-measures. The resolution was filed at session’s end. Riders with disabilities remain stranded underground.

      Resolution 0009-2022 was introduced on February 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It called on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to ensure any subway station undergoing enhancement or renovation becomes fully accessible to people with disabilities. The matter title reads: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make any subway stations undergoing enhancement or renovation fully accessible to people with disabilities.' Council Member Darlene Mealy sponsored the resolution, joined by Brannan, Menin, Louis, Yeger, Hanif, Hudson, Marte, Joseph, Riley, and Brooks-Powers. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. Only 117 out of 493 subway stations are accessible. The Council’s action highlights the city’s failure to guarantee safe, equal passage for all riders. Elevators and upgrades are overdue. The bill’s filing leaves vulnerable New Yorkers waiting.
