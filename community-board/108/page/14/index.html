      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB8?

      York Avenue Bleeds: How Many More Must Die Before City Hall Acts?

      Written June 20, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB8: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      A 90-year-old woman tried to cross York Avenue. A yellow cab made a U-turn and struck her. Moments later, a second car hit her. She died at the hospital. Both drivers stayed. No charges. Frances Rickard was crossing at York Avenue and East 72nd Street around 5:40 p.m. when the 68-year-old man driving the cab made a U-turn and hit her, authorities said.

      A Tesla sped down the FDR, hit the guardrail, flipped, and burned. The driver died. The passenger survived. A witness described the speed and destruction: “At least 120, 130 [mph]. At least. The damage was just so much that it split in half. And that just started going on fire.”

      In three and a half years, 11 people have died on these streets. 44 more suffered serious injuries. The numbers keep rising.

      The Most Vulnerable Pay the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists are the first to bleed. In the last year, two people over 75 died. Children were hurt. The old, the young, the ones who walk—these are the bodies left behind. Cars and SUVs killed four. Trucks killed two. Bikes and mopeds left their mark, but the weight of steel does most of the damage.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      City Hall talks of Vision Zero. They say, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” But the dead keep coming. The city has the power now to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. They have not used it. Speed cameras work, but Albany lets their future hang by a thread. The law could expire. The cameras could go dark. The city builds new bike lanes and claims progress, but the blood on the crosswalks says otherwise.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city use its power. Lower the speed limit. Keep the cameras on. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Take action now.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Manhattan Community Board 8

      Int 0080-2024
      Menin co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by deterring hazardous vehicle obstruction.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants within half a mile of schools. Fines set at $175. Civilians can report violations. DOT must act on complaints. Council aims to clear paths for people, not cars.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after its introduction on February 8, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations," creates a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of a school. The penalty is $175 per violation. The Department of Transportation must launch a civilian reporting program. If DOT prosecutes a case using civilian evidence, the complainant gets 25% of the proceeds. Council Member Carlina Rivera leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Joseph, Menin, Nurse, Hudson, Brannan, Farías, Brewer, Salaam, Hanif, Avilés, Won, Bottcher, Krishnan, Gutiérrez, Marte, and the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill aims to keep streets clear for vulnerable road users, especially near schools.

      Int 0099-2024
      Menin co-sponsors bill increasing overnight truck parking, reducing pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council bill would carve out curbside overnight truck parking in city industrial zones. Only commercial vehicles allowed, 8 p.m. to 6 a.m. DOT must consult locals and report yearly. Bill sits in committee. Streets stay risky for those on foot or bike.

      Int 0099-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, would require the Department of Transportation to create curbside overnight parking for commercial vehicles in every Industrial Business Zone. The bill, introduced February 8, 2024, mandates that only commercial vehicles may park in these zones between 8:00 p.m. and 6:00 a.m. The matter title states: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the creation of curbside overnight truck parking sections in Industrial Business Zones.' Council Members Althea V. Stevens (primary sponsor), Nantasha M. Williams, Lincoln Restler, Farah N. Louis, Kevin C. Riley, and others back the measure. DOT must consult community boards and report annually on effectiveness. No safety analysis for vulnerable road users is available. The bill remains in committee, with no vote scheduled.

      Int 0095-2024
      Menin sponsors study on school-area traffic calming, immediate safety impact neutral.
      ▸

      Council eyes danger at school gates. Bill orders a citywide study on raised crosswalks, intersections, and speed reducers near schools. Streets choke with cars. Children cross. The city stalls. The bill sits in committee. Lives hang in the balance.

      Int 0095-2024, introduced February 8, 2024, sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill demands a study on 'the feasibility of installing raised crosswalks, raised intersections, and speed reducers at intersections and roadways adjacent to schools.' Council Member Julie Menin leads, joined by Schulman, Hudson, Brooks-Powers, Riley, Lee, Louis, Restler, and Gutiérrez as sponsors. The bill would repeal and replace Section 19-189 of the city code, requiring the Department of Transportation to report crash data and feasibility findings to the Mayor and Council Speaker. After the study, the Commissioner may install traffic-calming measures where possible. The bill remains 'Laid Over in Committee' as of June 25, 2024. No safety analyst note is available, but the bill targets systemic risk where children walk and drivers speed.

      • File Int 0095-2024, NYC Council – Legistar, Published 2024-02-08
      Int 0094-2024
      Powers sponsors bill restricting low bass sound permits, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council bill targets deep bass. No more permits for sound devices thumping below 40 hertz. Police commissioner loses power to approve these. Schulman and Powers push the change. Action sits with Public Safety Committee. No direct impact on street safety for walkers or riders.

      Int 0094-2024, introduced February 8, 2024, sits with the Committee on Public Safety. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting the issuance of permits for the use of sound apparatuses to produce low bass sounds,' would stop the police commissioner from granting permits for devices that emit bass at 40 hertz or below for 10 seconds or more. Council Members Keith Powers (primary sponsor, District 4) and Lynn C. Schulman (co-sponsor, District 29) lead the effort. The bill was referred to committee on introduction. The measure amends existing sound permit rules but does not address traffic violence or the safety of pedestrians and cyclists. No safety analyst has found a direct effect on vulnerable road users.
