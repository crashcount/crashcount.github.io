      No More Second Chances: End NYC’s Deadly Streets Now

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 6, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed citation formatting, deduped citations.)

      Manhattan CB8: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      Three people killed. Eight seriously hurt. In the last year, Manhattan CB8 saw 430 injuries from 909 crashes. The dead are not numbers. A 90-year-old woman, Frances Rickard, was crossing York Avenue at dusk. A yellow taxi made a U-turn and hit her. Seconds later, an SUV struck her again. She died at the hospital. Both drivers stayed. No charges filed. Police said the 68-year-old man driving the cab made a U-turn and hit her, and moments later, a 35-year-old woman driving a 2023 Chevrolet Trailblazer also struck her.

      A day earlier, a Tesla on the FDR flipped and burned. The driver was ejected and died. Her passenger survived. A witness described the speed and destruction: “At least 120, 130 [mph]. At least. The damage was just so much that it split in half. And that just started going on fire.”

      Most victims are walkers. Most drivers walk away.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      The city touts Vision Zero. They say every life matters. They point to new speed cameras and street redesigns. But the deaths keep coming. Speed cameras now run 24/7. Red-light cameras are growing. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph, but has not done so citywide. Each delay is another risk. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program lapsed. No word on renewal.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand the return of the Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program. Join Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Show up. Speak out. Every day of silence is another day someone dies.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB8 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 5, assembly district AD 76 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB8?
      It includes the Upper East Side-Lenox Hill-Roosevelt Island, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, and Upper East Side-Yorkville neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 4 and District 5, Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 73, and AD 76, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 29.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB8?
      Cars and Trucks: 6 deaths, 82 serious or moderate injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 10 serious or moderate injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 16 serious or moderate injuries. Data source
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speed, distraction, and street design. These deaths and injuries are preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, restore the Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program, expand protected bike lanes, and push for more automated enforcement. Every delay costs lives.
