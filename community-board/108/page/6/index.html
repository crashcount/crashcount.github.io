      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB8?

      York Avenue Bleeds: How Many More Must Die Before City Hall Acts?

      Written June 20, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Manhattan CB8: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      A 90-year-old woman tried to cross York Avenue. A yellow cab made a U-turn and struck her. Moments later, a second car hit her. She died at the hospital. Both drivers stayed. No charges. Frances Rickard was crossing at York Avenue and East 72nd Street around 5:40 p.m. when the 68-year-old man driving the cab made a U-turn and hit her, authorities said.

      A Tesla sped down the FDR, hit the guardrail, flipped, and burned. The driver died. The passenger survived. A witness described the speed and destruction: “At least 120, 130 [mph]. At least. The damage was just so much that it split in half. And that just started going on fire.”

      In three and a half years, 11 people have died on these streets. 44 more suffered serious injuries. The numbers keep rising.

      The Most Vulnerable Pay the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists are the first to bleed. In the last year, two people over 75 died. Children were hurt. The old, the young, the ones who walk—these are the bodies left behind. Cars and SUVs killed four. Trucks killed two. Bikes and mopeds left their mark, but the weight of steel does most of the damage.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      City Hall talks of Vision Zero. They say, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” But the dead keep coming. The city has the power now to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. They have not used it. Speed cameras work, but Albany lets their future hang by a thread. The law could expire. The cameras could go dark. The city builds new bike lanes and claims progress, but the blood on the crosswalks says otherwise.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city use its power. Lower the speed limit. Keep the cameras on. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Take action now.

      Powers Backs NYC E-Bike Trade-In Safety Program
      ▸

      New York opens its e-bike trade-in. Four hundred delivery workers can swap dangerous, uncertified bikes for safe, legal models. The program targets deadly battery fires. But 400 is a drop in the bucket. Tens of thousands remain at risk. The danger lingers.

      On January 28, 2025, the Department of Transportation launched New York City's e-bike and moped trade-in program, born from a 2023 law introduced by Council Member Keith Powers. The program, praised by Council Member Oswald Feliz, allows 400 delivery workers to exchange uncertified, fire-prone bikes for certified, street-legal models. The law followed a spike in lithium-ion battery fires—277 in 2024, killing six. The $2 million program offers bikes and spare batteries, but only scratches the surface for the city’s estimated 80,000 delivery workers. Ligia Guallpa of the Worker’s Justice Project called the effort a step toward safer, cleaner mobility. Applications close February 17. The program’s reach is small, leaving most workers exposed to the same deadly risks.

      S 3387
      Krueger co-sponsors bill mandating safer, more inclusive street designs citywide.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 3387 orders complete street design for all DOT projects using state or federal funds. The law aims to force safer roads for walkers, cyclists, and riders. Sponsors demand the state stop building deadly streets.

      Senate bill S 3387 was introduced on January 27, 2025, and is now in the sponsorship stage. The bill, titled 'Relates to requiring inclusion of complete street design for state and local transportation projects subject to oversight by the department of transportation,' requires that all DOT projects—state or local, with federal or state funding—must include complete street design. Senator Andrew Gounardes leads as primary sponsor, joined by Patricia Fahy, Brad Hoylman-Sigal, and Liz Krueger as co-sponsors. The measure targets every project under DOT oversight. The bill’s summary is blunt: 'Requires inclusion of complete street design for state and local transportation projects undertaken by DOT or which receive both federal and state funding and are subject to DOT oversight.' No safety analyst note was provided, but the bill’s intent is clear—force safer, more inclusive streets for all.

      A 2299
      Bores co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Drivers who rack up points or camera tickets face forced speed control. Assembly bill A 2299 targets repeat violators. The law would require intelligent speed assistance devices. The goal: stop reckless drivers before they kill.

      Assembly Bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 17, 2025, it awaits committee review. The bill summary reads: 'Requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Assembly Member Emily Gallagher leads as primary sponsor, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, Tony Simone, and others. No votes have been recorded yet. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving by forcing speed limiters on offenders. This targets a core threat to pedestrians and cyclists: unchecked speed and repeat lawbreaking.

      S 1675
      Krueger co-sponsors bill for online pedestrian safety ratings, no major safety change.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 1675 would force carmakers to face the facts. It creates a pedestrian safety rating system for all vehicles. The ratings go public on the DMV website. Lawmakers want drivers to see the danger. The streets demand it.

      Senate bill S 1675 was introduced on January 13, 2025. It sits in the sponsorship stage. The bill, titled 'Creates a pedestrian safety rating system for motor vehicles,' would require the Department of Motor Vehicles to post these ratings online. Primary sponsor Andrew Gounardes leads a slate of co-sponsors: Leroy Comrie, Michael Gianaris, Brad Hoylman-Sigal, Robert Jackson, Liz Krueger, Zellnor Myrie, Gustavo Rivera, Julia Salazar, Luis R. Sepúlveda, and Toby Ann Stavisky. The bill aims to expose the risk cars pose to people on foot. By making safety data public, it puts pressure on automakers and drivers alike. The measure has not yet moved to committee or received a vote.
