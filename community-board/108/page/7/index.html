      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB8?

      York Avenue Bleeds: How Many More Must Die Before City Hall Acts?

      Written June 20, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Manhattan CB8: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      A 90-year-old woman tried to cross York Avenue. A yellow cab made a U-turn and struck her. Moments later, a second car hit her. She died at the hospital. Both drivers stayed. No charges. Frances Rickard was crossing at York Avenue and East 72nd Street around 5:40 p.m. when the 68-year-old man driving the cab made a U-turn and hit her, authorities said.

      A Tesla sped down the FDR, hit the guardrail, flipped, and burned. The driver died. The passenger survived. A witness described the speed and destruction: “At least 120, 130 [mph]. At least. The damage was just so much that it split in half. And that just started going on fire.”

      In three and a half years, 11 people have died on these streets. 44 more suffered serious injuries. The numbers keep rising.

      The Most Vulnerable Pay the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists are the first to bleed. In the last year, two people over 75 died. Children were hurt. The old, the young, the ones who walk—these are the bodies left behind. Cars and SUVs killed four. Trucks killed two. Bikes and mopeds left their mark, but the weight of steel does most of the damage.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      City Hall talks of Vision Zero. They say, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” But the dead keep coming. The city has the power now to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. They have not used it. Speed cameras work, but Albany lets their future hang by a thread. The law could expire. The cameras could go dark. The city builds new bike lanes and claims progress, but the blood on the crosswalks says otherwise.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city use its power. Lower the speed limit. Keep the cameras on. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Take action now.

      A 324
      Bores co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 324 demands every state- or federally-funded road project weigh complete street design. The bill pushes agencies to publish clear guidance. Streets built for cars alone kill. This bill aims to force change at the blueprint.

      Assembly Bill 324 was introduced on January 1, 2025, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in the New York State Assembly, with Didi Barrett as the primary sponsor and a long list of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Amy Paulin, and others. The bill summary reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding; authorizes the department to compile and make publicly available guidance and reference material related to consideration of complete street design.' This measure would require state and federally funded transportation projects to consider designs that protect all road users, not just drivers. The bill’s broad sponsorship signals growing support for safer, more inclusive streets. No safety analyst note was provided.

      A 116
      Seawright co-sponsors e-scooter bill that likely reduces street safety.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 116 would force e-scooter riders in New York City to get a license, read a safety manual, and buy insurance. The bill blocks sales to unlicensed buyers. It aims to regulate e-scooters but leaves questions for those on foot and bike.

      Assembly Bill 116, now in sponsorship, sits in the Assembly. Filed January 1, 2025, it seeks to 'establish a bicycle and electric scooter operator's safety manual; provide for the issuance of a license for residents who successfully read and comprehend the operator's safety manual; require residents who operate an electric scooter in New York City to have a license to operate such devices; require instruction in electric scooter safety as part of the drivers pre-licensing course; require liability insurance for electric scooters in cities having a population of one million or more; prohibit the sale of an electric scooter to a purchaser unless the purchaser has a license to operate the electric scooter and has insurance in a city having a population of one million or more.' Assemblymember John Zaccaro Jr. leads sponsorship, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Michael Benedetto, Deborah Glick, David McDonough, and Michael Novakhov.       Intro. 606, known as Priscilla’s Law, was introduced by Council Member Robert F. Holden of District 30. The bill, debated on December 12, 2024, sits before the City Council. It would require all e-mobility devices not covered by the state DMV to register with the city’s Department of Transportation. The bill’s summary states it aims to increase accountability for e-bike riders after fatal crashes. Holden, the sponsor, cited dangers from unregulated e-vehicles and hit-and-runs. Both sides agree cars cause far more deaths, but the bill’s critics say better street design, not registration, would protect vulnerable road users.

      Int 1138-2024
      Menin co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1138-2024 would ban parking and standing within 20 feet of crosswalks. It forces the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections each year. The bill sits in committee. Sponsors include Bottcher, Won, and the Public Advocate.

      Int 1138-2024, introduced December 5, 2024, is under review by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill is 'Laid Over in Committee.' Its title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.' Council Member Erik D. Bottcher is the primary sponsor, joined by Julie Won, Jumaane Williams, and others. The bill prohibits standing or parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and mandates the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections per year. The law also requires citywide outreach and reporting. This measure aims to keep sightlines clear at crossings, a known danger zone for people on foot and bike. The bill has not yet received a vote.
