      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx CB3?

      Bronx Streets Bleed—Leaders Stall

      Written August 11, 2025 (Revised August 12, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      Bronx CB3: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 11, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      Five dead. Thirteen seriously hurt. That’s just the last twelve months in Bronx CB3. The numbers do not flinch. In the same year, 517 people were injured on these streets. The names fade, but the pain does not.

      Just days ago, a woman was killed crossing 174th Street in Morris Heights. The driver fled. Police searched an alleyway for her body. She died at St. Barnabas Hospital. The car did not stop. The city did not stop. A 44-year-old woman was fatally struck in a hit-and-run crash in the Bronx.

      A neighbor saw it. “I looked over, and he just rolled over that woman and killed her,” she said. The driver kept going. The street stayed the same.

      The Pattern: No One Is Safe

      Pedestrians, cyclists, children. No one is spared. In the last year, 62 children were injured. No children were killed. Cars and SUVs did most of the damage—over 290 crashes. Trucks and buses hit too. Even bikes and mopeds left people bleeding. The violence is not random. It is built into the road.

      Leadership: Votes, Bills, and Silence

      Local leaders have moved, but not fast enough. Senator Sepúlveda voted yes to extend school speed zones and to curb repeat speeders with mandatory speed limiters. He backed the bill that would force the worst drivers to slow down. Assembly Member Jackson voted to extend speed cameras near schools. These are steps. But the blood on the street says it is not enough.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand It Now

      Every day of delay is another life at risk. Call your council member. Call your senator. Tell them the deaths are not accidents. Tell them to lower the speed limit, fix the streets, and end the silence. The dead cannot speak. You can. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Bronx CB3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 17, assembly district AD 79 and state senate district SD 32.
      ▸ Which areas are in Bronx CB3?
      It includes the Morrisania, Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Crotona Park East, and Crotona Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 15, District 16, and District 17, Assembly Districts AD 79 and AD 85, and State Senate District SD 32.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Bronx CB3?
      Cars and Trucks: 3 deaths, 293 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 11 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 2 injuries. Cars and trucks are the main killers.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The pattern is clear. Speed, reckless driving, and unsafe streets make these deaths predictable. They can be prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, and pass laws to rein in repeat speeders. They can fund enforcement and demand accountability. Every delay costs lives.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Bronx Community Board 3

      S 6808
      Sepúlveda votes yes to create safety zones, improving street safety for all.
      ▸

      Senate passes S 6808. The bill creates first responder safety zones. It sets speed limits in these zones. Lawmakers act after crashes and close calls. The vote is strong. The danger is real. The streets demand change.

      Senate bill S 6808, titled 'Relates to establishing first responder safety zones and setting speed limits in such safety zones,' moved through committee and passed multiple Senate votes between May 2023 and March 2024. Primary sponsor John Mannion led the push, joined by Joseph P. Addabbo Jr., Iwen Chu, and Pamela Helming. The bill aims to carve out protected zones for first responders and lower speed limits in those areas. The measure passed with broad support, reflecting urgency after repeated crashes near emergency scenes. The bill’s text and votes show lawmakers responding to the deadly toll of reckless driving near first responders. Vulnerable road users—pedestrians, passengers, and responders—stand in harm’s way. The bill targets that risk.

      S 2714
      Sepúlveda votes yes, boosting street safety and access for everyone.
      ▸

      Senate passes S 2714. Bill pushes complete street design. Aim: safer roads for all. Pedestrians, cyclists, and riders get space. Car dominance challenged. Lawmakers move to cut street carnage.

      Senate bill S 2714, titled 'Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles,' advanced through committee and passed several Senate votes, most recently on March 27, 2024. Sponsored by Timothy M. Kennedy with support from Jake Ashby, Jamaal Bailey, and others, the bill mandates street designs that protect everyone—not just drivers. The measure saw strong support but faced opposition from some senators. By requiring complete street principles, S 2714 aims to reduce danger for pedestrians, cyclists, and transit users. The bill marks a shift away from car-first planning, forcing cities to build streets for people, not just traffic.

      S 6802
      Sepúlveda votes yes on Albany school speed cameras, boosting pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Senate backs S 6802. Albany gets school speed cameras. One no vote. Cameras aim to slow drivers near kids. Program ends 2028. Lawmakers move to shield children from reckless speed.

      Senate bill S 6802, introduced by Neil D. Breslin, establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in Albany. The bill passed committee on May 30, 2023, with only one senator, Andrew J. Lanza, voting no. The official summary reads: 'Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the city of Albany; repeals authorization of program December 31, 2028.' Breslin led sponsorship. Seventeen senators voted yes. The program targets driver speed near schools, aiming to protect children and other vulnerable road users. The law sunsets at the end of 2028.

      Salamanca Supports Safety Boosting Crackdown on Fake Plates
      ▸

      Councilmember Restler joins Bronx and Queens colleagues to target fake license plates. The bill slaps heavy fines on dealers selling sham tags. Lawmakers say ghost cars dodge cameras, endanger streets, and let reckless drivers vanish. The crackdown aims to stop the chaos.

      Intro 988, introduced on April 11, 2023, targets the sale of fake license plates in New York City. The bill, sponsored by Oswald Feliz, Marjorie Velázquez, and Lincoln Restler, amends laws to ban selling counterfeit tags, with fines starting at $1,000 for first offenses and $2,000 for repeat violations. The measure was brought before the City Council and discussed at a City Hall press conference. The matter summary states the bill 'goes after the unscrupulous used car dealers profiting considerably off a huge black market for phony tags.' Restler, as a sponsor, joins efforts to curb the spread of ghost cars, which evade speed cameras and tolls, making streets more dangerous for everyone. Lawmakers stress that the goal is deterrence, not revenue, and call for further action from online marketplaces and federal agencies.
