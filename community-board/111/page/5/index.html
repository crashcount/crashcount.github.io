      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB11?

      No More Names on Asphalt: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written July 19, 2025 (Revised July 21, 2025 – Fixed duplicate headline in body, deduped and cleaned citations.)

      Manhattan CB11: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 19, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Human Cost

      Twelve people are dead. Twenty-eight more are seriously hurt. In just the last twelve months, Manhattan CB11 saw 1,411 crashes. Six people died. Seven hundred forty-three were injured. The numbers are blunt, but the pain is sharp. A man steps into the street. A car crushes his body. The city moves on. His family does not.

      Just last summer, a 51-year-old man was killed on East 105th Street. Another man, 60, was left bruised and broken beside him. The crash involved a motorcycle, a truck, two sedans, and an SUV. Five vehicles. Two men on foot. One never made it home. NYC Open Data

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay the highest price. Cars and SUVs killed six. Trucks and buses killed two. Motorcycles and mopeds killed one. Bikes killed one. The rest are numbers in a spreadsheet, but every number is a life cut short or changed forever.

      The system hides behind paperwork. As one survivor put it, “Ninety percent of their crashes have no police report and in 90 percent of these crashes over 90 percent the rider flees the scene.” The city counts the dead, but the wounded are lost in the shuffle. “They do have correct stats on those killed but completely skewed stats on those injured.” West Side Spirit

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      Local leaders have taken steps. Council Member Diana Ayala co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, aiming to clear sightlines and protect people on foot. Senator Jose Serrano voted yes on bills to extend school speed zones and to require speed limiters for repeat dangerous drivers.

      But the pace is slow. The city still waits for a default 20 mph speed limit. Protected bike lanes are rare. Every delay means another family gets the call no one wants.

      Call to Action: Demand More, Now

      Do not wait for another name to become a number. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every crosswalk. Demand real enforcement against the drivers who kill and maim. The dead cannot speak. You can.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB11 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 8, assembly district AD 68 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB11?
      It includes the East Harlem (South), East Harlem (North), and Randall’S Island neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8 and District 9, Assembly District AD 68, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 30.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB11?
      Cars and SUVs: 6 deaths, 354 injuries. Trucks and buses: 2 deaths, 33 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 death, 18 injuries. Bikes: 1 death, 39 injuries. These numbers show that cars and trucks do the most harm, but every mode can kill. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and real enforcement save lives. Calling them ‘accidents’ hides the truth.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, daylight crosswalks, and protected bike lanes. They can demand data-driven enforcement focused on the most dangerous drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      ▸

      Hundreds rode through Manhattan. They protested NYPD’s push to criminalize minor bike infractions. Delivery riders, many undocumented, face court summonses for sidewalk riding and red lights. Judges toss charges. Police say it’s about compliance. Riders say it’s about targeting.

      West Side Spirit reported on June 2, 2025, that hundreds of cyclists protested in Manhattan against the NYPD’s new policy of issuing criminal court summonses for minor bike infractions. The crackdown targets delivery workers, especially undocumented riders, for offenses like sidewalk riding and nonstandard bike setups. NYPD Commissioner Jessica Tisch defended the move, stating, 'When it comes to traffic safety, compliance is not optional.' Protesters and advocates argue the policy is discriminatory and escalates minor violations, risking severe consequences for vulnerable workers. Ben Furnas of Transportation Alternatives called it 'really inappropriate escalation.' The article highlights confusion over traffic rules and the risk of criminalization for actions previously handled as civil matters, raising questions about enforcement priorities and the impact on immigrant communities.
