      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB11?

      Bodies in the Street, Blood on City Hall’s Hands

      Written June 21, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Manhattan CB11: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Manhattan CB11, the numbers do not lie. Twelve people are dead. Twenty-seven suffered serious injuries. In the last twelve months alone, 680 were hurt, six killed. The dead do not speak. The living limp, bleed, or grieve. A man steps off the curb and does not come home. A cyclist is thrown to the pavement, head split open. A mother is beaten for her e-bike, left with a broken skull and a family waiting in the dark.

      On May 16, Diana Agudela was attacked on Randall’s Island. Her daughter demanded more protection and lights: “We need more protection, we need more lights. It is ridiculous that the park that my mom would ride from to go from work to home had no lights and no cameras.” The path was dark. The city was silent.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs are the main killers. Six deaths, 243 minor injuries, 94 moderate, three serious. Trucks and buses: two dead, 17 minor, 13 moderate, one serious. Motorcycles and mopeds: one dead, 14 minor, two moderate, one serious. Bikes: one dead, 20 minor, 15 moderate, two serious. The numbers are not just numbers. They are bodies. They are lives cut short or broken.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      City Hall talks about Vision Zero. Albany passed Sammy’s Law, letting the city lower speed limits. But the limit is not yet lowered. The city could act now. It waits. Speed cameras save lives, but the law that keeps them running is always about to expire.

      After a crash in Central Park, a daughter described her mother as defenseless: “She was a defenseless person who was leaving tired from work, coming back to her kids.” The city has not defended her. The council and mayor have the power. They have not used it.

      What You Can Do

      The disaster is slow, but it is not fate. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras that never go dark. Demand lights and cameras where the city leaves you in the shadows. Do not wait for another body in the road. Take action now.

      Int 0928-2024
      Ayala co-sponsors cool pavement pilot bill with no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council wants DOT to test cool pavement in four heat-vulnerable, disadvantaged neighborhoods. The pilot aims to fight the city’s heat island effect. The bill sits in committee. No direct impact on pedestrian or cyclist safety. Streets stay hot. Danger remains.

      Int 0928-2024, introduced May 23, 2024, sits 'Laid Over in Committee' at the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill orders the Department of Transportation, with Health and Mental Hygiene, to run a pilot project testing cool pavement—materials that lower street temperatures—across at least four neighborhoods facing heat and disadvantage. The official summary reads: 'A Local Law in relation to requiring the department of transportation to conduct a pilot project on the use of cool pavement.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by 29 co-sponsors. The bill requires DOT to report findings to the Mayor and Council. Safety analysts note no direct effect on vulnerable road users: the measure targets heat, not traffic violence. The city’s most dangerous streets remain unchanged.

      Int 0875-2024
      Ayala co-sponsors bill to boost step street lighting, improving pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council wants lights on city step streets. The bill orders the DOT to brighten at least 25 stairways a year. Dark stairs mean danger. Light means fewer falls, fewer hidden threats. The push comes from a broad coalition. The goal is simple: safer steps.

      Int 0875-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced May 16, 2024, it commands the Department of Transportation to install pedestrian lighting on at least 25 step streets each year. The bill’s matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the installation of pedestrian lighting on step streets.' Prime sponsor Joann Ariola leads, joined by Council Members Hanks, Ayala, Salaam, Brooks-Powers, Banks, Restler, Brannan, Ung, Gutiérrez, Louis, Hudson, Schulman, Ossé, Krishnan, Nurse, Moya, and Marmorato. Step streets are public staircases linking streets at different heights. The bill aims to ensure these stairs are well-lit, reducing risk for walkers. Once all step streets are lit, the mandate ends. The measure targets a clear hazard: darkness on city stairs.
