      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB11?

      No More Dead Neighbors: Lower the Speed, Save a Life

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB11: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      Three people are dead in Queens CB11 in the past year. One was a child. One was over 65. One was in the prime of life. Four more suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In total, 643 people were hurt in 1,106 crashes in just twelve months. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      The dead do not get a second chance. The living carry scars. A 75-year-old woman was killed crossing 47th Avenue. The cause: driver inattention. The record shows only this: “Fracture - Distorted - Dislocation.” NYC Open Data.

      The Machines: Cars, SUVs, and the Weight of Steel

      SUVs and sedans are the main killers. In the last three years, SUVs and cars took two lives and left dozens bleeding. Trucks and vans added to the toll. Bikes and mopeds did not kill anyone here, but they are not immune elsewhere. The street is a field of risk, and the largest machines win.

      The Response: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      City Hall says the right things. “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many,” said Mayor Adams. The NYPD promises to be “highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action” said Commissioner Caban.

      Sammy’s Law passed. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit is not yet lowered. The law sits. The streets wait. Speed cameras work, but their future is always in doubt. Each delay is another risk. Each day of inaction is another family’s nightmare.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      Int 1145-2024
      Lee sponsors bill capping new e-bike speeds, potentially reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill targets e-bike and e-scooter share systems. Speedometers become mandatory. New riders lose electric assist at 10 mph. Brewer, Lee, and Banks sponsor. The measure sits in committee. The city moves to slow the machines. Streets may change.

      Int 1145-2024 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on December 19, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that electric bicycles and electric scooters that are part of share systems have speedometers and limit electric speed assistance to new riders," would force all shared e-bikes and e-scooters to have working speedometers. For new riders, electric assist would cut out at 10 miles per hour. Council Members Linda Lee (primary sponsor), Gale A. Brewer, and Chris Banks back the measure. The bill aims to slow inexperienced users and make speeds visible. It remains in committee. No safety analyst has yet assessed its impact on vulnerable road users.

      Paladino co-sponsors bill for autism warning plaques, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council wants autism warning plaques on city streets. Parents could request signs. The bill sits in committee. Sponsors say it alerts drivers. No proven safety gain for children. The city keeps counting on signs. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 1142-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 19, 2024.       John Liu Supports Safety Boosting Car Free Streets
      ▸

      City data shows open streets thrive. Storefronts fill up. Pedestrians and cyclists bring life and cash. Cars do not. Vacancy rates drop where traffic is banned. Volunteers keep these corridors alive, but city support lags behind their success.

      On November 18, 2024, the Department of City Planning released a report titled 'Storefront Activity in NYC Neighborhoods.' The analysis, covered by Streetsblog NYC, finds that open streets—car-free corridors—have about half the vacant storefronts of car-filled streets. The report states: 'vibrant public spaces are key to the success of local businesses.' City officials like Ya-Ting Liu, chief public realm officer, and volunteers such as Alex Morano and Brent Bovenzi, praised the program's impact. Bovenzi noted, 'the program is shrinking because too much of the burden falls upon volunteer labor.' The Open Streets program, now permanent, covers over 130 locations but relies heavily on volunteers. Advocates urge the city to invest more, as the data shows people-centric design drives economic recovery and safer, more vibrant neighborhoods.
