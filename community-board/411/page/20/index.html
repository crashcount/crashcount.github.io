      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB11?

      No More Dead Neighbors: Lower the Speed, Save a Life

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB11: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      Three people are dead in Queens CB11 in the past year. One was a child. One was over 65. One was in the prime of life. Four more suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In total, 643 people were hurt in 1,106 crashes in just twelve months. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      The dead do not get a second chance. The living carry scars. A 75-year-old woman was killed crossing 47th Avenue. The cause: driver inattention. The record shows only this: “Fracture - Distorted - Dislocation.” NYC Open Data.

      The Machines: Cars, SUVs, and the Weight of Steel

      SUVs and sedans are the main killers. In the last three years, SUVs and cars took two lives and left dozens bleeding. Trucks and vans added to the toll. Bikes and mopeds did not kill anyone here, but they are not immune elsewhere. The street is a field of risk, and the largest machines win.

      The Response: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      City Hall says the right things. “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many,” said Mayor Adams. The NYPD promises to be “highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action” said Commissioner Caban.

      Sammy’s Law passed. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit is not yet lowered. The law sits. The streets wait. Speed cameras work, but their future is always in doubt. Each delay is another risk. Each day of inaction is another family’s nightmare.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      Res 0235-2022
      Lee co-sponsors MTA lead cleanup resolution, boosting street safety and public health.
      ▸

      Paint chips rain down from old elevated tracks. Lead coats the streets. The council calls on the MTA to clean up. Riders, walkers, and children breathe poison. The city waits. The bill is filed. The danger lingers.

      Res 0235-2022 was introduced on June 16, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution calls on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to remediate any transportation structure, primarily elevated train lines, with extremely high levels of lead. Council Member Sandy Nurse led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Cabán, Hanif, Abreu, Louis, Holden, Gutiérrez, Joseph, Won, Brewer, and Lee. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. The resolution cites studies showing lead levels in paint chips from elevated tracks far above legal limits. The council demands action to protect New Yorkers from toxic exposure. The MTA, a state-run agency, holds the power to act. The bill’s filing leaves the hazard unresolved.

      ▸

      Council passed Albany’s speed camera expansion. Cameras now run all day, every day. Seven members opposed. Supporters cited lives lost when cameras slept. Opponents called it a tax. The vote followed a deadly year. Danger stalks city streets, especially near schools.

      On May 26, 2022, the New York City Council voted 43-7 to approve Albany’s extension and expansion of the city’s speed camera program. The measure, handled by the Transportation Committee, allows speed cameras to operate 24/7. The matter summary: 'The City Council approved Albany's extension and expansion of New York's speed camera program, allowing cameras to operate 24/7, but not before several council members voiced opposition.' The vote came after a year of high road deaths, with special risk near schools.

      Liu Supports Maintaining Speed Cameras Without Endorsing Expansion
      ▸

      Albany stalls on S5602. The mayor stays quiet. Speed cameras hang in the balance. Some senators push for 24/7 enforcement. Others hedge. Council members want camera cash for roads. The clock runs out. Streets stay dangerous. Lives hang on the vote.

      Senate bill S5602, sponsored by Sen. Andrew Gounardes, would expand New York City’s speed camera program to 24/7 operation. The legislative session nears its end with no clear action. State Sen. Jabari Brisport supports the bill, calling for round-the-clock enforcement. Sen. John Liu backs maintaining cameras but won’t commit to S5602. Some City Council members demand that camera revenue fund local road improvements. Assembly Member Dick Gottfried doubts the Council will send the required home rule message in time, while Assembly Member William Magnarelli promises to advance the bill if they do. Mayor Adams’ team claims safety was discussed in Albany, but the mayor himself stayed silent. The bill faces opposition over increased penalties and insurance notifications. Without action, the city’s speed cameras—and the safety of its streets—are at risk.

      Int 0312-2022
      Lee co-sponsors bill raising fines for excessive vehicle noise.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to hike penalties for loud cars and trucks. The measure targeted blaring horns, illegal claxons, and roaring engines. It died at session’s end. No direct safety gains for people walking or biking. Noise remains. Streets stay loud.

      Int 0312-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety. The bill aimed to amend the city’s administrative code to increase penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles, including those under and over 10,000 pounds, and for unauthorized horns and claxons. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to increasing certain penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles.' Council Member Sandra Ung sponsored the bill, joined by 26 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. There is no evidence the bill would have improved safety for vulnerable road users. The city’s noise problem, and its threat to livable streets, remains unresolved.

      Int 0336-2022
      Lee co-sponsors bill shifting sidewalk repair to city, boosting pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0336-2022 would force the Parks Department to repair sidewalks wrecked by city trees. Homeowners escape blame. The city takes the hit. The bill died in committee. Broken sidewalks stay broken. Cyclists and pedestrians still trip.

      Int 0336-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to shift responsibility for sidewalk damage caused by city-owned trees from homeowners to the Parks Department. The matter title reads: 'requiring the parks department to repair damage caused by trees owned by the city of New York.' Council Member Kalman Yeger sponsored the bill, joined by Holden, Ung, Ayala, Restler, Dinowitz, Williams, Gutiérrez, Riley, Schulman, Brooks-Powers, Lee, Paladino, and Vernikov. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. If passed, it could have led to safer, smoother sidewalks for pedestrians and cyclists, removing a hazard that city government created and left to fester.
