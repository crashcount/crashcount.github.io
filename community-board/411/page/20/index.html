      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB11?

      No More Dead Neighbors: Lower the Speed, Save a Life

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB11: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      Three people are dead in Queens CB11 in the past year. One was a child. One was over 65. One was in the prime of life. Four more suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In total, 643 people were hurt in 1,106 crashes in just twelve months. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      The dead do not get a second chance. The living carry scars. A 75-year-old woman was killed crossing 47th Avenue. The cause: driver inattention. The record shows only this: “Fracture - Distorted - Dislocation.” NYC Open Data.

      The Machines: Cars, SUVs, and the Weight of Steel

      SUVs and sedans are the main killers. In the last three years, SUVs and cars took two lives and left dozens bleeding. Trucks and vans added to the toll. Bikes and mopeds did not kill anyone here, but they are not immune elsewhere. The street is a field of risk, and the largest machines win.

      The Response: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      City Hall says the right things. “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many,” said Mayor Adams. The NYPD promises to be “highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action” said Commissioner Caban.

      Sammy’s Law passed. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit is not yet lowered. The law sits. The streets wait. Speed cameras work, but their future is always in doubt. Each delay is another risk. Each day of inaction is another family’s nightmare.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      Int 0758-2022
      Paladino co-sponsors e-bike licensing bill, likely reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council tried to force plates on e-bikes and scooters. The bill died in committee. Riders faced new rules, more scrutiny. The city’s cars kept killing. The council moved on. Vulnerable road users stayed exposed. The system failed to shift blame from drivers.

      Int 0758-2022 was introduced in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on October 12, 2022. The bill aimed to require every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter, and other legal motorized vehicle to be registered with the Department of Transportation and display a visible license plate. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered.' Council Member Inna Vernikov sponsored the bill, joined by Robert F. Holden and 29 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. No safety analyst assessed the impact on vulnerable road users. The bill’s failure left the city’s most exposed—cyclists, pedestrians, passengers—without new protections. The streets stayed dangerous. The cars kept their edge.
