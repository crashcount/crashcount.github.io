      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB11?

      No More Dead Neighbors: Lower the Speed, Save a Life

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB11: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      Three people are dead in Queens CB11 in the past year. One was a child. One was over 65. One was in the prime of life. Four more suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In total, 643 people were hurt in 1,106 crashes in just twelve months. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      The dead do not get a second chance. The living carry scars. A 75-year-old woman was killed crossing 47th Avenue. The cause: driver inattention. The record shows only this: “Fracture - Distorted - Dislocation.” NYC Open Data.

      The Machines: Cars, SUVs, and the Weight of Steel

      SUVs and sedans are the main killers. In the last three years, SUVs and cars took two lives and left dozens bleeding. Trucks and vans added to the toll. Bikes and mopeds did not kill anyone here, but they are not immune elsewhere. The street is a field of risk, and the largest machines win.

      The Response: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      City Hall says the right things. “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many,” said Mayor Adams. The NYPD promises to be “highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action” said Commissioner Caban.

      Sammy’s Law passed. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit is not yet lowered. The law sits. The streets wait. Speed cameras work, but their future is always in doubt. Each delay is another risk. Each day of inaction is another family’s nightmare.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      Liu Supports Safety Boosting Open Streets Permit Reforms
      ▸

      City hall wants to strip red tape from open streets. The plan slashes insurance demands and trains new groups. Advocates say high costs and paperwork choke car-free events. The move could revive lost corridors and let more New Yorkers reclaim the road.

      On June 18, 2024, Chief Public Realm Officer Ya-Ting Liu released a report proposing major reforms to New York City's open streets program. The plan, highlighted in the report 'Realm of Possibility,' aims to 'lift some of the bureaucratic and cost hurdles faced by the largely volunteer open streets groups.' Key elements include lowering or waiving liability insurance for small events, standardizing maintenance agreements, and launching a 'Public Space Academy' to train organizers. Jim Burke, organizer for 34th Avenue, called for removing barriers to make the program equitable. Gib Veconi of Prospect Heights Neighborhood Development Council and Jackson Chabot of Open Plans both praised the reforms, citing burdensome permit processes and high costs as threats to open streets. The proposal responds to advocates' warnings that city policies have stifled car-free spaces, risking the loss of vital corridors for pedestrians and cyclists.

      Res 0449-2024
      Paladino co-sponsors resolution to raise overnight commercial parking fines cap.
      ▸

      Council calls for higher fines on commercial vehicles parked overnight. Current caps let companies treat tickets as business costs. Streets fill with trucks. Residents lose space. The city wants power to set real deterrents. Albany holds the key.

      Resolution 0449-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced June 6, 2024, it urges the State Legislature to let cities raise the cap on commercial overnight parking fines. The matter title reads: "Resolution calling on the State legislature to increase the cap on commercial overnight fines for cities." Council Members Eric Dinowitz (primary sponsor), Vickie Paladino, and Robert F. Holden back the measure. They argue that capped fines are too low—companies absorb them, streets clog with trucks, and residents pay the price. The Council wants authority to set fines that actually deter illegal parking. The resolution points to nearly 27,000 complaints in 2021 and notes that current enforcement tools are weak. The bill is still in committee, awaiting further action.
