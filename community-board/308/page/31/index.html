      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB8?

      No More Dead Kids: Force the City to Fix Eastern Parkway Now

      Written June 29, 2025

      Brooklyn CB8: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      Blood on the Parkway

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy was killed crossing Eastern Parkway. He walked with his sister. A black SUV struck him. The driver stayed. The boy did not. Police said, “They were walking, he was crossing the street with his sister.” No arrest. The street stayed open. The city moved on.

      “Yeah, this street is dangerous,” a neighbor said by the tape.

      In the last year, Brooklyn CB8 saw 1 death, 5 serious injuries, and 388 people hurt in 693 crashes. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers—no one is spared. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      The Toll and the Tools

      Cars and trucks did the most harm. In three and a half years, they killed 3, seriously injured 5, and hurt 254 more. Motorcycles and mopeds left 8 injured. Bikes, 9. The street is not neutral. It picks its victims.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Council Members Chi Ossé and Crystal Hudson have backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and support the SAFE Streets Act. They co-sponsored a bill to keep cars away from crosswalks—still stuck in committee. They called for a full redesign of Atlantic Avenue. But the city moves slow. The dead do not wait.

      Senator Zellnor Myrie rode the streets, saw the blocked lanes, and said, “We should be making this as easy as possible and as safe as possible for as many people as possible.”

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by the city. Call your council member. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand action, not delay. The street will not change itself. You must force it.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB8 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 36, assembly district AD 43 and state senate district SD 20.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB8?
      It includes the Prospect Heights, Crown Heights (North), and Lincoln Terrace Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 35, District 36, and District 41, Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 55, AD 56, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 20 and SD 25.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB8?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 3 deaths, 5 serious injuries, and 254 total pedestrian injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 8 injuries. Bikes caused 9 injuries. The danger is not spread evenly.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. They happen on streets designed for speed, with weak enforcement and poor protection for people walking or cycling. Policy can prevent them.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, ban parking near crosswalks, build protected bike lanes, and redesign dangerous streets. They can push for action, not delay.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      EMP Capital proposes a 17-story, 200-unit mixed-use building at 1034 Atlantic Ave., Brooklyn. The project seeks rezoning to waive parking minimums, offering only 20 spaces instead of 90-95.       Res 0071-2022
      Hudson co-sponsors multilingual transit announcements, boosting public safety for non-English speakers.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to speak to the city. Four languages, not just English. Riders lost in translation miss warnings, miss stops, miss safety. The bill died in committee. The silence continues. Millions still ride in the dark.

      Resolution 0071-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on March 10, 2022. The resolution urged the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make bus and subway announcements in the four most commonly spoken languages in New York City. The official title: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make bus and subway announcements in the four most commonly spoken languages in New York City.' Council Members Shahana K. Hanif (primary sponsor), Sandra Ung, Crystal Hudson, Julie Won, and Tiffany Cabán backed the measure. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided, but the intent was clear: break the language barrier, reach millions of riders, and give all New Yorkers equal warning and access on transit. The MTA still makes most announcements in English only.

      Crystal Hudson Supports Safety Boosting Willoughby Avenue Open Street
      ▸

      Tempers flared as Fort Greene residents clashed over the Willoughby Avenue open street. Barriers vanished without warning, then returned after public outcry. Councilmember Crystal Hudson stood firm. DOT cited rising crash deaths. Most neighbors backed the car-free stretch.

      On March 8, 2022, Councilmember Crystal Hudson hosted a heated debate on the Willoughby Avenue open street in District 35. The meeting followed the abrupt, unannounced removal of barriers from the eight-block stretch, which sparked swift backlash. Hudson, joined by DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodríguez, faced vocal opponents worried about lost parking and emergency access. Hudson said, 'This is about more than just open streets, this is about safe streets.' DOT surveys showed 88 percent support for permanent safety changes. Mayor Eric Adams ordered the street restored after learning Hudson opposed its closure. Rodríguez highlighted a spike in traffic deaths, noting, 'We have a responsibility to think about, first of all, protecting our senior citizens.' According to NYC Crash Mapper, 72 people—mostly cyclists and motorists—were injured on this stretch in the past decade, but no deaths. The open streets program, launched during the pandemic, remains popular and aims to protect vulnerable road users.

      Hudson Supports Safety Boosting End to Parking Minimums
      ▸

      Brooklyn lawmakers tell developers: no more parking mandates. They want special permits to drop parking. They say parking rules drive up costs, block affordable homes, and fuel car use. The message is clear—build for people, not for cars.

      On March 1, 2022, Brooklyn politicians announced a push to eliminate mandatory parking minimums for new developments. The action is not a formal bill, but a policy stance led by Borough President Antonio Reynoso and Council Member Lincoln Restler. The group, including Council Members Jennifer Gutierrez, Crystal Hudson, Chi Osse, Sandy Nurse, Alexa Avilés, Shahana Hanif, Rita Joseph, and Farah Louis, demands that developers seeking zoning changes first apply for a special permit to waive parking requirements. Restler said, 'Developers need our consent and approval, and we are telling them, plainly, that they have to file for a special permit to end parking requirements.' Reynoso added, 'You don't need to do that anymore.' Avilés called parking mandates a barrier to affordable housing and climate action. The group argues that parking minimums raise construction costs, encourage car use, and block green space. Restler warned he is 'far more likely to vote no' on projects without a parking waiver. Advocates and policy experts back the move, and related state legislation is pending.
