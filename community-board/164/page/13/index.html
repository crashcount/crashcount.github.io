      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB64?

      Central Park: Waiting for the Next Crash

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Manhattan CB64: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      Blood on the Loop

      A man walks in Central Park. A horse breaks free. Screams. Hooves. A split second to jump the fence. “I was there with my friend walking on the sidewalk and almost got trampled by the poor horse. It was so terrifying” said Hayley. No one died that day. Not yet.

      A week later, two carriage drivers collide. Both go to the hospital. The horses survive. The city shrugs. “Police say the operators are expected to be okay, and the horses were not severely injured” reported ABC7. The carriages keep rolling. The risk stays.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      In the last year, 35 people were injured in 75 crashes in Manhattan CB64. One was seriously hurt. No deaths in the last twelve months. But the years before left scars. Since 2022, 217 people have been injured here. Six were seriously hurt. One died. Most were walking, riding, or just trying to cross the street. Trucks, bikes, cars, mopeds—each left their mark. The numbers are cold. The pain is not.

      Leaders Talk. Streets Wait.

      Local leaders say the right words. They talk of Vision Zero, of safer streets, of new laws. But the carriages still run. The speed limits stay high. The cameras blink on and off. Each delay is a risk. Each silence is a choice. The city has the power to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not used it. The law to keep speed cameras alive is up for renewal. It hangs by a thread.

      What Now

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand permanent speed cameras. Join Transportation Alternatives or Families for Safe Streets. Stand with the living before they become the lost.

      Do not wait for the next siren. Act now.

      Res 0235-2022
      Brewer co-sponsors MTA lead cleanup resolution, boosting street safety and public health.
      ▸

      Paint chips rain down from old elevated tracks. Lead coats the streets. The council calls on the MTA to clean up. Riders, walkers, and children breathe poison. The city waits. The bill is filed. The danger lingers.

      Res 0235-2022 was introduced on June 16, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution calls on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to remediate any transportation structure, primarily elevated train lines, with extremely high levels of lead. Council Member Sandy Nurse led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Cabán, Hanif, Abreu, Louis, Holden, Gutiérrez, Joseph, Won, Brewer, and Lee. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. The resolution cites studies showing lead levels in paint chips from elevated tracks far above legal limits. The council demands action to protect New Yorkers from toxic exposure. The MTA, a state-run agency, holds the power to act. The bill’s filing leaves the hazard unresolved.

      Int 0556-2022
      Brewer sponsors bill to cut truck traffic, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force big buildings to cut truck chaos. Owners would need plans to shrink deliveries, use off-peak hours, and keep trucks off the street. The bill aimed to create a city office to enforce these rules. Session ended. No law.

      Int 0556-2022 was introduced in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on June 16, 2022. The bill required owners of commercial buildings over 500,000 square feet to submit and carry out delivery and servicing plans. The matter summary reads: 'creating an office of sustainable delivery systems and requiring large generator of truck traffic buildings to produce and implement a delivery and servicing plan.' Council Member Gale A. Brewer sponsored the bill, joined by Rivera, Gutiérrez, Restler, Won, Sanchez, and Cabán. The bill called for on-site loading, delivery reservations, and off-peak scheduling to cut truck traffic and street loading.       Int 0501-2022
      Brewer co-sponsors bill boosting civilian reporting to improve street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants near schools. Civilians can report violators. Each offense draws a $175 fine. The city pays whistleblowers a cut. The bill stalled. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0501-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on June 2, 2022. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations,' aimed to create a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 1,320 feet of a school. The penalty: $175 per violation, enforced through the Office of Administrative Trials and Hearings. Civilians, except city employees, could file complaints and receive 25% of collected fines. The Department of Transportation would support this with a phased-in reporting program and annual public reports. Council Member Carlina Rivera led as primary sponsor, joined by over two dozen co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Int 0500-2022
      Brewer co-sponsors bill to revoke private parking permits, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0500-2022 would kill private vehicle parking permits. All existing permits get revoked. Only disabled drivers and union contracts are spared. The bill targets abuse and privilege. It died in committee. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0500-2022 was introduced on June 2, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill sought to "prohibit the issuance of private vehicle parking permits and revoke such existing permits," with exceptions for people with disabilities and collective bargaining agreements. Council Member Christopher Marte led as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Brewer, Joseph, Nurse, Cabán, Avilés, Bottcher, Won, Ossé, and Richardson Jordan, plus the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill aimed to end a system that let private cars claim curb space, fueling illegal parking and danger for those on foot or bike. It was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. The status quo remains: permits still shield drivers, while vulnerable road users face the risk.

      Brewer Demands NYPD Enforce Safety Boosting Placard Law
      ▸

      On May 5, 2022, Council Member Gale Brewer demanded NYPD compliance with Local Law 6. The law requires weekly checks of at least 25 blocks for placard abuse and illegal parking, plus monthly reports to city officials. Brewer’s letter to Commissioner Keechant Sewell stated, "The Department has not been in compliance with this law, and I respectfully ask the Department to treat illegal parking and placard abuse with the level of urgency it deserves." Brewer’s push highlights the city’s ongoing struggle to protect streets from lawless parking and official neglect.

      Int 0075-2022
      Brewer sponsors bill banning moving billboards, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban moving billboards. The bill would have stopped cars from carrying giant ads through city streets. Sponsors wanted to codify an existing rule. The session ended. The bill died. The danger rolls on.

      Int 0075-2022 was introduced on March 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to ban moving billboards, echoing the matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to banning moving billboards.' Council Members Gale A. Brewer (primary), Justin L. Brannan, Erik D. Bottcher, Kalman Yeger, and Lincoln Restler sponsored the measure. The bill sought to codify an existing city rule (34 RCNY 4-12(j)), making it illegal to operate, stand, or park vehicles for commercial advertising. Violators would face a $500 penalty. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.
