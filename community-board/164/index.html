      Central Park: Waiting for the Next Crash

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Manhattan CB64: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      Blood on the Loop

      A man walks in Central Park. A horse breaks free. Screams. Hooves. A split second to jump the fence. “I was there with my friend walking on the sidewalk and almost got trampled by the poor horse. It was so terrifying” said Hayley. No one died that day. Not yet.

      A week later, two carriage drivers collide. Both go to the hospital. The horses survive. The city shrugs. “Police say the operators are expected to be okay, and the horses were not severely injured” reported ABC7. The carriages keep rolling. The risk stays.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      In the last year, 35 people were injured in 75 crashes in Manhattan CB64. One was seriously hurt. No deaths in the last twelve months. But the years before left scars. Since 2022, 217 people have been injured here. Six were seriously hurt. One died. Most were walking, riding, or just trying to cross the street. Trucks, bikes, cars, mopeds—each left their mark. The numbers are cold. The pain is not.

      Leaders Talk. Streets Wait.

      Local leaders say the right words. They talk of Vision Zero, of safer streets, of new laws. But the carriages still run. The speed limits stay high. The cameras blink on and off. Each delay is a risk. Each silence is a choice. The city has the power to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not used it. The law to keep speed cameras alive is up for renewal. It hangs by a thread.

      What Now

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand permanent speed cameras. Join Transportation Alternatives or Families for Safe Streets. Stand with the living before they become the lost.

      Do not wait for the next siren. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB64 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 6, assembly district AD 69 and state senate district SD 47.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB64?
      It includes the Central Park neighborhood. It also overlaps parts of Council District District 6, Assembly Districts AD 67, AD 68, AD 69, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28, SD 30, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB64?
      Cars and Trucks: 1 death, 3 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 2 moderate injuries. Bikes: 2 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These are not random. They happen because of choices—speed limits, enforcement, street design. They can be prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, make speed cameras permanent, redesign dangerous streets, and fund real enforcement. Every delay costs lives.

      A cyclist rode north through Heckscher Fields and struck a 57-year-old woman standing in his path. The bike hit her head, causing bleeding. She stayed down, conscious but still. The cyclist did not stop. The bike showed no damage.

      According to the police report, a 26-year-old man riding a bike northbound in Central Park's Heckscher Fields collided with a 57-year-old woman who was standing in his path. The report states, 'The bike struck her head. Blood ran. She stayed down, conscious but still.' The cyclist did not stop after the crash, and the bike showed no visible damage. The police report lists 'Pedestrian/Bicyclist/Other Pedestrian Error/Confusion' as a contributing factor. No driver-specific errors such as failure to yield or distraction are cited. The cyclist was wearing a helmet and suffered a minor abrasion to his lower leg. The incident highlights confusion and error as systemic dangers in shared park spaces.
