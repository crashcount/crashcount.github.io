      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB1?

      Brooklyn’s Streets Bleed—Hold Your Leaders to Account

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      In Brooklyn CB1—Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, East Williamsburg—the street is a wound that never closes. Sixteen dead. Forty-seven left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. In three and a half years, the count keeps rising. Cyclists, children, elders. No one is spared.

      A man on a bike, helmet strapped, is thrown and killed on Lynch Street. A child is crushed by a dump truck turning left on Withers. A pedestrian is struck dead by a speeding e-bike on India Street. The numbers are cold, but the stories burn.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and trucks are the main killers. They took four lives. Trucks alone killed three. Bikes and e-bikes killed two. Mopeds and motorcycles, another two. The rest are left broken—bones, heads, lives. The city’s own data shows the pattern: cars and trucks caused the most harm, but every mode can kill when the street is built for speed and chaos.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have put their names on bills. Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez and Lincoln Restler co-sponsored laws to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and push for citywide daylighting at intersections. But the bills sit in committee, waiting. The city promises, but the street stays the same.

      Assembly Member Emily Gallagher called out the city’s failure to act: “I truly believe that the opposition to a road diet on McGuinness is about fear, bad faith and control.” She urged the mayor to stay the course. Still, the dead keep coming.

      A neighbor saw the aftermath: “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open.” The driver had a mask. He hit the guy and he left.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. The street can be changed. The bills can move. The crosswalks can be cleared. The speed can be slowed. But only if you demand it.

      Call your council member. Demand daylight at every corner, protected lanes, and a street that does not kill. The silence of leaders is a choice. Make them choose life.

      Gallagher Supports Safety Boosting Idaho Stop Bill
      ▸

      Senate Republicans forced a vote on S533 to repeal congestion pricing. Democrats may let it advance with a hollow 'Aye Without Recommendation.' Meanwhile, the Idaho Stop bill, which could save cyclists’ lives, remains stalled. NYPD cracks down on riders. Cyclists keep dying.

      On May 13, 2025, the Senate Transportation Committee considered S533, a bill to repeal congestion pricing, after a procedural motion by Sen. Jack Martins. Committee Chair Jeremy Cooney was compelled to place it on the agenda. Democrats may use 'Aye Without Recommendation' to let the bill advance without clear support. A watchdog coalition, including Reinvent Albany and Bike New York, called the bill 'contrary to notions of basic fairness.' The same day, activists lobbied for the Idaho Stop bill (S639/A7071), sponsored by Sen. Rachel May and Assembly Member Karen McMahon. The bill would let cyclists treat red lights as stop signs and stop signs as yield signs, a move proven to reduce injuries. Despite support, the bill remains blocked. NYPD continues harsh enforcement against cyclists, who make up a small share of city trips but receive a disproportionate number of tickets. Cyclist deaths keep rising.
