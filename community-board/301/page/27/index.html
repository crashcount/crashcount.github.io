      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Brooklyn CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Recent Toll

      In Brooklyn CB1, the numbers do not lie. Sixteen people killed. Fifty-one left with serious injuries. In just the last twelve months, five more have died. Five more families torn open. 523 injured since January.

      A child crushed by a dump truck on Withers Street. A cyclist killed on Lynch Street, helmet split, blood on the curb. A pedestrian struck dead by an e-bike on India Street. These are not distant stories. They happened here, in the neighborhoods where we walk, ride, and wait for the light to change.

      The Human Cost

      A sister-in-law stands at a curb, voice flat: “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter.” said her daughter

      A driver, after spinning donuts in a parking lot, tells police: “He lost control when he was doing donuts with the vehicle.” tells police

      The street is silent after the sirens fade. Tire marks circle the lot for days. The loss stays.

      Leadership: Progress and Gaps

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Assembly Member Emily Gallagher has pushed for lower speed limits and supported the McGuinness Boulevard redesign, saying the opposition is about “fear, bad faith and control.” said Gallagher

      Senator Julia Salazar and Gallagher both voted to extend school speed zones and co-sponsored bills to force repeat speeders to install speed-limiting devices. voted to extend school speed zones But the pace is slow. The carnage continues. Every week without action is another risk.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call your state senator. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat offenders. Join Families for Safe Streets. Stand up at your community board. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 34, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB1?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, and East Williamsburg neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33 and District 34, Assembly Districts AD 50 and AD 53, and State Senate Districts SD 18 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB1?
      Cars and Trucks: 4 deaths, 284 minor injuries, 98 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries (total 385 incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 17 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries (total 25 incidents). Bikes: 2 deaths, 13 minor injuries, 8 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury (total 24 incidents).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns: repeat offenders, speeding, reckless turns. These deaths are preventable with better policy and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, pass and enforce laws against repeat speeders, and support automated enforcement. They can act now, not after another death.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0193-2024
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors taxi warning decal bill with neutral safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing taxis and for-hire cars to post bold warnings on doors. The signs tell passengers: look for cyclists before you open up. A small step. The city hands out the decals. No cost to drivers.

      Int 0193-2024 became law on May 31, 2025, after action by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...requiring taxis and for-hire vehicles to display a decal warning passengers to look for cyclists when opening the door,' was sponsored by Lincoln Restler and co-sponsored by over twenty council members, including Gutiérrez, Hudson, and Rivera. The law mandates clear warning decals on all rear passenger doors of taxis and for-hire vehicles. The Taxi and Limousine Commission will provide the signs at no cost. The measure aims to cut down on 'dooring'—a threat to cyclists citywide. The mayor returned the bill unsigned, but it became law.
