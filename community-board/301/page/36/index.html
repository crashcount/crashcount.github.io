      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB1?

      Six Dead, Hundreds Hurt—Brooklyn Streets Are Killing Fields

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed citation links, and cleaned citations.)

      Brooklyn CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A man steps into the street. He does not make it to the other side. In the last twelve months, six people died in crashes on Brooklyn CB1’s streets. Twenty-two more were seriously injured. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about age. Children, elders, workers—all caught in the same crosswalks, on the same corners. In total, 906 people were hurt in 1,879 crashes this past year alone (NYC Open Data).

      A cyclist is killed, his head split open. A witness says, “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open” (NY Daily News). The driver leaves. The street is quiet again. The blood dries. The city moves on.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the harm: 385 pedestrian injuries and deaths. Trucks and buses: 56. Motorcycles and mopeds: 25. Bikes: 24. The numbers are not just numbers. They are bodies in the road, families waiting at hospitals, shoes left behind on the curb (NYC Open Data).

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      Local leaders have signed on to bills. Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez and Assembly Member Emily Gallagher have backed laws to curb repeat speeders and expand protected bike lanes. But the bills sit in committee. The danger does not wait. “We want to make sure what happened doesn’t happen again,” said a neighbor after another crash (CBS New York).

      Some progress: daylighting bills to clear sightlines at crosswalks, and support for speed-limiting tech for the worst drivers. But every delay means another family gets the call. Every loophole is a life lost.

      What Now? No More Waiting

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by the city, by lawmakers, by those who look away. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action on bills that stop repeat speeders.

      The dead cannot speak. The living must. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 34, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB1?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, and East Williamsburg neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33 and District 34, Assembly Districts AD 50 and AD 53, and State Senate Districts SD 18 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB1?
      Cars and SUVs: 385 injuries and deaths. Trucks and buses: 56. Motorcycles and mopeds: 25. Bikes: 24. These numbers come from city open data for the period 2022–2025.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Crashes are the result of choices—by drivers, by city planners, by lawmakers. They can be prevented with better street design, lower speeds, and stronger enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, expand daylighting at intersections, require speed-limiting tech for repeat offenders, and build more protected bike lanes. They can also hold city agencies accountable for delays.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1115-2023
      Restler co-sponsors stricter alternate side parking enforcement, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1115-2023 targets repeat alternate side parking violators. First offense: $65. More violations: $100 each. Three strikes in a year, and the car gets towed. Filed at session’s end. Bottcher and Restler sponsor. No direct safety impact noted.

      Int 1115-2023 was introduced on June 22, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill sought to amend city code by setting penalties for alternate side parking violations at $65 for a first offense and $100 for each subsequent offense within a 12-month period. After three violations in a year, the vehicle would be towed if found in violation again. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to establishing penalties for alternate side parking violations and requiring towing of certain vehicles in violation of alternate side parking rules.' Council Members Erik D. Bottcher (primary sponsor) and Lincoln Restler (co-sponsor) led the effort. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst assessment was provided, and no direct impact on vulnerable road users was noted.

      Reynoso Supports Boroughwide Summer Streets Expansion and Connection
      ▸

      Mayor Adams expands Summer Streets to every borough. Five Saturdays. Seventeen miles. Cars banned from 7 a.m. to 1 p.m. Brooklyn and Queens get major corridors. Advocates want longer hours and connected routes. City cites costs and safety. Streets open, but only for a while.

      On June 12, 2023, Mayor Adams announced the expansion of New York City’s Summer Streets program to all five boroughs. The program, managed by the Department of Transportation, will run on five Saturdays in July and August, from 7 a.m. to 1 p.m., covering 17 miles. Borough presidents Antonio Reynoso (Brooklyn) and Donovan Richards (Queens) pushed for this expansion, with Reynoso praising the use of major corridors like Eastern Parkway. Mayor Adams said, “This is a five-borough city and we are now saying the Summer Streets program is going be a five-borough initiative. Everyone deserves to enjoy that.” Adams expressed interest in longer hours but cited public safety and NYPD staffing costs as barriers. Advocates want more hours and connected car-free routes. The city will spend $1.5 million, not including staffing. The expansion marks a step forward, but the early end time leaves advocates wanting more.

      Int 1063-2023
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors bill for EV charging info app, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to create a city app showing electric vehicle charging stations. The app would map locations, show voltage, connector types, and real-time status. It would include e-bike charging info. Strict privacy rules would limit data collection and retention.

      Bill Int 1063-2023 was introduced on June 8, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill called for the Department of Information Technology and Telecommunications to build a mobile app displaying all public electric vehicle charging stations in New York City. The app would show locations, voltage, charging level, connector types, e-bike compatibility, and real-time availability. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the creation of a mobile application that provides information about electric vehicle charging stations.' Council Member Keith Powers sponsored the bill, joined by Avilés, Cabán, Farías, Riley, Richardson Jordan, Ossé, Ung, Hanif, Restler, and Gutiérrez. The bill set strict privacy: no device data, no tracking, no forced consent. The bill was filed at session’s end, not enacted.
