      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB13?

      Queens Bleeds While Leaders Wait

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Queens CB13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Toll, Week by Week

      In Queens CB13, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-five dead. Twenty-eight seriously hurt. Over 4,400 injured since 2022. Each number is a life cut short or broken.

      Just days ago, a 76-year-old woman died in the back seat of a sedan on the Cross Island Parkway. She never made it home. On the Belt Parkway, a BMW went airborne. Two died. Others were thrown from the car, the road burning beneath them. “They went airborne and into the barrier,” a survivor said. “Jewel was already dead when she flew out the car.”

      A crane slammed into a city bus in Queens Village. Four hurt. “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us,” a passenger said. The bus driver did nothing wrong. The street did not forgive.

      Who Pays the Price

      The old, the young, the ones on foot. SUVs and cars did most of the killing. Trucks and buses followed. Bikes caused two minor injuries. No one died from a bike. The road is not equal. The strong crush the weak.

      Leadership: Words and Waiting

      Council Member Selvena Brooks-Powers has called for more transparency from DOT. “DOT gives us their word every hearing and we are not getting results,” she said. She voted to legalize jaywalking, a step to stop blaming the dead for crossing the street. She co-sponsored bills for more daylight at corners and better data on e-bikes. But the pace is slow. The bodies keep coming.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings. Demand action for the living, not excuses for the dead. The road will not change itself. Only you can force their hand.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 33 and state senate district SD 14. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB13?
      It includes the Glen Oaks-Floral Park-New Hyde Park, Bellerose, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, Laurelton, Springfield Gardens (South)-Brookville, Rosedale, and Montefiore Cemetery neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 31, Assembly Districts AD 26, AD 29, AD 31, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB13?
      Cars and Trucks were responsible for the vast majority of deaths and injuries: 4 deaths and 6 serious injuries from cars/SUVs, 2 deaths from trucks/buses. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused no reported pedestrian deaths or serious injuries. Bikes caused 2 minor injuries and no deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The pattern is clear. The same streets, the same vehicles, the same outcomes. These are preventable deaths and injuries, not random acts.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, install protected crossings, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can pass and enforce laws that protect people, not just cars.

      Brooks-Powers Collaborates on Safety-Boosting Resident Traffic Enforcement Bill
      ▸

      Council Member Restler’s bill lets New Yorkers ticket drivers who block bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, or hydrants near schools. Twenty-four council members back it. The bill targets driver impunity and NYPD inaction. Advocates say it protects cyclists and pedestrians.

      Bill number not specified. Sponsored by Council Member Lincoln Restler, the bill is advancing in the City Council with 24 out of 51 members signed on as of November 7, 2022. It sits with the Transportation Committee, chaired by Selvena Brooks-Powers, who is working with Restler to streamline the complaint process. The bill’s summary: it allows residents to ticket drivers who block bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or fire hydrants within 1,320 feet of a school, creating a $175 fine and a civilian complaint protocol. Restler and Brooks-Powers are refining the process to avoid the pitfalls of the city’s anti-idling law, which discourages participation. The NYPD and DOT oppose the bill, citing concerns about neighbor conflict. Advocates support it, highlighting NYPD’s failure to protect vulnerable road users. The bill is seen as a major improvement over the ineffective 311 system.

      Int 0291-2022
      Brooks-Powers votes yes, boosting citywide safety with new greenway plan.
      ▸

      The Council passed Int 0291-2022, forcing city agencies to map, plan, and report on greenways. The law demands annual updates and public engagement. It aims to carve out safe, car-free corridors for walkers and cyclists. The mayor returned it unsigned.

      Int 0291-2022, now Local Law 115 of 2022, was enacted by the City Council on November 27, 2022. The bill came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, first introduced April 28, 2022. The law's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to a citywide greenway plan.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers sponsored the bill, joined by dozens of co-sponsors including Rivera, Hudson, and others. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor on October 27, 2022. The law orders the Department of Transportation and Parks to identify, map, and report on greenways, and to consult with community boards. Proposals for new greenway segments or repairs must be presented to affected communities within 60 days. The mayor returned the bill unsigned, but it became law. The measure compels the city to plan and maintain safe routes for non-drivers, with regular public reporting and accountability.
