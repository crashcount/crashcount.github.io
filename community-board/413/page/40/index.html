      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB13?

      Speed Kills. Leaders Stall. Blood on Queens Streets.

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Death Count Keeps Climbing

      In Queens CB13, the road does not forgive. Since 2022, 25 people have died in crashes. 4,440 have been injured. 28 suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same (NYC Open Data). The numbers do not tell you about the silence after the sirens. They do not show the blood on the curb, or the shoes left behind.

      Just last month, a man on a motorized scooter was killed at the corner of 116th Avenue and Nashville Boulevard. He was ejected and crushed. The cause: unsafe speed (NYC Open Data). In February, a 27-year-old woman was killed on the Belt Parkway. She was ejected from her car. The cause: unsafe speed (NYC Open Data). The pattern is old. The pain is fresh.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did most of the harm. They killed 4, injured 387, and left 6 with serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 2 and injured 25. Bikes caused 2 minor injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds did not kill or seriously injure anyone in this period (NYC Open Data).

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Some leaders have moved. State Senator Leroy Comrie voted yes to the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the law (Stop Super Speeders Act). Others have backed the extension of school speed zones (school speed zones). But the carnage continues. Council Member Selvena Brooks-Powers said, “Historically in New York City in particular, the transportation system has had many barriers for communities that live in transportation deserts from reaching economic opportunity.” The barriers remain. The deaths remain.

      The Call No One Wants to Make

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. The city has the power to lower speed limits. The state has the power to rein in repeat offenders. The people have the power to demand more. Call your council member. Call your senator. Call the mayor. Tell them: Enough. Lower the speed. End the slaughter.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 33 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB13?
      It includes the Glen Oaks-Floral Park-New Hyde Park, Bellerose, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, Laurelton, Springfield Gardens (South)-Brookville, Rosedale, and Montefiore Cemetery neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 31, Assembly Districts AD 26, AD 29, AD 31, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB13?
      Cars and SUVs killed 4, injured 387, and left 6 with serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 2 and injured 25. Bikes caused 2 minor injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds did not kill or seriously injure anyone in this period (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure of policy, design, or enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, support enforcement against repeat dangerous drivers, and fund safer street designs. They can vote for bills like the Stop Super Speeders Act and push for more speed cameras and traffic calming.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Sanders Supports Increased Penalties for Construction Safety Violations
      ▸

      Twenty-two construction workers died last year in New York City. Lawmakers passed Carlos' Law, raising fines for negligent companies to $500,000. State Sen. Jessica Ramos calls it vital as migrants fill non-union jobs. Advocates warn: enforcement and worker awareness still lag.

      Carlos' Law, passed in early 2023, raises the maximum penalty for construction companies convicted of criminal negligence leading to worker injury or death from $10,000 to $500,000. The law, named after Carlos Moncayo, aims to curb a surge in construction worker deaths—22 in the past year, the highest in five years. State Sen. Jessica Ramos, representing District 13, called the law 'critically needed' as thousands of migrants enter non-union construction jobs lacking basic safety protections. Ramos and other lawmakers supported the bill, but advocates and union officials warn that without strong enforcement and education for new arrivals, deaths and injuries will persist. The law passed after years of advocacy, but the minimum fine was removed, and nonprofits struggle to train the influx of new workers. As Ramos said, 'Behind every worker is a family expecting them to return home.'

      ▸

Key dates: Announced January 12, 2023; hearings and negotiations run until June 30.       Brooks-Powers Supports Commuter Vans Opposes Safety Boosting Legislation
      ▸

      The Council’s Transportation Committee stalled. Three hearings. Two bills passed. No action on street safety, placard abuse, or protected lanes. Chair Selvena Brooks-Powers focused on commuter vans, not Vision Zero. Advocates saw inaction. Vulnerable road users paid the price.

      In 2022, the City Council’s Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, chaired by Selvena Brooks-Powers, held only three hearings on 87 bills and passed just two. The committee, according to the matter summary, was 'largely absent from major transportation issues, including street safety, placard abuse, school street safety, e-mobility planning, ghost plates, and NYPD enforcement.' Brooks-Powers led a resolution supporting commuter vans and spoke on transportation equity, but did not advance key street safety or Vision Zero legislation. The committee failed to hold the Adams administration accountable for missing protected bike and bus lane benchmarks. Advocates and council members voiced frustration at the lack of progress on urgent issues like traffic violence and the Streets Master Plan. The committee’s approach prioritized car-dependent communities and lagged behind previous leadership on safety reforms. Vulnerable road users saw little relief.
