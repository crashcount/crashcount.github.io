      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB13?

      Queens Streets Are Killing Fields—Who Will Stop the Bleeding?

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Twenty-five dead. Twenty-eight seriously hurt. These are not numbers from a war zone. They are the toll of traffic violence in Queens CB13 since 2022 (NYC Open Data). The bodies are not nameless. A 76-year-old woman, killed in the back seat of a sedan on Cross Island Parkway (CrashID 4825309). A 39-year-old man, ejected and crushed on Nashville Boulevard (CrashID 4823380). A 27-year-old woman, dead behind the wheel on the Belt Parkway, speed listed as a cause (CrashID 4791204). The list goes on. The pain does not end.

      Recent Crashes, Unanswered Questions

      The carnage does not slow. Just last month, a man on a motorized scooter was killed at 116th Avenue and Nashville Boulevard. The cause: unsafe speed. He was ejected. He died at the scene (CrashID 4823380). In February, a van crash on South Conduit Avenue killed a 78-year-old passenger and injured two others (CrashID 4792680). Each crash leaves a hole in a family. Each one could have been prevented.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Miles to Go

      Local leaders have not been silent. State Senator Leroy Comrie voted yes to extend school speed zones and to require speed limiters for repeat speeders. These votes matter. But the streets are still deadly. Council Member Selvena Brooks-Powers, who chairs the City Council’s transportation committee, has said, “Historically in New York City in particular, the transportation system has had many barriers for communities that live in transportation deserts from reaching economic opportunity.” The barriers remain. The danger remains.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand Now

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Contact your council member. Call your state senator. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The time to act is now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 33 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB13?
      It includes the Glen Oaks-Floral Park-New Hyde Park, Bellerose, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, Laurelton, Springfield Gardens (South)-Brookville, Rosedale, and Montefiore Cemetery neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 31, Assembly Districts AD 26, AD 29, AD 31, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB13?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB13 were caused by cars and trucks (397 incidents, 4 deaths), followed by SUVs (162 incidents, 1 death), and trucks (17 incidents, 2 deaths). Buses, vans, and bikes caused far fewer injuries and no deaths.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These crashes are preventable. Policies like lower speed limits, speed cameras, and street redesigns have been shown to reduce deaths and injuries. Every crash is a result of choices made by drivers and by those who set the rules of the road.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      Local politicians can vote for lower speed limits, support speed cameras, redesign dangerous streets, and push for enforcement against repeat dangerous drivers. They can also listen to residents and act quickly when lives are at stake.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1069-2024
      Brooks-Powers co-sponsors bill to shorten commercial vehicle parking times, safety impact neutral.
      ▸

      Council bill slashes legal parking time for big rigs. Ninety minutes max for tractor-trailers. Three hours for other commercial trucks. Streets clear faster. Heavy metal moves on.

      Int 1069-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced September 26, 2024. The bill reads: "reducing the maximum time commercial vehicles may park." Sponsored by Kevin C. Riley and 23 others, it limits parking to three hours for most commercial vehicles, and 90 minutes for tractor-trailers, unless signs say otherwise. The law takes effect 120 days after passage. Council aims to keep streets less clogged by oversized trucks. No safety analyst note was provided, but the bill targets long-term truck storage on city streets.

      Int 0346-2024
      Brooks-Powers votes yes to decriminalize jaywalking, boosting pedestrian safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians now cross anywhere, any time. No summons. Law strips drivers of excuses. Streets shift. Power tilts to people on foot.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, rewrites New York’s pedestrian rules. The bill, heard by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passed on September 26, 2024, and became law on October 26, 2024. Its title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way.” Sponsored by Council Member Tiffany Cabán and co-sponsored by Narcisse, Won, Hanif, Hudson, and Restler, the law lets pedestrians cross streets anywhere, even against signals. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks. The Department of Transportation must educate all street users on new rights and responsibilities. The law removes a tool police used to target walkers. It shifts blame from people on foot to the system and those behind the wheel.
