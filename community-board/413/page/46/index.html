      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Four crashes in nine days, and the map keeps bleeding

      Queens CB13: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      On Sep 9, 2025, at Braddock Ave and Ransom St, police records show a driver making a right turn hit a person on a bike going straight. The cyclist was injured. Source.

      Since Jan 1, 2022, in Queens Community Board 13, 34 people have been killed and 6,283 have been injured in traffic crashes, according to city data. People walking account for 11 of those deaths; people on bikes for 1. Source.

      This Week

      • Sep 7: Two people walking were injured near 237 St when one driver in an SUV was recorded going straight; another SUV was parked. Source.
      • Sep 6: At 260 St and 74 Ave, a driver in a sedan turned right and hit a 19‑year‑old on a bike going straight. Source.
      • Sep 2: At 223 St and 114 Ave, a 7‑year‑old girl crossing at the intersection was injured when a driver in an SUV went straight through. Source.

      The pattern is fixed to the pavement

      The deadliest places here are highways and feeders. The Belt Parkway, Cross Island Parkway, and South Conduit Avenue top the list of harm, with deaths and hundreds of injuries between them. Source.

      Deaths cluster in the dark hours and around evening. Police reports also record driver inattention and failure to yield among the factors tied to injuries. Source.

      ““The current state of the Conduit falls significantly short of meeting the needs of drivers, cyclists and pedestrians alike. It’s confusing, it’s poorly designed … and we know the lack of sufficient pedestrian and bike infrastructure makes it even more dangerous,"” Queens Borough President Donovan Richards said about the wider corridor. Source.

      The bills that would slow the worst

      State Senator Leroy Comrie co‑sponsored and voted yes on the Senate’s speed‑limiter bill, S 4045, to require intelligent speed assistance for repeat dangerous drivers. Source. Assembly Member Clyde Vanel co‑sponsored the Assembly version, A 2299. Source.

      At City Hall, Council Member Nantasha M. Williams is the prime sponsor of Int 1347‑2025, ordering maximum enforcement against unlicensed commuter vans; it sits in the Transportation and Infrastructure Committee. Source.

      What this board needs now

      High‑injury corridors here are known. Belt Parkway. Cross Island Parkway. South Conduit Avenue. Brookville Boulevard. Daylight the corners and harden the turns. Add protected space where people walk and bike. Target right‑turn conflicts that keep injuring cyclists at local crossings like Braddock Ave at Ransom St and 74 Ave at 260 St. Source.

      The law can do more work too. Lower speeds citywide and fit repeat speeders’ cars with limiters. The tools exist. The votes are on record. Use them.

      One more child hurt at 114 Ave. One more rider down on Braddock. It does not stop by itself.

      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes, Persons, Vehicles). We filtered records to crashes within Queens Community Board 13 between 2022‑01‑01 and 2025‑09‑18, and grouped by person type and outcome (killed, injured). Data were extracted on Sep 17, 2025. You can explore the base datasets here.

      Sanders Supports Increased Penalties for Construction Safety Violations
      ▸

      Jan 22 - Twenty-two construction workers died last year in New York City. Lawmakers passed Carlos' Law, raising fines for negligent companies to $500,000. State Sen. Jessica Ramos calls it vital as migrants fill non-union jobs. Advocates warn: enforcement and worker awareness still lag.

      Carlos' Law, passed in early 2023, raises the maximum penalty for construction companies convicted of criminal negligence leading to worker injury or death from $10,000 to $500,000. The law, named after Carlos Moncayo, aims to curb a surge in construction worker deaths—22 in the past year, the highest in five years. State Sen. Jessica Ramos, representing District 13, called the law 'critically needed' as thousands of migrants enter non-union construction jobs lacking basic safety protections. Ramos and other lawmakers supported the bill, but advocates and union officials warn that without strong enforcement and education for new arrivals, deaths and injuries will persist. The law passed after years of advocacy, but the minimum fine was removed, and nonprofits struggle to train the influx of new workers. As Ramos said, 'Behind every worker is a family expecting them to return home.'
