      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB13?

      Twelve Dead, No Answers: Queens Streets Still Bleed

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB13

      The numbers do not lie. In the last twelve months, 12 people died and 1,392 were injured in crashes across Queens CB13. Eight suffered serious injuries. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, mothers, sons. In the past year, four people over 75 died. One was only 18. The violence does not stop for age.

      Just last week, July 5, a 76-year-old woman died in the back seat of a sedan on Cross Island Parkway. Two days earlier, a 39-year-old man was killed, ejected from his scooter on Nashville Boulevard. The week before, a 26-year-old man died behind the wheel on Hillside Avenue. The list does not end. It only grows.

      The Human Cost

      The stories are sharp. A BMW vaults the divider on Belt Parkway. The car catches fire. Passengers are thrown onto the road. “They went airborne and into the barrier,” a survivor said. “Jewel was already dead when she flew out the car,” the survivor added.

      A bus turns on Springfield Boulevard. A crane slams into its side. Windows shatter. Riders cry out. “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us,” a passenger told reporters. “The bus driver didn’t do anything wrong,” the passenger said.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have passed bills. Council Member Selvena Brooks-Powers voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed the dead for their own deaths. She has called out the Department of Transportation: “DOT gives us their word every hearing and we are not getting results,” Brooks-Powers said.

      But the streets are not safe. The city has not met its own promises. The bodies keep coming. Most deaths are caused by cars and trucks. The old, the young, the careful, the unlucky. All are at risk.

      Act Now—Demand Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings. Demand that the city stop blaming the dead and start protecting the living. Every day of delay is another family broken.

      S 131
      Comrie co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 131 demands complete street design in state- and federally-funded road projects. It forces agencies to consider people on foot and bike, not just cars. The bill orders public guidance on safer street layouts. Sponsors push for streets that protect all.

      Senate bill S 131, introduced on January 1, 2025, is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in the Senate, awaiting committee action. Its summary reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding; authorizes the department to compile and make publicly available guidance and reference material related to consideration of complete street design.' Primary sponsor Sean Ryan (District 61) leads, joined by co-sponsors Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, Samra Brouk, Cordell Cleare, Leroy Comrie, Jeremy Cooney, Nathalia Fernandez, Michael Gianaris, Kristen Gonzalez, Robert Jackson, Rachel May, Kevin S. Parker, Christopher Ryan, and Luis R. Sepúlveda. The bill compels agencies to put vulnerable road users first in every new project that takes public money. It also demands transparency, requiring the department to publish guidance on safer street design for all.

      Sanders Supports Safety Boosting Traffic Laws and Lower Speeds
      ▸

      New York rolls out sweeping traffic laws for 2025. Charter bus seatbelts, lower speed limits, and congestion pricing hit the streets. Senator Sanders and Governor Hochul push for safer roads. School zones, bridges, and bus riders see new protections. Change comes fast.

      On December 31, 2024, New York announced new transportation laws for 2025. Senator James Sanders sponsored a ban on car lease turn-in fees, stating, "The legislation prohibits charging a turn-in fee at the expiration of the term of a vehicle lease, the basis of which is solely for administrative, handling or clerical charges." Governor Kathy Hochul signed this and other safety bills into law. Charter bus passengers ages 8 to 16 must now wear seatbelts, a response to a fatal crash in Orange County. Sammy's Law, enacted in 2024, lets the city lower speed limits to 20 mph, and even 10 mph on redesigned streets. The Department of Transportation will target 250 locations, focusing on school zones. The MTA launches congestion pricing in Manhattan, with funds set for transit upgrades. Hochul said, "These enhancements to our roads and bridges will improve mobility for motorists, pedestrians and cyclists alike."

      Int 1154-2024
      Brooks-Powers co-sponsors pilot for high-visibility markings, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council moves to test bold pavement markings at crash sites. Five spots per borough. Focus: places where drivers have killed or hurt the most. The city must report back. The law dies when the pilot ends. Streets remember every scar.

      Int 1154-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced December 19, 2024, the bill orders a pilot for high visibility pavement markings. The official title: 'A Local Law in relation to establishing a high visibility pavement marking pilot program and the repeal of this local law upon the expiration thereof.' Council Member Yusef Salaam leads as primary sponsor, joined by Menin, De La Rosa, Brooks-Powers, Cabán, Brannan, and Hanif. The bill demands at least five marked sites per borough, targeting places with the most injuries and deaths from bad driving. After the pilot, the city must report on results and recommend next steps. The law sunsets when the pilot ends. No safety analyst note was provided.
