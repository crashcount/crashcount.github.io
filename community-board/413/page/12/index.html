      No One Walks Away: Queens Streets Are Killing Us

      Written July 18, 2025

      Queens CB13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Death Count Keeps Climbing

      Twelve dead. Fifteen hundred and seven injured. Seven left with wounds that will never heal. These are not numbers from a war. These are the streets of Queens CB13 since last July. The dead are not just statistics. They are neighbors. They are children, elders, workers. They are gone.

      Just weeks ago, a 39-year-old man was killed at 116th Avenue and Nashville Boulevard. He was ejected from his motorized scooter. The cause: driver inattention. His body was crushed. He died there, on the street. NYC Open Data.

      On the Cross Island Parkway, a 76-year-old woman died in the back seat. The crash report lists “unsafe lane changing.” She did not walk away. She did not get a second chance.

      Buses, Cars, and the Unforgiving Road

      The violence is not random. Cars and SUVs did the most harm—four killed, 393 more hurt. Trucks and buses killed two, injured 25. Bikes injured two. No one was spared. The sidewalk is not safe. The crosswalk is not safe. The back seat is not safe.

      Last week, an MTA bus in Flushing jumped the curb. Eight people were hurt. The driver, just 25, told investigators he “misjudged the curb.” But the video showed more. He had fallen asleep at the wheel. The MTA pulled him from service. “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus,” said Ken Baur.

      Leaders Act—But Is It Enough?

      Local leaders have taken steps. Senator Leroy Comrie voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed limiters. He co-sponsored it too. Assembly Member Clyde Vanel voted to extend school speed zones. These are good votes. They are not enough.

      Council Member Selvena Brooks-Powers says, “Historically in New York City in particular, the transportation system has had many barriers for communities that live in transportation deserts from reaching economic opportunity.” She wants more protected bike lanes and bus lanes. But the streets are still killing people.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand Now

      Every day of delay is another life at risk. Call your council member. Call your state senator. Tell them: Lower the speed limit. Build real protection for people walking and biking. End the carnage. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 33 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB13?
      It includes the Glen Oaks-Floral Park-New Hyde Park, Bellerose, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, Laurelton, Springfield Gardens (South)-Brookville, Rosedale, and Montefiore Cemetery neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 31, Assembly Districts AD 26, AD 29, AD 31, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB13?
      SUVs and cars killed 4 and injured 387 pedestrians. Trucks and buses killed 2 and injured 17. Bikes injured 2 pedestrians but caused no deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no pedestrian deaths or injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Unsafe speed, distraction, and poor street design are choices, not fate. Every death is a failure of policy and protection.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, redesign dangerous intersections, and pass laws like the Stop Super Speeders Act to keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1160-2025
      Brooks-Powers votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.
