      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB13?

      Queens Streets Bleed—City Hall Shrugs

      Written July 9, 2025

      Queens CB13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Queens CB13, the road takes its due. Twenty-five people dead. Twenty-nine seriously hurt. Four thousand more injured since 2022. The numbers are blunt. The pain is not.

      Just days ago, July 5th, a BMW hit a divider on the Belt Parkway. It vaulted into oncoming traffic. Two young lives ended. The car burned. Police said, “No one in the doomed BMW was wearing a seat belt” (NY Daily News). Three others survived. The road was closed for hours. The scars will last longer.

      On June 26th, a man riding a motorized scooter on Nashville Boulevard was killed. The crash report lists “Driver Inattention/Distraction.” He was ejected. He died there. Another number. Another family left behind (NYC Open Data).

      The Streets Are Not Safe

      Most of the dead are not drivers. They are passengers, walkers, riders. The old and the young. Four children under 18 have died here since 2022. Four more over 75. The city calls them accidents. But the pattern is clear. Unsafe speeds. Lane changes. Distraction. The same mistakes, over and over.

      A bus crash in Queens Village left four hurt. Emily McKinnon was on board. “I was holding on for my life back there,” she said. “The bus driver didn’t do anything wrong… then the crane just hits us” (ABC7).

      Leaders Talk. Streets Wait.

      Council Member Selvena Brooks-Powers has called out the city’s failures. “DOT gives us their word every hearing and we are not getting results” (Streetsblog NYC). She has voted to legalize jaywalking, to collect micromobility data, to lower speed limits on Open Streets. She co-sponsored bills for better lighting, more speed humps, and safer crossings. But the deaths keep coming. Promises do not stop cars.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. The city can lower speed limits. It can redesign deadly roads. It can end the reign of repeat speeders. But it will not act unless you force it.

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking. Do not wait for the next siren. The next name. The next number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 33 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB13?
      It includes the Glen Oaks-Floral Park-New Hyde Park, Bellerose, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, Laurelton, Springfield Gardens (South)-Brookville, Rosedale, and Montefiore Cemetery neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 31, Assembly Districts AD 26, AD 29, AD 31, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB13?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians here were caused by cars and trucks (383 incidents, 4 deaths), followed by trucks and buses (27 incidents, 2 deaths), and bikes (2 minor injuries, 0 deaths).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not just accidents. The same causes—unsafe speed, distraction, unsafe turns—repeat again and again. Policies like lower speed limits, safer street design, and better enforcement can prevent them.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous roads, install more speed cameras, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking. They can hold city agencies accountable for delays and demand results.
      ▸ How many people have been killed or seriously injured in Queens CB13 since 2022?
      Since 2022, 25 people have died and 29 have been seriously injured in traffic crashes in Queens CB13.

      ▸

      Resolution 0735-2025 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure.       Int 1160-2025
      Brooks-Powers votes yes to require quick pavement markings, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      City Council passed a law forcing DOT to repaint pavement markings within five days after resurfacing. Streets stripped bare by fresh asphalt must get lines back fast. Delays require public notice. The law aims to keep crosswalks and lanes visible, protecting walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It was introduced January 8, 2025, passed March 15, and became Local Law 28 of 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law orders DOT to install pavement or temporary markings within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must post notices explaining the delay and set a new timeline. DOT must also report yearly on compliance. The law responds to a simple fact: missing markings mean danger for people on foot and bike. With this law, the city moves to close that deadly gap.

      Int 0552-2024
      Brooks-Powers votes yes, boosting street safety by speeding up resurfacing.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to finish street resurfacing within 15 business days. Delays trigger public notice. The bill aims to end long stretches of torn-up streets. Broken pavement endangers walkers, cyclists, and wheelchair users. The law takes effect in 2025.

      Int 0552-2024, now Local Law 21 of 2025, was enacted by the City Council on March 15, 2025. The bill moved through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, with key votes on February 13, 2025. The law, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring street resurfacing coordination,' requires the Department of Transportation to coordinate with agencies and utilities and finish resurfacing within 15 business days after milling. If DOT cannot meet the deadline, it must post a public notice and alert the community board. Council Member Diana I. Ayala sponsored the bill, joined by dozens of co-sponsors. The measure responds to the hazard of prolonged, uneven streets, which put pedestrians, cyclists, and people with mobility devices at risk. The law’s timeline and notice rules take effect in 2025 and 2027, respectively.
