      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB13?

      Queens Bleeds While Leaders Stall

      Written July 12, 2025

      Queens CB13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Steel

      Twelve dead. Seven seriously hurt. That is the cost in Queens CB13 in the last year alone. The names fade. The numbers do not. A 76-year-old woman, killed in the back seat of a sedan on the Cross Island Parkway. A 39-year-old man, thrown from his motorized scooter on Nashville Boulevard. A 78-year-old woman, dead in a van on Conduit Avenue. The list runs on. The road does not forgive.

      Crashes do not slow. In the first half of 2025, the district saw 1,372 crashes—up 44% from last year. Injuries rose by half. The old and the young are not spared. Four people over 75 have died since January. Ninety-three children have been hurt this year alone, according to NYC Open Data.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. In three years, they killed four people on foot and left hundreds more hurt. Trucks and buses killed two. Bikes, none. The numbers are clear. The danger is not shared equally, as shown by NYC Open Data.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Council Member Selvena Brooks-Powers sits atop the Transportation Committee. She has called out the city’s failures: “DOT gives us their word every hearing and we are not getting results.” She has voted to legalize jaywalking, to demand more data, to lower speed limits on Open Streets, and to co-sponsor daylighting and speed hump bills. But the carnage continues. Promises pile up. Streets stay deadly.

      When the NYPD began ticketing cyclists for minor offenses, Brooks-Powers questioned the crackdown: “Why criminal court?” She has pushed for transparency and for more protected bike lanes, but progress is slow. The dead do not wait.

      The Call: Demand Action Now

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every crossing. Demand real protection for people on foot and bike. Do not wait for another name to join the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27, assembly district AD 33 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB13?
      It includes the Glen Oaks-Floral Park-New Hyde Park, Bellerose, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, Laurelton, Springfield Gardens (South)-Brookville, Rosedale, and Montefiore Cemetery neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23, District 27, and District 31, Assembly Districts AD 26, AD 29, AD 31, and AD 33, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 11, and SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB13?
      Cars and Trucks: 6 deaths, 397 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 0 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 2 injuries. Most harm comes from cars, SUVs, trucks, and buses, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. They are the result of policy choices—speed limits, street design, and enforcement. Every death could have been prevented with better action.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, install daylighting at crossings, build protected bike lanes, and demand real enforcement against reckless drivers. They can pass laws that put safety first for people on foot and bike.

      ▸

      Open Streets are shrinking. Volunteers warn more cuts loom unless City Hall steps up. Federal relief is gone. Groups want $48 million over three years. Current grants barely keep programs alive. The city’s inaction puts public space and safety at risk.

      On March 14, 2025, a coalition of 16 volunteer open street groups sent a letter to City Council Transportation Chair Selvena Brooks-Powers. The groups seek $48 million over three years, separate from the city’s $30 million contract with the Horticultural Society. Current grants max out at $20,000 per year, forcing cutbacks. Speaker Adrienne Adams and Mayor Adams are starting budget talks. Council spokesperson Julia Agos called Open Streets 'critical components of our city's public realm.' The Department of Transportation said suggestions will be reviewed. Volunteer groups handle most operations, but stalled redesigns and lack of resources threaten the program’s survival.
