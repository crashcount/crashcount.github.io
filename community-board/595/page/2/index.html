      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Staten Island CB95?

      Staten Island Streets: Blood on Their Hands, Speed in Their Veins

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised August 7, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Staten Island CB95: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      One death. Fifty-seven injuries. One serious injury. That is the toll in Staten Island CB95 since 2022. These are not just numbers—they are people. A man crossing Lincoln Avenue, struck by an SUV, died at the intersection. The record says only this: “Head trauma. Apparent death.” NYC Open Data.

      In the last year, the pace has not slowed. Thirteen injuries in 23 crashes. No deaths this year, but luck will not hold. The old and the young are not spared. Six injuries among those over 65. No children hurt. The street does not care.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm. One pedestrian killed by an SUV. Five more injured by sedans. One cyclist injured by a car. A single bike crash hurt a pedestrian. Trucks and buses did not kill, but their size is always waiting.

      Leaders Talk, Streets Bleed

      Local leaders have not done enough. Senator Andrew Lanza voted against safer school speed zones for children—again and again, as documented by Streetsblog NYC. He called speed cameras a “cash register” in another Streetsblog NYC report. Assembly Member Mike Tannousis missed the vote on school speed zones. The silence is loud.

      The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has not used it. The law is on the books. The bodies are in the street.

      What Next? The Choice Is Yours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day the city waits, another family waits for the call. Call your council member. Call your senator. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras that work. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name on the list.

      E-Scooter Kills Pedestrian On Staten Island
      ▸

      A man crossed Targee Street. An e-scooter hit him hard. He died at the hospital. The driver flew from the scooter. Police are still investigating. No arrests. The street stayed quiet after the crash.

      amNY reported on May 19, 2025, that Jose Luis Gomez-Guallazaca, 58, died after an e-scooter struck him while he crossed Targee Street on Staten Island. The article states, "upon impact, the driver was violently ejected off the scooter while Gomez-Guallazaca sustained severe trauma." Police have not confirmed the e-scooter's speed or direction on the one-way street. Both the pedestrian and the 25-year-old scooter driver were taken to Richmond University Medical Center; Gomez-Guallazaca was pronounced dead. The NYPD Highway District's Collision Investigation Squad is investigating. No arrests have been made. The crash highlights the risks faced by pedestrians on residential streets and the need for clear rules and enforcement for e-scooter use.

      Int 1105-2024

      ▸

      Int 1160-2025
      Carr votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.
