      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB5?

      Pennsylvania and Blake: a walk signal, then silence

      Written August 29, 2025 (Revised August 29, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 29, 2025

      She stepped into the crosswalk at Pennsylvania and Blake with the signal. An SUV turned left. She died there on a winter morning. NYC Open Data says the driver failed to yield.

      They were one of 13 people killed on Brooklyn CB5 streets since Jan 1, 2022, through Aug 29, 2025 — including 7 pedestrians and 1 cyclist. Another 5,188 people were hurt. The city’s crash database shows it all. NYC Open Data.

      Police said of another death in the district: “A driver struck and killed a 47-year-old pedestrian… then left the scene.” NY Daily News.

      Where the street bites back

      Pennsylvania Avenue shows up again and again. Two deaths and 281 injuries are tied to that corridor in this period. NYC Open Data. Linden Boulevard is worse for injuries, with 296 people hurt. NYC Open Data.

      Failure to yield is in the tape. So is speed. In one fatal case, police logged failure to yield to a pedestrian with the walk at Pennsylvania and Blake. NYC Open Data. In another, a Genesis sedan hit a 21-year-old near Gateway Drive after midnight; investigators cited distraction and unsafe speed. NYC Open Data.

      Danger peaks when light fades. Deaths cluster in the evening: 5 PM through 9 PM claims the most lives in this dataset, with the single highest hour at 6 PM. NYC Open Data.

      Names we elect, choices they made

      Council Member Chris Banks co-sponsored bills to discount senior bike share and require safety notices on shared devices. NYC Council – Legistar. He also co-sponsored a crackdown on unlicensed commuter vans. NYC Council – Legistar.

      Assembly Member Nikki Lucas voted yes to extend school speed zones. Open States. State Senator Roxanne Persaud voted yes to the same, and twice voted yes in committee on a bill to require speed limiters for repeat speeders. Open States.

      The toll of the last year

      So far this year, crashes in this community board are up compared with last year to date: 1,694 this year vs. 1,480 last year. Injuries rose to 1,126 from 884. Deaths went from 1 to 5. NYC Open Data.

      Pedestrians take the hardest hits. Since 2022, 789 people on foot were injured here. Seven were killed. NYC Open Data. SUVs and sedans dominate those injuries. NYC Open Data.

      What will stop the next siren?

      Start where the harm is. Daylight the corners on Pennsylvania and Linden. Add hardened left turns and leading pedestrian intervals at Blake and other signals. Target evening hours with failure‑to‑yield enforcement on turning movements.

      Then scale the fixes. The Senate’s speed‑limiter bill advanced with local support; the Assembly can pass its companion and send it to the governor. Open States. City Hall can lower speeds and harden turns across the map.

      The list of the dead is already long. The work is simple. Do it.

      Take one step now. Ask leaders to act: /take_action/.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What area does this report cover?
      Brooklyn Community Board 5. It includes Cypress Hills, East New York (North), East New York–New Lots, Spring Creek–Starrett City, East New York–City Line, and Highland Park–Cypress Hills Cemeteries (South).
      ▸ How many people were killed or hurt on these streets since 2022?
      From Jan 1, 2022 to Aug 29, 2025, 13 people were killed and 5,188 were injured in crashes in Brooklyn CB5. Of the dead, 7 were pedestrians and 1 was a cyclist, according to NYC’s crash data.
      ▸ When are crashes most deadly here?
      Evenings. In this dataset the highest death count lands around 6 PM, with sustained risk in the 5–9 PM band, per the hourly distribution.
      ▸ What policies have local officials supported?
      Council Member Chris Banks co‑sponsored a senior bike share discount and a micromobility safety‑notice bill; he also co‑sponsored a crackdown on unlicensed commuter vans (all per NYC Council Legistar). Assembly Member Nikki Lucas and State Senator Roxanne Persaud voted to extend school speed zones. Persaud also voted yes in committee twice on the Senate speed‑limiter bill S 4045.
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4). Filters: date 2022-01-01 to 2025-08-29; geography limited to Brooklyn Community Board 5; modes and roles per dataset fields. We counted fatalities and injuries by person role and summarized top locations and hours from the same filters. Data were accessed Aug 29, 2025. You can reproduce our filtered queries starting here.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
