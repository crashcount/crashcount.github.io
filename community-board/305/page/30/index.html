      Brooklyn Bleeds While Leaders Stall: Streets Demand Justice, Not Excuses

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Brooklyn CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Brooklyn CB5, the numbers do not lie. Ten people are dead. Forty-eight more suffered serious injuries. Since 2022, there have been over 8,000 crashes. Each number is a life changed or ended. Each crash leaves a mark on the street and on the families left behind.

      Just this spring, a 28-year-old man died on Seaview Avenue. The cause: driver inattention and unsafe speed. He bled out in the street, another body for the tally. In January, a 57-year-old woman was killed crossing Pennsylvania Avenue. She had the signal. The driver, in an SUV, did not yield. She died at the intersection, her right of way ignored. The city records the cause as failure to yield and distraction (NYC Open Data).

      The Most Vulnerable Pay the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Of the dead, most were on foot. SUVs and sedans are the main weapons. In the last twelve months, injuries have jumped. Deaths have tripled compared to last year. Children, the elderly, the ones who walk because they must—these are the faces behind the numbers.

      Leadership: Votes and Silence

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Members Chris Banks and Sandy Nurse co-sponsored bills for discounted bike share for seniors and students. They voted to remove abandoned vehicles. But the core crisis remains: speed and reckless driving. State Senator Roxanne Persaud voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit. But the streets are still deadly.

      A mother lost her only daughter. “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter,” said Ruiz’s sister-in-law. The pain does not fade. The tire marks stay in the lot for days. The city moves on.

      What Now? Demand More

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Call your senator. Tell them: lower the speed limit, redesign the streets, stop repeat offenders. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB5 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 42, assembly district AD 60 and state senate district SD 19.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB5?
      It includes the Cypress Hills, East New York (North), East New York-New Lots, Spring Creek-Starrett City, East New York-City Line, and Highland Park-Cypress Hills Cemeteries (South) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 37 and District 42, Assembly Districts AD 54 and AD 60, and State Senate Districts SD 18 and SD 19.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB5?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB5 were caused by SUVs and Sedans (6 deaths, over 640 injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (no deaths, 53 injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 18 injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 10 injuries).
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Most deaths and injuries in Brooklyn CB5 came from driver inattention, unsafe speed, and failure to yield. These are choices, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets for safety, pass and enforce laws like the Stop Super Speeders Act, and invest in infrastructure that protects people walking and biking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
