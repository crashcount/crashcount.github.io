      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB5?

      1,602 Crashes, 5 Dead — Why Are Crosswalks Still Parking Spots?

      Written August 15, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Copy-edited body, converted raw references to descriptive inline links, and cleaned/reordered citations to match first appearance.)

      Brooklyn CB5: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 15, 2025

      Five dead. Corners still blind with parked cars. A woman had the light; a turning SUV took her. Call Council: pass the daylighting bill. Call Albany: pass S4045 speed limiters. Demand fixes at Blake and Pennsylvania before another body.

      Just Weeks Ago

      A woman crossed with the signal at Pennsylvania and Blake. A left-turning SUV hit her. She died. The record says failure to yield and distraction. It happened January 24. The city moved on. The street did not.

      Early July, near Gateway Drive, a 21-year-old pedestrian was struck and killed. Unsafe speed. Driver inattention. Two lines in a log. A family undone.

      Late July, at Jamaica and Richmond, a moped met a turning sedan. One rider never got up.

      On the subway, the toll continued. Just last Sunday at Sutter Ave in East New York, “Around 6 p.m., a man fell onto the southbound tracks … and was struck by a train,” police said. “EMS pronounced the victim dead at the scene,” an FDNY spokesman said.

      The Pattern

      This year in CB5: 1,602 crashes, 1,062 injuries, 12 serious injuries, 5 deaths — all up from last year to date. Crashes rose 14%, injuries 29%, deaths 400%. These are people. They are also proof; city data show.

      Cars and trucks do most of the harm to people on foot. Since 2022, they account for 759 pedestrian impacts, including 7 deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds: 18. Bikes: 10.

      Gothamist reported another local crash: “Police said both vehicles were heading westbound on Pitkin Avenue” when a driver hit and killed a moped rider; charges were pending.

      What Leaders Did — And Didn’t

      Council Member Sandy Nurse co-sponsored a bill to daylight 1,000 intersections a year — ban parking near crosswalks so people can see and be seen. It was laid over in committee.

      On zoning and parking mandates, her office said the compromise “wasn’t something that was pushed for specifically by Council Member Nurse.”

      State Sen. Roxanne Persaud voted yes in committee to require speed-limiters for repeat speeders on S 4045. That targets the worst drivers. It does not fix the street at Blake and Penn.

      Do Something Now

      Call your council members. Tell them to pass daylighting. Call your state reps. Tell them to pass S 4045. Demand redesigns where people keep dying. Do not wait for the next body to make the case.

      Banks co-sponsors discounted senior bike share bill, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council pushes cheaper bike share for seniors. More elders could ride. Streets may see more slow, unprotected cyclists. Danger from cars remains. Bill sits in committee. No safety fixes for traffic threats.

      Int 1288-2025, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced May 28, 2025. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to a discounted bike share rate for seniors,' requires the Department of Transportation to set lower bike share rates for those 65 and older. Council Member Farah N. Louis leads, joined by Marte, Powers, Lee, Nurse, Hanif, Brewer, and Banks. The measure aims to boost senior cycling but does not address street safety or car violence.       ▸

      Senate backs S 8117. Oneida County gets school speed zone cameras. Law sunsets in 2030. Lawmakers act. Streets near schools may slow. Children walk safer. Drivers face new eyes.

      Senate bill S 8117, titled 'Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the county of Oneida,' passed committee votes on May 27 and June 10, 2025. The bill, sponsored by Senator Joseph A. Griffo (District 53), sets up speed cameras near schools in Oneida County. The program ends December 31, 2030. The bill now moves forward in the legislative process.

      Pregnant Woman Killed In Brooklyn Hit-Run
      ▸

      A driver struck Tiffany Cifuni after a fender-bender in Bedford-Stuyvesant. She was pregnant. The driver dragged her down a one-way street, crashed again, then fled. Cifuni’s family mourns. The street holds the mark of violence and loss.

      NY Daily News reported on May 26, 2025, that Tiffany Cifuni, 32, was killed after a Chevy Trax rear-ended her on Van Buren St. in Brooklyn. Cifuni exited her vehicle to confront the driver, who then ran her over and dragged her down the street. The driver fled, crashing into two more vehicles before abandoning the car. The article quotes Cifuni’s husband: “I lost my whole family tonight.” Surveillance footage captured the confrontation and the fatal impact. The incident highlights the lethal risk of driver aggression and the dangers of hit-and-run crashes. The driver’s decision to flee and drive the wrong way down a one-way street escalated the harm, underscoring systemic failures in street safety and enforcement.

      Driver Runs Down Woman After Crash
      ▸

      A woman stepped from her car after a crash in Bed-Stuy. The other driver hit her, dragged her, then sped off the wrong way. He crashed again, abandoned the SUV, and fled. She died at the hospital. The street stayed silent.

      NY Daily News reported on May 25, 2025, that a 32-year-old woman was killed after a minor collision near Van Buren St. and Marcus Garvey Blvd. in Brooklyn. After the initial crash, the Chevy Trax driver struck the woman as she exited her vehicle, then drove against traffic on Lafayette Ave., hitting two parked cars before fleeing on foot. Witness Shane Bridges described, "They dragged her like to the middle of the street, and then they turned wrong up Lafayette and she was just left there." The SUV had temporary paper plates. The incident highlights the dangers posed by reckless driving and hit-and-run behavior, especially when drivers ignore traffic direction and abandon crash scenes.
