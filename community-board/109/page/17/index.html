      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB9?

      Manhattan Bleeds. City Waits. How Many More Must Die?

      Written July 28, 2025 (Revised July 29, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, deduped and reordered citations, tightened prose.)

      Manhattan CB9: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 28, 2025

      The Slow Grind of Loss

      In Manhattan CB9, the violence comes steady and slow. Since 2022, eight people have died in crashes. Fourteen more were left with serious injuries. The numbers do not flinch: 1,240 injured, 2,569 crashes. Each figure is a body broken or a life ended. Most are not behind the wheel.

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and SUVs caused the most harm—two deaths, 169 injuries. Trucks and buses added more. Bikes, mopeds, and motorcycles left their own mark. The street does not care who you are. It only takes.

      Recent Blood on the Asphalt

      Just this May, a 73-year-old man was killed crossing at West 135th. The SUV kept going. Last November, a cyclist died at St. Nicholas and 155th. The cause: driver inattention and speed. The stories repeat. The faces change. The outcome does not.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Some leaders have moved. State Senator Cordell Cleare voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed limiters. Assembly Member Jordan Wright and others backed the renewal of 24-hour school speed cameras. These are steps. They are not enough.

      Council Member Shaun Abreu called out the city’s failures on the Hudson River Greenway detour, saying the city must create a “sustainable, workable detour” for cyclists who are forced onto dangerous streets. Still, the detour stands. The injuries keep coming.

      The Only Way Out Is Through

      Every day of delay is another day of loss. “We lost a true hero this morning with the tragic passing of Firefighter Matthew Goicochea,” said Deputy Mayor Kaz Daughtry. The city mourns. The street does not.

      Call your council member. Call your state senator. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed limiters for repeat offenders. Demand streets where the most vulnerable are not left to die.

      If you wait, the street will not.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB9 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 7, assembly district AD 70 and state senate district SD 30.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB9?
      It includes the Morningside Heights, Manhattanville-West Harlem, and Hamilton Heights-Sugar Hill neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 7 and District 9, Assembly Districts AD 69, AD 70, and AD 71, and State Senate District SD 30.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB9?
      Most injuries and deaths were caused by Cars and SUVs (2 deaths, 169 injuries), followed by Bikes (no deaths, 14 injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 8 injuries), and Trucks and Buses (no deaths, 11 injuries).
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Crashes are preventable. Speed, inattention, and unsafe streets make them inevitable unless leaders act.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, pass and enforce speed limiter laws for repeat offenders, redesign streets for safety, and fund real protections for pedestrians and cyclists.

      Abreu Praises West Harlem Pilot Boosting Street Cleanliness
      ▸

      Mayor Adams backs taking 150,000 parking spots for garbage containers. He calls it a small price for cleaner streets. The plan shifts trash from sidewalks to bins, clearing paths for pedestrians. Council member Abreu supports the West Harlem pilot. Resistance remains.

      "What’s happening in West Harlem – it’s working." -- Shaun Abreu

      On October 11, 2023, Mayor Eric Adams announced support for the Sanitation Department's plan to repurpose 150,000 parking spaces for containerized garbage collection. The policy, unveiled by DSNY, aims to move trash from sidewalks into closed bins, targeting cleaner streets and less sidewalk clutter. The mayor said, 'Everyday New Yorkers are tired of the rodents, they’re tired of the trash, and this is a small price to pay on ensuring that you can have cleaner streets.' Council member Shaun Abreu, representing West Harlem, voiced strong support, citing visible improvements from the local pilot. The plan requires smaller buildings to use wheeled bins on sidewalks, while larger buildings would get shared curbside containers. The city faces pushback from some residents, but Adams insists street cleanliness is a top concern. No formal safety analysis was provided, but the move could clear sidewalks for pedestrians and reduce hazards from trash piles.

      Shaun Abreu Supports Safety Boosting Containerized Garbage Collection Pilot
      ▸

      Sanitation trucks now lift curbside bins in West Harlem. No more sidewalk trash bags. Ten blocks and fourteen schools lose parking, gain cleaner streets. Rats lose ground. Council Member Abreu and DSNY back the change. Some residents cheer. Others worry about access.

      On September 15, 2023, New York City launched a containerized garbage collection pilot in West Harlem. The Department of Sanitation (DSNY) rolled out mechanical lifts on trucks to empty curbside containers, covering ten residential blocks and fourteen schools. The pilot aims to 'eliminate sidewalk trash bags, combat rats, and reclaim public roadway space from private car storage.' Council Member Shaun Abreu and DSNY officials publicly supported the program, with Abreu stating, 'Rats are a huge problem uptown, and we’ll never get the problem under control as long as we’re just throwing bags on the sidewalk.' Jonathan Viguers, DSNY assistant commissioner, acknowledged the removal of parking spaces as necessary. The initiative is part of Mayor Adams's anti-rat campaign. While some residents praise cleaner sidewalks, others raise concerns about accessibility and overflow. The city may expand the program, but faces cost and political hurdles.
