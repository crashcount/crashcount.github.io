      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Manhattan CB9?

      Blood on the Crosswalk: How Many Deaths Until They Act?

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Manhattan CB9: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Shattered, Streets Unchanged

      Eight dead. Fourteen left with serious injuries. In the last three and a half years, Manhattan CB9 has seen 2,470 crashes. The numbers are blunt. Behind them are bodies—pedestrians, cyclists, children. A 73-year-old man, crossing at 135th Street, struck by an SUV and left semiconscious, bleeding from the head. A cyclist, ejected and killed by an SUV at St. Nicholas and 155th. These are not accidents. They are the price of inaction.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      The carnage does not slow. In the past twelve months alone, 342 people have been injured in crashes here. One person died. Serious injuries are up 50% from the year before. The wounds are not abstract. They are broken bones, blood on the street, a child in the back seat of a car struck by a runaway food cart loaded with propane tanks. “Firefighters forced entry into the van, removing 76 20-pound propane cylinders and 15 five-gallon fuel containers,” reported the NY Daily News. The woman and child inside survived. This time.

      Leadership: Progress and Gaps

      Local leaders have moved, but not fast enough. Senator Cordell Cleare voted yes to curb repeat speeders with speed-limiting tech, a step toward fewer deaths. Council Member Shaun Abreu has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks and called out unsafe detours that force cyclists into danger. But the streets remain deadly. The policies come slow. The bodies pile up faster.

      The Call: No More Waiting

      Every crash is preventable. Every delay is a choice. The city has the power to lower speed limits, redesign streets, and end the reign of repeat offenders. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit, more protected crossings, and real enforcement. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB9 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 7, assembly district AD 70 and state senate district SD 30.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB9?
      It includes the Morningside Heights, Manhattanville-West Harlem, and Hamilton Heights-Sugar Hill neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 7 and District 9, Assembly Districts AD 69, AD 70, and AD 71, and State Senate District SD 30.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB9?
      Cars and SUVs: 2 deaths, 98 minor injuries, 62 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries (total 164 incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 7 minor injuries, 1 moderate injury (total 8 incidents). Bikes: 0 deaths, 7 minor injuries, 5 moderate injuries (total 12 incidents).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and enforcement against repeat offenders can save lives. Every crash is a policy choice.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, expand protected bike lanes and crossings, and pass laws to stop repeat dangerous drivers. They can act now, not later.

      Helicopter Breaks Apart Over Hudson River
      ▸

      Six died when a sightseeing helicopter shattered midair over the Hudson. The fuselage, rotor, and tail tore loose. Loud bangs echoed. The craft plunged. No black box. No warning. Only fragments and silence left behind.

      NY Daily News reported on May 7, 2025, that federal officials released images showing a sightseeing helicopter breaking apart in midair before crashing into the Hudson River, killing six. The National Transportation Safety Board’s preliminary report details how the Bell 206L-4 split into three pieces: 'the fuselage, the main rotor system, and the tail boom.' Witnesses heard 'several loud 'bangs'' before the breakup. The helicopter had flown eight tours that day, all with the same pilot, who had less than 50 hours in this model. The aircraft had a prior maintenance issue with its transmission assembly and lacked flight data recorders. The NTSB noted the pilot wore video-capable sunglasses, but they remain missing. The crash highlights gaps in oversight and the risks of repeated tour flights without robust recording or inspection requirements.

      Int 0193-2024
      Abreu votes yes on taxi dooring warning decals, no major safety change.
      ▸

      Council passes law. Taxis and for-hire cars must post bold warnings on rear doors. Riders face the message: look for cyclists before swinging the door. A move to cut dooring. City acts. Cyclists stay in the crosshairs.

      Int 0193-2024, now law, cleared the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and passed City Council on May 1, 2025. The bill, titled 'A Local Law...requiring taxis and for-hire vehicles to display a decal warning passengers to look for cyclists when opening the door,' demands clear warning signs on all rear passenger doors. Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Gutiérrez, Hudson, Rivera, Won, and others. The Taxi and Limousine Commission must provide decals at no cost. The law aims to fight dooring, a threat to cyclists citywide. Enacted May 31, 2025, it takes effect in 120 days.
