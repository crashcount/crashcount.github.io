      Blood on Broadway: NYPD Crash Exposes City’s Deadly Delay

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned citations, and improved citation formatting in FAQ.)

      Manhattan CB9: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      A police car swerves on Broadway. Two people eating outside are hit. Sirens, then silence. Both diners and the officers are rushed to St. Luke’s. All survive, but the street is marked. The cab driver gets a summons for failure to yield. The investigation drags on. Police told the press that “the NYPD squad car struck two pedestrians Monday afternoon while trying to avoid a collision with a Manhattan taxi.”

      In the last twelve months, 310 people have been injured in crashes in Manhattan CB9. Six were seriously hurt. One never made it home. The numbers do not rest. They do not heal. They only climb.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists bleed first. In three years, seven people have died on these streets. Over a thousand have been hurt. SUVs, sedans, taxis, trucks—steel against flesh. The city counts the bodies. The city moves on.

      A dirt biker is struck by an NYPD vehicle on the University Heights Bridge. He dies. His family gets a bill from the city for the police car’s damage. As the paper reports, “Williams’ family received a bill from the city for damages to the police vehicle two months after the crash.” The NYPD says nothing. The case sits in silence.

      Promises, Delays, and the Cost of Waiting

      Local leaders talk of Vision Zero. They tout new speed limits, more cameras, safer crossings. But the pace is slow. Cameras are threatened by politics in Albany. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not used it. Each day of delay is another roll of the dice for the next child, the next elder, the next cyclist.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city use its power—lower the speed limit, protect the cameras, redesign the streets. Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB9 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 7, assembly district AD 70 and state senate district SD 30.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB9?
      It includes the Morningside Heights, Manhattanville-West Harlem, and Hamilton Heights-Sugar Hill neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 7 and District 9, Assembly Districts AD 69, AD 70, and AD 71, and State Senate District SD 30.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB9?
      Cars and Trucks: 1 death, 59 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 1 moderate injury, 0 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 5 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries. See NYC Open Data for details.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. They follow patterns—speed, street design, enforcement. Policy can prevent them. Delay costs lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign dangerous streets, and push for stronger enforcement. They can act now, or answer for the next tragedy.
