      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Queens Streets: Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised July 1, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB1

      The streets do not forgive. In Queens CB1, the numbers pile up. Thirteen people killed. Twenty seriously hurt. Over 2,700 injured. Each number is a name, a family, a life split open. The dead include children, cyclists, elders. The living carry scars.

      Just last fall, a cyclist named Amanda Servedio was struck and killed in Astoria. She was riding home from a cycling event. A pickup, fleeing police, hit her at the intersection of 37th Street and 34th Avenue. A witness said, “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.” (NY Daily News).

      A father mourned. “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood,” he said.

      The Pattern: No End in Sight

      The violence does not slow. In the last year alone, three more people died. Children, teens, adults—all struck down. Hundreds more were hurt. The killers are cars, SUVs, trucks, mopeds. The wounds are broken bones, crushed skulls, lives erased at the curb.

      The city counts the bodies. The city counts the broken. But the city does not stop the blood.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Member Tiffany Cabán voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable and did nothing to stop the real danger—drivers who do not yield. Cabán also co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and speed up protected bike lanes. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez and others backed the 31st Street protected bike lane plan, a proven way to save lives.

      But the pace is slow. The danger is not. Every day without change is another day someone does not come home.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call your state senator. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted intersections, and a 20 mph speed limit. Demand that leaders act before another family gets the call. The dead do not get a second chance. The living must not wait.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1 came from Cars and Trucks (2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 393 total incidents), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 1 serious injury, 27 incidents), and Bikes (no deaths, 1 serious injury, 15 incidents).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Protected bike lanes, daylighted intersections, and lower speed limits have all been shown to prevent deaths and injuries. The violence on these streets can be stopped.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for protected bike lanes, ban parking near crosswalks, lower speed limits, and fund street redesigns. They can also push for stronger penalties for reckless drivers and demand faster action from city agencies.

      A 3180
      Gonzalez-Rojas co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 3180 demands every state- or federally-funded road project weigh complete street design. It pushes agencies to publish clear guidance. The bill aims to force planners to see people, not just cars. Streets must serve all. No more blind spots.

      Assembly Bill A 3180 was introduced on February 3, 2023, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in committee, awaiting further action. Its matter title reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding.' Didi Barrett leads as primary sponsor, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Amy Paulin, Carrie Woerner, and many others as co-sponsors. The bill compels agencies to consider complete street design—meaning streets built for pedestrians, cyclists, and transit users, not just drivers. It also requires the department to publish guidance and reference materials for public use. This measure, if enforced, could reshape how New York builds and rebuilds its roads, putting vulnerable road users at the center of every project.

      Res 0482-2023
      Cabán co-sponsors bill boosting subway air quality, improving rider safety.
      ▸

      Council members demand the MTA reveal air pollution at every subway stop. Riders breathe metal and dust, trapped underground. The bill calls for data and action. The session ended before passage. Riders and workers wait. Danger lingers in the tunnels.

      Resolution 0482-2023 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on February 2, 2023. The bill urges the State Legislature and Governor to require the MTA to 'publish air pollution data for each subway station and mitigate the highest concentrations of air pollutants.' Council Member Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Hudson, Cabán, Hanif, Yeger, Brewer, Richardson Jordan, Avilés, Brooks-Powers, and Riley. The measure highlights research showing subway air holds two to seven times more hazardous particles than city streets. These pollutants, from grinding wheels and diesel soot, threaten riders and workers—especially lower-income, Black, Hispanic, and immigrant New Yorkers. The bill was filed at session’s end, leaving the city’s most vulnerable still exposed.

      Int 0905-2023
      Cabán co-sponsors bill to speed up street resurfacing, boosting safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force street resurfacing jobs to finish in two weeks. Delays must be explained to neighbors. Streets torn up for months endanger walkers and riders. The bill died at session’s end. The danger remains. The clock keeps ticking.

      Int 0905-2023 was introduced on February 2, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to amend city law to require the Department of Transportation to finish all street resurfacing work within two weeks. If more time was needed, the department would have to notify the community, stating the reason and new timeline. The matter summary reads: 'ensure that all work associated with the resurfacing of any street is finalized within 2 weeks from the start of work.' Council Member Diana I. Ayala sponsored the bill, joined by thirty other members. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. No safety analyst reviewed the impact, but torn-up streets are a known threat to pedestrians and cyclists. The bill is dead. The hazard lives on.

      Mamdani Opposes Fare Hikes Leaves Riders Behind
      ▸

      Gov. Hochul’s budget plugs the MTA’s deficit with casino money and higher payroll taxes. Riders face a 5.5% fare hike. Service stays flat. City pays more. No new long-term funding. Advocates warn: vulnerable New Yorkers left behind.

      On February 2, 2023, Gov. Hochul unveiled her Fiscal Year 2024 budget proposal to address the MTA’s looming fiscal crisis. The plan, described as aiming to 'expand our public transit access, affordability, safety,' includes a one-time $300 million state payment, a payroll mobility tax hike, and casino revenue estimated at $462–$826 million annually. The city would pay $500 million more each year. MTA Chair Janno Lieber praised the plan for preserving service without cuts. Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani criticized it for raising fares and failing low-income riders. Mayor Adams and Comptroller Lander objected to the city’s increased burden, calling for congestion pricing and opposing fare hikes. The budget does not expand service or secure long-term funding, leaving vulnerable riders exposed to higher costs and stagnant service.
