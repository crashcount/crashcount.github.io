      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Streets Bleed While Leaders Stall

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A cyclist, age 36, killed at 34th Avenue and 37th Street. A 15-year-old moped rider, dead on 43rd Street. A 17-year-old, gone after a crash on Astoria Boulevard. In the last twelve months, two people have died and five have been seriously injured on the streets of Queens CB1. In that same year, 786 people were hurt in 1,363 crashes (NYC Open Data).

      The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do. Cars and SUVs led the body count—two deaths, four serious injuries. Trucks and buses, motorcycles, mopeds, bikes: all have left their mark. The most vulnerable—children, cyclists, pedestrians—pay the highest price.

      “We Didn’t See the Crane Coming”

      The stories pile up. A crane slams into a bus in Queens Village. Emily McKinnon, a passenger, recalled, “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us.” Four people hurt. The bus driver did nothing wrong. The street did not forgive.

      A BMW vaults the divider on the Belt Parkway. Passengers thrown from the car. Fire. Death. “They went airborne and into the barrier,” said a survivor. No seatbelts. No second chances.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán has co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and back the SAFE Streets Act. Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani has pushed for lower speed limits and more bus lanes. Senator Kristen Gonzalez has backed speed-limiting tech for repeat offenders.

      But the deaths keep coming. Every delay is another name on a slab.

      Act Now—Before the Next Siren

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand daylight at every crosswalk.

      Do not wait for another child’s name in the news. The city has the power. Make them use it.

      A Toyota RAV4 jumped the curb on 90th Avenue, slammed into a garage, and collapsed the structure. The driver, Mamadou Barry, was trapped. First responders pulled him out, but he died at the hospital. No other injuries reported.

      According to NY Daily News (2025-01-22), Mamadou Barry, 63, was driving his Toyota RAV4 along 90th Ave. in Jamaica, Queens, around 5:20 a.m. when he lost control, hopped a curb at 143rd St., and crashed into a detached garage. The impact caused the garage to collapse onto both his SUV and a parked, unoccupied Prius. Police said Barry was trapped and later died at Jamaica Hospital. The article notes, 'he lost control of the SUV, which went crashing into a detached garage in Queens, police said.' Family members stated Barry had no known medical issues. The cause of the crash remains unclear. No other injuries were reported. The incident highlights the dangers faced by drivers and bystanders in residential areas where structures sit close to the street.

      A 2299
      Gonzalez-Rojas co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Drivers who rack up points or camera tickets face forced speed control. Assembly bill A 2299 targets repeat violators. The law would require intelligent speed assistance devices. The goal: stop reckless drivers before they kill.

      Assembly Bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 17, 2025, it awaits committee review. The bill summary reads: 'Requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Assembly Member Emily Gallagher leads as primary sponsor, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, Tony Simone, and others. No votes have been recorded yet. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving by forcing speed limiters on offenders. This targets a core threat to pedestrians and cyclists: unchecked speed and repeat lawbreaking.

      Gianaris Criticizes Hochul Subway Safety Plan as Symbolic
      ▸

      Governor Hochul pledged more police and brighter lights in the subway. Barriers and new fare gates will come to a few stations. Critics called the plans symbolic, lacking funding and detail. Andrea Stewart-Cousins stood with Hochul as the city waits for real change.

      On January 14, 2025, during the State of the State address, Governor Kathy Hochul announced new subway safety measures. The proposals include NYPD officers on every overnight train, LED lighting in all stations, and limited installation of platform barriers and modern fare gates. The matter summary states: 'Hochul finally acknowledges NYC subway crime in State of the State speech—but proposals slammed as largely symbolic.' Andrea Stewart-Cousins, mentioned in the announcement, stood with Hochul. Critics, including State Senate Deputy Majority Leader Michael Gianaris, called the measures 'symbolic.' The Working Families Party warned that tax cuts without new revenue could threaten transit funding. No direct safety analyst assessment was provided, but advocates and lawmakers questioned whether these steps will protect riders or simply signal action.

      S 1675
      Gianaris co-sponsors bill for online pedestrian safety ratings, no major safety change.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 1675 would force carmakers to face the facts. It creates a pedestrian safety rating system for all vehicles. The ratings go public on the DMV website. Lawmakers want drivers to see the danger. The streets demand it.

      Senate bill S 1675 was introduced on January 13, 2025. It sits in the sponsorship stage. The bill, titled 'Creates a pedestrian safety rating system for motor vehicles,' would require the Department of Motor Vehicles to post these ratings online.       A 1077
      Gonzalez-Rojas co-sponsors bill boosting street safety for all users.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 1077 aims to open streets to all. Sponsors demand safe access for walkers, cyclists, and drivers. The bill calls for complete street design. It stands as a direct answer to traffic violence. The fight for safer roads moves forward.

      Assembly Bill A 1077, sponsored by Jonathan Rivera and over sixty co-sponsors, sits in the sponsorship stage. The bill, introduced January 8, 2025, in the New York State Assembly, is titled: "Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles." Rivera and his colleagues call for streets built for people, not just cars. The bill has not yet reached committee or vote. Its language is clear: every user deserves safe passage. Complete streets mean sidewalks, bike lanes, and crossings that protect the most vulnerable. This is a direct response to the daily toll of crashes. The measure signals strong legislative support for systemic change.

      A 803
      Gonzalez-Rojas co-sponsors bill boosting cyclist safety through automated bike lane enforcement.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 803 lands in Albany. It targets drivers who block bike lanes. Cameras will catch violators. Streets choke with cars. Cyclists swerve, risk death. Lawmakers push for clear lanes. The city’s riders wait for action.

      Assembly Bill 803 was introduced on January 6, 2025, in the New York State Assembly. The bill sits in the sponsorship stage. It aims to 'establish in the city of New York a bicycle lane safety program to enforce certain restrictions on the use of bicycle lanes and/or protected bicycle lanes by means of bicycle lane photo devices.' Primary sponsor Zohran Mamdani leads a coalition of fourteen co-sponsors, including Brian Cunningham, Jessica Gonzalez-Rojas, and Harvey Epstein. The bill has not yet advanced to committee or vote. If passed, it would allow automated cameras to ticket drivers who block bike lanes, a move designed to keep lanes clear for cyclists and prevent deadly crashes. Lawmakers back the measure, but the city’s vulnerable road users still face daily danger until enforcement begins.

      Gonzalez Supports Fair Congestion Pricing for Transit District
      ▸

      Congestion pricing hits Manhattan at midnight. Cars pay to cross south of 60th. Lawmakers split. Some call it a burden. Others hail cleaner air, safer streets, and better transit. The toll stirs anger, hope, and a fight over who pays and who breathes.

      On January 3, 2025, congestion pricing (no bill number cited) will begin in Manhattan after a federal judge allowed the toll to proceed. The policy charges most drivers $9 to enter south of 60th Street during peak hours. The matter, titled 'Congestion pricing set to begin at midnight Sunday as federal judge clears path for Manhattan toll,' has drawn sharp lines. Council Member Robert F. Holden (District 30, Queens) opposes the toll, warning, 'I don't know how my district is going to be impacted.' He fears more cars parking in Queens and unclear pollution risks. Council Member Kristy Marmorato (Bronx) calls it a 'cash grab.' In contrast, State Sen. Kristen Gonzalez and Assembly Member Jessica González-Rojas support the measure, citing cleaner air, safer streets, and transit upgrades. González-Rojas says, 'Congestion pricing isn't about penalizing anyone—it's about modernizing our transportation system and tackling climate change.' The judge ordered further study on environmental impacts, but the toll begins as scheduled.

      S 131
      Gianaris co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 131 demands complete street design in state- and federally-funded road projects. It forces agencies to consider people on foot and bike, not just cars. The bill orders public guidance on safer street layouts. Sponsors push for streets that protect all.

      Senate bill S 131, introduced on January 1, 2025, is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in the Senate, awaiting committee action. Its summary reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding; authorizes the department to compile and make publicly available guidance and reference material related to consideration of complete street design.' Primary sponsor Sean Ryan (District 61) leads, joined by co-sponsors Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, Samra Brouk, Cordell Cleare, Leroy Comrie, Jeremy Cooney, Nathalia Fernandez, Michael Gianaris, Kristen Gonzalez, Robert Jackson, Rachel May, Kevin S. Parker, Christopher Ryan, and Luis R. Sepúlveda. The bill compels agencies to put vulnerable road users first in every new project that takes public money. It also demands transparency, requiring the department to publish guidance on safer street design for all.

      Gonzalez-Rojas co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 324 demands every state- or federally-funded road project weigh complete street design. The bill pushes agencies to publish clear guidance. Streets built for cars alone kill. This bill aims to force change at the blueprint.

      Assembly Bill 324 was introduced on January 1, 2025, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in the New York State Assembly, with Didi Barrett as the primary sponsor and a long list of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Amy Paulin, and others. The bill summary reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding; authorizes the department to compile and make publicly available guidance and reference material related to consideration of complete street design.' The bill’s broad sponsorship signals growing support for safer, more inclusive streets. No safety analyst note was provided.
