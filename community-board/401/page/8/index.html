      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Blood on Their Hands, Paint on the Pavement: Demand Real Safety Now

      Written June 27, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Fixed headline duplication, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, and deduped citations.)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      A cyclist rides home from a community event. She never makes it. At 37th Street and 34th Avenue, a pickup truck fleeing police strikes her. She flies from her bike into a parked car. She dies at Elmhurst Hospital. Her name was Amanda Servedio. She was 36. Her father said they were upset police did not stop the chase: “It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened.”

      In the last twelve months, three people have died and 790 have been injured in crashes in Queens CB1. Five suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Children are not spared. One child died. Fifty-eight more were hurt, according to NYC Open Data.

      Patterns in Metal and Flesh

      The numbers do not lie. SUVs and cars are the main weapons: 2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 392 crashes. Trucks and buses wounded dozens more. Motorcycles and mopeds left bodies broken. Bikes, too, are not blameless, but the scale is not the same.

      Each crash is a story cut short. A man in his fifties, struck by a black SUV, left unresponsive in the road. The driver kept going, reported ABC7.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Some leaders act. Council Member Tiffany Cabán voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed the dead for their own deaths. She co-sponsored bills to speed protected bike lanes and ban parking near crosswalks. She called for the SAFE Streets Act, demanding lower speed limits and more rights for crash victims, as shown in NYC Council records.

      Others stand with her. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez and Assembly Member Jessica González-Rojas backed protected bike lanes on 31st Street, writing that it would “protect pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers while making our streets safer and less congested,” according to Streetsblog NYC.

      But the pace is slow. The blood dries before the paint goes down. Every delay is another name on a slab.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call your state senator. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand that the city stop blaming the dead and start protecting the living.

      Do not wait for another name. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Cars and Trucks: 2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 392 crashes. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 27 crashes. Bikes: 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, 15 crashes, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The pattern is clear and relentless. Crashes follow the same deadly script: speeding, reckless driving, poor street design. These are preventable deaths, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, and safer crossings. They can stop blaming victims and start building streets for people, not cars.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Gonzalez-Rojas co-sponsors bill boosting cyclist safety through automated bike lane enforcement.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 803 lands in Albany. It targets drivers who block bike lanes. Cameras will catch violators. Streets choke with cars. Cyclists swerve, risk death. Lawmakers push for clear lanes. The city’s riders wait for action.

      Assembly Bill 803 was introduced on January 6, 2025, in the New York State Assembly. The bill sits in the sponsorship stage. It aims to 'establish in the city of New York a bicycle lane safety program to enforce certain restrictions on the use of bicycle lanes and/or protected bicycle lanes by means of bicycle lane photo devices.' Primary sponsor Zohran Mamdani leads a coalition of fourteen co-sponsors, including Brian Cunningham, Jessica Gonzalez-Rojas, and Harvey Epstein. The bill has not yet advanced to committee or vote. If passed, it would allow automated cameras to ticket drivers who block bike lanes, a move designed to keep lanes clear for cyclists and prevent deadly crashes. Lawmakers back the measure, but the city’s vulnerable road users still face daily danger until enforcement begins.

      Gonzalez Supports Fair Effective Congestion Pricing Implementation
      ▸

      Congestion pricing hits Manhattan at midnight. Cars pay to cross south of 60th. Lawmakers split. Some call it a burden. Others hail cleaner air, safer streets, and better transit. The toll stirs anger, hope, and a fight over who pays and who breathes.

      On January 3, 2025, congestion pricing (no bill number cited) will begin in Manhattan after a federal judge allowed the toll to proceed. The policy charges most drivers $9 to enter south of 60th Street during peak hours. The matter, titled 'Congestion pricing set to begin at midnight Sunday as federal judge clears path for Manhattan toll,' has drawn sharp lines. Council Member Robert F. Holden (District 30, Queens) opposes the toll, warning, 'I don't know how my district is going to be impacted.' He fears more cars parking in Queens and unclear pollution risks. Council Member Kristy Marmorato (Bronx) calls it a 'cash grab.' In contrast, State Sen. Kristen Gonzalez and Assembly Member Jessica González-Rojas support the measure, citing cleaner air, safer streets, and transit upgrades. González-Rojas says, 'Congestion pricing isn't about penalizing anyone—it's about modernizing our transportation system and tackling climate change.' The judge ordered further study on environmental impacts, but the toll begins as scheduled.

      Gianaris co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.

      Senate bill S 131 demands complete street design in state- and federally-funded road projects. It forces agencies to consider people on foot and bike, not just cars. The bill orders public guidance on safer street layouts. Sponsors push for streets that protect all.

      Int 1154-2024
      Cabán co-sponsors pilot for high-visibility markings, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council moves to test bold pavement markings at crash sites. Five spots per borough. Focus: places where drivers have killed or hurt the most. The city must report back. The law dies when the pilot ends. Streets remember every scar.

      Int 1154-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced December 19, 2024, the bill orders a pilot for high visibility pavement markings. The official title: 'A Local Law in relation to establishing a high visibility pavement marking pilot program and the repeal of this local law upon the expiration thereof.' Council Member Yusef Salaam leads as primary sponsor, joined by Menin, De La Rosa, Brooks-Powers, Cabán, Brannan, and Hanif.
