      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Streets Bleed While Leaders Stall

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A cyclist, age 36, killed at 34th Avenue and 37th Street. A 15-year-old moped rider, dead on 43rd Street. A 17-year-old, gone after a crash on Astoria Boulevard. In the last twelve months, two people have died and five have been seriously injured on the streets of Queens CB1. In that same year, 786 people were hurt in 1,363 crashes (NYC Open Data).

      The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do. Cars and SUVs led the body count—two deaths, four serious injuries. Trucks and buses, motorcycles, mopeds, bikes: all have left their mark. The most vulnerable—children, cyclists, pedestrians—pay the highest price.

      “We Didn’t See the Crane Coming”

      The stories pile up. A crane slams into a bus in Queens Village. Emily McKinnon, a passenger, recalled, “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us.” Four people hurt. The bus driver did nothing wrong. The street did not forgive.

      A BMW vaults the divider on the Belt Parkway. Passengers thrown from the car. Fire. Death. “They went airborne and into the barrier,” said a survivor. No seatbelts. No second chances.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán has co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and back the SAFE Streets Act. Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani has pushed for lower speed limits and more bus lanes. Senator Kristen Gonzalez has backed speed-limiting tech for repeat offenders.

      But the deaths keep coming. Every delay is another name on a slab.

      Act Now—Before the Next Siren

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand daylight at every crosswalk.

      Do not wait for another child’s name in the news. The city has the power. Make them use it.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Queens Community Board 1

      Int 1011-2023
      Cabán votes yes on fare evasion info bill, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing police to hand out flyers on reduced fare programs to anyone ticketed or arrested for subway fare evasion. The flyers list eligibility, discounts, and how to apply. The law aims to inform, not punish. It is now in effect.

      Int 1011-2023, now enacted as Local Law 30 of 2024, moved through the Committee on Public Safety and passed the City Council on January 20, 2024. The bill requires police officers patrolling subway stations to carry and distribute flyers about city-run reduced fare programs to anyone arrested or summoned for fare evasion. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to providing information on the reduced fare programs to persons issued a summons for fare evasion.' Council Member Farah N. Louis sponsored the bill, joined by Brooks-Powers, Brewer, Dinowitz, Barron, Krishnan, and others. The flyers must explain eligibility, discount details, and application steps. The law took effect 120 days after passage. No direct safety impact for vulnerable road users was noted.

      Int 0781-2022
      Cabán votes yes on vehicle stop reporting bill, safety impact neutral.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing NYPD to spell out why they stop cars. Each stop now gets logged with the reason, time, and outcome. No names. Just facts. The city posts these reports online. A move for sunlight. No more secrets.

      Int 0781-2022, now law, came through the Committee on Public Safety. The Council passed it on January 20, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the police department to report on the justification for the stop in vehicle encounter reports," demands the NYPD document every vehicle stop. Reports must include the reason for the stop, the level of offense, and demographic details, but no personal identifiers. Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Lincoln Restler (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, and others led the charge. The law aims to expose patterns in police stops and holds officers to account. Reports go online, open to all. This is a step toward transparency, not just for drivers, but for every New Yorker who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.

      Gonzalez Criticizes Misguided McGuinness Blvd Safety Rollback
      ▸

      Mayor Adams gutted the McGuinness Blvd. safety plan. Two lanes each way remain. Bike lanes go unprotected. Crossing distances stretch. Local leaders say nothing changed for people on foot. Cyclists dodge cars and illegal parking. The danger stays. The fight continues.

      On November 29, 2023, Mayor Eric Adams’s administration scaled back the Department of Transportation’s original McGuinness Boulevard redesign. The DOT’s first plan would have cut traffic lanes and added a parking-protected bike lane. Adams ordered a weaker version after business pushback. The compromise left two car lanes in each direction during the day. Elected officials—U.S. Rep. Nydia Velázquez, Assembly Member Emily Gallagher, State Sen. Kristen Gonzalez, Borough President Antonio Reynoso, and Council Member Lincoln Restler—sent a letter urging DOT to restore the full safety plan, writing, “Nothing has been done to improve conditions for pedestrians.” Advocates say the new design increases crossing distances and leaves cyclists and pedestrians exposed. DOT says work will continue into 2024. The boulevard remains dangerous for vulnerable road users.

      Gonzalez Supports Safety Boosting Clean Deliveries Act
      ▸

      Red Hook chokes on truck fumes. Lawmakers move. The Clean Deliveries Act aims to cut emissions from sprawling e-commerce warehouses. Kristen Gonzalez and others demand action. Diesel trucks crowd narrow streets. Pollution and danger rise. Residents pay the price.

      Bill: Clean Deliveries Act. Announced November 29, 2023. Status: Proposed. Committee: Not specified. Lawmakers, including State Sen. Kristen Gonzalez (District 59), call for strict regulation of emissions from last-mile e-commerce warehouses. The bill would require environmental reviews for warehouses over 50,000 square feet, mandate plans to cut air pollution, and push for zero-emission delivery vehicles. Gonzalez said, 'The unchecked growth of large warehouses in neighborhoods across NYC has worsened air quality, noise pollution, and traffic safety for everyday New Yorkers.' Assembly Member Marcela Mitaynes, a co-sponsor, highlighted the link between warehouse traffic, pollution, and traffic violence. The Red Hook Business Alliance and community advocates back the bill, citing heavy truck traffic, poor air, and threats to safety in working-class neighborhoods. The legislation aims to hold warehouse operators accountable and protect residents from the mounting toll of delivery-driven pollution and danger.
