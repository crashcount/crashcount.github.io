      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 26, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      A cyclist rides home from an event. She never makes it. On a Tuesday night in Astoria, a pickup truck, chased by police, runs a red and hits Amanda Servedio. She is thrown from her bike. She dies in the street. Her father criticized the police pursuit: “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood.”

      A few blocks away, a driver without a license reverses into a box truck, then mounts the sidewalk outside a school. A seven-year-old girl is left with a broken femur and a head wound. A teenager and a man are also hit. The driver is charged with reckless endangerment and driving without a license. Three people in the path of the sedan were struck, including the 7-year-old girl, who also suffered a head injury.

      In the last twelve months, Queens CB1 saw 3 deaths, 5 serious injuries, and 797 people hurt in crashes. Children, cyclists, elders—no one is spared. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie.

      Who Pays, Who Acts

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. In three years: 2 deaths and 4 serious injuries from cars and SUVs. Trucks and buses: 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 serious injury. Bikes: 1 serious injury. The street is a gauntlet. The sidewalk is no refuge.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán has backed bills to daylight intersections, legalize jaywalking, and speed up protected bike lanes. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act and called for bolder action after children and cyclists died. But many bills sit in committee. Some laws pass, but the street stays the same. Every delay is another risk.

      The city passed a law to let pedestrians cross anywhere. It ended jaywalking tickets. But the cars keep coming. The city promises daylighting, but corners stay blind. The dead do not come back.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by someone in power. If you want change, do not wait for another child to bleed on the sidewalk. Call your council member. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand daylighted corners with real barriers. Demand the city use its power to lower speed limits now. Reward those who fight for safety. Call out those who stall.

      The street will not change itself. You must.

      González-Rojas Criticizes Car-Centered Opposition to Safety Bill
      ▸

      Albany failed New York’s streets. Lawmakers blocked Sammy’s Law and other safety bills. Cyclists, pedestrians, and bus riders lost. Car culture ruled. Advocates called it a wasted session. Only transit funding and a bus pilot survived. No real progress for the vulnerable.

      In the 2023 legislative session, Albany lawmakers failed to pass major street safety bills, including Sammy’s Law (which would let New York City set its own speed limits). The session ended June 13, 2023, with most safety and transit measures stalled in committee or blocked from a vote. Council members and advocates like Eric McClure, Jessica Gonzalez-Rojas, Amy Cohen, and Sara Lind voiced anger and disappointment. McClure said, 'We have to give the state legislature an 'F' for wasting an entire session without advancing legislation to make streets safer or to improve public transit.' The Assembly’s inaction left vulnerable road users exposed. Only increased MTA funding and a free bus pilot moved forward. The rest—protection for cyclists, pedestrians, and bus riders—died in committee.

      Int 1063-2023
      Cabán co-sponsors bill for EV charging info app, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to create a city app showing electric vehicle charging stations. The app would map locations, show voltage, connector types, and real-time status. It would include e-bike charging info. Strict privacy rules would limit data collection and retention.

      Bill Int 1063-2023 was introduced on June 8, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill called for the Department of Information Technology and Telecommunications to build a mobile app displaying all public electric vehicle charging stations in New York City. The app would show locations, voltage, charging level, connector types, e-bike compatibility, and real-time availability. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the creation of a mobile application that provides information about electric vehicle charging stations.' Council Member Keith Powers sponsored the bill, joined by Avilés, Cabán, Farías, Riley, Richardson Jordan, Ossé, Ung, Hanif, Restler, and Gutiérrez. The bill set strict privacy: no device data, no tracking, no forced consent. The bill was filed at session’s end, not enacted.

      Int 1071-2023
      Cabán sponsors bill speeding up removal of hazardous utility poles, boosting safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force quick removal of abandoned or dangerous utility poles and wires. Owners would have sixty days to clear junk, or act at once if hazards loom. The bill died in committee. Streets stay cluttered. Danger lingers.

      Int 1071-2023 was introduced on June 8, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to require owners to remove disused or abandoned utility poles, wires, and appurtenances within 60 days, and to act immediately if conditions were dangerous. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the timelines for the removal of abandoned or unsafe utility poles, wires, and appurtenances, and the transfer of appurtenances to newly erected poles.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán sponsored the bill, joined by Kevin C. Riley, Jennifer Gutiérrez, Lincoln Restler, Kristin Richardson Jordan, Amanda Farías, and Robert F. Holden. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The bill’s failure leaves hazardous poles and wires on city streets, a threat to those on foot and bike.
