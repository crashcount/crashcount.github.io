      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Another Body on the Asphalt: Queens Streets Demand Action Now

      Written July 13, 2025

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Toll: Death and Injury on Familiar Streets

      In Queens CB1, the numbers do not flinch. Fourteen dead. Twenty-one seriously injured. More than 2,800 hurt since 2022. Each number is a body. Each body is a story cut short or broken. In the last twelve months alone: three deaths, six left with life-altering wounds, and over 850 injured. The streets do not forgive mistakes. They do not care about intent.

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. They killed two, left four with grave injuries, and hurt hundreds more. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes all add to the count. No one is spared. Not the young, not the old. Not the cyclist, not the pedestrian.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      The carnage does not pause. Last October, a woman on a bike was killed at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She wore a helmet. It did not matter. In June, a 17-year-old died as a passenger in a sedan on Astoria Boulevard. In July, a 39-year-old moped rider was killed at 23rd Avenue and 37th Street. The details are always the same: a body, a street, a vehicle, a family left behind.

      On the bus, no one is safe either. “It must be very devastating for the people that were on the bus,” said Jacqueline Cox, after a Q20 bus jumped the curb and smashed into a pole, injuring seven. The driver, just 25, told investigators he “misjudged the curb.” But video showed he had fallen asleep at the wheel. “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus,” said Ken Baur. Eight people hurt. The MTA pulled the driver from service. The investigation drags on.

      Leadership: Progress and Pressure

      Local leaders have not been silent. Council Member Tiffany Cabán, State Senator Kristen Gonzalez, and Assembly Member Jessica González-Rojas backed the new protected bike lanes on 31st Street, writing that the lanes would “protect pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers while making our streets safer and less congested”. Cabán has also co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and speed up protected bike lanes. Senator Gonzalez voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, targeting repeat reckless drivers. But the pace is slow. The bodies keep coming.

      Call to Action: Demand More, Demand Now

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day of delay is another life at risk. Call your council member. Call your state senator. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted intersections. Demand action against repeat speeders. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Cars and SUVs: 2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, over 400 total injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 serious injury, 27 total injuries. Bikes: 1 serious injury, 15 total injuries. Cars and SUVs are the main killers, but every mode adds to the toll.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns: speeding, failure to yield, drivers falling asleep. These are preventable. Policy and design can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, daylight intersections, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can refuse to delay life-saving changes.

      Gonzalez Supports Safety Boosting 20MPH Slow Zone Plan
      ▸

      Community Board 1 voted 18-15 to cut speed limits in Greenpoint and Williamsburg to 20 mph. Residents and officials demand action after deadly crashes. DOT has yet to respond. The fight pits safety against drivers. Lives hang in the balance.

      On March 6, 2025, Brooklyn Community Board 1 voted 18-15 to request a 20 mph speed limit for Greenpoint and Williamsburg. The measure, supported by 40 groups and nearly all local elected officials, marks the first neighborhood-wide slow zone request since the passage of Sammy's Law. The board's action follows letters from Sen. Kristen Gonzalez, Assembly Member Emily Gallagher, Council Member Jennifer Gutierrez, and U.S. Rep. Nydia Velazquez to DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. They cited CB1's high fatality rate—3.27 deaths per 10,000 residents, above the city average. The board's vote was contentious, with pro-driving members opposing the change. DOT spokesperson Mona Bruno acknowledged the support and promised more information on slow zones for outer boroughs. Council Member Lincoln Restler later joined the call for lower speeds. The measure now awaits DOT action.
