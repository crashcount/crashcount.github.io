      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Queens CB1: Blood on the Corners, Silence in the Gaps

      Written August 16, 2025 (Revised August 16, 2025 – Finisher pass: tightened prose, fact-checked dates and figures, added citywide policy paragraph, corrected FAQ vehicle figures.)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 16, 2025

      Just 2025-08-12 in Astoria

      On 2025-08-12 an 84-year-old driver jumped a curb at 19th Ave and 42nd St and killed two people gathered at a coffee cart. Police and the city crash log confirm the driver also died at the scene amNY.

      A witness stood with his head in his hands. “I have never seen anything like this,” he said to reporters amNY.

      Since 2022 Queens CB1 has recorded 19 deaths, 3,015 injuries, and 21 serious injuries in city crash reports NYC Open Data.

      The Ones We Keep Losing

      2025-07-07: a 39-year-old moped rider died at 23rd Ave and 37th St NYC Open Data.

      2025-01-02: a 53-year-old pedestrian died after being struck at Ditmars Blvd and 31st St NYC Open Data.

      2024-10-22: a 36-year-old cyclist was killed at 34th Ave and 37th St NYC Open Data.

      Morning rush and late nights are deadly here. Crashes cluster on Northern Boulevard and 34th Avenue — repeat hotspots the city data flags. The city’s logs list common factors: driver inattention/distraction, failure to yield, alcohol involvement, and entries labeled “vulnerable road user error.” Passenger vehicles — sedans and SUVs — account for the bulk of pedestrian harm in CB1 (sedans: 207 cases; SUVs: 154 cases), with trucks and buses adding more serious strikes NYC Open Data.

      The fixes are proven and local: daylighted corners, leading pedestrian intervals (LPIs) at crosswalks, hardened turns and raised crossings, protected bike lanes, better night lighting, and targeted late‑night enforcement on repeat hotspots.

      Leaders: Words and Votes

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán called for rapid action and backed universal daylighting and the 31st Street safety plan after the Astoria crash Streetsblog.

      Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani urged accelerating Sammy’s Law adoption and cutting car traffic near schools Streetsblog.

      Cabán co‑sponsored Int. 1353‑2025 to force faster installation of school safety devices (60 days from study to hardware) Int 1353-2025. In Albany, Senator Kristen Gonzalez voted in committee for S4045, a bill to require intelligent speed‑assistance devices for repeat violators S 4045.

      What Must Change Now

      • Daylight every dangerous corner and install LPIs at high‑volume crossings. Harden turns on 34th Avenue and Northern Boulevard.
      • Build the 31st Street safety plan with protected lanes and raised crossings.
      • Target late‑night danger with brighter lighting and focused enforcement on repeat hotspots.

      Citywide policy must follow local facts. Use Sammy’s Law to lower the default speed limit to 20 mph across the city. Require intelligent speed assistance for habitual speeders — the bill S4045 moves exactly that tool in Albany S 4045. Move Int. 1353‑2025 so schools get safety hardware fast Int 1353-2025.

      Do not wait for another body on the asphalt. Call, write, show up. Demand 20 mph and speed limiters. Push CB1, DOT, Council Member Cabán, Assembly Member Mamdani, and Senator Gonzalez to act now. Start here: Take Action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      From 2022–2025 passenger vehicles cause most pedestrian harm in Queens CB1. City crash logs show sedans account for 207 pedestrian cases and SUVs 154. Trucks and buses add about 34 cases combined. Motorcycles and mopeds total 31; bikes total 16. There are five pedestrian deaths reported in CB1. Source: NYC Open Data (Crashes, Persons, Vehicles) NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these just “accidents”?
      No. The pattern is clear in the city’s own logs and in Queens CB1’s deadly corners. After the Astoria deaths, local officials pushed for slower streets and daylighted crossings — “the full implementation of Sammy’s Law” and “Universal Daylighting” Streetsblog. Albany has a bill to rein in repeat speeders with limiters S 4045. These are preventable crashes.
      ▸ What can local politicians do right now?
      • Use Sammy’s Law to lower the default speed to 20 mph; back it publicly Streetsblog.
      • Pass and fund daylighting and LPIs citywide; advance the 31st Street Safety Plan Streetsblog.
      • Move Int 1353-2025 to speed school safety installs Legistar.
      • Pass S4045 to require speed limiters for repeat violators S 4045.
      ▸ How can I help right now?
      Call your council member, assembly member, and state senator. Demand 20 mph, daylighting, and speed limiters. Show up at CB1. Start here: Take Action.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Queens Community Board 1

      S 5677
      Stavisky votes yes on school speed cameras, boosting safety for children.
      ▸

      Lawmakers back speed cameras near Schenectady schools. The bill passed both chambers. Cameras catch drivers who endanger kids. The program ends in 2030. Streets near schools may get safer. The vote was not unanimous.

      Senate Bill S 5677, titled 'Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the city of Schenectady,' moved through the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. The Senate passed it on June 12, with primary sponsor James Tedisco (District 44) and co-sponsor Patricia Fahy (District 46) leading the push. The Assembly approved it on June 13. The bill sets up automated speed enforcement near schools and sunsets December 31, 2030. The measure aims to catch speeding drivers near children. Some lawmakers voted no, but most supported the move. No formal safety analyst note was provided, but the bill targets a known danger zone for vulnerable road users.

      S 6815
      Stavisky votes yes to exempt some employees from bus lane rules.
      ▸

      Senate and Assembly clear S 6815. MTA workers get a pass for driving in bus lanes while on duty. Law shields agency vehicles from tickets. Streets grow more crowded. Vulnerable users face more risk.

      Bill S 6815, titled 'Relates to bus lane restrictions in New York city,' passed the Senate on June 12, 2025, and the Assembly on June 13, 2025. The bill states, 'it shall be a defense to any prosecution for a violation of a bus lane restriction ... when an employee of the metropolitan transportation authority is performing authorized duties.' Sponsored by Senator Leroy Comrie and co-sponsored by Nathalia Fernandez, the measure exempts MTA employees from bus lane tickets while working. No safety analysis was provided. The bill opens bus lanes to more agency vehicles, crowding space meant for buses, cyclists, and pedestrians.

      S 8344
      Stavisky votes yes to extend school speed zones, improving child pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Senate passes S 8344. School speed zone rules in New York City get extended. Lawmakers make technical fixes. The bill keeps pressure on drivers near schools. Streets stay a little safer for kids.

      Bill S 8344, titled 'Extends provisions and makes technical corrections to school speed zones in NYC; repealer,' moved through the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. Sponsored by Senator Andrew Gounardes, the bill passed Senate votes on June 12 and June 13, and cleared the Assembly on June 17. The measure extends and corrects school speed zone laws in New York City, repealing outdated provisions. Gounardes led the push, with strong support from most Senate Democrats and a split Assembly. The bill's technical fixes aim to keep protections in place for children and other vulnerable road users near schools. No safety analyst note was provided.

      S 4045
      Gianaris co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Senate backs S 4045. Repeat speeders face forced installation of speed assistance tech. Eleven points or six camera tickets triggers action. Law targets reckless drivers. Streets may get safer for those outside the car.

      Senate bill S 4045, sponsored by Andrew Gounardes and co-sponsored by over two dozen senators, passed committee votes on June 11 and June 12, 2025. The bill, titled 'Relates to requiring the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices for repeated violation of maximum speed limits,' mandates these devices for drivers who rack up eleven or more points in 24 months, or six speed or red light camera tickets in a year. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving. Senators including Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, and others voted yes. The bill targets drivers with a pattern of violations, seeking to cut risk for pedestrians and cyclists by limiting repeat speeding.

      S 7785
      Gianaris votes yes to weaken bus rules, increasing pedestrian and cyclist risk.
      ▸

      Senate passed S 7785. The bill carves out large Mitchell-Lama housing from bus traffic rules. Lawmakers voted yes. The carve-out weakens enforcement. Streets grow less safe for people on foot and bike.

      Bill S 7785, titled 'Relates to bus operation-related traffic regulations,' passed the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. The measure, sponsored by Senator Jamaal Bailey, excludes violations in cooperative housing developments with at least 10,000 Mitchell-Lama units from bus operation traffic regulations. The bill advanced through committee and received broad support in both chambers. By exempting these large complexes, the law weakens traffic enforcement where thousands live. This move increases risk to pedestrians and cyclists, stripping away protections that save lives. Vulnerable road users lose another layer of safety in dense city streets.
