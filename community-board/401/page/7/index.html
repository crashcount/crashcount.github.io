      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Blood on 34th Ave: No More Deaths, No More Excuses

      Written June 29, 2025

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      Three people killed. Five left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. In the last year, Queens CB1 saw 1,387 crashes. Seven hundred eighty-nine people hurt. The numbers are blunt. The pain is not.

      On October 22, 2024, Amanda Servedio was riding home from a cycling event. A pickup, fleeing police, struck her at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She flew thirty feet. She did not scream. She died in the street. “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. Her father said they were upset police didn’t stop the chase, and that it was not the place for a high-speed pursuit.

      A 17-year-old died in a car crash on Astoria Boulevard. A 15-year-old moped rider was killed by a bus on 19th Avenue. The dead do not get second chances.

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and the Young

      Cars and SUVs killed two. Trucks killed one. In the last three years, 13 people have died on these streets. Twenty suffered injuries so severe they are counted as “serious.” The young are not spared. One child under 18 died. Fifty-eight more were injured.

      Leadership: Promises, Votes, and the Waiting

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and legalize jaywalking—measures meant to clear sightlines and protect those on foot and bike. She voted yes to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed the victim. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act, demanding safer design and more rights for crash victims. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez and Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani have supported protected bike lanes and lower speed limits. But the pace is slow. The blood dries before the paint goes down.

      What Now: The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted crosswalks, and a 20 mph speed limit. Join Transportation Alternatives or Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name on the list. The street remembers. So should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Most injuries and deaths came from Cars and SUVs (2 deaths, 392 injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (1 death, 29 injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 27 injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 15 injuries). Data source
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The pattern is clear. These deaths and injuries are preventable. Safer street design, lower speeds, and enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for protected bike lanes, daylighted crosswalks, lower speed limits, and end policies that blame the victim. They can fund and build safer streets now, not later.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How many children have been killed or hurt in Queens CB1 recently?
      In the last year, one child under 18 was killed and 58 more were injured in traffic crashes.

      A 2299
      Gonzalez-Rojas co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Drivers who rack up points or camera tickets face forced speed control. Assembly bill A 2299 targets repeat violators. The law would require intelligent speed assistance devices. The goal: stop reckless drivers before they kill.

      Assembly Bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 17, 2025, it awaits committee review. The bill summary reads: 'Requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Assembly Member Emily Gallagher leads as primary sponsor, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, Tony Simone, and others. No votes have been recorded yet. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving by forcing speed limiters on offenders. This targets a core threat to pedestrians and cyclists: unchecked speed and repeat lawbreaking.

      S 1675
      Gianaris co-sponsors bill for online pedestrian safety ratings, no major safety change.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 1675 would force carmakers to face the facts. It creates a pedestrian safety rating system for all vehicles. The ratings go public on the DMV website. Lawmakers want drivers to see the danger. The streets demand it.

      Senate bill S 1675 was introduced on January 13, 2025. It sits in the sponsorship stage. The bill, titled 'Creates a pedestrian safety rating system for motor vehicles,' would require the Department of Motor Vehicles to post these ratings online. Primary sponsor Andrew Gounardes leads a slate of co-sponsors: Leroy Comrie, Michael Gianaris, Brad Hoylman-Sigal, Robert Jackson, Liz Krueger, Zellnor Myrie, Gustavo Rivera, Julia Salazar, Luis R. Sepúlveda, and Toby Ann Stavisky. The bill aims to expose the risk cars pose to people on foot. By making safety data public, it puts pressure on automakers and drivers alike. The measure has not yet moved to committee or received a vote.

      A 1077
      Gonzalez-Rojas co-sponsors bill boosting street safety for all users.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 1077 aims to open streets to all. Sponsors demand safe access for walkers, cyclists, and drivers. The bill calls for complete street design. It stands as a direct answer to traffic violence. The fight for safer roads moves forward.

      Assembly Bill A 1077, sponsored by Jonathan Rivera and over sixty co-sponsors, sits in the sponsorship stage. The bill, introduced January 8, 2025, in the New York State Assembly, is titled: "Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles." Rivera and his colleagues call for streets built for people, not just cars. The bill has not yet reached committee or vote. Its language is clear: every user deserves safe passage. Complete streets mean sidewalks, bike lanes, and crossings that protect the most vulnerable. This is a direct response to the daily toll of crashes. The measure signals strong legislative support for systemic change.
