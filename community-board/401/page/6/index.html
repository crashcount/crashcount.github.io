      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Blood on the Crosswalks: Queens Kids and Cyclists Pay for City Inaction

      Written June 25, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Shattered

      A woman on a bike, Amanda Servedio, was struck and killed at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She was coming home from a cycling event. The driver was fleeing police. Amanda went airborne and landed against a parked car. She died at Elmhurst Hospital. Her father criticized the police chase: “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened”.

      A seven-year-old girl, a teenager, and a man were hit outside a school on 35th Avenue. The driver was unlicensed. The girl’s femur snapped. She also suffered a head injury. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment and driving without a license according to police.

      In the last twelve months, 3 people died and 795 were injured in 1,395 crashes in Queens CB1. Five suffered serious injuries.

      Who Pays the Price?

      The dead are not numbers. They are daughters, sons, neighbors. A witness described Amanda’s final moments: “She didn’t even scream… She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force”.

      Children bleed on sidewalks. Cyclists die in the street. The city counts the bodies. The danger does not let up.

      Leadership: Promises, Laws, and the Slow Grind

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, legalize jaywalking, and require protected bike lanes. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act and called for broader traffic calming after Dolma Naadhun’s death. She voted to end jaywalking enforcement, shifting blame away from people on foot. But laws move slow. Streets change slower. The city still lets cars rule the crossings.

      The Next Step: Demand Action—Not Excuses

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. Call your council member. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand enforcement that targets danger, not the vulnerable. Do not wait for another child to die.

      Find your council member and take action now.

      Int 0346-2024
      Cabán votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.

      Cabán Opposes Biased Jaywalking Enforcement Harms Pedestrian Safety
      ▸

      Council pulled the jaywalking decriminalization bill before a vote. Advocates warned it could shield drivers who hit pedestrians. NYPD tickets mostly Black and Hispanic New Yorkers. Councilmember Cabán says the law enables biased policing. Streets remain dangerous for those on foot.

      Bill to decriminalize jaywalking, sponsored by Councilmember Tiffany Cabán (District 22), was pulled for revision before a full City Council vote on September 23, 2024. The measure, under review in committee, aimed to end NYPD enforcement that disproportionately targets people of color. The bill’s summary states, 'NYPD issues hundreds of jaywalking tickets a year. Vast majority go to people of color.' Cabán, a key sponsor, argued police use jaywalking as a pretext for stop-and-frisk, calling the law archaic and motorist-centered. Advocates raised concerns that the bill’s language might let drivers escape liability for hitting pedestrians, prompting its withdrawal. The law’s enforcement remains unequal, leaving vulnerable road users exposed.
