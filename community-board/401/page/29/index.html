      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 26, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      A cyclist rides home from an event. She never makes it. On a Tuesday night in Astoria, a pickup truck, chased by police, runs a red and hits Amanda Servedio. She is thrown from her bike. She dies in the street. Her father criticized the police pursuit: “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood.”

      A few blocks away, a driver without a license reverses into a box truck, then mounts the sidewalk outside a school. A seven-year-old girl is left with a broken femur and a head wound. A teenager and a man are also hit. The driver is charged with reckless endangerment and driving without a license. Three people in the path of the sedan were struck, including the 7-year-old girl, who also suffered a head injury.

      In the last twelve months, Queens CB1 saw 3 deaths, 5 serious injuries, and 797 people hurt in crashes. Children, cyclists, elders—no one is spared. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie.

      Who Pays, Who Acts

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. In three years: 2 deaths and 4 serious injuries from cars and SUVs. Trucks and buses: 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 serious injury. Bikes: 1 serious injury. The street is a gauntlet. The sidewalk is no refuge.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán has backed bills to daylight intersections, legalize jaywalking, and speed up protected bike lanes. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act and called for bolder action after children and cyclists died. But many bills sit in committee. Some laws pass, but the street stays the same. Every delay is another risk.

      The city passed a law to let pedestrians cross anywhere. It ended jaywalking tickets. But the cars keep coming. The city promises daylighting, but corners stay blind. The dead do not come back.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by someone in power. If you want change, do not wait for another child to bleed on the sidewalk. Call your council member. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand daylighted corners with real barriers. Demand the city use its power to lower speed limits now. Reward those who fight for safety. Call out those who stall.

      The street will not change itself. You must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Cars and SUVs: 2 deaths, 4 serious injuries. Trucks and buses: 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 serious injury. Bikes: 1 serious injury. Cars and SUVs are the main killers. Data source
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not fate. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by city planners, by lawmakers. Better street design, lower speeds, and real enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for protected bike lanes, daylighted corners, lower speed limits, and real barriers. They can fund and demand street redesigns that protect people, not just cars.

      Int 0500-2022
      Cabán co-sponsors bill to revoke private parking permits, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0500-2022 would kill private vehicle parking permits. All existing permits get revoked. Only disabled drivers and union contracts are spared. The bill targets abuse and privilege. It died in committee. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0500-2022 was introduced on June 2, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill sought to "prohibit the issuance of private vehicle parking permits and revoke such existing permits," with exceptions for people with disabilities and collective bargaining agreements. Council Member Christopher Marte led as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Brewer, Joseph, Nurse, Cabán, Avilés, Bottcher, Won, Ossé, and Richardson Jordan, plus the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill aimed to end a system that let private cars claim curb space, fueling illegal parking and danger for those on foot or bike. It was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. The status quo remains: permits still shield drivers, while vulnerable road users face the risk.

      Int 0312-2022
      Cabán co-sponsors bill raising fines for excessive vehicle noise.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to hike penalties for loud cars and trucks. The measure targeted blaring horns, illegal claxons, and roaring engines. It died at session’s end. No direct safety gains for people walking or biking. Noise remains. Streets stay loud.

      Int 0312-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety. The bill aimed to amend the city’s administrative code to increase penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles, including those under and over 10,000 pounds, and for unauthorized horns and claxons. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to increasing certain penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles.' Council Member Sandra Ung sponsored the bill, joined by 26 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. There is no evidence the bill would have improved safety for vulnerable road users. The city’s noise problem, and its threat to livable streets, remains unresolved.

      Int 0256-2022
      Cabán co-sponsors bill boosting safety by tracking police vehicle force incidents.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0256-2022 would force NYPD to count every time an officer uses a car to control someone. The law closes a reporting loophole. Police vehicles are now named as weapons. The bill was filed at session’s end. No action taken.

      Int 0256-2022, introduced April 28, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety, sought to amend the city code to require NYPD to report when officers use a motor vehicle as force. The bill’s matter title reads: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to use of force incidents involving police department use of a motor vehicle.” Council Member Althea V. Stevens led as primary sponsor, joined by 26 co-sponsors including Hudson, Williams, Restler, Krishnan, and others. The bill would have added 'use of a motor vehicle to gain control of a subject' as a reporting category. It was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. This measure aimed to expose police vehicle violence by demanding full transparency in use-of-force reporting.

      Int 0293-2022
      Cabán co-sponsors bill letting institutions use adjacent outdoor spaces.
      ▸

      Council bill sought to let schools, centers, and institutions claim curb and sidewalk space for public use. Permits would run one year. Safety plans required. The measure stalled. Streets stayed in the hands of cars. Vulnerable users got no new shield.

      Int 0293-2022 was introduced on April 28, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to require the Department of Transportation to create a program letting community centers, schools, arts and cultural institutions, and religious institutions use adjacent outdoor spaces. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law...requiring the department of transportation to establish a program to allow community centers, schools, arts and cultural institutions and religious institutions to use adjacent outdoor spaces.' Council Member Shekar Krishnan sponsored the bill, joined by Stevens, Hanif, Avilés, and others. The bill required a permit process, a site safety plan, and compliance with local rules. It was filed at session's end on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst weighed in. The bill died, leaving streets unchanged for pedestrians and cyclists.
