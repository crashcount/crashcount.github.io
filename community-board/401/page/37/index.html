      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Queens CB1: Blood on the Corners, Silence in the Gaps

      Written August 16, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 16, 2025

      Just 2025-08-12 in Astoria

      On 2025-08-12 an 84-year-old driver jumped a curb at 19th Ave and 42nd St and killed two people gathered at a coffee cart. Police and the city crash log confirm the driver also died at the scene amNY.

      A witness stood with his head in his hands. “I have never seen anything like this,” he said to reporters amNY.

      Since 2022 Queens CB1 has recorded 19 deaths, 3,015 injuries, and 21 serious injuries in city crash reports NYC Open Data.

      The Ones We Keep Losing

      2025-07-07: a 39-year-old moped rider died at 23rd Ave and 37th St NYC Open Data.

      2025-01-02: a 53-year-old pedestrian died after being struck at Ditmars Blvd and 31st St NYC Open Data.

      2024-10-22: a 36-year-old cyclist was killed at 34th Ave and 37th St NYC Open Data.

      Morning rush and late nights are deadly here. Crashes cluster on Northern Boulevard and 34th Avenue — repeat hotspots the city data flags. The city’s logs list common factors: driver inattention/distraction, failure to yield, alcohol involvement, and entries labeled “vulnerable road user error.” Passenger vehicles — sedans and SUVs — account for the bulk of pedestrian harm in CB1 (sedans: 207 cases; SUVs: 154 cases), with trucks and buses adding more serious strikes NYC Open Data.

      The fixes are proven and local: daylighted corners, leading pedestrian intervals (LPIs) at crosswalks, hardened turns and raised crossings, protected bike lanes, better night lighting, and targeted late‑night enforcement on repeat hotspots.

      Leaders: Words and Votes

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán called for rapid action and backed universal daylighting and the 31st Street safety plan after the Astoria crash Streetsblog.

      Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani urged accelerating Sammy’s Law adoption and cutting car traffic near schools Streetsblog.

      Cabán co‑sponsored Int. 1353‑2025 to force faster installation of school safety devices (60 days from study to hardware) Int 1353-2025. In Albany, Senator Kristen Gonzalez voted in committee for S4045, a bill to require intelligent speed‑assistance devices for repeat violators S 4045.

      What Must Change Now

      • Daylight every dangerous corner and install LPIs at high‑volume crossings. Harden turns on 34th Avenue and Northern Boulevard.
      • Build the 31st Street safety plan with protected lanes and raised crossings.
      • Target late‑night danger with brighter lighting and focused enforcement on repeat hotspots.

      Citywide policy must follow local facts. Use Sammy’s Law to lower the default speed limit to 20 mph across the city. Require intelligent speed assistance for habitual speeders — the bill S4045 moves exactly that tool in Albany S 4045. Move Int. 1353‑2025 so schools get safety hardware fast Int 1353-2025.

      Do not wait for another body on the asphalt. Call, write, show up. Demand 20 mph and speed limiters. Push CB1, DOT, Council Member Cabán, Assembly Member Mamdani, and Senator Gonzalez to act now. Start here: Take Action.

      Gianaris Backs Misguided Residential Parking Permit Proposal
      ▸

      Mar 16 - Albany’s push for residential parking permits risks locking in car dominance. Critics say low fees fuel car ownership, crowding out transit, bikes, and green space. Experts warn it’s a subsidy for drivers, not a fix for congestion or emissions.

      On March 16, 2023, a Senate proposal surfaced to create a Residential Parking Permit system in New York City. The plan, now under City Council review, would charge drivers up to $30 per month for curbside parking, with revenue aimed at funding the MTA. The proposal leaves key details—like reserving 20% of spaces for non-residents—to the Council. The bill’s summary claims it will reduce emissions and support transit. Senator Michael Gianaris supports the measure, but critics are blunt. Bike New York’s Jon Orcutt calls it a 'big nothingburger.' Donald Shoup says the $30 fee is a subsidy for cars. Elizabeth Adams of Transportation Alternatives argues, 'public space should be used for transit, bike lanes, and green space, not car storage.' Studies show similar programs in other cities have failed to cut congestion or emissions, and may even increase car ownership. The Department of Transportation has testified against the plan, warning it entrenches private car storage at the expense of vulnerable road users.
