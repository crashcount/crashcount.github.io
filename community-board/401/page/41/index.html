      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Streets Bleed While Leaders Stall

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A cyclist, age 36, killed at 34th Avenue and 37th Street. A 15-year-old moped rider, dead on 43rd Street. A 17-year-old, gone after a crash on Astoria Boulevard. In the last twelve months, two people have died and five have been seriously injured on the streets of Queens CB1. In that same year, 786 people were hurt in 1,363 crashes (NYC Open Data).

      The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do. Cars and SUVs led the body count—two deaths, four serious injuries. Trucks and buses, motorcycles, mopeds, bikes: all have left their mark. The most vulnerable—children, cyclists, pedestrians—pay the highest price.

      “We Didn’t See the Crane Coming”

      The stories pile up. A crane slams into a bus in Queens Village. Emily McKinnon, a passenger, recalled, “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us.” Four people hurt. The bus driver did nothing wrong. The street did not forgive.

      A BMW vaults the divider on the Belt Parkway. Passengers thrown from the car. Fire. Death. “They went airborne and into the barrier,” said a survivor. No seatbelts. No second chances.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán has co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and back the SAFE Streets Act. Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani has pushed for lower speed limits and more bus lanes. Senator Kristen Gonzalez has backed speed-limiting tech for repeat offenders.

      But the deaths keep coming. Every delay is another name on a slab.

      Act Now—Before the Next Siren

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand daylight at every crosswalk.

      Do not wait for another child’s name in the news. The city has the power. Make them use it.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Queens Community Board 1

      Res 0108-2022
      Cabán sponsors bill expanding Atlantic Ticket, boosting transit safety and equity.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to expand and lock in the Atlantic Ticket. They want Far Rockaway and more LIRR stations in the fare program. The move aims to cut commute times and boost transit use. The bill is filed, not enacted.

      Res 0108-2022 was introduced on April 14, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution urges the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to 'expand and make permanent the Atlantic Ticket pilot in order to include Far Rockaway, and other stations along the LIRR corridor, in the fare affordability program.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Yeger, Stevens, Hanif, Bottcher, Krishnan, and many others. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. The Atlantic Ticket pilot, launched in 2018, gave Southeast Queens and Brooklyn riders cheaper LIRR fares. Advocates say expanding it citywide could mean faster, safer commutes and more transit riders. The council’s action highlights the need for affordable, accessible transit—especially for neighborhoods with long, punishing commutes.

      Int 0172-2022
      Cabán sponsors bill that could slow or block open streets safety upgrades.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0172-2022 would force DOT to warn communities before changing open streets. Sixty days’ notice. Four weeks for comments. Two weeks for answers. Streets can’t shift without neighbors knowing. The bill died at session’s end. Streets stay uncertain.

      Int 0172-2022, filed by the NYC Council and handled by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to amend city law on open streets. Introduced April 14, 2022, the bill required the Department of Transportation to give 60 days’ notice to council members, boards, and local groups before any permanent open street changes. The bill’s summary reads: “notification and community input regarding designation of, removal of and changes to open streets.” Sponsors included Tiffany Cabán (primary), Crystal Hudson, Kamillah Hanks, Oswald Feliz, Farah N. Louis, Kalman Yeger, Sandy Nurse, Shahana K. Hanif, and Althea V. Stevens. The bill mandated a four-week comment window and a two-week response period, plus annual reporting and advance notice for temporary changes. The bill was filed at the end of session, leaving open streets policy unchanged.

      Res 0109-2022
      Cabán sponsors resolution backing Interborough Express, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council members push for more study of the Interborough Express. The plan aims to link Brooklyn and Queens by rail or bus. The resolution supports more review, not construction. No direct safety impact for pedestrians or cyclists yet. The bill is filed.

      Resolution 0109-2022, filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, urges continued exploration and feasibility evaluation of the proposed Interborough Express (IBX). Introduced April 14, 2022, and filed at session's end, the measure states: "Resolution supporting the continued exploration and feasibility evaluation of the proposed Interborough Express." Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Bottcher, Krishnan, and others. The IBX would run along a 14-mile corridor from Bay Ridge to Jackson Heights, connecting 17 subway lines and the LIRR. The resolution does not authorize construction, only further study. No safety analyst has assessed the impact on vulnerable road users. The bill's status is 'Filed (End of Session)'.

      Res 0110-2022
      Cabán sponsors resolution to boost rush-hour subway service, improving commuter safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to run more peak-hour trains from distant subway terminals. Riders in transit deserts face long waits, missed trains, and crowded platforms. The resolution highlights gaps in service that strand working New Yorkers far from Manhattan.

      Res 0110-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution, introduced April 14, 2022, and filed at session's end on December 31, 2023, urges the MTA to 'adjust schedules for distant subway terminal lines to include more peak-direction rush hour trains for commuters to travel to central economic hubs.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Bottcher, and others. The bill responds to service gaps hitting outer boroughs, end-of-line stations, and transit deserts—areas where 600,000 New Yorkers live with poor transit access. The council points to pandemic-era staffing shortages and train cancellations that left riders stranded. The measure aims to push the MTA to close these gaps and connect more New Yorkers to jobs and opportunity.

      Mamdani Opposes Misguided Gas Tax Holiday Boosting Driving Risks
      ▸

      Albany slashed the gas tax. Critics say it boosts driving, cuts transit funds, and worsens air. Councilmember Mamdani voted no. Advocates slam the move as reckless. Congestion pricing still waits. Vulnerable New Yorkers pay the price.

      On April 8, 2022, Governor Hochul signed a budget bill eliminating New York State’s gas tax. The measure passed quickly, bypassing lengthy review. The bill’s summary: relief from rising fuel prices. Councilmember Zohran Kwame Mamdani voted against it, citing environmental and equity harms: “This subsidizes the fossil fuel industry by $585M... I voted no.” Advocates like Danny Pearlstein (Riders Alliance) and Eric McClure (StreetsPAC) condemned the holiday as bad policy, warning it undercuts transit funding and encourages driving. Kate Slevin (Regional Plan Association) questioned future road repair funding. Meanwhile, congestion pricing—meant to cut traffic and fund transit—remains stalled. The swift gas tax cut, critics say, leaves vulnerable road users exposed to more cars, more danger, and less support.

      Res 0071-2022
      Cabán co-sponsors multilingual transit announcements, boosting public safety for non-English speakers.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to speak to the city. Four languages, not just English. Riders lost in translation miss warnings, miss stops, miss safety. The bill died in committee. The silence continues. Millions still ride in the dark.

      Resolution 0071-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on March 10, 2022. The resolution urged the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make bus and subway announcements in the four most commonly spoken languages in New York City. The official title: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make bus and subway announcements in the four most commonly spoken languages in New York City.' Council Members Shahana K. Hanif (primary sponsor), Sandra Ung, Crystal Hudson, Julie Won, and Tiffany Cabán backed the measure. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided, but the intent was clear: break the language barrier, reach millions of riders, and give all New Yorkers equal warning and access on transit. The MTA still makes most announcements in English only.
