      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Blood on Their Hands, Paint on the Pavement: Demand Real Safety Now

      Written June 27, 2025 (Revised June 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      A cyclist rides home from a community event. She never makes it. At 37th Street and 34th Avenue, a pickup truck fleeing police strikes her. She flies from her bike into a parked car. She dies at Elmhurst Hospital. Her name was Amanda Servedio. She was 36. Her father said they were upset police did not stop the chase: “It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened.”

      In the last twelve months, three people have died and 790 have been injured in crashes in Queens CB1. Five suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Children are not spared. One child died. Fifty-eight more were hurt, according to NYC Open Data.

      Patterns in Metal and Flesh

      The numbers do not lie. SUVs and cars are the main weapons: 2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 392 crashes. Trucks and buses wounded dozens more. Motorcycles and mopeds left bodies broken. Bikes, too, are not blameless, but the scale is not the same.

      Each crash is a story cut short. A man in his fifties, struck by a black SUV, left unresponsive in the road. The driver kept going, reported ABC7.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Some leaders act. Council Member Tiffany Cabán voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed the dead for their own deaths. She co-sponsored bills to speed protected bike lanes and ban parking near crosswalks. She called for the SAFE Streets Act, demanding lower speed limits and more rights for crash victims, as shown in NYC Council records.

      Others stand with her. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez and Assembly Member Jessica González-Rojas backed protected bike lanes on 31st Street, writing that it would “protect pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers while making our streets safer and less congested,” according to Streetsblog NYC.

      But the pace is slow. The blood dries before the paint goes down. Every delay is another name on a slab.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call your state senator. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand that the city stop blaming the dead and start protecting the living.

      Do not wait for another name. Act now.

      ▸

      State Senate passed a bill to double fines for illegal moped dealers. Dealers must register mopeds or face $2,000 penalties. Lawmakers shift blame from riders to vendors. Delivery workers back the move. The bill now heads to the Assembly.

      Senate Bill, sponsored by Sen. Liz Kruger (D-Manhattan), passed on June 3, 2024. It doubles fines for unauthorized moped dealers from $1,000 to $2,000 and requires registration at the point of sale. The bill targets vendors, not riders, aiming to protect vulnerable delivery workers and pedestrians.
