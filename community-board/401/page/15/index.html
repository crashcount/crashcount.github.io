      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Streets Bleed While Leaders Stall

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A cyclist, age 36, killed at 34th Avenue and 37th Street. A 15-year-old moped rider, dead on 43rd Street. A 17-year-old, gone after a crash on Astoria Boulevard. In the last twelve months, two people have died and five have been seriously injured on the streets of Queens CB1. In that same year, 786 people were hurt in 1,363 crashes (NYC Open Data).

      The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do. Cars and SUVs led the body count—two deaths, four serious injuries. Trucks and buses, motorcycles, mopeds, bikes: all have left their mark. The most vulnerable—children, cyclists, pedestrians—pay the highest price.

      “We Didn’t See the Crane Coming”

      The stories pile up. A crane slams into a bus in Queens Village. Emily McKinnon, a passenger, recalled, “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us.” Four people hurt. The bus driver did nothing wrong. The street did not forgive.

      A BMW vaults the divider on the Belt Parkway. Passengers thrown from the car. Fire. Death. “They went airborne and into the barrier,” said a survivor. No seatbelts. No second chances.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán has co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and back the SAFE Streets Act. Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani has pushed for lower speed limits and more bus lanes. Senator Kristen Gonzalez has backed speed-limiting tech for repeat offenders.

      But the deaths keep coming. Every delay is another name on a slab.

      Act Now—Before the Next Siren

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand daylight at every crosswalk.

      Do not wait for another child’s name in the news. The city has the power. Make them use it.

      Councilmember Shekar Krishnan called out the surge of reckless mopeds and e-bikes on 34th Avenue. Residents spoke of fear, injury, and death. City officials promised enforcement and education. Advocates demanded safer streets, not criminalization. The crisis rolls on. Action lags.

      On June 11, 2024, Councilmember Shekar Krishnan (District 25) convened a town hall in Jackson Heights to address what he called a 'moped crisis.' The meeting followed a deadly year: a 75-year-old man killed by an e-bike, two injured in a dirt bike crash. Residents described near-misses and constant danger on the 34th Avenue open street. The matter, titled 'On Jackson Heights’ open street, NYC’s ‘moped crisis’ is in full throttle,' drew city officials, including DOT and Mayor Adams, who backed enforcement and moped registration. Krishnan slammed DOT’s reliance on signage, pushing instead for a dedicated micromobility lane. Delivery worker advocates opposed criminalization, demanding labor protections and safe infrastructure. Despite new signs and vehicle seizures, mopeds still speed through. The call for urgent, systemic change grows louder.

The pause leaves the city’s transit future—and the safety of those who rely on it—uncertain.

      Gianaris Supports Risky MTA Bailout After Toll Cancellation
      ▸

      Albany scrambles after Hochul kills congestion pricing. Lawmakers float a $1 billion-a-year promise for the MTA. No details. No guarantees. Michael Gianaris calls it a stopgap. Riders and streets hang in the balance. The future stays uncertain.

      On June 7, 2024, Albany lawmakers responded to Governor Hochul’s cancellation of NYC congestion pricing. The proposed measure, still without a bill number or final language, would guarantee $1 billion annually for the MTA over 15 years. State Senate Deputy Majority Leader Michael Gianaris explained, “It’s very simply something that just says there will be a billion dollars for the MTA in the following year’s budget, but without any specifics as to what that means.” Gianaris and others are working with Hochul and the MTA to keep capital projects alive. The bill’s fate is unclear, with some lawmakers already planning to vote no. No safety analyst has assessed the impact on vulnerable road users. The measure’s details and its effect on street safety remain unknown.

      A 7652
      Gonzalez-Rojas votes yes, advancing school speed cameras that improve pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Assembly passes A 7652. The bill launches speed cameras near Schenectady schools. Cameras catch drivers who speed. The program ends in 2028 unless renewed. Most members vote yes. Some vote no. Streets near schools remain risky for children on foot.

      Bill A 7652, titled "Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the city of Schenectady," advanced with a committee vote in the Assembly on June 7, 2024. The measure creates a pilot program to install speed cameras near schools, aiming to catch drivers who exceed posted limits. The authorization for the program expires December 31, 2028. Assemblymembers Phil Steck (District 110, primary sponsor) and Angelo Santabarbara (District 111, co-sponsor) led the push. The vote passed with strong support but faced opposition from several members. The bill’s summary reads: "Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the city of Schenectady; repeals authorization of program December 31, 2028." No formal safety analyst note was provided, but the measure targets driver speed in zones where children walk, bike, and cross.
