      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Right turn, no right of way: a woman killed at 30th Street and 39th Avenue

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      Just after midday on Aug 31, 2025, at 30th Street and 39th Avenue, the driver of a Ford SUV turned right and hit a 38-year-old woman in a marked crosswalk. Police recorded failure to yield by the driver; she died at the scene (NYC Open Data).

      She is one of 26 people killed on these streets since Jan 1, 2022 (NYC Open Data). Year to date, deaths stand at 7, up from 5 at this point last year (NYC Open Data).

      This Week

      • Aug 31, 2025: A driver turning right in a Ford SUV hit a woman in the crosswalk at 30th Street and 39th Avenue; police cited failure to yield, and she died (NYC Open Data).
      • Aug 12, 2025: On 42nd Street at 19th Avenue, two people walking were killed amid a multi-vehicle crash; an 84-year-old driver also died (NYC Open Data).

      The map keeps the score

      Northern Boulevard has logged 174 injuries and 6 serious injuries in this district since 2022. Three people died on 34th Avenue. Two were killed on the Grand Central Parkway, with 227 injuries tallied along it (NYC Open Data).

      Police records show deadly hours when families should be safe: 5 deaths at 8 AM, 4 at 11 PM, 3 at 4 AM (NYC Open Data). In the Aug 31 crash, police cited the driver’s failure to yield. That is a fixable mistake at the curb, not an act of God (NYC Open Data).

      Streets named. Leaders named.

      After a deadly Astoria crash in August, Council Member Tiffany Cabán said, “I also strongly support the 31st Street Safety Plan, Council Member [Julie] Won’s Universal Daylighting legislation, and the full implementation of Sammy’s Law and other traffic calming measures” (Streetsblog NYC). Cabán also co-sponsored a bill to speed up installing safety devices at schools (NYC Council – Legistar).

      In Albany, State Senator Kristen Gonzalez co-sponsored and voted yes in committee for S 4045, a bill to require intelligent speed assistance for repeat speeders (Open States). Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani missed a key vote on extending school speed zones this summer, while Assembly Member Jessica González-Rojas voted yes, according to the record in this file set (Open States).

      What stops a turn like this

      The crosswalk at 30th Street and 39th Avenue is like hundreds here. Drivers turn. People walk. Hardened corners, daylighted curbs, and leading pedestrian intervals make those turns slower and clearer. Protected bike lanes and raised crossings do the same. Northern Boulevard and 34th Avenue need them most; their ledgers say so (NYC Open Data).

      The city can lower speeds on local streets. Electeds here have called for a 20 MPH limit citywide after fresh deaths in Astoria (Streetsblog NYC). Albany can curb the worst repeat offenders with S 4045’s speed limiters; Gonzalez has already put her name to it (Open States).

      One woman in a crosswalk. One turn. The fix is known. Make them do it. Then help make it citywide. Act here.
