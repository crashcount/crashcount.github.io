      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Another Cyclist Dead. How Many Bodies Before They Act?

      Written July 4, 2025

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 4, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A cyclist rides home from a community event. She never makes it. On October 22, 2024, Amanda Servedio, 36, was struck and killed at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. The driver was fleeing police, running red lights, speeding through bike lanes. “The defendant allegedly led police on a 10-minute chase through the crowded streets,” said Queens District Attorney Melinda Katz. Amanda was thrown from her bike into a parked car. She died at Elmhurst Hospital. Her ride ended in blood and silence.

      In the last twelve months, Queens CB1 saw 2 deaths and 786 injuries from crashes. Five people suffered serious, life-changing wounds. The numbers do not slow. They do not care how old you are. Children, teens, adults, the old—all are at risk. In the same period, a 17-year-old died as a passenger in a sedan. A 15-year-old moped rider was killed by a bus. The street does not forgive.

      Local Leaders: Words, Votes, and the Waiting

      The council member, Tiffany Cabán, has backed bills to daylight intersections, speed up protected bike lanes, and legalize jaywalking. She voted yes to erase penalties for crossing mid-block, shifting blame away from pedestrians. The new law requires the city to educate all road users about these rights. “By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable,” reads the council record.

      Senator Kristen Gonzalez and Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani have supported protected bike lanes and slow zones. They signed on to plans for a protected bike lane under the 31st Street tracks. “It would protect pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers while making our streets safer and less congested,” they wrote.

      But the pace is slow. Promises pile up. The dead do not come back.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Every crash is preventable. The city has the power to lower speed limits, build real protection, and end the carnage. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph limit. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted corners, and enforcement that targets reckless drivers—not people on foot or bike. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians came from Cars and Trucks (2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 394 total incidents), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 1 serious injury, 27 total incidents), and Bikes (no deaths, 1 serious injury, 15 total incidents).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Every crash is preventable. The city can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, and enforce laws that target reckless drivers. These are not random acts—they are the result of choices and policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, daylight intersections, and shift enforcement to target dangerous drivers instead of blaming pedestrians and cyclists. They can act now, not later.

      Cabán co-sponsors bill increasing overnight truck parking, reducing pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council bill would carve out curbside overnight truck parking in city industrial zones. Only commercial vehicles allowed, 8 p.m. to 6 a.m. DOT must consult locals and report yearly. Bill sits in committee. Streets stay risky for those on foot or bike.

      Int 0099-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, would require the Department of Transportation to create curbside overnight parking for commercial vehicles in every Industrial Business Zone. The bill, introduced February 8, 2024, mandates that only commercial vehicles may park in these zones between 8:00 p.m. and 6:00 a.m. The matter title states: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the creation of curbside overnight truck parking sections in Industrial Business Zones.' Council Members Althea V. Stevens (primary sponsor), Nantasha M. Williams, Lincoln Restler, Farah N. Louis, Kevin C. Riley, and others back the measure. DOT must consult community boards and report annually on effectiveness. No safety analysis for vulnerable road users is available. The bill remains in committee, with no vote scheduled.

      Int 0079-2024
      Cabán co-sponsors bill to boost pedestrian lighting, improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council members want 500 corridors lit for walkers each year. The bill demands bright sidewalks—no less than 1 footcandle. Most corridors must connect, forming safer, well-lit routes. The measure sits in committee, waiting for action. Darkness remains a threat.

      Int 0079-2024, introduced on February 8, 2024, sits 'Laid Over in Committee' with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installation of pedestrian lighting fixtures,' would require the transportation commissioner to install sidewalk lighting in at least 500 commercial corridors per year, each lit to a minimum of 1 footcandle (11 lux). At least 450 corridors must be contiguous to others with new or existing lighting. Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Krishnan, Bottcher, and many others. The bill aims to cut through the city’s darkness, demanding light for those on foot. It remains stalled in committee, its promise unrealized.
