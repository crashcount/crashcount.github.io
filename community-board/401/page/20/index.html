      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Queens Bleeds While Leaders Stall—How Many More Must Die?

      Written June 28, 2025

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      Blood on the Streets

      In Queens CB1, the killing does not stop. Since January 2022, 13 people have died in crashes. Twenty more were left with serious injuries. The toll is not just numbers. It is a cyclist thrown from her bike, a child struck on the way to school, a man left unresponsive in the road. In the last twelve months alone, three more lives ended. Nearly 800 people were hurt. The youngest victim was under 18. The oldest was over 75. No one is spared.

      On October 22, 2024, Amanda Servedio was riding home in Astoria. A pickup, fleeing police, hit her at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She went airborne. “She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. Amanda died at Elmhurst Hospital. Her father said, “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood” he told the Daily News.

      The Usual Weapons: Cars, Trucks, and Silence

      Cars and SUVs killed two. Trucks and buses left two more with serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds hurt dozens. Bikes were involved in 15 crashes, but none killed. The deadliest machines are still cars and trucks. The city’s own data shows the pattern: the bigger the vehicle, the greater the harm, as NYC Open Data shows.

      What Leaders Do—and Don’t Do

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks and require protected bike lanes. She voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed victims instead of drivers. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez and Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani have supported protected bike lanes and lower speed limits. But the pace is slow. Promises pile up. The street stays the same.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a policy choice. Call your council member. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand the city use its new power to lower speed limits. Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 2 deaths and 6 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 27 crashes. Bikes were involved in 15 crashes but caused no deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Every crash is preventable. The same streets see the same blood. Speed, street design, and enforcement decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for protected bike lanes, daylighting at crosswalks, and lower speed limits. They can stop blaming victims and start building streets for people.

      Int 0099-2024
      Cabán co-sponsors bill increasing overnight truck parking, reducing pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council bill would carve out curbside overnight truck parking in city industrial zones. Only commercial vehicles allowed, 8 p.m. to 6 a.m. DOT must consult locals and report yearly. Bill sits in committee. Streets stay risky for those on foot or bike.

      Int 0099-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, would require the Department of Transportation to create curbside overnight parking for commercial vehicles in every Industrial Business Zone. The bill, introduced February 8, 2024, mandates that only commercial vehicles may park in these zones between 8:00 p.m. and 6:00 a.m. The matter title states: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the creation of curbside overnight truck parking sections in Industrial Business Zones.' Council Members Althea V. Stevens (primary sponsor), Nantasha M. Williams, Lincoln Restler, Farah N. Louis, Kevin C. Riley, and others back the measure. DOT must consult community boards and report annually on effectiveness. No safety analysis for vulnerable road users is available. The bill remains in committee, with no vote scheduled.

      Int 0079-2024
      Cabán co-sponsors bill to boost pedestrian lighting, improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council members want 500 corridors lit for walkers each year. The bill demands bright sidewalks—no less than 1 footcandle. Most corridors must connect, forming safer, well-lit routes. The measure sits in committee, waiting for action. Darkness remains a threat.

      Int 0079-2024, introduced on February 8, 2024, sits 'Laid Over in Committee' with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installation of pedestrian lighting fixtures,' would require the transportation commissioner to install sidewalk lighting in at least 500 commercial corridors per year, each lit to a minimum of 1 footcandle (11 lux). At least 450 corridors must be contiguous to others with new or existing lighting. Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Krishnan, Bottcher, and many others. The bill aims to cut through the city’s darkness, demanding light for those on foot. It remains stalled in committee, its promise unrealized.

      González-Rojas Highlights Safety Risks From Speeding Drivers
      ▸

      Advocates stormed Albany for Sammy’s Law. They want New York City to set its own speed limits. Last year, Speaker Heastie blocked a vote. Mothers starved in protest. Lawmakers stalled. Now, survivors and families demand action. Data shows lower speeds save lives.

      On January 23, 2024, advocates launched a renewed push for Sammy’s Law in Albany. The bill, which would let New York City control its own speed limits, stalled last year when Assembly Speaker Carl Heastie refused to allow a vote, despite majority support. Brooklyn Assembly Member Emily Gallagher, a strong supporter, noted, 'Even [lawmakers] who had City Council members from their districts supporting the bill were still not interested.' Queens Assembly Member Jessica González-Rojas, recently injured by a driver, said, 'Had the car been going faster ... I might not be here.' Amy Cohen of Families for Safe Streets highlighted broad support: 'We have organizations from across the city supporting Sammy’s Law.' City data shows a 36% drop in pedestrian deaths after the 2014 speed limit reduction. The bill remains in limbo, but advocates are not backing down.
