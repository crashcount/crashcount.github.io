      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Queens Streets Bleed—Speed Kills, Leaders Stall

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed citation links, deduped citations.)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      Two dead. Four seriously hurt. In the past year alone, Queens CB1 has seen 803 crashes, 458 injuries, and two lives ended on its streets. These are not just numbers—they are bodies on the pavement, families left with empty chairs. A 17-year-old passenger, dead in a tangle of sedans on Astoria Boulevard. A 15-year-old moped rider, crushed by a bus on 43rd Street. A cyclist, a pedestrian, a child—none spared by the machinery of the street.

      The Voices in the Aftermath

      After the bus jumped the curb in Flushing, Jacqueline Cox said, “It must be very devastating for the people that were on the bus.” Eight people hurt. A pole down. The driver, just 25, told investigators he “misjudged the curb,” but video showed he had fallen asleep at the wheel. The MTA pulled him from service. “I have a baby with me. That would be scary. I’ll be more cautious of my surroundings,” said Samantha Hart. The crash could have killed more. It was luck, not policy, that spared them.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Missed

      Local leaders have moved, but not fast enough. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez voted yes on the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed limiters. It passed committee in June. But the carnage continues. Council Member Tiffany Cabán and Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani have called for safer streets, but the pace is slow. Cameras catch speeders, but the worst keep driving. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so citywide. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made upstream. Residents must demand more. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat offenders. Join groups like Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another child to die.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Over the past three years, Cars and Trucks (including SUVs, sedans, vans, and taxis) were involved in 405 pedestrian injuries and 2 deaths. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 27 injuries. Bikes were involved in 15 injuries. Cars and trucks remain the deadliest threat. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. They follow patterns—speed, street design, repeat offenders. Policies like lower speed limits and speed limiters for repeat violators can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to reduce traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, support and enforce speed limiter laws for repeat offenders, redesign streets for safety, and prioritize enforcement against the most dangerous drivers. They can act now, not later.

      Mamdani Calls for Political Will to Fund Transit Safety
      ▸

      Lawmakers wrangle over how to fund the MTA’s crumbling system. Assemblymember Robert Carroll pushes for a higher congestion toll, putting it above his own delivery fee plan. Albany stalls. Riders wait. Infrastructure ages. Danger grows for those outside cars.

      ""The question is whether we have the political will to collect that [tax] and ensure that it's used to transform the lives of working-class New Yorkers,"" -- Zohran Mamdani

      On January 31, 2025, the New York City Council debated MTA funding options. Assemblymember Robert Carroll (District 44) voiced support for increasing the congestion toll, stating it should come before his earlier $3 delivery fee proposal. The debate, covered in the article '5 ways the MTA could get the money it needs to fix aging infrastructure,' highlights the lack of consensus among lawmakers. Governor Hochul’s budget falls short. Senate and Assembly leaders have not chosen a revenue source. Carroll’s stance: 'increasing the congestion toll should come first.' Other options—taxing the rich, payroll tax hikes, vice taxes—face resistance. MTA Chair Janno Lieber urges focus on repairs, not expansion. No direct safety analysis was provided, but the delay in funding leaves vulnerable road users exposed to the risks of failing infrastructure.

      A 2299
      Gonzalez-Rojas co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 2299 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets in a year triggers forced speed control tech. Lawmakers move to curb repeat speeders. Streets demand fewer deadly risks.

      Assembly bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 16, 2025, the bill 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Primary sponsor Emily Gallagher leads a bloc of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, and others. The measure aims to clamp down on repeat speeders with mandatory speed-limiting tech. No safety analyst note was provided.
