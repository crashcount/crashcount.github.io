      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Queens Streets Bleed—How Many More Until Leaders Act?

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB1

      The streets do not forgive. In Queens CB1, the numbers do not lie. Thirteen people are dead. Twenty more are seriously injured. Over 2,700 have been hurt since 2022. Each number is a body, a family, a life split open on the asphalt (NYC Open Data).

      The last year alone saw three deaths and five serious injuries. The victims are not just numbers. A cyclist, a child, a man behind the wheel. Some never made it home. Some never stood a chance.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      The violence does not slow. In October, a pickup fleeing police struck and killed Amanda Servedio, a cyclist returning from a ride. She “went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. The chase ran through crowded streets. The driver did not stop. Amanda died in the road.

      Her father spoke after the arrest. “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened,” he told reporters.

      Elsewhere, a 42-year-old man died at 36th Avenue and 36th Street. A 15-year-old was killed on a moped. The list grows. The pain does not fade.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      Local leaders have moved, but not fast enough. Council Member Tiffany Cabán co-sponsored the law that ended jaywalking tickets, shifting blame away from pedestrians and back to drivers (Legislation to end jaywalking tickets). She also backed bills for protected bike lanes and daylighting crosswalks. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez and others supported new protected bike lanes on 31st Street, over the loud objections of some business owners (protected bike lanes on 31st Street).

      But the streets are still deadly. Promises do not stop cars. Every delay means another family waits for a knock at the door.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted corners, and enforcement that targets reckless drivers—not the people they hit. Join Transportation Alternatives or Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1 came from cars and trucks (2 deaths, 277 minor injuries, 109 moderate injuries, 4 serious injuries, 392 total incidents). Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 27 incidents (no deaths, 18 minor injuries, 9 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries). Bikes were involved in 15 incidents (no deaths, 10 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury). See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These are not just accidents. They are the result of policy choices—speed limits, street design, and enforcement. Every delay in action means more lives lost.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, daylight crosswalks, and shift enforcement to target reckless drivers. They can back bills that put safety first and move quickly to implement them.

      Mamdani Supports Safety Boosting Cameras to Protect Bike Lanes
      ▸

      Assemblyman Mamdani and Senator Hoylman push for cameras in 50 protected bike lanes. Drivers who block lanes face $50 fines. DOT backs the plan. Lawmakers say enforcement is needed. Cyclists face danger daily. Cameras promise real consequences for reckless drivers.

      Assembly Bill, proposed June 2, 2022, by Zohran Mamdani (District 36) and co-sponsored by Brad Hoylman, seeks to deploy automated enforcement cameras at 50 protected bike lanes. The bill aims to fine drivers $50 for each infraction, targeting those who block or drive in bike lanes. The matter summary states: 'NYC pols propose traffic cameras to deter drivers from using bike lanes.' Mamdani and Hoylman argue that enforcement is critical, with Mamdani stating, 'You consistently see cars driving in the bike lane. We know that these cameras work to deter drivers from breaking the law.' DOT supports the measure, calling it 'life-saving automated enforcement technology.' The bill awaits City Council approval, with Mamdani pledging to advance it through the summer and fall.

      González-Rojas Frames MTA Bridge Bike Access as Justice Issue
      ▸

      MTA pledges better access for cyclists and walkers. The agency seeks public input. Sam Schwartz Engineering will draft the plan. New bike parking appears at Grand Central. Permit rules ease on commuter trains. Advocates push for more. History shows slow progress.

      On May 27, 2022, the MTA announced a strategic action plan to improve bicycle, pedestrian, and micromobility access to its trains and facilities. This follows a state bill signed last year mandating better conditions for cyclists. The plan, still in development, aims to create 'equitable access to the transit system and support sustainable transportation modes.' MTA Chairman and CEO Janno Lieber said, 'We want to improve customer access to our environmentally friendly MTA services, however they get to their train or bus.' Queens Assembly Member Jessica González-Rojas called bridge access 'a matter of environmental justice, public health, and labor justice.' The MTA has ended bike permits on LIRR and Metro-North and added bike parking at Grand Central, but its record on bike access remains mixed. The agency is now seeking public comment and has hired Sam Schwartz Engineering to help shape the plan.

      Int 0312-2022
      Cabán co-sponsors bill raising fines for excessive vehicle noise.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to hike penalties for loud cars and trucks. The measure targeted blaring horns, illegal claxons, and roaring engines. It died at session’s end. No direct safety gains for people walking or biking. Noise remains. Streets stay loud.

      Int 0312-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety. The bill aimed to amend the city’s administrative code to increase penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles, including those under and over 10,000 pounds, and for unauthorized horns and claxons. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to increasing certain penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles.' Council Member Sandra Ung sponsored the bill, joined by 26 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. There is no evidence the bill would have improved safety for vulnerable road users. The city’s noise problem, and its threat to livable streets, remains unresolved.

      Int 0256-2022
      Cabán co-sponsors bill boosting safety by tracking police vehicle force incidents.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0256-2022 would force NYPD to count every time an officer uses a car to control someone. The law closes a reporting loophole. Police vehicles are now named as weapons. The bill was filed at session’s end. No action taken.

      Int 0256-2022, introduced April 28, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety, sought to amend the city code to require NYPD to report when officers use a motor vehicle as force. The bill’s matter title reads: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to use of force incidents involving police department use of a motor vehicle.” Council Member Althea V. Stevens led as primary sponsor, joined by 26 co-sponsors including Hudson, Williams, Restler, Krishnan, and others. The bill would have added 'use of a motor vehicle to gain control of a subject' as a reporting category. It was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. This measure aimed to expose police vehicle violence by demanding full transparency in use-of-force reporting.

      Int 0293-2022
      Cabán co-sponsors bill letting institutions use adjacent outdoor spaces.
      ▸

      Council bill sought to let schools, centers, and institutions claim curb and sidewalk space for public use. Permits would run one year. Safety plans required. The measure stalled. Streets stayed in the hands of cars. Vulnerable users got no new shield.

      Int 0293-2022 was introduced on April 28, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to require the Department of Transportation to create a program letting community centers, schools, arts and cultural institutions, and religious institutions use adjacent outdoor spaces. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law...requiring the department of transportation to establish a program to allow community centers, schools, arts and cultural institutions and religious institutions to use adjacent outdoor spaces.' Council Member Shekar Krishnan sponsored the bill, joined by Stevens, Hanif, Avilés, and others. The bill required a permit process, a site safety plan, and compliance with local rules. It was filed at session's end on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst weighed in. The bill died, leaving streets unchanged for pedestrians and cyclists.
