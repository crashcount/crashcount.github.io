      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Blood on 34th Ave: No More Deaths, No More Excuses

      Written June 29, 2025

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      Three people killed. Five left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. In the last year, Queens CB1 saw 1,387 crashes. Seven hundred eighty-nine people hurt. The numbers are blunt. The pain is not.

      On October 22, 2024, Amanda Servedio was riding home from a cycling event. A pickup, fleeing police, struck her at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She flew thirty feet. She did not scream. She died in the street. “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. Her father said they were upset police didn’t stop the chase, and that it was not the place for a high-speed pursuit.

      A 17-year-old died in a car crash on Astoria Boulevard. A 15-year-old moped rider was killed by a bus on 19th Avenue. The dead do not get second chances.

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and the Young

      Cars and SUVs killed two. Trucks killed one. In the last three years, 13 people have died on these streets. Twenty suffered injuries so severe they are counted as “serious.” The young are not spared. One child under 18 died. Fifty-eight more were injured.

      Leadership: Promises, Votes, and the Waiting

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and legalize jaywalking—measures meant to clear sightlines and protect those on foot and bike. She voted yes to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed the victim. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act, demanding safer design and more rights for crash victims. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez and Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani have supported protected bike lanes and lower speed limits. But the pace is slow. The blood dries before the paint goes down.

      What Now: The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted crosswalks, and a 20 mph speed limit. Join Transportation Alternatives or Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name on the list. The street remembers. So should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Most injuries and deaths came from Cars and SUVs (2 deaths, 392 injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (1 death, 29 injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 27 injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 15 injuries). Data source
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The pattern is clear. These deaths and injuries are preventable. Safer street design, lower speeds, and enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for protected bike lanes, daylighted crosswalks, lower speed limits, and end policies that blame the victim. They can fund and build safer streets now, not later.

The measure now awaits DOT action.

      Mamdani Calls for Safer Streets and Better Infrastructure
      ▸

      Delivery workers rallied against Hochul’s e-bike crackdown. Council weighs bills to license e-bikes, raise pay, and study conditions. DOT opposes registration. Advocates warn of criminalization. City Hall silent. Streets stay dangerous for those on foot and bike.

      On February 27, 2025, the City Council considered three bills: Intro 606 (register and license all e-bikes), Intro 1133 (expand minimum wage, study delivery worker conditions), and Intro 1135 (set minimum pay for grocery delivery). Intro 606 faces opposition from DOT, which says, 'there is no evidence that registration improves safety.' The matter’s summary warns that Hochul’s plan would let NYPD enforce bike lane speed limits and reclassify heavy e-bikes as mopeds, requiring licenses and registration. Council members are split: Brad Lander supports regulation targeting app companies, not workers; Jessica Ramos and Zohran Mamdani call for better street design; Michael Blake urges targeted safety solutions and more infrastructure. Advocates like Ligia Guallpa say these measures would criminalize delivery workers and create a 'ticketing-to-deportation pipeline.' The Council press office did not comment. The debate exposes a city divided on how to protect its most vulnerable road users.
