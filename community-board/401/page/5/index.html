      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 26, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      A cyclist rides home from an event. She never makes it. On a Tuesday night in Astoria, a pickup truck, chased by police, runs a red and hits Amanda Servedio. She is thrown from her bike. She dies in the street. Her father criticized the police pursuit: “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood.”

      A few blocks away, a driver without a license reverses into a box truck, then mounts the sidewalk outside a school. A seven-year-old girl is left with a broken femur and a head wound. A teenager and a man are also hit. The driver is charged with reckless endangerment and driving without a license. Three people in the path of the sedan were struck, including the 7-year-old girl, who also suffered a head injury.

      In the last twelve months, Queens CB1 saw 3 deaths, 5 serious injuries, and 797 people hurt in crashes. Children, cyclists, elders—no one is spared. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie.

      Who Pays, Who Acts

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. In three years: 2 deaths and 4 serious injuries from cars and SUVs. Trucks and buses: 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 serious injury. Bikes: 1 serious injury. The street is a gauntlet. The sidewalk is no refuge.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán has backed bills to daylight intersections, legalize jaywalking, and speed up protected bike lanes. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act and called for bolder action after children and cyclists died. But many bills sit in committee. Some laws pass, but the street stays the same. Every delay is another risk.

      The city passed a law to let pedestrians cross anywhere. It ended jaywalking tickets. But the cars keep coming. The city promises daylighting, but corners stay blind. The dead do not come back.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by someone in power. If you want change, do not wait for another child to bleed on the sidewalk. Call your council member. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand daylighted corners with real barriers. Demand the city use its power to lower speed limits now. Reward those who fight for safety. Call out those who stall.

      The street will not change itself. You must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Cars and SUVs: 2 deaths, 4 serious injuries. Trucks and buses: 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 serious injury. Bikes: 1 serious injury. Cars and SUVs are the main killers. Data source
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not fate. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by city planners, by lawmakers. Better street design, lower speeds, and real enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for protected bike lanes, daylighted corners, lower speed limits, and real barriers. They can fund and demand street redesigns that protect people, not just cars.

      Int 1138-2024
      Cabán co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1138-2024 would ban parking and standing within 20 feet of crosswalks. It forces the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections each year. The bill sits in committee. Sponsors include Bottcher, Won, and the Public Advocate.

      Int 1138-2024, introduced December 5, 2024, is under review by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill is 'Laid Over in Committee.' Its title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.' Council Member Erik D. Bottcher is the primary sponsor, joined by Julie Won, Jumaane Williams, and others. The bill prohibits standing or parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and mandates the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections per year. The law also requires citywide outreach and reporting. This measure aims to keep sightlines clear at crossings, a known danger zone for people on foot and bike. The bill has not yet received a vote.
