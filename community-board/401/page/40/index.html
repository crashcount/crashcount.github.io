      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Blood on Their Hands, Paint on the Pavement: Demand Real Safety Now

      Written June 27, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Fixed headline duplication, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, and deduped citations.)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      A cyclist rides home from a community event. She never makes it. At 37th Street and 34th Avenue, a pickup truck fleeing police strikes her. She flies from her bike into a parked car. She dies at Elmhurst Hospital. Her name was Amanda Servedio. She was 36. Her father said they were upset police did not stop the chase: “It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened.”

      In the last twelve months, three people have died and 790 have been injured in crashes in Queens CB1. Five suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Children are not spared. One child died. Fifty-eight more were hurt, according to NYC Open Data.

      Patterns in Metal and Flesh

      The numbers do not lie. SUVs and cars are the main weapons: 2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 392 crashes. Trucks and buses wounded dozens more. Motorcycles and mopeds left bodies broken. Bikes, too, are not blameless, but the scale is not the same.

      Each crash is a story cut short. A man in his fifties, struck by a black SUV, left unresponsive in the road. The driver kept going, reported ABC7.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Some leaders act. Council Member Tiffany Cabán voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed the dead for their own deaths. She co-sponsored bills to speed protected bike lanes and ban parking near crosswalks. She called for the SAFE Streets Act, demanding lower speed limits and more rights for crash victims, as shown in NYC Council records.

      Others stand with her. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez and Assembly Member Jessica González-Rojas backed protected bike lanes on 31st Street, writing that it would “protect pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers while making our streets safer and less congested,” according to Streetsblog NYC.

      But the pace is slow. The blood dries before the paint goes down. Every delay is another name on a slab.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call your state senator. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand that the city stop blaming the dead and start protecting the living.

      Do not wait for another name. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Cars and Trucks: 2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 392 crashes. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries, 27 crashes. Bikes: 0 deaths, 1 serious injury, 15 crashes, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The pattern is clear and relentless. Crashes follow the same deadly script: speeding, reckless driving, poor street design. These are preventable deaths, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, and safer crossings. They can stop blaming victims and start building streets for people, not cars.

      Res 0108-2022
      Cabán sponsors bill expanding Atlantic Ticket, boosting transit safety and equity.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to expand and lock in the Atlantic Ticket. They want Far Rockaway and more LIRR stations in the fare program. The move aims to cut commute times and boost transit use. The bill is filed, not enacted.

      Res 0108-2022 was introduced on April 14, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution urges the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to 'expand and make permanent the Atlantic Ticket pilot in order to include Far Rockaway, and other stations along the LIRR corridor, in the fare affordability program.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Yeger, Stevens, Hanif, Bottcher, Krishnan, and many others. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. The Atlantic Ticket pilot, launched in 2018, gave Southeast Queens and Brooklyn riders cheaper LIRR fares. Advocates say expanding it citywide could mean faster, safer commutes and more transit riders. The council’s action highlights the need for affordable, accessible transit—especially for neighborhoods with long, punishing commutes.

      Int 0172-2022
      Cabán sponsors bill that could slow or block open streets safety upgrades.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0172-2022 would force DOT to warn communities before changing open streets. Sixty days’ notice. Four weeks for comments. Two weeks for answers. Streets can’t shift without neighbors knowing. The bill died at session’s end. Streets stay uncertain.

      Int 0172-2022, filed by the NYC Council and handled by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to amend city law on open streets. Introduced April 14, 2022, the bill required the Department of Transportation to give 60 days’ notice to council members, boards, and local groups before any permanent open street changes. The bill’s summary reads: “notification and community input regarding designation of, removal of and changes to open streets.” Sponsors included Tiffany Cabán (primary), Crystal Hudson, Kamillah Hanks, Oswald Feliz, Farah N. Louis, Kalman Yeger, Sandy Nurse, Shahana K. Hanif, and Althea V. Stevens. The bill mandated a four-week comment window and a two-week response period, plus annual reporting and advance notice for temporary changes. The bill was filed at the end of session, leaving open streets policy unchanged.

      Res 0110-2022
      Cabán sponsors resolution to boost rush-hour subway service, improving commuter safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to run more peak-hour trains from distant subway terminals. Riders in transit deserts face long waits, missed trains, and crowded platforms. The resolution highlights gaps in service that strand working New Yorkers far from Manhattan.

      Res 0110-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution, introduced April 14, 2022, and filed at session's end on December 31, 2023, urges the MTA to 'adjust schedules for distant subway terminal lines to include more peak-direction rush hour trains for commuters to travel to central economic hubs.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Bottcher, and others. The bill responds to service gaps hitting outer boroughs, end-of-line stations, and transit deserts—areas where 600,000 New Yorkers live with poor transit access. The council points to pandemic-era staffing shortages and train cancellations that left riders stranded. The measure aims to push the MTA to close these gaps and connect more New Yorkers to jobs and opportunity.
