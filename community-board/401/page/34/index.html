      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Queens Streets Are Killing Fields—Who Will Stop the Bloodshed?

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB1

      The bodies keep coming. Fourteen dead. Twenty-one left with serious wounds. In just over three years, Queens CB1 has seen 5,229 crashes. The numbers do not bleed, but people do. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. The street does not care if you are careful. It only cares if you are in the way.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      Last week, a 39-year-old moped rider died at 23rd Avenue and 37th Street. He was ejected, helmet on, dead at the scene. The crash involved an SUV. The cause: “Traffic Control Disregarded” and “Driver Inattention/Distraction” (NYC Open Data). No one walks away from that.

      Last year, a 36-year-old woman on a bike was killed at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. A pickup truck, a parked BMW, and a demolished bicycle. The record says only: “Apparent Death.”

      The Human Cost

      A crash is not an accident. It is a system working as designed. “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us,” said Emily McKinnon after a bus crash in Queens Village (ABC7). The bus driver did nothing wrong. The street did not care.

      A 17-year-old boy died as a passenger on Astoria Boulevard. The car was speeding. The record says: “Unsafe Speed.” The boy is gone.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Some leaders have moved. Council Member Tiffany Cabán voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable for crossing the street (NYC Council – Legistar). She co-sponsored bills for more protected bike lanes and to ban parking near crosswalks. These are steps. But the pace is slow. The street is faster.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another body on the street. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted intersections, and enforcement against reckless drivers—not against people on foot or bike. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1 came from SUVs and Cars (2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 404 total incidents), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (1 serious injury, 27 total), and Bikes (1 serious injury, 15 total).
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. They follow patterns—speed, distraction, failure to yield. Policies like lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, and daylighted intersections can prevent them. The record shows: change the street, save a life.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      Local politicians can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, and clear crosswalks. They can end policies that punish people for walking or biking and focus enforcement on reckless drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Mamdani Urges Fix the MTA Bills to Prevent Cuts
      ▸

      MTA leaders warn: no new money, no full service. Peak hour cuts, longer waits. Riders and workers brace for frustration. Advocates and lawmakers push Albany to fill the gap. The board’s budget opens the door to fare hikes and more pain.

      On December 22, 2022, MTA leaders announced that without recurring state funding, service cuts are inevitable. The MTA Board approved a 2023 budget allowing fare hikes and service changes, pending Albany’s action. Chairman Janno Lieber said, "All good things can happen if people are willing to pay for them, or help figure out recurring funding sources, which is what MTA needs to achieve fiscal stability." New York City Transit President Richard Davey explained that peak hour service would be trimmed, with modest increases in wait times, while some weekend service would expand. Transit advocates and union leaders, including Richard Davis, condemned the cuts, warning of increased rider frustration and assaults on workers. Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani urged passage of bills to freeze fares and fill the MTA’s fiscal gap, stating, "We have to fix the rot at the heart of the transit system." No formal safety analyst assessment was provided, but the debate centers on the risk to riders and workers if service shrinks.
