      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Queens Streets: Counting Corpses, Not Courage

      Written July 2, 2025

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 2, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      Thirteen dead. Twenty seriously hurt. Over 2,700 injured. That’s the count in Queens CB1 since 2022. Each number is a body on the street, a family left waiting. The dead do not speak. The living remember.

      Just months ago, a cyclist was struck and killed at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She was coming home from a ride. The driver was fleeing police, tearing through the neighborhood. A witness said, “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” as reported by the NY Daily News. The chase ended. She did not come home.

      A few blocks away, a pickup slammed into a parked car. A passenger bled out in the seat. On Astoria Boulevard, a BMW turned and crushed a pedestrian. The sidewalk was quiet again by morning. The city moved on.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. In three years, they killed two people and injured nearly 400 more on these streets. Trucks and buses hurt dozens more. Motorcycles and mopeds left bodies broken. Even bikes, squeezed into traffic, added to the toll. No one is safe when the street is built for speed.

      The city counts the bodies. The city counts the crashes. But the city does not count the cost to the living.

      Leaders: Action or Excuses?

      Some leaders have stepped up. Council Member Tiffany Cabán voted to end jaywalking enforcement, shifting blame off pedestrians and onto the drivers who kill, according to NYC Council Legistar. She co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and speed up protected bike lanes. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez backed the 31st Street protected bike lane plan, calling it a way to “protect pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers while making our streets safer and less congested,” as reported by Streetsblog NYC.

      But progress crawls. Promises pile up. The street stays the same. The next crash waits around the corner.

      Call to Action: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand protected bike lanes, not just paint. Demand a 20 mph speed limit for every block. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Most injuries and deaths were caused by Cars and SUVs (2 deaths, 4 serious injuries, 394 total incidents), followed by Trucks and Buses (2 serious injuries, 29 incidents), Motorcycles and Mopeds (1 serious injury, 27 incidents), and Bikes (1 serious injury, 15 incidents).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. These are not random accidents. They are the result of street design, speed, and policy choices. Safer infrastructure and lower speeds can prevent deaths.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for protected bike lanes, daylight every crosswalk, lower speed limits, and end policies that blame pedestrians and cyclists. They can act now or answer for every life lost.

      Gianaris Opposes Incremental MTA Funding Calls for Bold Action
      ▸

      State lawmakers Mamdani and Gianaris unveiled a four-year plan to fund free MTA buses. The proposal boosts bus service, freezes fares, and expands camera enforcement. They call it urgent. The plan aims to make transit faster, cheaper, and safer for all riders.

      On December 14, 2022, Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani and State Senator Michael Gianaris introduced the Formula Three Act, a legislative proposal to overhaul MTA funding and fare policy. The plan, part of the larger Fix The MTA Package, would phase in free local and Select Bus Service, increase bus frequency by 20%, freeze fares, and expand permanent bus lane camera enforcement. Funding would rise from $200 million in 2023 to $638 million in 2026, with $488 million yearly for more buses and $300 million annually for frequent subways. Mamdani called the proposal 'urgent and necessary,' stressing that 'seniors, working class New Yorkers, people who use the bus the most are able to do so without fear of being priced out.' Gianaris said, 'incrementalism...is not working.' The lawmakers urge bold action to ensure public transit is accessible, affordable, and excellent.

      Int 0291-2022
      Cabán votes yes, boosting citywide safety with new greenway plan.
      ▸

      The Council passed Int 0291-2022, forcing city agencies to map, plan, and report on greenways. The law demands annual updates and public engagement. It aims to carve out safe, car-free corridors for walkers and cyclists. The mayor returned it unsigned.

      Int 0291-2022, now Local Law 115 of 2022, was enacted by the City Council on November 27, 2022. The bill came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, first introduced April 28, 2022. The law's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to a citywide greenway plan.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers sponsored the bill, joined by dozens of co-sponsors including Rivera, Hudson, and others. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor on October 27, 2022. The law orders the Department of Transportation and Parks to identify, map, and report on greenways, and to consult with community boards. Proposals for new greenway segments or repairs must be presented to affected communities within 60 days. The mayor returned the bill unsigned, but it became law. The measure compels the city to plan and maintain safe routes for non-drivers, with regular public reporting and accountability.

      González-Rojas Celebrates Safety-Boosting MTA Bridge Bike Access
      ▸

      Cyclists can now cross the Cross Bay Bridge. The Henry Hudson Bridge will follow after renovations. Assembly Member Gonzalez-Rojas pushed the law. Advocates pressed the MTA. Progress is slow, but real. Bike access grows. Riders still face gaps and delays.

      Assembly Member Jessica Gonzalez-Rojas sponsored the 2021 bill that forced the MTA to plan bike and pedestrian access on its bridges. On October 26, 2022, the MTA announced legal cycling on the Cross Bay Bridge, with sharrows painted on the path. The Henry Hudson Bridge will open to cyclists after a major renovation, expected by 2025. The MTA will award a contract for that work next year. Gonzalez-Rojas said, 'This is what we worked toward when...we introduced and passed the MTA Bike Access bill.' Bike New York’s Jon Orcutt called the move 'pretty good progress.' The MTA also plans to add bike parking at 37 subway stations and expand bike access near transit. The Verrazzano Bridge remains off-limits for now. The announcement marks a step forward, but cyclists still face barriers and delays.
