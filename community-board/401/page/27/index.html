      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Queens Streets Bleed—How Many More Until Leaders Act?

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB1

      The streets do not forgive. In Queens CB1, the numbers do not lie. Thirteen people are dead. Twenty more are seriously injured. Over 2,700 have been hurt since 2022. Each number is a body, a family, a life split open on the asphalt (NYC Open Data).

      The last year alone saw three deaths and five serious injuries. The victims are not just numbers. A cyclist, a child, a man behind the wheel. Some never made it home. Some never stood a chance.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      The violence does not slow. In October, a pickup fleeing police struck and killed Amanda Servedio, a cyclist returning from a ride. She “went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. The chase ran through crowded streets. The driver did not stop. Amanda died in the road.

      Her father spoke after the arrest. “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened,” he told reporters.

      Elsewhere, a 42-year-old man died at 36th Avenue and 36th Street. A 15-year-old was killed on a moped. The list grows. The pain does not fade.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      Local leaders have moved, but not fast enough. Council Member Tiffany Cabán co-sponsored the law that ended jaywalking tickets, shifting blame away from pedestrians and back to drivers (Legislation to end jaywalking tickets). She also backed bills for protected bike lanes and daylighting crosswalks. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez and others supported new protected bike lanes on 31st Street, over the loud objections of some business owners (protected bike lanes on 31st Street).

      But the streets are still deadly. Promises do not stop cars. Every delay means another family waits for a knock at the door.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted corners, and enforcement that targets reckless drivers—not the people they hit. Join Transportation Alternatives or Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1 came from cars and trucks (2 deaths, 277 minor injuries, 109 moderate injuries, 4 serious injuries, 392 total incidents). Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 27 incidents (no deaths, 18 minor injuries, 9 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries). Bikes were involved in 15 incidents (no deaths, 10 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury). See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These are not just accidents. They are the result of policy choices—speed limits, street design, and enforcement. Every delay in action means more lives lost.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, daylight crosswalks, and shift enforcement to target reckless drivers. They can back bills that put safety first and move quickly to implement them.

      Mamdani Backs Safety-Boosting Fees on Heavy SUVs
      ▸

      SUVs kill. Their bulk crushes bodies and streets. Lawmakers want heavier vehicles to pay more. Revenue would fund safer roads. The bill follows a grim rise in deaths, especially among children. The city bleeds. The council moves. The fight is on.

      Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani and Senator Andrew Gounardes introduced a bill to increase registration fees for heavy vehicles and SUVs in New York. The proposal, announced June 23, 2023, aims to direct new revenue toward street safety projects. The report behind the bill states: 'Injuries from crashes involving large vehicles increased by 91 percent and fatalities by 75 percent between 2016 and 2019.'       González-Rojas Criticizes Car-Centered Opposition to Safety Bill
      ▸

      Albany failed New York’s streets. Lawmakers blocked Sammy’s Law and other safety bills. Cyclists, pedestrians, and bus riders lost. Car culture ruled. Advocates called it a wasted session. Only transit funding and a bus pilot survived. No real progress for the vulnerable.

      In the 2023 legislative session, Albany lawmakers failed to pass major street safety bills, including Sammy’s Law (which would let New York City set its own speed limits). The session ended June 13, 2023, with most safety and transit measures stalled in committee or blocked from a vote. The matter summary reads: 'This year's legislative session in Albany was considered one of the worst for livable streets and street safety, largely due to the failure to pass key bills such as Sammy's Law.' Council members and advocates like Eric McClure, Jessica Gonzalez-Rojas, Amy Cohen, and Sara Lind voiced anger and disappointment. McClure said, 'We have to give the state legislature an 'F' for wasting an entire session without advancing legislation to make streets safer or to improve public transit.' The Assembly’s inaction left vulnerable road users exposed. Only increased MTA funding and a free bus pilot moved forward. The rest—protection for cyclists, pedestrians, and bus riders—died in committee.

      Int 1063-2023
      Cabán co-sponsors bill for EV charging info app, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to create a city app showing electric vehicle charging stations. The app would map locations, show voltage, connector types, and real-time status. It would include e-bike charging info. Strict privacy rules would limit data collection and retention.

      Bill Int 1063-2023 was introduced on June 8, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill called for the Department of Information Technology and Telecommunications to build a mobile app displaying all public electric vehicle charging stations in New York City. The app would show locations, voltage, charging level, connector types, e-bike compatibility, and real-time availability. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the creation of a mobile application that provides information about electric vehicle charging stations.' Council Member Keith Powers sponsored the bill, joined by Avilés, Cabán, Farías, Riley, Richardson Jordan, Ossé, Ung, Hanif, Restler, and Gutiérrez. The bill set strict privacy: no device data, no tracking, no forced consent. The bill was filed at session’s end, not enacted.

      Int 1071-2023
      Cabán sponsors bill speeding up removal of hazardous utility poles, boosting safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force quick removal of abandoned or dangerous utility poles and wires. Owners would have sixty days to clear junk, or act at once if hazards loom. The bill died in committee. Streets stay cluttered. Danger lingers.

      Int 1071-2023 was introduced on June 8, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to require owners to remove disused or abandoned utility poles, wires, and appurtenances within 60 days, and to act immediately if conditions were dangerous. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the timelines for the removal of abandoned or unsafe utility poles, wires, and appurtenances, and the transfer of appurtenances to newly erected poles.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán sponsored the bill, joined by Kevin C. Riley, Jennifer Gutiérrez, Lincoln Restler, Kristin Richardson Jordan, Amanda Farías, and Robert F. Holden. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The bill’s failure leaves hazardous poles and wires on city streets, a threat to those on foot and bike.
