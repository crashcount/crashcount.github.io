      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Queens CB1?

      Blood on the Crosswalk: Queens Kids and Cyclists Pay While City Stalls

      Written June 25, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations.)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB1

      Three dead. Five maimed. In the last year, the streets of Astoria and its neighbors have not forgiven the unwary. A cyclist, Amanda Servedio, was thrown from her bike and killed by a fleeing driver at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She was coming home from a ride. She never made it. A witness said, “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.” (NY Daily News)

      A seven-year-old girl, walking outside her school, was struck by an unlicensed driver. Her femur snapped. Her head hit the pavement. Two others fell with her. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment and driving without a license. (New York Post)

      In the last twelve months: 3 killed, 5 seriously injured, 795 hurt. The dead include a child. The wounded are young and old. The numbers do not heal. They do not end.

      Who Pays, Who Acts

      Cars and trucks did the worst. They killed 2, injured 385. Motorcycles and mopeds left 27 hurt. Bikes, 15. The sidewalk is no shield. The crosswalk is no promise.

      Local leaders have not been silent. Council Member Tiffany Cabán has called for safer streets after each death. She co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, to daylight corners, to legalize crossing mid-block, to demand protected bike lanes. She voted to end jaywalking tickets, saying the law should not be used to blame the dead or punish the poor (Gothamist).

      But the city moves slow. Bills sit in committee. Promises pile up. The blood dries. The next crash comes.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. The city can slow cars. It can build barriers. It can end the killing. But it will not act unless you force it. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real daylighting. Demand protected bike lanes. Do not wait for another child’s name on the street.

      Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Cars and Trucks killed 2 and injured 385. Motorcycles and Mopeds injured 27. Bikes injured 15. These numbers come from city crash data. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. These deaths and injuries are preventable. They happen because of choices—speed, street design, lack of enforcement—not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, daylighted intersections, and real accountability for reckless drivers. They can refuse to delay or water down safety bills.

      Res 0071-2022
      Cabán co-sponsors multilingual transit announcements, boosting public safety for non-English speakers.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to speak to the city. Four languages, not just English. Riders lost in translation miss warnings, miss stops, miss safety. The bill died in committee. The silence continues. Millions still ride in the dark.

      Resolution 0071-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on March 10, 2022. The resolution urged the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make bus and subway announcements in the four most commonly spoken languages in New York City. The official title: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make bus and subway announcements in the four most commonly spoken languages in New York City.' Council Members Shahana K. Hanif (primary sponsor), Sandra Ung, Crystal Hudson, Julie Won, and Tiffany Cabán backed the measure. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided, but the intent was clear: break the language barrier, reach millions of riders, and give all New Yorkers equal warning and access on transit. The MTA still makes most announcements in English only.

The bill required the Department of Transportation, with other agencies, to file annual reports on package delivery volumes, traffic, and environmental impacts. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring a report on package deliveries.' Council Member Keith Powers sponsored the bill, joined by Selvena N. Brooks-Powers, Gale A. Brewer, Tiffany Cabán, and Public Advocate Jumaane Williams.
