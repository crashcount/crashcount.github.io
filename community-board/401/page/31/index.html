      Queens Streets Bleed—How Many More Until Leaders Act?

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Queens CB1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Toll in Queens CB1

      The streets do not forgive. In Queens CB1, the numbers do not lie. Thirteen people are dead. Twenty more are seriously injured. Over 2,700 have been hurt since 2022. Each number is a body, a family, a life split open on the asphalt (NYC Open Data).

      The last year alone saw three deaths and five serious injuries. The victims are not just numbers. A cyclist, a child, a man behind the wheel. Some never made it home. Some never stood a chance.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      The violence does not slow. In October, a pickup fleeing police struck and killed Amanda Servedio, a cyclist returning from a ride. She “went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. The chase ran through crowded streets. The driver did not stop. Amanda died in the road.

      Her father spoke after the arrest. “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened,” he told reporters.

      Elsewhere, a 42-year-old man died at 36th Avenue and 36th Street. A 15-year-old was killed on a moped. The list grows. The pain does not fade.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      Local leaders have moved, but not fast enough. Council Member Tiffany Cabán co-sponsored the law that ended jaywalking tickets, shifting blame away from pedestrians and back to drivers (Legislation to end jaywalking tickets). She also backed bills for protected bike lanes and daylighting crosswalks. State Senator Kristen Gonzalez and others supported new protected bike lanes on 31st Street, over the loud objections of some business owners (protected bike lanes on 31st Street).

      But the streets are still deadly. Promises do not stop cars. Every delay means another family waits for a knock at the door.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted corners, and enforcement that targets reckless drivers—not the people they hit. Join Transportation Alternatives or Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Queens CB1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22, assembly district AD 36 and state senate district SD 59.
      ▸ Which areas are in Queens CB1?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, Sunnyside Yards (North), St. Michael’S Cemetery, and Astoria Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Queens CB1 came from cars and trucks (2 deaths, 277 minor injuries, 109 moderate injuries, 4 serious injuries, 392 total incidents). Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 27 incidents (no deaths, 18 minor injuries, 9 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries). Bikes were involved in 15 incidents (no deaths, 10 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury). See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These are not just accidents. They are the result of policy choices—speed limits, street design, and enforcement. Every delay in action means more lives lost.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, daylight crosswalks, and shift enforcement to target reckless drivers. They can back bills that put safety first and move quickly to implement them.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Cabán co-sponsors annual bicycle study bill, boosting citywide street safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force DOT to study bike traffic each year. The law would have mapped busy bike routes, flagged gaps in protection, and pushed safety fixes. The session ended. The bill died. Cyclists wait. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0926-2023, introduced on February 16, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to require the Department of Transportation to conduct and publish an annual study on bicycle activity. Brooks-Powers (primary sponsor), Lincoln Restler, Tiffany Cabán, Crystal Hudson, Kevin C. Riley, and Lynn C. Schulman sponsored the measure. The bill would have identified the most biked streets and bridges, noted which lacked protected lanes, and demanded safety recommendations. The council filed the bill at session’s end on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The bill’s failure leaves cyclists exposed, with no citywide data push to guide urgent fixes.

      Int 0923-2023
      Cabán co-sponsors bill to study last-mile delivery traffic impacts.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force a city study on truck and delivery traffic from last mile warehouses. The bill targets congestion, collisions, and harm to neighborhoods. It demands hard numbers on vehicle flow, street damage, and danger to people outside cars.

      Int 0923-2023 was introduced on February 16, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, sponsored by Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and Council Member Alexa Avilés, with over thirty co-sponsors, sought a city study on the impact of truck and delivery traffic from last mile facilities. The official summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to conducting a study of the impact that truck and delivery traffic generated by last mile facilities have on local communities and infrastructure.' The bill required the Department of Transportation to report on delivery vehicle volumes, parking, congestion, collisions, and pedestrian injuries near these hubs. It called for identifying the most affected streets and estimating the costs and possible fixes. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, without passage.

      A 3180
      Gonzalez-Rojas co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 3180 demands every state- or federally-funded road project weigh complete street design. It pushes agencies to publish clear guidance. The bill aims to force planners to see people, not just cars. Streets must serve all. No more blind spots.

      Assembly Bill A 3180 was introduced on February 3, 2023, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in committee, awaiting further action. Its matter title reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding.' Didi Barrett leads as primary sponsor, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Amy Paulin, Carrie Woerner, and many others as co-sponsors. The bill compels agencies to consider complete street design—meaning streets built for pedestrians, cyclists, and transit users, not just drivers. It also requires the department to publish guidance and reference materials for public use. This measure, if enforced, could reshape how New York builds and rebuilds its roads, putting vulnerable road users at the center of every project.

      Mamdani co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 3180 demands every state- or federally-funded road project weigh complete street design. It pushes agencies to publish clear guidance. The bill aims to force planners to see people, not just cars. Streets must serve all. No more blind spots.

      Assembly Bill A 3180 was introduced on February 3, 2023, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in committee, awaiting further action. Its matter title reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding.' Didi Barrett leads as primary sponsor, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Amy Paulin, Carrie Woerner, and many others as co-sponsors. The bill compels agencies to consider complete street design—meaning streets built for pedestrians, cyclists, and transit users, not just drivers. It also requires the department to publish guidance and reference materials for public use. This measure, if enforced, could reshape how New York builds and rebuilds its roads, putting vulnerable road users at the center of every project.
