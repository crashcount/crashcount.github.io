      Speed Kills in Harlem. City Hall Lets It Happen.

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 6, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed citation formatting, and deduped citations.)

      Manhattan CB10: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Harlem: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      A child’s shoe at the curb. A mother’s hand, empty. In the last twelve months, two people died on the streets of Manhattan CB10. Nine more suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In all, 426 people were hurt in 831 crashes—each one a story cut short, a body broken, a family changed forever.

      A three-year-old girl was killed crossing with the signal at Lenox and 135th. Her mother was hit too. The SUV kept turning. The numbers do not soften: children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians pay the price, as shown by NYC Open Data.

      Local Leaders: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      The city has tools. It does not always use them. Albany passed Sammy’s Law, letting New York City lower speed limits to 20 mph. The Council could act now. They have not. Speed cameras, proven to save lives, face renewal fights in Albany. Each delay is measured in blood.

      After Devon Hughley was killed by a fleeing driver, his sister said the arrest brought “some peace,” as reported by the NY Daily News. But peace is not safety. Memorial posters fade. The danger remains.

      The Pattern: Who Pays, Who Waits

      SUVs, sedans, and trucks cause most of the harm. In three years, cars and trucks killed one person and seriously injured five more. Motorcycles and mopeds left four with moderate injuries. Bikes caused six moderate injuries. The streets are not safe for the old, the young, or anyone moving under their own power.

      Each crash is called an accident. But the pattern is clear. Speed, size, and inaction kill.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Demand streets that put people first.

      Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home. The time for action is now.

      Take action today.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Manhattan CB10 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 9, assembly district AD 70 and state senate district SD 30.
      ▸ Which areas are in Manhattan CB10?
      It includes the Harlem (South) and Harlem (North) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council District District 9, Assembly Districts AD 70 and AD 71, and State Senate District SD 30.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Manhattan CB10?
      Cars and Trucks: 1 death, 85 moderate injuries, 5 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 4 moderate injuries, no deaths or serious injuries. Bikes: 6 moderate injuries, no deaths or serious injuries, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. The pattern is clear: speed, vehicle size, and street design drive the harm. These deaths and injuries are preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, expand and protect speed camera programs, and redesign streets to protect people walking and cycling.
