      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bronx CB26?

      Blood on the Asphalt: Bronx Leaders Stall While Pedestrians Die

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Bronx CB26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Bone

      A woman, age 64, died on the Major Deegan Expressway. She was walking. A sedan hit her. The crash broke her body. The street stayed open. NYC Open Data

      In the last twelve months, eighty-four people were injured in crashes across Bronx CB26. Two were hurt so badly their lives will never be the same. No one calls it a massacre. But it is a steady bleed. NYC Open Data

      Children are not spared. Eight kids were hurt in the past year. No deaths. Not this time. NYC Open Data

      The Machines That Maim

      Cars and SUVs did the killing. One death. Four serious injuries. No bikes. No trucks. No mopeds. Just cars, over and over. NYC Open Data

      On Jerome Avenue, a sedan struck a man at the intersection. He survived, but his head bled. The record says “unsafe speed.” The street stays the same. NYC Open Data

      Leadership: Words, Not Deeds

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They say every life matters. They point to new speed limits and more cameras. But in Bronx CB26, the numbers do not move fast enough. One death. Dozens of broken bodies. The council and the mayor have the power to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. They have not done it. NYC Open Data

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Streets can be changed. Speeds can be lowered. Cameras can be kept on. But only if you demand it.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to use the power they have. Lower the speed. Harden the streets. Protect the people who walk.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Act now.

      Int 0746-2024
      Dinowitz votes yes, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety with greener medians.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to turn bare medians green. The city must plant trees or use medians for stormwater. Agencies must report progress. The law targets high-risk areas. Votes showed strong support. The mayor returned it unsigned. Streets will change.

      Int 0746-2024, now Local Law 94 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 12, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the improvement and maintenance of medians through the planting of vegetation or use in stormwater management," requires the city to improve at least one mile of paved medians every two years until 2046. The Department of Transportation, Parks, and Environmental Protection must add planted medians, tree beds, or stormwater infrastructure, focusing on high-priority investment areas. Council Member Shekar Krishnan sponsored the bill, joined by Feliz, Avilés, Nurse, Banks, Restler, Ung, Schulman, Holden, Hudson, and Yeger. The Council passed the bill with broad support, though the mayor returned it unsigned. The law mandates annual progress reports and public tracking of improved medians and responsible agencies.

      Res 0449-2024
      Dinowitz sponsors resolution to raise overnight commercial parking fines, no direct safety impact.
      ▸

      Council calls for higher fines on commercial vehicles parked overnight. Current caps let companies treat tickets as business costs. Streets fill with trucks. Residents lose space. The city wants power to set real deterrents. Albany holds the key.

      Resolution 0449-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced June 6, 2024, it urges the State Legislature to let cities raise the cap on commercial overnight parking fines. The matter title reads: "Resolution calling on the State legislature to increase the cap on commercial overnight fines for cities." Council Members Eric Dinowitz (primary sponsor), Vickie Paladino, and Robert F. Holden back the measure. They argue that capped fines are too low—companies absorb them, streets clog with trucks, and residents pay the price. The Council wants authority to set fines that actually deter illegal parking.       Dinowitz Supports Safety Boosting Citywide Red Light Cameras
      ▸

      Red-light running kills. Twenty-nine dead last year. Highest ever. Reckless drivers surge post-pandemic. City report demands more cameras—jump from 150 to 1,325 intersections. Officials back the bill. Victims’ families demand action. Cameras cut crashes. But the most dangerous drivers remain loose.

      On March 20, 2024, the Department of Transportation released a report urging passage of a bill to expand New York City’s red-light camera program. The bill, supported by DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez and sponsored by State Senator Andrew Goundardes, would boost camera coverage from 150 to 1,325 intersections—about 10% of the city’s 13,700 signals. The report states: “Expanding the number of intersections with red light cameras... could substantially enhance the deterrent effect of the program and return New York City to the consistently downward trend of red light-running behavior we had seen prior to the pandemic.” Assembly Member Jeffrey Dinowitz also supports expansion, calling for cameras at every intersection. The DOT notes that cameras have slashed T-bone crashes by 65% and rear-end collisions by 49% at monitored sites, but the statutory cap blocks broader safety gains. Relatives of crash victims joined the call, demanding the city confront driver negligence and protect the community.

      Int 0606-2024
      Dinowitz co-sponsors e-bike and scooter registration bill, reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council wants plates on every e-bike and scooter. The bill sits in committee. Riders face new rules. Lawmakers push for registration. Streets wait. No change yet. The city debates control. Vulnerable users watch. The system holds its breath.

      Int 0606-2024 was introduced on March 7, 2024, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered," would force all e-bikes, e-scooters, and similar vehicles not already covered by DMV rules to register with the Department of Transportation and display a visible plate. Council Member Inna Vernikov led as primary sponsor, joined by Holden, Farías, Riley, Brannan, and many others. The bill was laid over in committee on December 11, 2024. The measure aims to track these vehicles, but its impact on vulnerable road users remains unassessed. The city’s debate continues, with no law yet in force.
