      Brooklyn Bleeds While City Sleeps: Demand Action on Deadly Streets

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Brooklyn CB11: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      One death. Eight serious injuries. Over 600 wounded. In the last year, Brooklyn CB11—Bensonhurst, Bath Beach, Gravesend—has seen the slow grind of traffic violence. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, children, elders. A 75-year-old woman did not make it home. Seventy children were hurt. The street does not care if you are young or old. It only takes.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      The stories do not stop. A mother and her two daughters were killed on a Brooklyn sidewalk. The car that hit them had racked up over 93 violations—20 for speeding, five for running red lights. The driver’s license was suspended, but the car kept moving. The city did not stop her. The law did not stop her. The family is gone. Dashcam footage of the Saturday afternoon crash shows the overturned Audi mowing down the family as they crossed the road, with several others injured in the crash.

      A few weeks later, a man slipped into an MTA bus depot, took a bus, and crashed it on Cropsey Avenue. No one died that day. But the question hangs: How many more lapses before luck runs out? The suspect was somehow able to drive off the lot with the operator on board, making it down the block to 25th and Cropsey avenues, where he crashed into a parked car, police said.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has tools. Speed cameras. Lower speed limits. Laws that could keep reckless drivers off the road. But tools unused are as good as none. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program—meant to force repeat offenders into safety courses—has lapsed. The city can now set speed limits as low as 20 mph, but has not done so citywide. The cameras that catch speeders may go dark if Albany does not act. Each delay is another risk. Each silence is another wound.

      What Comes Next: Demand Action

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand the return of the Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program. Demand that speed cameras stay on. Do not wait for another mother, another child, another neighbor. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn CB11 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 43, assembly district AD 47 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in Brooklyn CB11?
      It includes the Bensonhurst, Bath Beach, and Gravesend (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38, District 43, District 44, District 47, and District 50, Assembly Districts AD 46, AD 47, and AD 49, and State Senate Districts SD 17 and SD 23.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn CB11?
      Cars and SUVs: 3 deaths, 99 moderate injuries, 5 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 4 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 5 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries. Bikes: 1 death, 6 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—repeat offenders, ignored violations, failed enforcement. These are preventable crashes, not random accidents.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, restore the Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program, keep speed cameras running, and redesign streets for safety. Delay costs lives.
