      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB14?

      Brooklyn CB14: Crosswalk deaths, truck turns, and a clock that won’t stop

      Written August 25, 2025 (Revised August 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB14: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      Flatbush, Ditmas Park, Midwood. The map calls it Brooklyn CB14. The street tells another story.

      The corners that don’t forgive

      Flatbush Avenue leads the harm. One death. 164 injuries. Seven serious injuries since 2022, the most in the district. See the city ledger for Flatbush Avenue. Ocean Avenue follows with 126 injuries.

      An SUV turned right at Avenue L and East 12th. A 73‑year‑old woman crossing with the signal was hit and killed. The city log lists the point of impact as the center front end. The driver was licensed. The year was 2019 for the Jeep. The date was Dec. 1, 2024.

      At Bedford and Tilden, a dump truck made a right turn. Failure to yield, oversized vehicle, the record says. A 58‑year‑old woman crossing with the signal was struck and died. That was Mar. 7, 2024.

      A cyclist, 47, was hit head‑on by an SUV near 1608 Avenue O. He was ejected and killed on Mar. 13, 2024.

      When the hours turn deadly

      The danger spikes after school and into the commute. The 5 p.m. hour brought two deaths and 192 injuries. At 6 p.m., three deaths and 147 injuries. At 2 p.m., three deaths and 173 injuries. The city’s own clock says so in the hourly distribution.

      Since 2022, nine pedestrians and two cyclists are dead here. Pedestrians suffered 609 injuries and 15 serious injuries. Cyclists: 292 injuries, five serious. The roll‑up shows SUVs and cars as the main killers of people on foot—six pedestrian deaths—followed by trucks and buses with three. The counts are in the district totals.

      What keeps breaking bones

      “Failure to yield right‑of‑way.” “Disregarded traffic control.” “Inattention.” The forms use dry words. The bodies don’t. The largest bucket is “other,” with 15 deaths and 1,039 injuries attached to it. Inattention and distraction show 48 injuries and four serious injuries. The sheet is here: contributing factors.

      One Audi sedan struck a 64‑year‑old man not in a crosswalk on Flatbush near Caton. Apparent death at the scene, the entry says. That was Dec. 31, 2024.

      Promises and a thin fuse

      Council Member Farah N. Louis signed onto a bill to force DOT to install school‑zone safety devices within 60 days after a study says they’re needed. The measure was introduced and sent to committee on Aug. 14, 2025. The file is Int 1353‑2025.

      On the same day, she cosponsored a crackdown on unlicensed commuter vans, ordering maximum penalties for each violation on a checklist. That is Int 1347‑2025.

      Up in Albany, State Sen. Kevin Parker voted yes in committee to require intelligent speed assistance for repeat speeders under S 4045. The bill targets drivers who rack up points or multiple camera tickets. Committee votes passed on June 11 and June 12, 2025.

      Fix what we can see

      The harm is concentrated. Two streets—Flatbush Avenue and Ocean Avenue—carry the worst numbers.

      Local fixes are not theory. Daylight the corners so turning drivers can see people crossing. Harden the turns at right‑turn hot spots like Bedford and Tilden. Add leading pedestrian intervals on signalized crossings and keep trucks out of crosswalks while people walk. These are standard tools for right‑turn deaths and failure‑to‑yield cases.

      Citywide steps that end the cycle

      The city now has the power to set safer speeds. Advocates want a lower default limit and action against the worst repeat speeders. Albany renewed 24‑hour school‑zone cameras and advanced speed limiters for habitual violators. Use the tools.

      • Lower the default limit citywide.
      • Pass and enforce intelligent speed assistance for repeat speeders, as pushed in S 4045.

      Lives hinge on the follow‑through. If you want change, push for it. Act now: take action.

      ▸ Where are the worst crash locations in Brooklyn CB14?
      Flatbush Avenue leads with one death and 164 injuries, including seven serious injuries. Ocean Avenue shows 126 injuries. Source: NYC Open Data crash records.

      The 2–3 p.m., 5–6 p.m., and 6–7 p.m. hours show the heaviest harm, with multiple deaths and the largest injury counts. Source: NYC Open Data hourly distribution.
      ▸ Who faces the most risk on these streets?
      People walking and biking. Since 2022, nine pedestrians and two cyclists have been killed, with 609 pedestrian injuries and 292 cyclist injuries recorded. Source: NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What can be done now at the local level?
      Daylight corners, harden right turns at known danger spots, and add leading pedestrian intervals. Focus on Flatbush and Ocean, where injuries cluster.

      Judge Halts Bedford Ave Bike Lane Removal
      ▸

      A judge stopped the city from tearing out Bedford Avenue’s protected bike lane. The lane shields cyclists from traffic. City Hall wanted it gone. Advocates sued. The court order keeps the barrier. Streets stay safer, for now.

      On June 18, 2025, NY Daily News reported that Judge Carolyn Walker-Diallo issued a temporary restraining order blocking the Adams administration from removing the protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue in Brooklyn. The city planned to restore the old painted lane, citing complaints about fast bikes and e-bikes. But advocates and residents sued, arguing the old design was unsafe and that 'safety of all road users has improved since the upgraded design was installed.' Transportation Alternatives called the ruling a win for street safety, saying, 'We won't let anyone make our streets more dangerous.' The case highlights ongoing tension between street safety improvements and city policy decisions.

      Mayor Removes Bedford Avenue Bike Lane
      ▸

      Mayor Adams ripped out Bedford Avenue’s protected bike lane. Cyclists now dodge cars, buses, and chaos. City ignored safer designs. Riders left exposed. Anger boils. The street grows more dangerous. The city shrugs. Blood waits on the asphalt.

      Streetsblog NYC reported on June 17, 2025, that Mayor Adams ordered the removal of the protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue after complaints from some residents. Cyclists called the move "unwise and unsafe." The article notes the city had previously identified this stretch as among the most dangerous. Officials responded to concerns by painting pedestrian islands and loading zones but did not install physical barriers or bus boarding islands. Cyclists described the decision as political, with one saying, "There’s a very important voting bloc that’s using their leverage over the mayor to remove the bike lane." The mayor’s refusal to sacrifice parking for safety left vulnerable road users exposed. The city’s Streets Master Plan, which mandates more protected lanes, remains unmet.

      S 7785
      Hermel votes yes to weaken bus rules, increasing pedestrian and cyclist risk.
      ▸

      Senate passed S 7785. The bill carves out large Mitchell-Lama housing from bus traffic rules. Lawmakers voted yes. The carve-out weakens enforcement. Streets grow less safe for people on foot and bike.

      Bill S 7785, titled 'Relates to bus operation-related traffic regulations,' passed the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. The measure, sponsored by Senator Jamaal Bailey, excludes violations in cooperative housing developments with at least 10,000 Mitchell-Lama units from bus operation traffic regulations. The bill advanced through committee and received broad support in both chambers. By exempting these large complexes, the law weakens traffic enforcement where thousands live. This move increases risk to pedestrians and cyclists, stripping away protections that save lives. Vulnerable road users lose another layer of safety in dense city streets.

      City Removes Bedford Avenue Bike Lane
      ▸

      Three blocks of Bedford Avenue lose their shield. Paint replaces protection. Crashes haunt the stretch. Children struck crossing. Officials cite community outcry. Critics warn of more danger. The city strips safety from a deadly road.

      amNY reported on June 13, 2025, that Mayor Eric Adams and the Department of Transportation will remove three blocks of the protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue, replacing it with a painted lane. The change follows 'months of pushback from local leaders' after several crashes, including incidents where children were struck crossing the lane. Adams said the redesign aims to 'better reflect community feedback.' Council Member Lincoln Restler criticized the move, saying, 'the Mayor is making a purely political decision to rip out a bike lane with no alternative.' Data shows nearly 700 injuries and four pedestrian deaths along this stretch between 2016 and 2020. The decision highlights ongoing tension between street safety measures and local opposition, with vulnerable road users at continued risk.

      S 6815
      Hermel votes yes to exempt some employees from bus lane rules.
      ▸

      Senate and Assembly clear S 6815. MTA workers get a pass for driving in bus lanes while on duty. Law shields agency vehicles from tickets. Streets grow more crowded. Vulnerable users face more risk.

      Bill S 6815, titled 'Relates to bus lane restrictions in New York city,' passed the Senate on June 12, 2025, and the Assembly on June 13, 2025. The bill states, 'it shall be a defense to any prosecution for a violation of a bus lane restriction ... when an employee of the metropolitan transportation authority is performing authorized duties.' Sponsored by Senator Leroy Comrie and co-sponsored by Nathalia Fernandez, the measure exempts MTA employees from bus lane tickets while working. No safety analysis was provided. The bill opens bus lanes to more agency vehicles, crowding space meant for buses, cyclists, and pedestrians.
