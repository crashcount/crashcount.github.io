      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn CB14?

      Brooklyn CB14: Crosswalks, Corners, and the Clock That Won’t Stop

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn CB14: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Flatbush, Ditmas Park, Parkville, Midwood. Streets wide as runways. Curbs chewed by tires. Since 2022, this board has logged 3,569 crashes, 2,501 injuries, and 13 dead. Pedestrians carry the worst of it: 9 walkers killed, 609 hurt. SUVs and cars do most of the damage. Trucks and buses add weight to the body count. The hours between 5 p.m. and 7 p.m. run hot.

      • Pedestrians killed: 9; cyclists: 2; vehicle occupants: 2. SUVs/cars were tied to most pedestrian injuries; trucks and buses killed, too. Data.

      Hylan Avenue is not here. But the pattern is. The sirens are the same.

      Where the street bites back

      Flatbush Avenue leads the pain. One death. 164 injuries. Seven serious. Flatbush Avenue keeps filling the log. Ocean Avenue follows with 126 injuries. Ocean Avenue does not forgive.

      At Avenue L and E 12th, a Jeep turned right and a 73‑year‑old woman died in the crosswalk. The record says she was crossing with the signal. The SUV struck her with the center front end. She died there. Open Data: CrashID 4775450.

      At Tilden and Bedford, a dump truck made a right. Failure to yield is on the sheet. Oversized vehicle is on the sheet. A 58‑year‑old woman crossing with the signal was hit. She died. Open Data: CrashID 4707890.

      A 47‑year‑old man on a bike was struck near Avenue O. He was ejected and killed. An SUV was going straight. Open Data: CrashID 4709603.

      The clock’s worst hours

      The counts spike in daylight and dusk. From 5 p.m. to 7 p.m., five people died and 506 were hurt. At 2 p.m., three more deaths. Lunchtime still draws blood. Hourly distribution.

      “Speed kills” is not a slogan here. It’s the roll call. Citywide, a driver with 29 camera tickets ran a red and killed a mother and two daughters in Midwood, blocks from here. The numbers on repeat offenders are stark: 1.5% of drivers cause 21% of pedestrian deaths. 30+ tickets multiplies risk fifty‑fold. Streetsblog and Daily News.

      Heavy vehicles, heavy losses

      Trucks turn and people die. The dump truck at Tilden and Bedford. The right‑turning SUVs at corners across Midwood. In this board, trucks and buses killed three pedestrians; SUVs/cars killed six. Pedestrian injury causes.

      The sheets list “failure to yield.” They list “oversized vehicle.” They list “inattention.” The names are missing. The injuries are not.

      What officials did — and didn’t

      Council Member Farah N. Louis signed onto a bill to force the city to install school‑zone safety devices within 60 days of a DOT study. Council file Int 1353-2025. She also co‑sponsored a crackdown on unlicensed commuter vans. Int 1347-2025.

      In Albany, State Sen. Kevin Parker voted yes in committee for S 4045, a bill to require intelligent speed limiters for repeat speeders. Open States: S 4045. The bill targets patterns the cameras already see.

      Concrete fixes, now

      • Daylight every corner on Flatbush and Ocean. Harden the turns that killed at Tilden and Bedford. Give walkers the head start at signals. Open Data crashes.
      • Keep trucks off tight turns during peak. Enforce failure‑to‑yield at the worst hours: 5–7 p.m. Hourly distribution.
      • Build the protected bike links that advocates keep asking for on nearby corridors like Empire Boulevard. The board next door called for it. DOT has not moved. Streetsblog.

      Citywide levers that save lives

      • Lower the speed limit. Albany passed a law giving the city the power. The city hasn’t used it citywide. Take Action.
      • Stop super speeders. Back S 4045’s mandate for speed limiters on cars tied to repeat violations. Parker voted yes in committee. Open States: S 4045.

      One more hour. One more turn. The names will come later. The corners already know.

      —

      Want this to stop? Do one thing today. Tell City Hall to drop the default speed to 20 mph and back speed limiters for repeat offenders. Act now.

      A 8936 Parker votes yes to require safer complete street designs, improving safety.
      ▸

      Jun 1 - Albany passed A 8936. Cities get more state cash if they build complete streets. Lawmakers want safer roads. The bill sailed through both chambers. Money now follows safety.

      Bill A 8936, titled 'Relates to complete street design features and funding of construction and improvements at a municipalities' expense,' passed the Assembly on May 23, 2022, and the Senate on June 1, 2022. The bill boosts state funding for transportation projects when municipalities add complete street features. Assemblymember Fahy led as primary sponsor, joined by Hunter, Seawright, Woerner, and others. The Assembly and Senate both voted yes, with broad support. The law aims to push cities to design streets for everyone—pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers—by tying state dollars to safety upgrades.
