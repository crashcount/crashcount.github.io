      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Two Dead in Chinatown. Who’s Next While City Hall Waits?

      Written July 21, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 21, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      In the last year, 266 people died on New York City streets. Over 51,000 were injured. Seven hundred twenty-seven suffered injuries so severe they will never be the same. These are not numbers. They are lives cut short, bodies broken, families left to mourn.

      Just days ago, a cyclist and a pedestrian were killed at Bowery and Canal. A stolen car, driven fast, jumped the curb. Kevin Cruickshank was out for a morning ride. May Kwok was sitting on a bench. Both are gone. Police said, “It appears to be excessive speed. Two people are in custody, unfortunate incident. No one should lose their life in that manner.” police said

      This is not rare. It is routine.

      The Faces Behind the Numbers

      A 23-year-old driver, unlicensed, was out on the street after a previous hit-and-run. Three months before the Chinatown crash, she “clipped a woman stepping out of an Uber in Brooklyn, causing serious injuries, and fled.” She was released without bail. released without bail

      On McGuinness Boulevard, a cyclist was struck where the city left a painted lane instead of protection. The road diet was promised, then watered down. “The fact is that this wouldn’t be a problem if Mayor Adams had just done what he had promised to do, which was, make all of McGuinness safe. The road diet works where it’s been installed and it’s needed for the entire corridor before this happens again,” said Kevin LaCherra. said Kevin LaCherra

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      Leaders have tools. They do not always use them. Sammy’s Law lets the city set its own speed limits. The Council could drop the default to 20 mph. They have not. Speed cameras are renewed, but the most dangerous drivers—those with dozens of violations—still keep their keys. Promises to redesign streets stall or shrink. Each delay is another risk, another life lost.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your Council Member. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand real street redesigns, not watered-down paint. Demand that repeat dangerous drivers are taken off the road. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      From 2022 to July 2025, cars and trucks—including sedans, SUVs, taxis, vans, and trucks/buses—were responsible for the vast majority of pedestrian injuries and deaths: over 31,000 incidents. Motorcycles and mopeds accounted for about 1,380. Bikes were involved in 1,497. The greatest harm comes from cars and trucks. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns: speeding, unlicensed drivers, repeat offenders, and streets designed for speed over safety. These deaths are preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits citywide, redesign dangerous streets, and take repeat dangerous drivers off the road. They can act now, or more people will die.
      ▸ What recent steps have NYC leaders taken on traffic safety?
      Leaders renewed speed cameras and have the power to lower speed limits under Sammy’s Law. But the default citywide speed is still 25 mph, and many promised street redesigns are delayed or watered down.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in NYC traffic since 2022?
      From 2022 to July 2025, 1,063 people died and 2,400 suffered serious injuries in NYC traffic crashes.
