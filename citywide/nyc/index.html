      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Asphalt: New York’s Leaders Stall, Lives Shatter

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Blood

      The city keeps moving. The bodies keep falling. In the last twelve months, 259 people died on New York City streets. 733 were left with serious injuries. Each number is a name, a family, a life cut short or broken. In the same year, more than 51,000 were hurt. The city’s roads do not forgive.

      Just days ago, a driver in the Bronx failed a left turn and plowed into six people, sending them to the hospital. The driver ran. The sidewalk was not safe. “Cops are looking for a man who rammed into six people after botching a turn at an intersection in the Bronx,” the report said. The victims survived. This time.

      In Brooklyn, a young woman sat on a curb. Her boyfriend spun his car in circles. The car lost control. She died. “He lost control when he was doing donuts with the vehicle,” he told police. The tire marks stayed for days. The loss will last longer.

      Who Pays the Price

      Older adults and children are not spared. In the past year, 25 people over 75 died. Eleven children under 18 died. The city’s most vulnerable are the first to bleed. Cars and SUVs do most of the killing—311 deaths since 2022. Trucks and buses, another 95. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. The street is a gauntlet.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has started to act. Speed cameras now watch day and night. The law lets the city lower speed limits. But the default is still too high. Leaders talk about Vision Zero. The dead keep coming. The city can set a 20 mph limit now. It has not. The cameras catch the reckless, but the worst repeat offenders keep driving. The council and the mayor have the power. They need to use it.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand real consequences for repeat speeders. Every day of delay is another life at risk. The city will not change unless you force it.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Since 2022, cars and SUVs caused 311 deaths and over 27,000 injuries. Trucks and buses caused 95 deaths and over 2,300 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 10 deaths and over 1,100 injuries. Bikes caused 11 deaths and nearly 1,500 injuries. Cars and trucks do most of the killing.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speed, reckless driving, and unsafe streets are the cause. Lowering speed limits and enforcing the law saves lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the citywide speed limit to 20 mph, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws to keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road. They can redesign streets to protect people, not cars.
      ▸ What has the city done lately to improve street safety?
      The city renewed 24/7 speed cameras, started lowering speed limits in some zones, and began redesigning dangerous streets. But the default speed limit is still too high, and repeat offenders keep driving.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously hurt in NYC traffic crashes recently?
      In the last 12 months, 259 people died and 733 were seriously injured on New York City streets.
      ▸ Who is most at risk in NYC traffic crashes?
      Older adults and children are especially at risk. In the last year, 25 people over 75 and 11 children under 18 died in crashes.
