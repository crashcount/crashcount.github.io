      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Streets of Sorrow: New York’s Leaders Let the Killing Continue

      Written August 5, 2025 (Revised August 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 5, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      Just last week, a 23-year-old man was killed by a speeding sedan on 101st Street. He was not at an intersection. He did not make it home. The city recorded 281 deaths and 766 serious injuries in the last 12 months. The numbers do not slow. They do not care who you are.

      A 13-year-old boy now lies in a hospital bed, his head split open after a moped collided with an MTA bus in Staten Island. It happened at 1 a.m. The street was quiet. The boy was not. “He was taken to Richmond University Medical Center in critical condition with a head injury.”

      In Queens, two police cruisers crashed while racing to a call. Four officers were sent to Jamaica Hospital. “The officers were responding to a call for shots fired nearby when they crashed.” No civilians were hurt this time.

      The Numbers Are Names

      Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians pay the price. In the last year, 14 children died. Forty-five people aged 55-64. Thirty-six aged 65-74. The city’s streets do not forgive mistakes. They do not give second chances.

      The killers are familiar: cars, SUVs, trucks, buses. Sedans alone took 93 pedestrian lives. SUVs killed 200 more. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, mopeds, and even bikes left families broken. The city’s data is a ledger of loss.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has tools. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The Council can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. They have not. Speed cameras were renewed, but the most dangerous drivers still roam free. The Stop Super Speeders Act sits in Albany, waiting for a vote. Every day of delay is another risk, another name on the list.

      What You Can Do

      Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action on repeat offenders. The city will not save itself. The dead cannot speak. You must speak for them.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in NYC traffic crashes recently?
      In the last 12 months, 281 people died and 766 suffered serious injuries on New York City streets, according to city data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and trucks—including sedans, SUVs, trucks, and buses—were responsible for the vast majority: 325 deaths and 545 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 17 deaths and 58 serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 11 deaths and 58 serious injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speed limits, better street design, and stronger enforcement can save lives. Every delay means more loss.
      ▸ What have local politicians done to address traffic violence?
      Albany passed Sammy’s Law, giving NYC the power to lower speed limits. The city renewed speed cameras. But leaders have not yet set a 20 mph citywide speed limit or passed the Stop Super Speeders Act.
      ▸ What can I do to help stop traffic violence in NYC?
      Call your Council Member and the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit and action against repeat dangerous drivers. Join advocacy groups and speak out.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
