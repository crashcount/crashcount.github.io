      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Streets Run Red—New York Leaders Stall as Lives Shatter

      Written July 18, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Blood Price of the Streets

      In New York City, the war on the street never stops. Since 2022, 1,053 people have died in crashes. Another 2,396 have been left with serious injuries. The numbers are blunt. The pain is sharper.

      Just this month, a city worker was slashed by a delivery cyclist after a near miss at Broadway and Cedar. The DOT called it an “abhorrent assault of a NYC DOT employee who performs critical work to keep our city moving”. The worker survived. Many do not.

      Six pedestrians were struck on a Bronx sidewalk after a driver botched a left turn and fled. All survived, but the sidewalk is not safe. The city’s children, elders, and workers pay the price for every failure to act.

      Who Bears the Brunt

      Cars and SUVs kill most often. Since 2022, they have taken 311 lives and left 524 seriously injured. Trucks and buses killed 95. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 21. Bikes killed 11. The dead do not care about the make of the machine. The living must.

      A young woman died in Brooklyn when her boyfriend lost control doing donuts in a parking lot. Police charged him with negligent homicide. He was later charged with criminally negligent homicide, reckless endangerment and reckless driving.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Failed to Do

      Speed kills. Albany finally passed Sammy’s Law, giving the city power to set its own speed limits. The city has started lowering limits in some zones. But the default is still too high. Every day of delay means another family broken.

      Automated speed cameras have been renewed. The city is daylighting intersections, building bike lanes, and redesigning streets. But enforcement still falls hardest on the most vulnerable. Cyclists and delivery workers face crackdowns while the worst repeat drivers keep their keys.

      What You Can Do

      Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph limit. Tell them Sammy’s Law gives them the power. Tell them to use it. Tell them to stop the carnage. Every day they wait, someone else bleeds.

      Do not wait for another name to become a number. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in NYC traffic crashes since 2022?
      Since 2022, 1,053 people have died and 2,396 have suffered serious injuries in New York City traffic crashes.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and SUVs: 311 deaths, 524 serious injuries. Trucks and buses: 95 deaths, 104 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 21 deaths, 45 serious injuries. Bikes: 11 deaths, 57 serious injuries. (Source: NYC Open Data vehicle rollup)
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or are they just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and stronger enforcement against reckless drivers can save lives.
      ▸ What have local politicians done to address traffic violence?
      Albany passed Sammy’s Law, giving NYC the power to lower speed limits. The city has renewed speed cameras, started daylighting intersections, and built more bike lanes. But the default speed limit is still too high, and enforcement often targets cyclists and delivery workers instead of the most dangerous drivers.
      ▸ What can residents do to push for safer streets?
      Call your Council Member and the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit and stronger action against repeat dangerous drivers. Join advocacy groups and keep the pressure on.
