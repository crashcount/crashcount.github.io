      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Asphalt: City Speed Kills, Leaders Stall

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Toll: Death on City Streets

      A man steps into the crosswalk. A car keeps going. The man does not get up. In the last twelve months, 241 people died on New York City streets. 700 were left with serious injuries—the kind that do not heal. More than 48,000 were hurt. The city counts the bodies. The city moves on.

      Pedestrians, cyclists, children—the most fragile—take the worst of it. In the past year, 12 children died. The old are not spared. 24 people over 75 did not survive the year. Every age, every borough, every block. The numbers do not flinch.

      Recent Crashes: The Blood Does Not Dry

      Just last week, a pedestrian was killed at Broadway and East New York Avenue. A moped rider died on Clarendon Road. A 22-year-old woman was ejected and killed on the Belt Parkway. The city’s map of grief grows by the day. See the full list of recent crashes.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Speed kills. Albany gave New York City the power to lower speed limits. The city has started to drop some zones to 20 mph. But the default speed is still too high. The law lets the city act. The city waits. Speed cameras are renewed. Red-light cameras will multiply. Streets are being redesigned, but not fast enough. Enforcement cracks down on the wrong targets—cyclists and delivery workers—while the deadliest drivers keep driving. Review recent policy moves.

      Who Pays the Price

      Cars and SUVs are the main killers. In three years, they caused 301 deaths and over 26,000 injuries to pedestrians. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The city blames the vulnerable. The city protects the powerful.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. Every death is a policy choice. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join the fight. Take action now.
