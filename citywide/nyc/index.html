      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Three Dead in Queens. Zero Accountability. Lower the Speed.

      Written August 14, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 14, 2025

      The Toll This Week

      Just this week, two men stood at a Queens food truck. A car slammed into them. Both died. The driver died too. The street was left with bodies and wreckage. A witness put his head in his hands and said, “I have never seen anything like this.”

      Hours later, a 52-year-old man tried to cross 155th Street near JFK. A driver hit him and sped off. Police reported, “The driver sped off without stopping. No arrests have been made.” He died at the hospital. The street was empty again.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the last 12 months, 286 people have died on New York City streets. Over 52,000 were injured. More than 750 were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Children, elders, workers—all caught in the path of metal and speed.

      Cars and SUVs killed 329 people. Trucks and buses killed 100. Motorcycles and mopeds, 11. Bikes, 11. The city is a machine that eats its own.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Albany gave New York City the power to lower speed limits. The law is there. The city can set a 20 mph limit now. But the default is still 25. The city has started to sprinkle 20 mph zones, but not everywhere.

      Speed cameras have been renewed. Cameras catch speeders day and night. But the worst drivers rack up tickets and keep driving. One bill—the Stop Super Speeders Act—would force repeat offenders to slow down. It sits in Albany, waiting.

      What You Can Do

      Every day of delay means another body on the street. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to drop the speed limit to 20 mph. Tell Albany to pass the Stop Super Speeders Act. Join Families for Safe Streets and Transportation Alternatives. Stand with the families who have lost everything.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      From 2022 to August 2025, cars and SUVs killed 329 people and injured over 19,800. Trucks and buses killed 100 and injured over 1,500. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 11 and injured about 700. Bikes killed 11 and injured 924. Cars and SUVs do the most harm.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The numbers show a pattern. Speed, inattention, and dangerous streets kill people again and again. These deaths can be prevented with lower speed limits, better enforcement, and safer street design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the citywide speed limit to 20 mph, pass laws to stop repeat speeders, redesign streets for safety, and enforce the rules that protect people walking and biking.
      ▸ What has the city done lately to address traffic violence?
      The city renewed speed cameras and started adding 20 mph zones. Albany gave the city power to lower speed limits. But the citywide default is still 25 mph, and the Stop Super Speeders Act is still waiting in Albany.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in NYC traffic crashes recently?
      In the last 12 months, 286 people died and over 52,000 were injured. More than 750 suffered serious injuries.
