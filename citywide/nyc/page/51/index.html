      No More Blood for Speed: Make NYC Streets Safe Now

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 7, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll of the Streets

      A woman, 68, crossing with the signal in Brooklyn. Dead. A 14-year-old boy on a bike in Staten Island. Blood on the pavement, leg torn open. A 61-year-old cyclist in Manhattan. Bones crushed, helmet split. These are not rare stories. They are the city’s daily news.

      In the last 12 months, 260 people died on New York City streets. Over 51,000 were hurt. 732 suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again. Children, elders, workers. No one is spared. The numbers do not lie.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs are the main killers. In three years, they took 298 lives and left 498 with serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 92. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. The city is a machine that chews up flesh and bone. The street is not safe for the old, the young, or the careful.

      What Leaders Have Done—And What They Haven’t

      The city passed Sammy’s Law. Now, leaders can lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not done it citywide. Speed cameras, proven to save lives, are at risk if Albany does not act. The city built more bike lanes and redesigned intersections, but the blood still runs. The numbers drop, then rise again. Every delay is another body on the slab.

      The Cost of Waiting

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. They are choices. Choices to wait, to water down, to delay. Choices that kill. The city has the tools. The leaders have the power. They must use it.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand the protection of speed cameras. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name to be carved in stone. Act now.

      ▸

      Adam Clayton Powell Jr. Boulevard cuts through Harlem. Cars rule. Cyclists and walkers dodge danger. Local activists demand a two-way protected bike lane. Council Member Kristen Richardson Jordan backs them. Safety, not parking, must come first. Delay means more injuries.

      This advocacy opinion, published August 26, 2022, calls for immediate action on a Central Harlem Bikeway along Adam Clayton Powell Jr. Boulevard. The piece highlights that 83.2% of local residents bike, walk, or use transit, yet the boulevard lacks protected lanes and sees high injury rates. The matter is framed as urgent: 'Pedestrian and cyclist safety should be a higher priority than parking. Harlem is not a parking lot. People live here!'       Bill S. Introduced: Speed Governors, SUV Limits, Pedestrian Safety
      ▸

      Senator Hoylman’s bill targets deadly speed and blind spots. It demands speed governors in new cars and direct visibility for heavy SUVs. The measure aims to cut traffic deaths and force safer vehicles onto New York streets. Oversized cars face new scrutiny.

      On August 12, 2022, Manhattan State Senator Brad Hoylman introduced a bill in the New York State Senate. The bill would require all new cars registered in the state to have speed-limiting technology by 2024. It also directs the Department of Motor Vehicles to set visibility rules for vehicles over 3,000 pounds, like SUVs and pickups, to ensure drivers can see pedestrians and cyclists. The bill summary states: 'A new bill in the New York State Senate would require all new cars registered in the state to have speed-limiting technology by 2024.' Senator Hoylman sponsored the bill, citing the 'failure of the promise of Vision Zero.' Elizabeth Adams of Transportation Alternatives praised the measure, calling it 'common-sense.' The bill comes as pedestrian deaths hit a 40-year high. If passed, it would force automakers to include safety tech and could make some monster trucks illegal on city streets.

      • New Bill Would Mandate Speed Governors and Set Limits on Huge SUVs on NY Streets by 2024, Streetsblog NYC, Published 2022-08-22
      Placard Abuse Chokes Downtown: Streetsblog Report, August 2022
      ▸

      City, state, and federal placard cars flood Lower Manhattan. Many park illegally—crosswalks, bus stops, loading zones. Fake, expired, or defaced plates abound. Enforcement is weak. Streets grow more dangerous. Public space vanishes. Crashes and violations rise. The city looks away.

      On August 22, 2022, Streetsblog NYC published an investigative report exposing rampant placard abuse in Lower Manhattan. The report surveyed 727 placard-bearing cars, finding widespread illegal parking in crosswalks, bus stops, and truck zones. The article states: 'Lower Manhattan is overwhelmed by cars with city, state, and federal placards—many of them fake or misused—allowing public employees to park for free or illegally.' Advocacy leader Juan Restrepo of Transportation Alternatives called for ending placard abuse and repurposing parking for people, not cars. The NYPD rarely enforces the law, despite mounting crashes and blocked streets. The report urges strict enforcement and reclaiming space for vulnerable road users. The danger grows as city leaders fail to act.

      Policy Advocacy: Get Americans Out of Cars, August 2022
      ▸

      Experts called for breaking car dependency. They linked cars to rising costs, climate danger, and deadly streets. They urged investment in transit, bikes, and road pricing. The message was clear: fewer cars mean safer, fairer, cleaner cities.

      On August 17, 2022, a webinar hosted by America Walks spotlighted the urgent need to reduce car dependency in the U.S. The event, covered by Streetsblog NYC, featured Yonah Freemark of the Urban Institute and Nailah Pope-Harden of Climate Plan CA. Freemark argued, 'Reducing automobile dependency can play a major role in reducing costs... a society where fewer people drive is... less destructive to the environment.' Pope-Harden pushed for 'multimodal electrification' and road pricing to curb driving. Both experts condemned auto-centric infrastructure, calling for federal funds to support transit, bikes, and equitable electrification. No council bill was discussed, but the advocacy targeted systemic danger: car dominance fuels inequity, high costs, and unsafe streets for vulnerable road users.
