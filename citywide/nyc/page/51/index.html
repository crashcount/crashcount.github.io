      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      NYC Streets Run Red—How Many Kids Must Die Before City Hall Acts?

      Written June 29, 2025 (Revised June 29, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      Children, Blood, and Concrete

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy crossed Eastern Parkway with his sister. A black SUV hit him. He died on the street. The driver stayed. Police said, “They were walking, he was crossing the street with his sister” (police said). No arrest. The street is still open. The danger is still there.

      The day before, in the Bronx, a man ran a red light. His car jumped the curb and crushed three people on the sidewalk. One is still fighting for life. The driver ran but police caught him. He faces charges for assault, fleeing, and driving without a license (faces charges).

      In the last 12 months, 244 people have died on New York City streets. 713 were seriously hurt. Thirteen of the dead were children. The city logs over 81,000 crashes a year. Each number is a name, a family, a future gone.

      The Policy That Wasn’t

      Leaders talk. Streets stay the same. The mayor ripped out a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue after crashes involving children. He said he was listening to the community. Cyclists said, “I don’t think he cares if we die” (Cyclists said). The city put back paint. Paint does not stop cars.

      Elsewhere, the Department of Transportation built a new protected bike lane in Queens. A commuter said, “The old street was very dangerous… the new path is definitely safer because he’s no longer competing with trucks and cars for road space” (A commuter said). But these changes come slow. Too slow for the dead.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Cars and trucks killed 299 people. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 21. Bikes killed 11. The city counts 182,570 injuries since 2022. The bodies pile up. The city debates.

      What Now

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action, not words. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is policy.

      Take action now.

      Streetfilms Exposes Failure of NYC Car-Free School Streets
      ▸

      Children face danger near schools. Streets swarm with cars. Crashes and injuries spike at drop-off. The city’s open streets program falters. Few schools get protection. Most are in wealthy, white areas. Kids in Black and brown neighborhoods face more harm. The system fails the vulnerable.

      On September 8, 2022, Streetfilms released a report highlighting the dangers children face near New York City schools. The film, featuring Streetsblog reporter Jesse Coburn, documents that during the 8 a.m. hour, streets near schools see 57 percent more crashes and 25 percent more injuries per mile than other city streets. The matter summary states, 'School zones don’t have to be danger zones.' The city’s open streets for schools program, meant to keep cars away from children, is failing. Only 38 schools participate, down from 101, and most are in wealthier, whiter neighborhoods. The application process is burdensome, requiring schools to opt in rather than providing universal protection. Former DOT Commissioner Janette Sadik-Khan and StreetopiaUWS Director Carl Mahaney criticize the system. The film exposes how the city’s approach leaves children in Black and brown communities at greater risk, both from crashes and pollution.

      NYPD, DMV Ignore 2019 Law; Cyclists Ticketed for Legal LPI Crossing
      ▸

      Police and DMV judges punish cyclists for following the law. Riders cross with the pedestrian signal, as allowed since 2019. Cops ticket them anyway. Judges uphold fines. City agencies refuse to comment. Cyclists face danger from cars and the system alike.

      This enforcement controversy centers on the 2019 City Council law, sponsored by then-Council Member Carlos Menchaca, which allows cyclists to cross with the pedestrian signal during leading pedestrian intervals (LPIs). Despite the law, NYPD officers and DMV judges continue to ticket and fine cyclists for red light violations when they cross legally. The matter title states, 'The NYPD and DMV Are Punishing Cyclists For Legally Crossing With The Pedestrian Signal.' Cyclist Darren Goldner and Queens resident Chad Formaggio both received $190 tickets for obeying the pedestrian signal. Attorney Steve Vaccaro notes, 'They should have sent a memo about it. But I don't know if anyone did.' The Department of Transportation defers to NYPD and DMV, who remain silent. Cyclists are left exposed, targeted by enforcement instead of protected from reckless drivers. The law’s intent—to give vulnerable road users a head start—remains ignored by those tasked with upholding it.

      Seagirt Boulevard Redesign: DOT Cuts Lanes, Adds Bike Protection
      ▸

      Seagirt Boulevard in Far Rockaway, once a six-lane speed trap, will shrink to two lanes. DOT will add a parking-protected bike lane and more space for people on foot. The redesign aims to slow drivers and shield those outside cars from harm.

      On September 6, 2022, the Department of Transportation announced a major safety redesign for Seagirt Boulevard, a notorious crash corridor in Far Rockaway. The project, not a council bill but a city action, follows years of neglect and repeated crashes. DOT will cut traffic lanes from six to two, add a parking-protected bike lane, and expand pedestrian space. The plan also includes daylighting at intersections and improved markings. According to DOT, 'the project will improve safety for all users, providing connections to existing bike infrastructure and the reduction of a vehicle lane encouraging safer speeds.' Queens Borough President Donovan Richards praised the move, saying, 'Far too many people are killed and injured on our city’s streets, especially people of color, who are disproportionately impacted by traffic violence.' Laura Shepard of Transportation Alternatives added, 'DOT's move to turn car space into protected bike lanes on Seagirt Boulevard, while shortening crossings and expanding median space, will prevent speeding and make it safer to walk, bike, and take transit.' The redesign targets a stretch with 222 crashes and 87 injuries since 2019, aiming to end the long-standing danger for vulnerable road users.
