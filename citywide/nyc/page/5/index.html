      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Asphalt: City Leaders Choose Speed Over Lives

      Written August 8, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 8, 2025

      The Toll This Summer

      The city bleeds. In the last twelve months, 285 people died on New York City streets. 767 suffered serious injuries. The names fade, but the numbers do not. Just last week, a woman in Morris Heights was killed by a driver who struck her and fled. Police reported her death. Her family waits for answers. The driver is gone.

      On July 19, a stolen car tore through Chinatown at over 100 miles per hour. It killed Kevin Cruickshank and May Kwok. Kwok was sitting on a bench. Cruickshank was riding his bike. The city’s Department of Transportation promised upgrades, but the dead do not return. NY1 reported that “a stolen vehicle, allegedly driven at more than 100 miles per hour…crashed into them.”

      Patterns in the Blood

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay the highest price. In three and a half years, cars and SUVs killed 328 and seriously injured 549. Trucks and buses killed 99. Motorcycles and mopeds, 11. Bikes, 11. The city’s most vulnerable are left to cross streets built for speed, not safety.

      Drivers flee. They run red lights. They turn without looking. The city counts the bodies. The numbers do not lie. The pain is not abstract. It is a mother’s phone that will never ring. It is a bench left empty.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has taken steps. Speed cameras in school zones will stay on through 2030. The Council legalized jaywalking, ending a law that punished the poor and people of color. But the default speed limit is still 25 mph. The city has the power to lower it to 20 mph. It has not used it. The dead keep coming.

      What You Can Do

      Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit citywide. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join the fight with Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The city will not save itself.

      The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      From 2022 to August 2025, cars and SUVs killed 328 pedestrians and seriously injured 549. Trucks and buses killed 99. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 11, and bikes killed 11. Cars and trucks do the most harm. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The numbers show patterns: speeding, red-light running, and failure to yield. These deaths are preventable. They are not random.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the citywide speed limit to 20 mph, crack down on repeat speeders, redesign streets for safety, and support enforcement that targets dangerous driving—not vulnerable road users.
      ▸ What has the city done lately to address traffic violence?
      NYC renewed 24-hour speed cameras in school zones through 2030 and legalized jaywalking. But the city has not lowered the default speed limit to 20 mph, even though it now has the power.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in NYC traffic crashes recently?
      In the last 12 months, 285 people died and 767 suffered serious injuries on NYC streets. See NYC Open Data.

      City aims to clear cars from 34th Street. Buses crawl now. Riders wait. DOT wants faster trips, safer crossings. Community boards back the plan. Painted lanes, fewer cars, more eyes on the street. Change comes for riders and walkers.

      West Side Spirit reported on May 20, 2025, that the Department of Transportation (DOT) has proposed turning 34th Street into a mostly car-free busway, modeled after the 14th Street busway. The plan comes with support from three local community boards. According to the article, 'bus speeds on 14th Street increased by at least 24 percent, and bus ridership grew by 30 percent' after similar changes. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'far fewer New Yorkers are getting hurt in crashes' since the 14th Street redesign. The 34th Street proposal would divert most cars, add painted intersections for visibility, and focus on pedestrian safety. The move targets slow bus speeds—now just 3 mph—and aims to reduce systemic danger for bus riders and people on foot. No driver errors are cited, but the plan addresses longstanding risks from car traffic.

      NYPD Issues Criminal Summonses To Cyclists
      ▸

      Police now hand out criminal summonses to cyclists for traffic violations. Fines become court dates. Advocates warn of escalation. Lawmakers push for e-bike registration after a fatal crash. Streets grow tense. Riders and pedestrians caught in the crossfire.

      West Side Spirit reported on May 13, 2025, that NYPD has begun issuing criminal summonses, not just traffic tickets, to cyclists and e-bike riders for violations like running red lights or riding on sidewalks. The change means accused riders must appear in criminal court, not just pay a fine. The move follows the death of Priscilla Loke, struck by an e-bike in 2023. Advocates like Transportation Alternatives call the new policy 'a dangerous escalation.' The NYC E-Vehicle Safety Alliance urges lawmakers to require license plates for e-bikes and scooters. The article notes, 'Under the new policy, a person issued a criminal summons must turn up in person in criminal court.' The shift highlights growing tension over enforcement and the push for stricter regulation after high-profile crashes.
