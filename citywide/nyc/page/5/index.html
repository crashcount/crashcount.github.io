      City Streets, Broken Bodies: How Many Must Die Before We Act?

      Written June 8, 2025 (Revised June 10, 2025 – Fixed duplicate headline in body, improved FAQ citation, deduped and cleaned citations.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Shattered

      In the last 12 months, 261 people died on New York City streets. Over 51,000 were injured. Seven hundred thirty-four suffered serious injuries—lives changed in a heartbeat. Children, elders, workers. The city keeps moving. The dead do not.

      A 32-year-old woman was killed crossing Van Buren Street. A baby boy crushed on Linden Boulevard. An 87-year-old man pinned under an MTA bus in Brooklyn, left in critical condition. The numbers pile up. The stories do not end. NYC Open Data

      Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and cars do the most harm. In three years: 299 killed, 498 seriously hurt. Trucks and buses: 92 killed, 103 seriously hurt. Motorcycles and mopeds: 10 killed, 44 seriously hurt. Bikes: 11 killed, 52 seriously hurt. The street is not safe for the unprotected.

      Leaders Speak, Streets Bleed

      Speed kills. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not. Cameras catch speeders, but Albany drags its feet on renewal. “Speeding kills, and speed cameras save lives,” said State Senator Andrew Gounardes. Yet the law may expire, and the cameras may go dark.

      DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez stood with families and pleaded: “While we know most drivers stop speeding after their first or second ticket, an extremely reckless few continue to endanger themselves and everyone around them.” He called for action.

      The Blame Game

      Enforcement falls hardest on the vulnerable. Cyclists and delivery workers face crackdowns and court dates for minor offenses. “They actively made it more dangerous by standing in the middle of the bike lane,” said one cyclist. Drivers who kill often walk away.

      What Now?

      Lower the speed limit. Renew the cameras. Build real protection for people, not cars. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. Every day of delay is another life at risk.

      TLC Proposal Escalates Fines for Bike Lane Blockers
      ▸

      TLC wants stiffer penalties for cabbies who block bike lanes, crosswalks, and bus stops. Repeat offenders face points, higher fines, and possible license loss. Over 28,000 violations in 15 months. Advocates demand even tougher enforcement. Hearing set for March 12.

      The Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) has proposed new rules to escalate fines and penalties for taxi drivers who block bike lanes, crosswalks, and other critical spaces. The policy proposal, announced February 28, 2025, would add points to TLC licenses for each offense—one for the first, two for the second within 15 months, and increased fines for the third. Six points in 15 months could mean suspension; ten could mean revocation. The matter summary states, 'rules promise to increase safety for passengers, pedestrians, bicyclists, and motorists alike [and] target a relatively small group of repeat offenders.' TLC spokesperson Jason Kersten said the changes target repeat offenders who make streets dangerous. Street safety advocate Jehiah Czebotar and Transportation Alternatives director Ben Furnas support the proposal but call for automated camera enforcement to catch more violators. A public hearing is scheduled for March 12.

      DOT Data: Third Avenue Bike Lane Cuts Injuries, Deaths
      ▸

      Third Avenue changed. Protected bike lane. Injuries fell. Pedestrians, cyclists, passengers safer. No deaths since redesign. Bike traffic soared. DOT says green wave slows cars, helps walkers, riders. Streets once deadly now less so. Numbers show lives saved.

      On February 19, 2025, the city Department of Transportation (DOT) released new safety data for Third Avenue in Manhattan. The protected bike lane and street redesign, finished in late 2023 from East 59th to East 96th streets, cut total injuries by 33 percent. Pedestrian injuries dropped 63 percent. Cyclist injuries fell 10 percent, even as bike traffic jumped 79 percent. No pedestrians or cyclists have died since the changes. DOT spokesman Vin Barone said, "DOT's redesign of Third Avenue has significantly reduced serious injuries—whether you're walking, biking, or in a car on the corridor." The DOT also highlighted its 'green wave' signal timing, set to 15 mph, which slows drivers and helps vulnerable users. Ben Furnas of Transportation Alternatives called Third Avenue "one of the best streets in all of New York City" and urged similar redesigns elsewhere. The city estimates protected bike lanes cut deaths and serious injuries by 18.1 percent for all, and 29.2 percent for pedestrians.

      On February 19, 2025, the Trump administration revoked federal approval for New York City’s congestion pricing plan. The move, announced by U.S. DOT Secretary Sean Duffy, argued the program’s main goal was revenue, not congestion relief. The MTA immediately filed a lawsuit, calling the action unlawful. The bill is not a council measure but a federal policy reversal, with local officials and advocates condemning the decision. State Senator Zellnor Myrie was mentioned among those opposing the move. The matter title reads: 'Trump Withdraws Federal Approval for Congestion Pricing, Blowing Hole in Transit Budget, as MTA Files Lawsuit.'
