      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Bronx midnight. A woman falls. The city keeps counting.

      Written August 31, 2025 (Revised August 31, 2025)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 31, 2025

      Just after midnight on Aug 26, 2025, a 32-year-old woman was struck and killed at Bruckner Blvd and Leggett Ave in the Bronx, at the intersection. NYC’s crash file lists her as a pedestrian, dead at the scene.

      She was one of 1,111 people killed on New York City streets since Jan 1, 2022. That count is citywide, all modes, drawn from the same dataset as of Aug 31, 2025. The record is public.

      The numbers don’t blink

      In this year’s tallies through Aug 31, NYC logged 187 deaths, compared with 201 at the same point last year. Serious injuries rose to 511 from 491. Crashes fell from 60,698 to 54,253. Those are the city’s own numbers.

      The sheet is long. It shows people by age, borough, and mode. It shows where we lose them.

      Queens at 2:30 AM, a BMW, and speed

      At 2:30 AM on Aug 26, a 24-year-old man died as a passenger on the Cross Island Parkway. The file marks “unsafe speed.” It’s there in black and white.

      Speed shows up again and again. It is not a headline. It is a habit, logged line by line.

      Brooklyn morning, a box truck leaves, charges follow

      About 9 AM on Aug 27, an off-duty NYPD officer on a motorcycle collided with a box truck on the BQE near Atlantic Ave. The truck did not stay. Police later arrested a driver and charged him with leaving the scene resulting in death. That’s per ABC7’s report.

      A name, an arraignment, a charge. Another family left with a date and a place.

      Fewer chases, fewer funerals?

      After a run of crashes tied to pursuits, New York officials said they would curb police vehicle chases for minor offenses. As Attorney General Letitia James put it, “the evidence is clear: police vehicle pursuits and high-speed car chases can be dangerous and even fatal, and it is time for a change.” Policy shifts like this save lives when they are followed, measured, and kept.

      Slow it down. Stop the repeat offenders.

      Tools sit on the shelf. NYC now has the power to set safer default speeds on local streets. Albany renewed 24/7 school‑zone speed cameras through 2030. And a bill in Albany would force the worst repeat speeders to install speed limiters after a set number of violations. The details and contacts are here: Take Action.

      The woman in the Bronx will not come home. Others still can. Slow the cars. Hold the worst drivers to account. The City Council and the Mayor can do their part today. Start with lower speeds and real limits on repeat offenders. Here’s how to push them.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How many people have been killed on NYC streets since 2022?
      From Jan 1, 2022 through Aug 31, 2025, NYC’s crash dataset shows 1,111 deaths citywide across all modes. This figure is drawn from NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions tables.
      ▸ What do the year-to-date numbers show?
      Through Aug 31, 2025, NYC recorded 187 traffic deaths, down from 201 at the same point in 2024. Serious injuries rose to 511 from 491, and total crashes fell to 54,253 from 60,698, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What policies can cut deaths now?
      Lower NYC’s default speed limit on local streets and require speed limiters for repeat speeders. Both steps are laid out on our Take Action page with contacts and bill details. See: /take_action/.

      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes: h9gi-nx95; Persons: f55k-p6yu; Vehicles: bm4k-52h4). We filtered for crash dates between 2022-01-01 and 2025-08-31, citywide, all modes. We counted total fatalities and referenced the platform’s year-to-date comparisons included in our August 31, 2025 extraction. You can view a reproducible filtered query here.
