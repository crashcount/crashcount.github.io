      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Another Child Dead. How Many More Before City Hall Acts?

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised July 1, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, cleaned citations, and tightened prose.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      The Toll This Month

      The city bleeds. In the last twelve months, 243 people died on New York streets. 708 were left with serious injuries. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers—none are spared. Just last week, an eight-year-old boy was killed crossing Eastern Parkway in Crown Heights. His sister watched. His mother grieves. “He was a boy who loved life,” said his mother.

      Scooter riders, teens on mopeds, and cyclists have all fallen. On June 26, a 39-year-old man was thrown from his scooter and died on 116th Avenue. No arrests. No comfort. The street is unchanged.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In the last three years, they took 299 lives and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. The numbers are cold, but the pain is not. Every crash is a family torn open.

      Leaders Act—But Not Fast Enough

      The city has moved, but the pace is slow. Speed cameras in school zones are now extended through 2030. “It’s simple: speed cameras save lives and keep New Yorkers safe,” said Governor Hochul. But cameras alone are not enough. Streets need to be rebuilt. Speed limits must drop. The city has the power to set a 20 mph limit. It has not used it.

      Some leaders speak plain. “At this point, there are no questions, just facts: speeding kills, and speed cameras save lives,” said State Senator Gounardes. But others drag their feet, or worse, rip out bike lanes and leave riders exposed.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand that repeat speeders are stopped. Do not wait for another child’s name to be written in chalk on the street.

      Act now. Or count the dead.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in traffic crashes in New York City recently?
      In the last twelve months, 243 people died and 708 suffered serious injuries on New York City streets.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      SUVs and Cars: SUVs and cars killed 299 pedestrians; trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 10 deaths. Bikes: 11 deaths. These numbers show the greatest danger comes from cars and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or are they just accidents?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speeds, protected bike lanes, and better street design save lives. Policy choices, not fate, decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What have local leaders done to address traffic violence?
      Leaders extended speed cameras in school zones through 2030 and have the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph, but have not yet done so citywide.
      ▸ What can local politicians do right now to make streets safer?
      They can set a 20 mph citywide speed limit, build more protected bike lanes, and crack down on repeat speeders. They can act now, or more will die.

      Call your council member and demand safer speeds and protected bike lanes. Join advocacy groups like Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another tragedy.

      Council Vision Zero Hearing: Multiple Safety Bills Under Review
      ▸

      Council weighs bills to curb traffic violence. Advocates demand daylighting, civilian enforcement, and street redesigns. Open Plans calls for urgent action. Focus: less driving, more protection for walkers and cyclists. The hearing marks a push for safer, saner streets.

      On February 14, 2023, the City Council Transportation Committee holds a hearing on Vision Zero. The agenda covers several bills: Intro 415 (dangerous driving study), Intro 555 (school safety signs), Intro 679 (traffic calming near seniors), Intro 805 (pedestrian safety reporting), Intro 854 (annual daylighting), Intro 879 (bollards at sidewalks), and Intro 441 (5 mph on Open Streets). Open Plans' Sara Lind supports daylighting, civilian and automated enforcement, and home rule for speed limit reductions, stating, 'Self-enforcing streets are best.'       Comptroller Lander: Agencies Must Pay for Crash Settlements
      ▸

      Comptroller Lander wants city agencies to pay for crashes caused by their drivers. He calls for smaller fleets, safer vehicles, and real accountability. Safety advocates back him. City Hall pushes back. The fight is over money, lives, and responsibility.

      On February 12, 2023, Comptroller Brad Lander released a report urging city agencies to bear financial responsibility for crash settlements involving their employees, instead of using the general city budget. The proposal, detailed in the report titled "Make City Drivers Safer By Punishing Agencies for Crashes," calls for accelerated adoption of vehicle safety technology, a direct vision standard for fleet vehicles, reduction in both fleet size and vehicle size, and stronger accountability for city drivers. Lander states, "The city should place the budgeted allocation for projected settlement costs within each agency's budget." Safety advocates, including Elizabeth Adams of Transportation Alternatives, support the move, calling it "an important step toward creating a safer city." City Hall responded by highlighting ongoing safety efforts and questioning the link between settlements and fleet crashes. The proposal has not yet advanced to a formal council stage or committee.

      Federal Grant Funds Vision Zero Plans for Half of U.S.
      ▸

      Washington sends $800 million to 510 communities. Most get planning money, not shovels. Big projects win the rest. Cyclists and walkers see most of the gains. Advocates cheer, but warn: plans alone do not save lives. More cash, more action needed.

      On February 2, 2023, the U.S. Department of Transportation announced the first round of 'Safe Streets and Roads For All' grants. The $800 million, part of a $5 billion, five-year program, will reach 510 communities. Most grants fund local Vision Zero action plans, aiming to cut car crash deaths. Only 37 projects get money for real infrastructure. These receive 60 percent of the funds, with 80 percent benefiting cyclists and 90 percent helping pedestrians. Leah Shahum of the Vision Zero Network called the funding 'an encouraging start,' but warned, 'plans don’t always lead to real results.' Corinne Kisner of NACTO said the program 'aligns transportation investments to the safety, equity, and climate crises.' Still, advocates note the grants are less than 1 percent of federal infrastructure spending. Road deaths remain high, hitting vulnerable users hardest.
