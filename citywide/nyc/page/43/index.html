      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Right turn at 30th and 39th. She never made it across.

      Written September 6, 2025 (Revised September 6, 2025)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 6, 2025

      Just before 1 PM on Aug 31, at 30 St and 39 Ave, a driver in a 2020 Ford SUV turned right and hit a 38-year-old woman in a marked crosswalk. Police recorded failure to yield by the driver. She died (NYC Open Data).

      This Week

      • About 5 AM on Aug 30 at York Ave and E 72 St, a driver in a Toyota taxi going straight hit a pedestrian outside an intersection. Police recorded failure to yield by the driver. He died (NYC Open Data).
      • The afternoon of Aug 23 near United Nations Ave S, a driver in a Ford sedan going straight hit a pedestrian outside an intersection. Police recorded driver inattention. He died (NYC Open Data).

      The Count Does Not Stop

      They were three of 1,119 people killed on New York City streets since Jan 1, 2022 (NYC Open Data). In that same span, drivers of SUVs have killed 205 pedestrians, and drivers of sedans 96, with taxis linked to 16 pedestrian deaths (NYC Open Data). The bodies are counted in the city’s crash files. The names are not.

      Year To Date, Fewer Dead—But More Shattered

      Through this year, the city has logged 195 deaths, down from 208 at this point last year. Serious injuries rose to 528, up from 510. Crashes dropped to 55,741 from 62,218, and injuries to 34,816 from 37,005 (NYC Open Data). Fewer crashes. Many still don’t walk away.

      Across Borough Lines, The Pattern Holds

      Queens. Manhattan. Brooklyn too. On Aug 31 in East Flatbush, police said a hit-and-run driver struck an NYPD sergeant directing traffic at Schenectady and Church Avenues (ABC7; amNY). A uniform did not protect him. Paint lines did not protect the others.

      Slow the Cars. Stop the Repeat Offenders.

      The city now has the power to set lower speed limits on local streets and to use it. Advocates are pushing for a citywide 20 MPH default and for the state to force the worst repeat speeders to install speed limiters (Take Action). The tools exist. The question is whether leaders use them.

      What Happens Next Is A Choice

      A woman in a crosswalk in Queens. A man on York Avenue before dawn. Another near the UN in the afternoon. It’s the same story with different corners. Tell City Hall to slow the cars and rein in the worst drivers. Then keep calling. Start here.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets on NYC Open Data: Crashes (h9gi-nx95), Persons (f55k-p6yu), and Vehicles (bm4k-52h4). We filtered for New York City incidents occurring from 2022-01-01 through 2025-09-06. Totals like deaths, injuries, and serious injuries come from summing fields in the Persons table by outcome and mode; crashes come from the Crashes table. We accessed the data on Sep 6, 2025. You can start from the Crashes dataset here and use the related Persons and Vehicles tables to reproduce counts.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ Why highlight SUVs, sedans, and taxis in pedestrian deaths?
      The Persons table classifies the striking vehicle. From 2022-01-01 to 2025-09-06, our rollups show drivers of SUVs linked to 205 pedestrian deaths, sedans to 96, and taxis to 16. These figures come from the same NYC Open Data sources cited above.
      ▸ Did enforcement or policy change recently?
      Advocates are calling for the city to lower default speeds and for the state to require speed limiters for repeat speeders. See our summary and how to contact officials on our Take Action page.
      ▸ What can I do today?
      Ask City Hall to set a 20 MPH default on local streets and press Albany to mandate speed limiters for repeat offenders. Use our scripts and contacts at Take Action.
