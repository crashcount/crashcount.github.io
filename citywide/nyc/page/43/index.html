      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Another Child’s Shoe in the Gutter—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 22, 2025)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      A child’s shoe in the gutter. A bicycle bent in the crosswalk. In the last twelve months, 253 people died on New York City streets. 718 more were seriously injured. Behind every number, a family waits for news that will never be good. See the NYC Open Data.

      Children are not spared. Fourteen under 18 died. Sixty-two were left with injuries that will not heal. The old are not safe. Twenty-five over 75 died. The city’s streets do not forgive mistakes, but the mistakes are not theirs.

      Who Bears the Brunt

      Cars and SUVs kill most. In the past three years, sedans and SUVs together took nearly 300 lives and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers do not lie. The bigger the vehicle, the greater the harm.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      The city talks of Vision Zero. They point to new laws and lower speed limits. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can now set its own speed limits. But the limit is not yet lowered. Speed cameras work, but their future is always in doubt. Each year, Albany must vote to keep them on. Each year, advocates must beg for what should be automatic.

      The city built more bike lanes, redesigned intersections, and claimed progress. But the work is slow. The deaths do not wait. The numbers do not fall fast enough.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by those who decide how wide a street should be and how fast a car can go. The city has the tools. It must use them.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand permanent speed cameras. Demand streets where a child can cross without fear.

      Do not wait for another family to join the count. Take action now.

      Council weighs bills to curb traffic violence. Advocates demand daylighting, civilian enforcement, and street redesigns. Open Plans calls for urgent action. Focus: less driving, more protection for walkers and cyclists. The hearing marks a push for safer, saner streets.

      Comptroller Lander: Agencies Must Pay for Crash Settlements
      ▸

      Comptroller Lander wants city agencies to pay for crashes caused by their drivers. He calls for smaller fleets, safer vehicles, and real accountability. Safety advocates back him. City Hall pushes back. The fight is over money, lives, and responsibility.

      On February 12, 2023, Comptroller Brad Lander released a report urging city agencies to bear financial responsibility for crash settlements involving their employees, instead of using the general city budget. The proposal, detailed in the report titled "Make City Drivers Safer By Punishing Agencies for Crashes," calls for accelerated adoption of vehicle safety technology, a direct vision standard for fleet vehicles, reduction in both fleet size and vehicle size, and stronger accountability for city drivers. Lander states, "The city should place the budgeted allocation for projected settlement costs within each agency's budget." Safety advocates, including Elizabeth Adams of Transportation Alternatives, support the move, calling it "an important step toward creating a safer city." City Hall responded by highlighting ongoing safety efforts and questioning the link between settlements and fleet crashes. The proposal has not yet advanced to a formal council stage or committee.

      Secretary Pete Urges Mayors: Zero Traffic Deaths Now
      ▸

      Secretary Pete Buttigieg called mayors to arms. He named traffic deaths a national crisis. He demanded action. He told leaders to choose safety every time. He listed proven fixes: slower speeds, protected lanes, bold crosswalks. He said: zero deaths is the only goal.

      On February 8, 2023, Secretary Pete Buttigieg addressed the U.S. Conference of Mayors in Washington, D.C., spotlighting the deadly toll of traffic violence—43,000 lives lost in 2021. He declared, 'Our ultimate goal is to get traffic deaths down to zero.' Buttigieg urged mayors to adopt Vision Zero, citing cities like Hoboken and Jersey City as proof that progress is possible. He called for lower speed limits, protected bike and bus lanes, curb extensions, high-visibility crosswalks, and more frequent signals. Buttigieg stressed, 'Every infrastructure choice is a safety choice.' He met with Families for Safe Streets, grounding his plea in the pain of victims. The speech, though not a bill, marked a federal push for cities to act, prioritize vulnerable road users, and end the carnage.

      Federal Grant Funds Vision Zero Plans for Half of U.S.
      ▸

      Washington sends $800 million to 510 communities. Most get planning money, not shovels. Big projects win the rest. Cyclists and walkers see most of the gains. Advocates cheer, but warn: plans alone do not save lives. More cash, more action needed.

      On February 2, 2023, the U.S. Department of Transportation announced the first round of 'Safe Streets and Roads For All' grants. The $800 million, part of a $5 billion, five-year program, will reach 510 communities. Most grants fund local Vision Zero action plans, aiming to cut car crash deaths. Only 37 projects get money for real infrastructure. These receive 60 percent of the funds, with 80 percent benefiting cyclists and 90 percent helping pedestrians. Leah Shahum of the Vision Zero Network called the funding 'an encouraging start,' but warned, 'plans don’t always lead to real results.' Corinne Kisner of NACTO said the program 'aligns transportation investments to the safety, equity, and climate crises.' Still, advocates note the grants are less than 1 percent of federal infrastructure spending. Road deaths remain high, hitting vulnerable users hardest.

      Mayor Adams Urged: Push Speed Cameras for NYC Highways
      ▸

      Highway deaths surged. Surface streets got safer. Speed cameras work, but Albany blocks them on highways. Mayor Adams faces pressure. Families mourn. The city’s most violent crashes go unchecked. The data is clear. Highways need urgent action now.

      This editorial, published January 26, 2023, calls on Mayor Adams to advocate for speed cameras on New York City highways. The piece highlights that, despite Vision Zero and surface-street redesigns, highway deaths rose from 29 in 2019 to 49 in 2022. The article states: 'the most proven tool for reducing the most violent crashes—control by speed cameras—is not allowed by New York State on the city's highways.' Mayor Adams is urged to push Albany for legislative change. The editorial notes that surface-street interventions—road diets, bike lanes, intersection redesigns—have cut cyclist deaths by 45 percent and pedestrian deaths by 5 percent since 2019. But highways remain deadly, untouched by these tools. The city’s safety gains stall at the expressway on-ramp. Vulnerable road users pay the price.

      Vision Zero 2022: Deadly Year for Kids, Danger Persists
      ▸

      Sixteen children died on city streets in 2022. Most crashes hit known danger zones. The Bronx bled. Protected lanes lagged. City Hall claimed progress. The toll stayed high. Vulnerable lives paid the price. The crisis endures. Action stalls.

The report states, 'More children died in crashes last year than in any other year in the Vision Zero era and crashes remain concentrated in areas that are known to be treacherous.' The NYPD solved more hit-and-runs in white neighborhoods than in communities of color. DOT and City Hall touted small improvements, but the carnage continued. The report demands full, urgent implementation of the NYC Streets Plan to save lives.

      Thousands of Curbside EV Chargers Threaten NYC Sidewalks
      ▸

      City plans to flood sidewalks with electric car chargers. Pedestrian space shrinks. Bike and bus lanes face new barriers. Car dominance grows. Federal money fuels the push. Critics say: put people first, not parking. Sidewalks belong to the vulnerable.

      On January 18, 2023, Christine Berthet published a sharp critique of New York City’s curbside EV charger rollout. The opinion, titled 'Thousands of Chargers for Heavy E-Cars Are Coming to a Sidewalk Near You,' warns that federal grants will fund chargers at the expense of pedestrian, cyclist, and transit space. Berthet writes, 'chargers will enshrine the use of the curb for free parking, and will make it more difficult and expensive to expand sidewalks, bike lanes and bus lanes in the future.' She urges the city to direct incentives toward fast-charging hubs, transit, and Citi Bike, not private car infrastructure. Berthet calls for sidewalk widening, bike lanes, bus lanes, and public review before any curbside charger is installed. Her message: prioritize people, safety, and the environment over car convenience.
