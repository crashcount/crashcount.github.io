      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Deadly Streets, Silent Leaders: How Many More Must Die?

      Written August 13, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 13, 2025

      The Toll This Month

      Three dead at a Queens food truck. Two men were ordering coffee. The car came fast. It did not stop. It slammed into them and the truck. Both men died. The driver died too. It happened on 42nd Street, Astoria, just yesterday. A witness described the crash: “Must have been going at least 60+ miles an hour just right through the stop sign and within seconds I heard the crash and screams and just dropped everything and ran over there, it was instant.”

      In the Bronx, two moped riders were killed. A Mercedes tried to pass, hit another car, and set off a chain reaction. The driver was charged with DWI and released. The sister of one victim demanded accountability: “He’s just walking freely? Two people were killed. He was drunk. Think about how he took two lives. That’s not fair.”

      In the last 12 months: 286 people killed. 759 seriously injured. Over 52,000 hurt. The dead are not numbers. They are brothers, sisters, mothers, friends. The city’s streets are not safe for the old, the young, or anyone in between.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      Albany gave New York City the power to lower speed limits. The law is on the books. The city can set 20 mph as the default. They have not. Speed cameras were renewed, but the most dangerous drivers—those with dozens of violations—still drive. The city has started to redesign some streets. Fifth Avenue will get wider sidewalks. But the work is slow. The carnage is not.

      What Comes Next

      The crisis is not fate. These deaths are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by those who design our streets. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join with others. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and trucks—including sedans, SUVs, taxis, trucks, buses, and vans—are responsible for the vast majority of pedestrian injuries and deaths in New York City. According to city data since 2022, SUVs and sedans alone caused over 32,000 pedestrian injuries and more than 400 deaths. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes also injure and kill, but cars and trucks are by far the greatest threat to people on foot.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. These deaths and injuries are preventable. They happen because of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by those who design our streets. Every delay in action means more lives lost.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the citywide speed limit to 20 mph, redesign streets for safety, crack down on repeat speeders, and invest in infrastructure that protects people walking and cycling. The law now allows the city to act. Leaders must choose to use it.
      ▸ What has the city done recently to address traffic violence?
      Albany renewed speed cameras and gave New York City the power to lower speed limits. Some streets are being redesigned. But the city has not yet set a 20 mph default speed limit, and the most dangerous drivers still go unchecked.
      ▸ How many people have been killed or seriously injured in NYC traffic crashes recently?
      In the last 12 months, 286 people were killed and 759 seriously injured on New York City streets.
