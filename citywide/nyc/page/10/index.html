      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      NYC Streets: Death, Delay, and the Fix

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised August 22, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      A Bad Week on Open Streets

      In Queens near JFK, a driver hit a 52‑year‑old man at 155th Street and South Conduit Avenue around 2:30 a.m. The driver kept going. “The driver sped off without stopping,” police said (Daily News). ABC7 added: “The operator of the vehicle fled the scene after hitting the man.” No arrests yet (ABC7).

      On the Bronx River Parkway, two young moped riders were ejected and killed. The city’s crash file lists both as “Apparent Death” after a multi‑vehicle collision (NYC Open Data, CrashID 4834345).

      In Queens again, two pedestrians died and a driver was killed in a separate crash the same day, city records show. Names blur. The toll does not.

      The Pattern, by the Numbers

      Since 2022, New York has logged 344,225 crashes, with 1,105 deaths and 194,320 injuries. 2,533 were serious injuries (NYC Open Data period stats). Pedestrians take the hit: cars and SUVs alone account for most pedestrian harm.

      In this time window, a small slice tells the whole story: hit‑and‑run at 2:30 a.m.; ejection on a parkway; a body in the roadway. The city keeps counting. Families keep counting.

      What Leaders Did — And Didn’t

      On 14th Street, City Hall says a redesign is coming next year to “improve the pedestrian experience” (NY1). Good. But people are dying far from Union Square.

      In Queens court, a wrong‑way driver was sentenced after plowing into cars. DA Melinda Katz said he “terrorized other drivers” and that “Two motorists were badly hurt and still have not fully recovered.” (amNY). Accountability, after the fact.

      The Fix We Know Works

      The city has the tools. Lower the default speed limit. Mandate intelligent speed assistance for repeat offenders. Fewer high‑speed impacts, fewer funerals. Pair that with street redesigns beyond the rich corridors. Do it now, before the next siren.

      Take one step today: demand a citywide 20 mph limit and speed limiters for serial offenders. Don’t wait for another knock at the door.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and Trucks: 334 deaths and 27,403 injuries from cars/SUVs; 100 deaths and 2,291 injuries from trucks/buses. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 11 deaths and 1,069 injuries. Bikes: 11 deaths and 1,490 injuries. (Period stats, current vehicle rollup).
      ▸ What does it take to change traffic laws here?
      Street fixes need City Hall: a plan from NYC DOT, support from the local council member, passage by the City Council, and the mayor’s signature. Tools like automated enforcement need Albany: a bill must pass the State Assembly and Senate and be signed by the Governor. Local officials can push hard, but they can’t act alone.
