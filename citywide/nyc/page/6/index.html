      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Parkway, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed citation links, removed headline/body duplication, deduped and sorted citations.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      Another Child Dead, Another Family Broken

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy tried to cross Eastern Parkway in Crown Heights. A black Honda Pilot hit him. He was dragged from under the SUV. Blood pooled on the street. “I saw the kid being dragged from underneath the car by a woman,” a witness said. The boy’s sister stood nearby. The driver stayed. No arrest. The city moved on.

      He was not the only one. In the last twelve months, 241 people died on New York City streets. 706 were seriously injured. Children, elders, cyclists, walkers—the city’s most vulnerable. The numbers do not stop. They only grow.

      The Toll of Inaction

      The city has seen over 80,000 crashes in the past year. Cars and SUVs killed 299 people. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. Each number is a life. Each crash is a family changed forever. The pain is not abstract. It is a mother’s scream, a father’s silence, a child’s empty bed.

      Residents know the danger. “Yeah, this street is dangerous,” said one neighbor. The city knows it too. But the streets remain the same.

      Leaders Move, But Not Fast Enough

      There are new laws. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. Some streets have changed. Most have not. Red-light cameras have been expanded. But the deaths keep coming. When leaders act, they move slow. When they stall, people die. The mayor removed a protected bike lane in Williamsburg. Cyclists were left exposed. “I don’t think he cares if we die,” said one rider.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand It Now

      No more waiting. No more empty promises. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action for every child, every walker, every rider. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Make them choose life.

      TLC Proposal Escalates Fines for Bike Lane Blockers
      ▸

      TLC wants stiffer penalties for cabbies who block bike lanes, crosswalks, and bus stops. Repeat offenders face points, higher fines, and possible license loss. Over 28,000 violations in 15 months. Advocates demand even tougher enforcement. Hearing set for March 12.

      The Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) has proposed new rules to escalate fines and penalties for taxi drivers who block bike lanes, crosswalks, and other critical spaces. The policy proposal, announced February 28, 2025, would add points to TLC licenses for each offense—one for the first, two for the second within 15 months, and increased fines for the third. Six points in 15 months could mean suspension; ten could mean revocation. The matter summary states, 'rules promise to increase safety for passengers, pedestrians, bicyclists, and motorists alike [and] target a relatively small group of repeat offenders.' TLC spokesperson Jason Kersten said the changes target repeat offenders who make streets dangerous. Street safety advocate Jehiah Czebotar and Transportation Alternatives director Ben Furnas support the proposal but call for automated camera enforcement to catch more violators. A public hearing is scheduled for March 12.

      DOT Data: Third Avenue Bike Lane Cuts Injuries, Deaths
      ▸

      Third Avenue changed. Protected bike lane. Injuries fell. Pedestrians, cyclists, passengers safer. No deaths since redesign. Bike traffic soared. DOT says green wave slows cars, helps walkers, riders. Streets once deadly now less so. Numbers show lives saved.

      On February 19, 2025, the city Department of Transportation (DOT) released new safety data for Third Avenue in Manhattan. The protected bike lane and street redesign, finished in late 2023 from East 59th to East 96th streets, cut total injuries by 33 percent. Pedestrian injuries dropped 63 percent. Cyclist injuries fell 10 percent, even as bike traffic jumped 79 percent. No pedestrians or cyclists have died since the changes. DOT spokesman Vin Barone said, "DOT's redesign of Third Avenue has significantly reduced serious injuries—whether you're walking, biking, or in a car on the corridor." The DOT also highlighted its 'green wave' signal timing, set to 15 mph, which slows drivers and helps vulnerable users. Ben Furnas of Transportation Alternatives called Third Avenue "one of the best streets in all of New York City" and urged similar redesigns elsewhere. The city estimates protected bike lanes cut deaths and serious injuries by 18.1 percent for all, and 29.2 percent for pedestrians.

      Trump Withdraws Approval for Congestion Pricing, MTA Sues
      ▸

      Trump killed congestion pricing. The feds yanked approval. The MTA sued. Transit funding took a hit. Streets stay clogged. Pedestrians and cyclists lose. The city’s lifeline is at risk. Politicians bicker. The danger remains. The fight moves to court.

      On February 19, 2025, the Trump administration revoked federal approval for New York City’s congestion pricing plan. The move, announced by U.S. DOT Secretary Sean Duffy, argued the program’s main goal was revenue, not congestion relief. The MTA immediately filed a lawsuit, calling the action unlawful. The bill is not a council measure but a federal policy reversal, with local officials and advocates condemning the decision. State Senator Zellnor Myrie was mentioned among those opposing the move. The matter title reads: 'Trump Withdraws Federal Approval for Congestion Pricing, Blowing Hole in Transit Budget, as MTA Files Lawsuit.' The reversal threatens transit funding and leaves vulnerable road users exposed to ongoing traffic danger. No safety analyst assessment was provided, but the loss of congestion pricing means more cars, more risk, and less money for safer streets.
