      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Asphalt: NYC Streets Still Kill

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll This Month: Flesh and Blood on Concrete

      Just last week, a city worker was slashed by an e-bike rider at Broadway and Cedar. He was fixing a street sign. The cyclist nearly hit his truck. Words were exchanged. A box cutter flashed. The worker bled. The assailant fled. DOT called it an “abhorrent assault of a NYC DOT employee who performs critical work to keep our city moving”. No arrests. No peace.

      In the Bronx, a driver in a Ford Mustang missed a left turn and plowed onto the sidewalk. Six people were hit. The driver ran. The victims were taken to Lincoln Hospital. All survived, but the street did not forgive. Police are still looking for the driver.

      A Brooklyn parking lot, midnight. A young man spun his car in circles. His girlfriend sat on the curb. The car lost grip. She died. He faces charges. The car, the lot, the night—none will forget. Police charged Zachary Cando with negligent homicide.

      The Numbers: Relentless and Unforgiving

      In the last 12 months, 259 people died on New York City streets. 732 suffered serious injuries.

      Children are not spared. Eleven under 18 died. Sixty-one were seriously hurt. The old are not spared. Twenty-five over 75 died. Thirty-seven were left with wounds that do not heal.

      Cars and SUVs do most of the killing. 311 deaths. Trucks and buses, 95. Motorcycles and mopeds, 21. Bikes, 11. The numbers do not lie. The numbers do not care.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      The city lowered speed limits in some zones. Speed cameras run day and night. But the work is not done. Streets are still wide, turns still sharp, enforcement still falls hardest on the least protected. When the city cracks down, it is often on the cyclist, not the driver. Community leaders have called out the injustice. One board said it is “unjust to hold cyclists to a far higher standard than motor vehicle drivers”.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Every day, someone else is broken. Every day, someone else dies.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people who walk and bike. Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in NYC traffic crashes in the last year?
      In the last 12 months, 259 people died and 732 suffered serious injuries on New York City streets.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and better enforcement can save lives. Every delay costs another life.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and SUVs caused 311 deaths and 524 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 95 deaths and 104 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 21 deaths and 45 serious injuries. Bikes caused 11 deaths and 57 serious injuries.
      ▸ What have local leaders done to address traffic violence?
      The city has lowered speed limits in some zones and expanded speed camera enforcement. But many streets remain dangerous, and enforcement often targets cyclists more than drivers.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can set a citywide 20 mph speed limit, redesign streets for safety, and focus enforcement on the most dangerous driving behaviors.
      ▸ What should I do if I want safer streets in my neighborhood?
      Call your council member and the mayor. Demand lower speed limits, better street design, and protection for people walking and biking. Join local advocacy groups and speak out.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
