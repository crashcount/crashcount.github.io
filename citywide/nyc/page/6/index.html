      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Crosswalk, siren, silence

      Written September 1, 2025 (Revised September 1, 2025)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 1, 2025

      Just before dawn at 42 St and 19 Ave in Queens on Aug 12, 2025, two men on foot were hit and killed. An 84-year-old driver also died (NYC Open Data).

      They were three of 1,111 people killed in traffic crashes in New York City since Jan 1, 2022 (NYC Open Data). In the last 12 months alone, the city logged 84,853 crashes, 52,348 injuries, and 278 deaths (NYC Open Data). The count resets each day. The bodies do not.

      Brooklyn, Queens, Manhattan: the pattern holds

      On Ocean Pkwy at Avenue C in Brooklyn on Aug 9, 2025, a 45-year-old woman walking was struck and killed by an SUV (NYC Open Data). Three days earlier in Bensonhurst, a 76-year-old woman was killed in the crosswalk at 86 St and 18 Ave; the police database lists “Traffic Control Disregarded” for the driver (NYC Open Data). In Midtown East on Aug 23, 2025, a pedestrian was killed by a sedan on United Nations Ave S (NYC Open Data). Different corners. Same end.

      Since 2022, drivers of cars and SUVs have been involved in 336 pedestrian deaths. Trucks and buses account for another 100 (NYC Open Data). This year’s death toll is 187 so far, compared with 203 at this point last year. Serious injuries are up slightly (NYC Open Data). The line wavers. It does not break.

      A city that keeps moving; families that cannot

      On the BQE near Atlantic Ave on Aug 27, 2025, an off-duty NYPD officer on a motorcycle was struck by a box truck that left. A driver was later arrested and charged with leaving the scene after a death (ABC7).

      We count what we can. We cannot count the unopened texts on a phone that never lit again. But we can show the record. It is public. It is recent.

      Slow the cars. Stop the worst

      City leaders have tools in hand. The Council and the Mayor can lower the city’s default speeds. Albany is considering a bill to force the worst repeat speeders to install tech that keeps them under the limit. Advocates say the steps save lives; even Albany’s own record notes, “Speed cameras have cut speeding by over 60% in locations where installed” (NYS Senate).

      We do not need another vigil to act. We need a pen and a vote. The city can set safer speeds. The state can curb the super speeders. Details and contacts are here.

      Keep faith with the dead by protecting the living

      The three men at 42 St and 19 Ave will not come home. Neither will the woman on Ocean Pkwy. Or the elder in Bensonhurst. The list runs long. The fixes are known. Set 20 MPH on our residential streets. Fit repeat offenders with speed limiters. Then watch the count fall.

      Take one step now. Ask City Hall and Albany to move. Start here.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4), filtered to New York City from 2022-01-01 through 2025-09-01. Totals for crashes, injuries, deaths, serious injuries, and vehicle-type involvement were aggregated from those tables as of Sep 1, 2025. You can start from the dataset portal here.
      ▸ Why name specific intersections?
      Because that is where people died. The NYC crash database lists the locations, dates, modes, and basic contributing factors for each case, including the examples cited above (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ What solutions are on the table right now?
      NYC can lower default speed limits on local streets and Albany lawmakers are weighing a bill to require intelligent speed assistance for the worst repeat speeders. Both actions are detailed on our campaign page here.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      NYC DOT Targets Intersection Danger Zones
      ▸

      Jun 11 - City crews will block cars from corners. Barriers, granite, planters—hard lines drawn. Sightlines open. Pedestrians and cyclists step forward, visible. Intersections, once blind, now clear. The city moves to cut the toll where most blood spills.

      amNY reported on June 11, 2025, that the NYC Department of Transportation will redesign intersections to protect pedestrians and cyclists. The plan uses 'hardened daylighting'—physical barriers, granite blocks, and planters—to stop vehicles from parking near corners. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety.' Nearly half of city traffic deaths happen at intersections. The new design will first appear at high-crash sites in Brooklyn, including Ocean Avenue at Church Avenue and several others. The move highlights a systemic effort to address visibility failures and reduce deadly conflicts at crossings.

      DOT Plans Center Bus Lanes For Flatbush
      ▸

      Jun 5 - Flatbush Avenue chokes on traffic. Buses crawl. Pedestrians dodge cars at clogged crossings. The city will carve out center bus lanes, build boarding islands, and cut car lanes. Nearly 70,000 daily bus riders wait for relief.

      According to amny (published June 5, 2025), the NYC Department of Transportation will redesign Flatbush Avenue in Brooklyn, adding center-running bus lanes from Livingston Street to Grand Army Plaza. The plan includes boarding islands for safer passenger access and removes two car lanes. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, “Almost 70,000 daily bus riders are stuck waiting too long for slow buses, drivers are caught in a mess of traffic, and pedestrians are left crossing intersections clogged with vehicles.” The redesign aims to separate buses from car traffic, reduce conflicts, and improve safety for vulnerable road users. Parking will decrease, but the exact impact is unclear. The plan responds to chronic congestion and slow bus speeds, which often fall below five miles per hour during rush hour.
