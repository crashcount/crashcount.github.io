      No More Blood for Speed: Make NYC Streets Safe Now

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 6, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll of the Streets

      A woman, 68, crossing with the signal in Brooklyn. Dead. A 14-year-old boy on a bike in Staten Island. Blood on the pavement, leg torn open. A 61-year-old cyclist in Manhattan. Bones crushed, helmet split. These are not rare stories. They are the city’s daily news.

      In the last 12 months, 260 people died on New York City streets. Over 51,000 were hurt. 732 suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again. Children, elders, workers. No one is spared. The numbers do not lie.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs are the main killers. In three years, they took 298 lives and left 498 with serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 92. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. The city is a machine that chews up flesh and bone. The street is not safe for the old, the young, or the careful.

      What Leaders Have Done—And What They Haven’t

      The city passed Sammy’s Law. Now, leaders can lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not done it citywide. Speed cameras, proven to save lives, are at risk if Albany does not act. The city built more bike lanes and redesigned intersections, but the blood still runs. The numbers drop, then rise again. Every delay is another body on the slab.

      The Cost of Waiting

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. They are choices. Choices to wait, to water down, to delay. Choices that kill. The city has the tools. The leaders have the power. They must use it.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand the protection of speed cameras. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name to be carved in stone. Act now.

      Report: First Quarter 2022 Deadliest Since Vision Zero
      ▸

      Traffic deaths surged 44 percent in early 2022. Fifty-nine killed in three months. Pedestrians, children, seniors hit hardest. Queens and Manhattan saw the worst spikes. Advocates demand urgent action—redesign streets, expand cameras, fund safety. City leaders stall. Streets remain lethal.

      This report, published April 20, 2022, highlights a grim milestone: the first quarter of 2022 marked the deadliest start to any year since Vision Zero began in 2014. Transportation Alternatives found 59 people killed in traffic crashes in just three months, a 44 percent jump from last year. The report states, 'Traffic fatalities are up 44 percent so far this year—the deadliest start to any year since Vision Zero launched.' Danny Harris, executive director of Transportation Alternatives, called on Mayor Adams to include the City Council’s $3.1 billion Streets Master Plan request and to fast-track redesigns of 1,000 intersections. Elke Weiss of Families for Safe Streets urged passage of the Crash Victim Rights and Safety Act, saying, 'Crossing the street should not be a life-or-death risk.' The report urges more car-free space, stronger driver accountability, and expanded speed and red-light camera programs. Despite Vision Zero’s promise, the city’s streets remain deadly for its most vulnerable.

      Study: Megacar Drivers 4x Likelier to Hit Walkers Turning
      ▸

      SUV and pickup drivers strike walkers at turns far more than small cars. Blind spots, tall hoods, and thick pillars block sightlines. Federal rules ignore these dangers. The bigger the car, the deadlier the risk. Vulnerable lives pay the price.

      On March 17, 2022, a research report from the Insurance Institute of Highway Safety revealed that SUV and pickup drivers are three to four times more likely to hit a pedestrian while turning than drivers of smaller cars. The study, titled 'Study: Megacar Drivers Up to 4x More Likely to Hit Walkers While Turning,' points to design flaws—especially wide A-pillars and high, boxy fronts—that create deadly blind spots. Dr. Wen Hu, senior transportation research engineer, said, 'We really need to systematically study the impact of A-pillar design on pedestrian visibility.' The report calls out federal regulators for failing to address these hazards. It urges action on vehicle design, left-turn bans in pedestrian areas, and right-on-red bans. The evidence is clear: megacars endanger walkers at every turn.
