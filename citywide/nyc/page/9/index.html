      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Red Lights, Dead Streets: Demand Action Before More Lives Are Lost

      Written July 31, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 31, 2025

      The Week the City Bled

      Last week, a 65-year-old man rode his e-bike down Second Avenue. A 21-year-old, unlicensed driver in a Nissan Rogue struck him and fled. The cyclist hit the pavement, head first. Paramedics found him unconscious, his red light still flashing in the street. Police arrested the driver two hours later. He faces charges for leaving the scene and driving without a license. The biker was rushed by EMS to Bellevue Hospital in critical condition.

      On the FDR Drive, a bus and a car collided. Traffic stopped. Riders waited for another bus. One person was hurt. The city moved on. Traffic was halted on the northbound lanes, and riders were transferred to another bus.

      The Numbers No One Escapes

      In the last 12 months, 282 people died on New York City streets. 765 suffered serious injuries. The young, the old, the ones just trying to get home. Children under 18: 14 dead, 66 seriously hurt. Seniors 65 and up: 66 dead, 80 seriously hurt. These are not numbers. They are families, emptied out by a knock at the door.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      City leaders have taken steps. They renewed speed cameras in school zones. They passed Sammy’s Law, giving the city power to lower speed limits. But the default speed limit remains above 20 mph. The city sprinkles 20 mph zones, but the carnage continues. The most dangerous drivers—those with dozens of camera tickets—still roam free. The law to stop them sits in Albany.

      What Next: The Cost of Waiting

      Every day, another family joins the list. The city has the tools. It needs the will. Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph limit. Demand action against repeat speeders.

      Do not wait for another red light to flash in the dark.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Since 2022, cars and trucks (including SUVs) have killed 324 pedestrians and caused 541 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 11, with 45 serious injuries. Bikes killed 11, with 58 serious injuries. Cars and trucks do the most harm by far, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, distraction, and reckless driving kill and injure hundreds every year. Policies like lower speed limits and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the citywide speed limit to 20 mph, crack down on repeat speeders, redesign dangerous streets, and expand automated enforcement. They have the power. They need to use it.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in NYC traffic in the last year?
      In the last 12 months, 282 people died and 765 suffered serious injuries on NYC streets.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ What can I do to help stop traffic violence?
      Call your Council Member, the Mayor, and the DOT. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit and action against repeat speeders. Join advocacy groups and show up at community meetings.
      ▸ Have local leaders done anything lately to improve safety?
      Yes. They renewed speed cameras in school zones and passed Sammy’s Law, which lets the city lower speed limits. But the default limit is still above 20 mph, and the most dangerous drivers are not stopped.
