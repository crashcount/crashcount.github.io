      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      New York Streets Kill. City Hall Waits.

      Written August 11, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 11, 2025

      The Toll of the Streets

      Every day, New York City bleeds. In the last twelve months, 284 people died in crashes. Over 52,000 were injured. Seven hundred sixty-two suffered wounds so grave they will not walk the same again. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old. Fourteen children died. Forty-eight people aged 55 to 64. No one is spared. NYC Open Data

      Just last week, the carnage continued. A woman crossing 174th Street in the Bronx was struck and killed. The driver fled. Police said, “they’re looking for the driver” but gave no name, no face, only a void where a person once stood. In Morris Heights, another woman died after a hit-and-run. The news was blunt: “A Bronx woman is dead after a hit-and-run in Morris Heights.”

      On Canal Street, two more lives ended when a driver sped off the Manhattan Bridge at 109 mph. The city moved to add barriers and lower speed limits, but as one official said, “Canal Street is only as safe as its most dangerous block, and even after these changes, the vast majority of the corridor will remain deadly.”

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Some leaders speak. Some act. Most wait. After the Chinatown crash, Transportation Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, “We are taking immediate steps to fortify this intersection while developing a long-term safety redesign for the entire Canal Street corridor.” But the corridor remains a gauntlet. Seven dead since 2011. The city schedules meetings. The street keeps killing.

      Speed is the killer. In the Bronx, a cab driver died when an SUV hit him at 77 mph in a 25 mph zone. The District Attorney said, “This defendant was allegedly driving three times the speed limit when her SUV slammed into a livery cab, and she fled the scene without checking on the driver’s condition or waiting for first responders to arrive.”

      What Must Change

      The system is broken. The city has the power to lower speed limits. It can redesign streets. It can end the reign of repeat offenders. But every day of delay is another day of blood on the asphalt.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      The dead cannot speak. You must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      From 2022 to August 2025, cars and SUVs were responsible for 328 pedestrian deaths and over 27,000 injuries. Trucks and buses caused 99 deaths and over 2,300 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 11 deaths and over 1,100 injuries. Bikes were involved in 11 deaths and over 1,500 injuries. The majority of deaths and injuries come from cars, SUVs, and trucks. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      These are not random accidents. Speeding, reckless driving, and unsafe street design are preventable causes. Local leaders can lower speed limits, redesign streets, and crack down on repeat offenders to save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the citywide speed limit to 20 mph, redesign dangerous streets, and pass laws to keep repeat speeders off the road. They can act now, or more people will die.
      ▸ What recent steps have NYC leaders taken?
      After deadly crashes, the city has promised new barriers and lower speed limits on Canal Street, and scheduled redesign meetings. But most of the corridor remains dangerous. The city has the power to lower speed limits citywide but has not yet done so.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in NYC traffic crashes recently?
      In the last twelve months, 284 people died and 762 suffered serious injuries in NYC traffic crashes.

      ▸ How can I take action?
      Call your council member and the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join advocacy groups and show up at community meetings.
