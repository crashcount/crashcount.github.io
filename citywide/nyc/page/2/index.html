      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Asphalt: NYC Streets Demand Action Now

      Written August 5, 2025 (Revised August 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 5, 2025

      The Toll of the Streets

      Every week, New York City bleeds. In the last twelve months, 281 people died in crashes. Over 52,000 were hurt. Seven hundred sixty-six suffered injuries so grave they may never walk the same again. These are not numbers. They are fathers, daughters, neighbors, friends.

      Just last week, a 55-year-old woman riding her bike in Queens was killed by an SUV making a left turn. She was ejected from her bike and died in the street. On the same day, a 23-year-old man was struck and killed by a speeding sedan in Ozone Park. The car came fast. The street was slick. He did not get up. NYC crash data

      The Voices on the Ground

      The city is tired. In Washington Heights, after a hit-and-run left a cyclist sprawled on the pavement, a resident said, “No one stops at these stop signs. We see people go through these red lights all the time.” Another pleaded, “I really want there to be speed humps because it’s just terrifying.”

      In Queens, two NYPD cruisers collided while racing to a call. Four officers went to the hospital. The cause is still under review. Police are now trying to determine the cause of the crash.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has power. It does not always use it. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The Council can lower the speed limit to 20 mph citywide. They have not. Speed cameras now run 24/7, but the most reckless drivers still roam free. A handful of bills wait for action. Each day of delay is another day of loss.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand the Stop Super Speeders Act. Join groups like Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      From 2022 to August 2025, cars and trucks (including sedans, SUVs, taxis, vans, and trucks) were responsible for the most harm: 325 deaths and 545 serious injuries to pedestrians. SUVs alone caused 200 pedestrian deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 11 deaths and 45 serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 11 deaths and 58 serious injuries. The toll falls hardest on those outside a car.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. They follow patterns—speed, reckless driving, poor street design. Policies like lower speed limits and better enforcement save lives. Every delay means more loss.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the citywide speed limit to 20 mph, pass laws to rein in repeat dangerous drivers, and redesign streets to protect people walking and biking. They can act now, or answer for the next tragedy.
      ▸ What recent steps has New York City taken to address traffic violence?
      The city now runs speed cameras 24/7 and has the power to lower speed limits under Sammy’s Law. But leaders have not yet made 20 mph the default. Some bills to stop repeat offenders are still waiting.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in NYC traffic crashes recently?
      In the last 12 months, 281 people died and 766 suffered serious injuries on city streets.
