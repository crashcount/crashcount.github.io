      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Asphalt: NYC’s Deadly Streets Demand Action Now

      Written July 16, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Death Toll Grows, Quiet and Relentless

      In New York City, the numbers do not lie. 259 people killed, 735 seriously injured in the last year. The city’s streets are a slow-moving disaster. Every week brings new names. A 21-year-old woman, Madison Ruiz, killed as her boyfriend spun donuts in a Brooklyn parking lot. Her nephews, ages 12 and 17, left bleeding by the curb. Days later, the tire marks still scarred the pavement. “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter.”

      A teenage e-scooter rider, Nacere Ellis, died on Staten Island after colliding with a Hyundai. Head trauma. No charges. The city moves on. “Ellis suffered head trauma as a result of the crash.”

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs do most of the killing. In three and a half years: 311 deaths by cars and SUVs, 95 by trucks and buses, 21 by motorcycles and mopeds, 11 by bikes. The numbers are cold. The bodies are not. The city’s most vulnerable—pedestrians, cyclists, children—pay the price.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      City Hall claims progress. Speed cameras renewed. New 20 mph zones near schools. But the citywide speed limit remains 25 mph. The law lets leaders drop it to 20 mph now. They have not. Each day of delay risks another life. The city touts record bike lanes and intersection daylighting. But the crackdowns fall hardest on cyclists and e-bike delivery workers, not the drivers who do the most harm. Community boards call it out: “It sends the wrong message about the actual danger on our streets.”

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph citywide limit. Demand real action against repeat speeders. Join the fight. Every day you wait, another family waits for a call that will break them.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      From 2022 to July 2025, cars and SUVs caused 311 deaths and 524 serious injuries to pedestrians and cyclists. Trucks and buses caused 95 deaths and 104 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 21 deaths and 45 serious injuries. Bikes caused 11 deaths and 57 serious injuries. Cars and trucks do most of the killing.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      They are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement against the most dangerous drivers can save lives. Every delay in action means more families shattered.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the citywide speed limit to 20 mph, expand automated enforcement, redesign streets for safety, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can act now, or answer for every life lost.
      ▸ What has New York City done lately to address traffic violence?
      The city renewed speed cameras, created new 20 mph zones near schools, and built more protected bike lanes. But the default speed limit is still 25 mph, and enforcement often targets cyclists instead of the most dangerous drivers.
      ▸ Who is most at risk from traffic violence in NYC?
      Pedestrians, cyclists, and children are the most vulnerable. They are killed and injured at higher rates than drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How can I help stop traffic violence in my neighborhood?
      Call your Council Member and the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit and real action against repeat speeders. Join advocacy groups and speak up at community meetings.
