      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      No More Excuses. No More Bodies. Fix Our Streets Now.

      Written June 27, 2025 (Revised June 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Futures Broken

      In the last twelve months, 244 people died on New York City streets. Nearly 50,000 more were injured. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers—no one is spared. In June, a 17-year-old boy died after his moped collided with a car in Brooklyn. His passenger, 14, was thrown to the pavement and left unconscious. Days earlier, a cyclist named Amanda Servedio was killed in Astoria, struck by a man fleeing police through red lights and bike lanes. She was coming home from a ride. She never made it back. The chase lasted ten minutes. Ten minutes of sirens, speed, and fear.

      The Pattern: Policy Moves, But the Blood Stays

      The city talks safety. The city counts bodies. In June, Mayor Adams ripped out a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. He said it was for safety. Cyclists saw it for what it was. “I don’t think he cares if we die,” said Jeanette Samyn. The city rolled out new intersection designs—mini-protected bike lanes, daylighting, barriers. “Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. But the pace is slow. The danger is fast.

      The Blame: Who Pays, Who Waits

      Most deaths come from cars and trucks. In the last three and a half years, they killed 299 people and injured over 26,000. Bikes killed 11. Mopeds and motorcycles killed 10. The city blames speed, then delays lowering limits. The city blames e-bikes, then stalls on safe storage. The city blames the dead, then moves on.

      The Next Step: No More Waiting

      Every day, someone else is lost. The city has the power to lower speed limits. The city can build real protection at every corner. The city can end the bloodletting. But only if you force their hand. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph limit. Demand hardened intersections. Demand action, not excuses.

      If you wait, the street will take someone you love.

      Bill S. Introduced: Speed Governors, SUV Limits, Pedestrian Safety
      ▸

      Senator Hoylman’s bill targets deadly speed and blind spots. It demands speed governors in new cars and direct visibility for heavy SUVs. The measure aims to cut traffic deaths and force safer vehicles onto New York streets. Oversized cars face new scrutiny.

      On August 12, 2022, Manhattan State Senator Brad Hoylman introduced a bill in the New York State Senate. The bill would require all new cars registered in the state to have speed-limiting technology by 2024. It also directs the Department of Motor Vehicles to set visibility rules for vehicles over 3,000 pounds, like SUVs and pickups, to ensure drivers can see pedestrians and cyclists. The bill summary states: 'A new bill in the New York State Senate would require all new cars registered in the state to have speed-limiting technology by 2024.' Senator Hoylman sponsored the bill, citing the 'failure of the promise of Vision Zero.' Elizabeth Adams of Transportation Alternatives praised the measure, calling it 'common-sense.' The bill comes as pedestrian deaths hit a 40-year high. If passed, it would force automakers to include safety tech and could make some monster trucks illegal on city streets.

      Placard Abuse Chokes Downtown: Streetsblog Report, August 2022
      ▸

      City, state, and federal placard cars flood Lower Manhattan. Many park illegally—crosswalks, bus stops, loading zones. Fake, expired, or defaced plates abound. Enforcement is weak. Streets grow more dangerous. Public space vanishes. Crashes and violations rise. The city looks away.

      On August 22, 2022, Streetsblog NYC published an investigative report exposing rampant placard abuse in Lower Manhattan. The report surveyed 727 placard-bearing cars, finding widespread illegal parking in crosswalks, bus stops, and truck zones. The article states: 'Lower Manhattan is overwhelmed by cars with city, state, and federal placards—many of them fake or misused—allowing public employees to park for free or illegally.' Advocacy leader Juan Restrepo of Transportation Alternatives called for ending placard abuse and repurposing parking for people, not cars. The NYPD rarely enforces the law, despite mounting crashes and blocked streets. The report urges strict enforcement and reclaiming space for vulnerable road users. The danger grows as city leaders fail to act.
