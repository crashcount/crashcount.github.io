      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Another Child Dead—The Blood Is on City Hall’s Hands

      Written July 6, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Toll Mounts, The City Waits

      A boy is dead in Crown Heights. An SUV hit him at the corner of Eastern Parkway and Albany Avenue. He was eight. The street is wide. The cars are fast. The city counts another child lost. Advocates describe Eastern Parkway as a major thoroughfare for drivers and the sixth most dangerous street in Brooklyn for pedestrians. The numbers do not stop. In the last twelve months, 238 people have died on New York City streets. 705 were seriously injured. Children, elders, cyclists, walkers—the city’s most vulnerable, crushed by steel and speed.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do the most harm. In the current three-year period, they killed 300 people and seriously injured 509 more. Trucks and buses killed 93 and seriously injured 103. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10 and seriously injured 44. Bikes killed 11 and seriously injured 54. The dead do not care about the make or model. They are gone all the same. The city’s own data shows the pattern. The violence is not random. It is built into the street.

      Leaders Act—Or Don’t

      Some leaders move. Some stall. Some rip out what little safety we have. When the city tried to remove the Bedford Avenue protected bike lane, the Department of Transportation warned that removing the protected bike lane won’t remove cyclists—it will only make the street less safe. The city risks legal liability for knowingly making a Vision Zero corridor more dangerous. Cyclists and parents begged to keep the lane. One parent explained, “Before the protected bike lane my wife made me promise to never bike the children on it because the driving behavior was so outrageous… Now the street is so much calmer.” Still, the fight drags on.

      Elsewhere, the city installs new bike lanes, then threatens to cap e-bike speeds at 15 mph. A former DOT official called it “a war on bikes, a war on immigrants, and it undermines traffic safety”. The city’s priorities shift with the wind. The bodies stay in the ground.

      The Call

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every delay, every rollback, every excuse is another name on a slab. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand streets where children can walk and live. Do not wait for the next siren.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and Trucks: SUVs and cars killed 300 people and seriously injured 509. Trucks and buses killed 93 and seriously injured 103. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 10 killed, 44 seriously injured. Bikes: 11 killed, 54 seriously injured. (Source: NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      They are preventable. The same streets see the same tragedies. Protected bike lanes, lower speed limits, and safer crossings save lives. Delays and rollbacks cost lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce a citywide 20 mph speed limit, build and protect bike lanes, redesign dangerous streets, and stop policies that punish cyclists and pedestrians instead of drivers.
      ▸ What recent actions have NYC leaders taken on traffic safety?
      Some have fought to keep protected bike lanes, but others have tried to remove them. The city has proposed a 15 mph e-bike speed limit, which safety experts warn will make streets less safe. The fight for safer streets is ongoing.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured on NYC streets recently?
      In the last twelve months, 238 people died and 705 were seriously injured in traffic crashes.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?

      ▸ How can I take action?
      Call your council member and the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit, protected bike lanes, and safer streets for all. Do not wait for another tragedy.

      Letter From Sweden: Congestion Pricing’s Urban Safety Lessons
      ▸

      Swedish cities cut car traffic with congestion pricing. Streets grew quiet. Bike lanes widened. Pedestrians took space. Crashes fell. Air cleared. Shops thrived. Stockholm’s crash rate is a third of New York’s. The lesson is blunt: fewer cars, safer streets, more life.

      This opinion piece, published May 13, 2024, in Streetsblog NYC, draws on Sweden’s decade of congestion pricing. The article, titled 'Congestion Pricing Will Make New York a World-Class City,' details how Gothenburg and Stockholm used tolls to reduce car traffic, fund transit, and reclaim streets for people. Per Bergström-Jonsson, a Swedish official, says, 'Congestion pricing improved travel times far more than all other road investments.' Stockholm’s crash deaths are a third of New York’s. Council members are not directly named, as this is an advocacy article, but the stance is clear: support for congestion pricing, bike infrastructure, transit, and pedestrianization.
