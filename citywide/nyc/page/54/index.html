      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Dead Kids, Empty Promises: How Many More Must Die Before the City Acts?

      Written July 2, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 2, 2025

      Children, Cyclists, and the Cost of Delay

      An eight-year-old boy died crossing Eastern Parkway. An SUV hit him at the intersection. His name is not in the data. His age is. The street is busy. The city calls it one of the most dangerous for walkers. The group said, "[Eastern Parkway] acts as a major thoroughfare for drivers, and is the sixth most dangerous street in Brooklyn for pedestrians" (Streetsblog NYC).

      Fourteen people were hurt when a van slammed into a tree in Brooklyn. Twelve of them were adults with special needs, headed to a day program. The driver swerved to avoid another car. A witness said, “It’s sad because they definitely need a lot of attention more than anyone else. I hope that they’re all OK at the end of the day” (ABC7).

      A 17-year-old died riding a moped. A 39-year-old was killed on a motorcycle. The list goes on. In the past 12 months, 244 people have died on city streets. 709 were seriously injured. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers are among them (NYC Open Data).

      Leaders Act—But Not Fast Enough

      The city renewed its speed camera program. Governor Hochul said, “Speed cameras save lives and keep New Yorkers safe. There is no greater priority for me than the safety of New Yorkers” (amNY). Cameras slow some drivers. But the dead keep coming.

      Protected bike lanes work. The city’s own Department of Transportation warned, “Removing the protected bike lane won’t remove cyclists—it will only make the street less safe” (Streetsblog NYC). Still, the mayor ripped out the Bedford Avenue lane. Cyclists are left exposed. The city risks more deaths, more grief.

      The Numbers Are Names

      SUVs and cars kill most. In three years, cars and SUVs killed 300 people and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      Every day of delay is another body on the street. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand that leaders stop tearing out what little safety we have. Do not wait for another child’s name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      From 2022 to July 2025, cars and SUVs killed 300 people and caused over 26,000 injuries. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. Cars and SUVs are the main killers. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speed, street design, and enforcement make the difference. Policies that slow cars and protect walkers save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, keep and expand speed cameras, and redesign streets to protect people, not cars. They can stop removing safety measures.
      ▸ What has the city done lately to improve safety?
      The city renewed its speed camera program and installed some protected bike lanes. But it also removed the Bedford Avenue protected lane, making streets less safe for cyclists and walkers.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured recently?
      In the last 12 months, 244 people died and 709 were seriously injured on New York City streets. NYC Open Data

      NYC Lags Behind: Car-Free School Streets Far Fewer Than Paris, London
      ▸

      New York trails global cities in banning cars near schools. Paris, London, and Tirana have hundreds of car-free school streets. NYC has just 41. Streets near schools remain dangerous. City leaders lack urgency. Children, especially the most vulnerable, pay the price.

      This policy comparison, published August 1, 2022, highlights New York City's failure to match global peers on car-free school streets. The report notes, 'A movement to ban cars from streets near schools is taking off globally, with cities like London, Paris, and Tirana implementing hundreds of car-free school streets to protect children from traffic and air pollution.' In contrast, NYC has only 41 such streets. Advocates like Emily Stutts and Simon Battisti criticize the city’s lack of political will, funding, and urgency. Battisti points to Tirana’s swift removal of parking for safety, while David Belliard in Paris calls for cities to prioritize children and vulnerable people over cars. The report underscores that New York’s program is underfunded, limited, and absent from top officials’ agendas. Streets near schools remain hazardous, especially for children of color and low-income families. The city’s inaction leaves vulnerable road users exposed to daily danger.

      Senate GOP Bill Seeks Repeal of Drunk-Driving Tech Mandate
      ▸

      Three Republican senators want to scrap a law forcing new cars to detect drunk drivers. Their bill would erase a rule meant to stop deadly crashes. Safety advocates warn this move could cost lives. The senators cite privacy. Victims get no say.

      On August 1, 2022, Senators Mike Rounds, Mike Braun, and John Cornyn introduced a bill to repeal a key safety measure. The proposal targets a Bipartisan Infrastructure Law provision that requires automakers to install passive drunk-driving detection systems in all new vehicles by 2024. The bill is not yet assigned to a committee. The senators have not issued public statements, but sources cite concerns over driver privacy. The bill’s summary states it would 'overturn a key provision in the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law that requires automakers to install passive drunk-driving detection mechanisms.' Safety advocates argue the technology could prevent up to 94 percent of alcohol-related crash deaths. They say removing the mandate puts vulnerable road users—pedestrians, cyclists, passengers—at greater risk. The debate pits privacy against public safety, but the cost will be measured in lives lost on the street.

      Bronx Report: Road Deaths Surge, Vision Zero Stalls
      ▸

      Bronx streets are killing more people. Twenty-eight dead in six months. Kids hit harder. Hit-and-runs double. City promises action, but blood still runs. Advocates demand street redesign, car-free school zones, and tougher rules. The Bronx waits. Danger grows.

      A new report from Transportation Alternatives, released July 20, 2022, shows a grim rise in Bronx road deaths. In the first half of 2022, 28 people died—up 22 percent from last year, and double the toll from 2014, when Vision Zero began. The report, titled 'Bronx Bummer: Road Deaths are Up in the Boogie Down, Report Shows,' calls out the city’s failure to protect its most vulnerable. Danny Harris, TA’s executive director, urges 'bold action to physically redesign streets' and pushes for measures like the NYC 25X25 plan, more bus lanes, daylighting corners, higher taxes on heavy vehicles, and stronger enforcement against dangerous drivers. The Department of Transportation, represented by Vin Barone, claims historic investments and promises new bike and bus lanes, especially in underserved Bronx neighborhoods. Still, the death count climbs. Advocates want car-free school streets and safer crossings. The city’s response lags behind the crisis.

      DOT Converts Doyers Street to Car-Free Plaza in Chinatown
      ▸

      Doyers Street in Chinatown will close to cars and become a permanent pedestrian plaza. The city will add gravel, markings, and planters. The move follows years of temporary closures. Locals and businesses welcome the change. Streets once deadly for walkers now belong to them.

      On July 19, 2022, the NYC Department of Transportation announced the full pedestrianization of Doyers Street in Chinatown, formalizing its status as a car-free plaza. The project builds on the success of Covid-era open streets and seasonal closures. DOT spokesman Vin Barone said, "DOT is proud to build on the success of our Open Streets with proposals to formalize spaces like the Doyers Open Street as a permanent pedestrian plaza." Wellington Chen, executive director of the Chinatown Partnership, praised the move, calling it "healthier and safer for both shopkeepers and customers." The plan includes new plaza features and changes to nearby parking for commercial loading. The effort is part of a broader city push, with BIDs leading similar projects across New York. The conversion gives crowded Chinatown a rare, needed refuge for pedestrians and local businesses.
