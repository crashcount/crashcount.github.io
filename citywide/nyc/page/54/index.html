      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Parkway, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed citation links, removed headline/body duplication, deduped and sorted citations.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      Another Child Dead, Another Family Broken

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy tried to cross Eastern Parkway in Crown Heights. A black Honda Pilot hit him. He was dragged from under the SUV. Blood pooled on the street. “I saw the kid being dragged from underneath the car by a woman,” a witness said. The boy’s sister stood nearby. The driver stayed. No arrest. The city moved on.

      He was not the only one. In the last twelve months, 241 people died on New York City streets. 706 were seriously injured. Children, elders, cyclists, walkers—the city’s most vulnerable. The numbers do not stop. They only grow.

      The Toll of Inaction

      The city has seen over 80,000 crashes in the past year. Cars and SUVs killed 299 people. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. Each number is a life. Each crash is a family changed forever. The pain is not abstract. It is a mother’s scream, a father’s silence, a child’s empty bed.

      Residents know the danger. “Yeah, this street is dangerous,” said one neighbor. The city knows it too. But the streets remain the same.

      Leaders Move, But Not Fast Enough

      There are new laws. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. Some streets have changed. Most have not. Red-light cameras have been expanded. But the deaths keep coming. When leaders act, they move slow. When they stall, people die. The mayor removed a protected bike lane in Williamsburg. Cyclists were left exposed. “I don’t think he cares if we die,” said one rider.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand It Now

      No more waiting. No more empty promises. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action for every child, every walker, every rider. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Make them choose life.

      Bronx Report: Road Deaths Surge, Vision Zero Stalls
      ▸

      Bronx streets are killing more people. Twenty-eight dead in six months. Kids hit harder. Hit-and-runs double. City promises action, but blood still runs. Advocates demand street redesign, car-free school zones, and tougher rules. The Bronx waits. Danger grows.

      A new report from Transportation Alternatives, released July 20, 2022, shows a grim rise in Bronx road deaths. In the first half of 2022, 28 people died—up 22 percent from last year, and double the toll from 2014, when Vision Zero began. The report, titled 'Bronx Bummer: Road Deaths are Up in the Boogie Down, Report Shows,' calls out the city’s failure to protect its most vulnerable. Danny Harris, TA’s executive director, urges 'bold action to physically redesign streets' and pushes for measures like the NYC 25X25 plan, more bus lanes, daylighting corners, higher taxes on heavy vehicles, and stronger enforcement against dangerous drivers. The Department of Transportation, represented by Vin Barone, claims historic investments and promises new bike and bus lanes, especially in underserved Bronx neighborhoods. Still, the death count climbs. Advocates want car-free school streets and safer crossings. The city’s response lags behind the crisis.

      DOT Converts Doyers Street to Car-Free Plaza in Chinatown
      ▸

      Doyers Street in Chinatown will close to cars and become a permanent pedestrian plaza. The city will add gravel, markings, and planters. The move follows years of temporary closures. Locals and businesses welcome the change. Streets once deadly for walkers now belong to them.

      On July 19, 2022, the NYC Department of Transportation announced the full pedestrianization of Doyers Street in Chinatown, formalizing its status as a car-free plaza. The project builds on the success of Covid-era open streets and seasonal closures. DOT spokesman Vin Barone said, "DOT is proud to build on the success of our Open Streets with proposals to formalize spaces like the Doyers Open Street as a permanent pedestrian plaza." Wellington Chen, executive director of the Chinatown Partnership, praised the move, calling it "healthier and safer for both shopkeepers and customers." The plan includes new plaza features and changes to nearby parking for commercial loading. The effort is part of a broader city push, with BIDs leading similar projects across New York. The conversion gives crowded Chinatown a rare, needed refuge for pedestrians and local businesses.

      Hoboken Council Unanimously Cuts Speed Limit To 20 MPH
      ▸

      Hoboken wiped out traffic deaths for four years. Now, city leaders slash speed limits to 20 mph. They target injuries next. Streets get daylighted corners, curb extensions, and raised intersections. No ticket blitz. Just slower cars. Safety comes first.

      On July 14, 2022, Hoboken’s city council unanimously approved a new law to lower the citywide speed limit from 25 to 20 mph. The measure, part of Hoboken’s Vision Zero push, aims to end all traffic violence by 2030. The city’s matter summary reads: officials 'began the process of lowering speed limits from 25 miles per hour to 20, as part of its effort to end all forms of traffic violence.' Mayor Ravi Bhalla backed the move, saying, 'that extra time could very well end up saving the life of a child or senior citizen.' Ryan Sharp, parking and transportation director, stressed the need to keep pushing for safer streets. The city focuses on infrastructure—daylighting, curb extensions, raised intersections—over enforcement. Police will educate, not ticket. Hoboken’s model shows small cities can make streets safe for walkers, cyclists, and all vulnerable road users.

      Report: Bad Road Design Fuels U.S. Pedestrian Death Crisis
      ▸

      A new report exposes the deadly cost of American road design. Even as driving fell, pedestrian deaths soared. Wide, fast arterials kill most. The toll climbs, year after year. The fix is clear: redesign streets for people, not speed.

      On July 12, 2022, Smart Growth America released its annual 'Dangerous by Design' report. The report, covered by Streetsblog NYC, finds that 'it all starts with bad design.' Pedestrian deaths rose 4.7 percent in 2020, hitting a 40-year high in 2021. Beth Osborne, director of Transportation for America, highlights that over 70 percent of urban pedestrian fatalities occur on multi-lane arterial roads, which make up only 15 percent of streets. Osborne urges systemic change: 'Safety has to be the default.' The report calls for better data, stronger design standards, and smarter use of federal funds. No council members are named; this is a national policy report, not a local bill. The findings underline a simple truth: dangerous roads kill, and only bold redesign will save lives.
