      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 25, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Streets Unchanged

      A cyclist hit the pavement in Astoria. Amanda Servedio, 36, was riding home. A man fleeing police in a truck ran her down. She went flying into a parked car. She died at Elmhurst Hospital. The man sped off. The chase lasted ten minutes. The street was crowded. The city was silent. “The defendant allegedly led police on a 10-minute chase through the crowded streets of Long Island City and Astoria to avoid responsibility for his actions,” said the District Attorney.

      A 17-year-old boy rode a moped in Brooklyn. He crashed into a car. He died at Maimonides. His passenger was left broken. No arrests. The city moved on. “EMS transported the teen and passenger to Maimonides Medical Center in critical condition. The 17-year-old later died at the hospital.”

      In the last 12 months, 247 people died on New York City streets. 716 were left with serious injuries. Children, elders, riders, walkers. The city counts the bodies. The city keeps moving.

      Leadership: Promises, Delays, and the Blame Game

      The mayor ripped out a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. He blamed “community feedback” and “safety concerns.” The numbers say otherwise. The lane was a shield. Now it is paint. Cyclists are left exposed. “What the fuck! Where are we supposed to ride?” asked one rider.

      The city capped e-bike speeds. It stalled on secure bike storage. It announced new intersection designs, but only at a trickle. The streets stay deadly. The city blames the vulnerable. The drivers keep driving.

      The Numbers: Who Pays the Price

      Cars and SUVs killed 299. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11.

      The dead do not get a second chance. The injured do not get justice. The city counts. The city waits.

      What Comes Next: Demand More

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. Tell them to build real protection, not just paint. Tell them to stop blaming the people who walk and ride. Take action now.

      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The numbers and patterns show these are preventable. Speed, street design, and policy choices decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, redesign intersections, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can stop blaming the people who walk and ride.
      ▸ What has the city done lately to address traffic violence?
      The city removed a protected bike lane, capped e-bike speeds, and announced a few intersection redesigns. Progress is slow. The toll keeps rising.

      Bike New York Urges DOT: Fix Failing City Cycling Program
      ▸

      Bike New York blasts DOT for weak bike lanes and empty mileage targets. Advocates demand real protection, more concrete, and fewer painted lanes. They call for action, not excuses. The city’s cyclists face danger. The city must do better.

      On January 7, 2022, Bike New York sent a letter to new DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. The advocacy group declared, "The DOT bike program you inherit today is no longer a global leader or effective innovator." They criticized the agency for chasing mileage targets over safety and for letting police, film crews, and city vehicles block bike lanes. Bike New York called for physically protected lanes, more concrete, and an end to painted lanes and community board vetoes. Director Jon Orcutt said, "Bike lanes that are merely paint are obsolete and mostly useless to New Yorkers on bicycles in today’s city." DOT spokesman Seth Stein said Rodriguez agrees that a world-class protected bike lane network is key to safer streets and will consider the recommendations. The push is clear: vulnerable cyclists need real protection, not empty promises.

      Queens CB11 Demands DOT Build Protected Bike Lanes
      ▸

      Queens Community Board 11 rejected paint-only bike lanes. They voted for real protection. Their map calls for six east-west and four north-south protected routes. DOT and activists back the push. The board wants safe passage for teens, not just lines on asphalt.

      On January 4, 2022, Queens Community Board 11 unanimously passed a resolution demanding the Department of Transportation (DOT) install protected bike lanes, not just painted lines, in Bayside. The board’s Transportation Committee led the effort, sending DOT a map with six east-west and four north-south protected routes. The matter, described as a call for 'the safest bike lanes, not just paint,' drew support from activists and DOT officials. Laura Shepard said, 'This unanimous vote shows the broad, popular support for protected bike lanes throughout Queens.' Joby Jacob added, 'It is a far better proposal that will keep folks safer than what DOT originally proposed.' DOT spokesperson Seth Stein agreed, saying, 'A bike lane network is exactly what our streets need.' The board’s action centers safety for cyclists and teens, rejecting half-measures.
