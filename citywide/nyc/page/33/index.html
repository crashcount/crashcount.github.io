      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Another Child Dead. How Many More Before City Hall Acts?

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, cleaned citations, and tightened prose.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      The Toll This Month

      The city bleeds. In the last twelve months, 243 people died on New York streets. 708 were left with serious injuries. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers—none are spared. Just last week, an eight-year-old boy was killed crossing Eastern Parkway in Crown Heights. His sister watched. His mother grieves. “He was a boy who loved life,” said his mother.

      Scooter riders, teens on mopeds, and cyclists have all fallen. On June 26, a 39-year-old man was thrown from his scooter and died on 116th Avenue. No arrests. No comfort. The street is unchanged.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In the last three years, they took 299 lives and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. The numbers are cold, but the pain is not. Every crash is a family torn open.

      Leaders Act—But Not Fast Enough

      The city has moved, but the pace is slow. Speed cameras in school zones are now extended through 2030. “It’s simple: speed cameras save lives and keep New Yorkers safe,” said Governor Hochul. But cameras alone are not enough. Streets need to be rebuilt. Speed limits must drop. The city has the power to set a 20 mph limit. It has not used it.

      Some leaders speak plain. “At this point, there are no questions, just facts: speeding kills, and speed cameras save lives,” said State Senator Gounardes. But others drag their feet, or worse, rip out bike lanes and leave riders exposed.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand that repeat speeders are stopped. Do not wait for another child’s name to be written in chalk on the street.

      Act now. Or count the dead.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in traffic crashes in New York City recently?
      In the last twelve months, 243 people died and 708 suffered serious injuries on New York City streets.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      SUVs and Cars: SUVs and cars killed 299 pedestrians; trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 10 deaths. Bikes: 11 deaths. These numbers show the greatest danger comes from cars and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or are they just accidents?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speeds, protected bike lanes, and better street design save lives. Policy choices, not fate, decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What have local leaders done to address traffic violence?
      Leaders extended speed cameras in school zones through 2030 and have the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph, but have not yet done so citywide.
      ▸ What can local politicians do right now to make streets safer?
      They can set a 20 mph citywide speed limit, build more protected bike lanes, and crack down on repeat speeders. They can act now, or more will die.

City officials praise guards but offer little action. The debate exposes a systemic disregard for the safety of those protecting children at crosswalks.

      Advocates Demand Full Parking Ban at T Intersections
      ▸

      Advocates pressed DOT to ban all parking at T intersections. They called the current proposal weak. Parked cars block sightlines. Pedestrians, disabled people, and children pay the price. Speakers demanded daylighting at every intersection. DOT offered no timeline. The danger remains.

      On September 6, 2023, advocates confronted the Department of Transportation (DOT) at a rules hearing over a city proposal to ban parking at curb cuts in T-shaped intersections. The proposal aims to close a 2009 loophole that lets drivers park in crosswalks at three-way junctions if unmarked or lacking signals. The matter summary reads: 'A city proposal to ban parking at curbs cuts in "T" intersections must go further by banning all parking at the edges of such intersections, advocates demanded.' Christine Serdjenian Yearwood, Jean Ryan, Rosamond Gianutsos, and Jackson Chabot spoke out, demanding a total ban and daylighting at all intersections. Advocates argue that parking at T intersections blocks visibility and endangers pedestrians, especially the disabled and children. DOT supports the rule change but gave no timeline. The city still overrides state law requiring 20 feet of clear space at crosswalks. The fight for safety continues.

      DOT: McGuinness Calyer Compromise Redesign Starts Next Week
      ▸

      DOT will break ground on the McGuinness Boulevard redesign next week. The Calyer Compromise brings protected bike lanes, fewer car lanes south of Calyer, and offset crossings. It follows years of deadly crashes. Advocates call it a step forward. Some say it falls short.

      On August 29, 2023, the Department of Transportation announced work will begin on the McGuinness Boulevard redesign, known as the 'Calyer Compromise.' The plan, shaped by controversy and negotiation, keeps two lanes of car and truck traffic in both directions from the Pulaski Bridge to Calyer Street during the day, with a flex lane for parking at night. South of Calyer, the road narrows to one lane each way, with protected bike lanes, loading zones, and offset crossings. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said the compromise will 'save lives and make this corridor much easier to navigate for everyone, including drivers, pedestrians, and cyclists.' He thanked Mayor Adams, elected officials, and the community for their support. Supporters call it the biggest safety change since Robert Moses widened the road. Opponents, like Keep McGuinness Moving, say it ignores community concerns. The redesign comes after years of advocacy following multiple deaths and injuries on the corridor.
