      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Dead Kids, Empty Promises: How Many More Must Die Before the City Acts?

      Written July 2, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 2, 2025

      Children, Cyclists, and the Cost of Delay

      An eight-year-old boy died crossing Eastern Parkway. An SUV hit him at the intersection. His name is not in the data. His age is. The street is busy. The city calls it one of the most dangerous for walkers. The group said, "[Eastern Parkway] acts as a major thoroughfare for drivers, and is the sixth most dangerous street in Brooklyn for pedestrians" (Streetsblog NYC).

      Fourteen people were hurt when a van slammed into a tree in Brooklyn. Twelve of them were adults with special needs, headed to a day program. The driver swerved to avoid another car. A witness said, “It’s sad because they definitely need a lot of attention more than anyone else. I hope that they’re all OK at the end of the day” (ABC7).

      A 17-year-old died riding a moped. A 39-year-old was killed on a motorcycle. The list goes on. In the past 12 months, 244 people have died on city streets. 709 were seriously injured. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers are among them (NYC Open Data).

      Leaders Act—But Not Fast Enough

      The city renewed its speed camera program. Governor Hochul said, “Speed cameras save lives and keep New Yorkers safe. There is no greater priority for me than the safety of New Yorkers” (amNY). Cameras slow some drivers. But the dead keep coming.

      Protected bike lanes work. The city’s own Department of Transportation warned, “Removing the protected bike lane won’t remove cyclists—it will only make the street less safe” (Streetsblog NYC). Still, the mayor ripped out the Bedford Avenue lane. Cyclists are left exposed. The city risks more deaths, more grief.

      The Numbers Are Names

      SUVs and cars kill most. In three years, cars and SUVs killed 300 people and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      Every day of delay is another body on the street. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand that leaders stop tearing out what little safety we have. Do not wait for another child’s name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      From 2022 to July 2025, cars and SUVs killed 300 people and caused over 26,000 injuries. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. Cars and SUVs are the main killers. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speed, street design, and enforcement make the difference. Policies that slow cars and protect walkers save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, keep and expand speed cameras, and redesign streets to protect people, not cars. They can stop removing safety measures.
      ▸ What has the city done lately to improve safety?
      The city renewed its speed camera program and installed some protected bike lanes. But it also removed the Bedford Avenue protected lane, making streets less safe for cyclists and walkers.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured recently?
      In the last 12 months, 244 people died and 709 were seriously injured on New York City streets. NYC Open Data

      Opinion: Protected Bike Lanes Needed in Downtown Jamaica
      ▸

      Downtown Jamaica is a gauntlet for cyclists. Bus lanes cut through, but bikes get no safe path. The city’s plan dead-ends bike lanes at Hillside Avenue. Riders face danger or break the law. Paint is not protection. Cyclists demand real lanes.

      This advocacy opinion, published November 3, 2023, calls out the Jamaica NOW: Urban Design Strategy and Streetscape Plan for failing cyclists. The piece states, 'There is no safe, legal, and easy way to ride through downtown Jamaica. It's time for that to change.' The author supports busways but opposes the current streetscape plan, legalizing cycling on bus lanes, and expanding bike parking without access. The plan leaves the Queens Boulevard bike lane ending at Hillside Avenue, with no safe north-south or east-west bike routes. The author urges protected bike lanes on Sutphin, Parsons, Merrick, and 168th, plus Archer Avenue. The absence of safe infrastructure has left 255 cyclists injured since 2016. The message is clear: red paint is not protection. Downtown Jamaica needs real, separated bike lanes to protect vulnerable road users.

      Lander: City Failed Reckless Driver Law, Calls for Fix
      ▸

      Brad Lander slammed the city for botching his Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program. Few reckless drivers faced real consequences. The law expired. Lander demands reauthorization, tougher penalties, and better enforcement. The Council reviews his call. Vulnerable road users remain exposed.

      On October 26, 2023, Comptroller Brad Lander, author of the Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program (DVAP), criticized the city’s failed rollout of the law. The program, aimed at curbing reckless driving by mandating safety courses and allowing car seizures, expired without replacement. Lander’s report urges the City Council to reauthorize and expand DVAP, improve the safety course, and escalate consequences for repeat offenders. He states, “Without the [program]... the city’s DOT and traffic courts have no tools at their disposal to hold the most dangerous drivers accountable outside of a fine that is currently less than a parking ticket.” Lander supports pending Council and state bills for stricter penalties, including speed governors and registration suspensions. The Council acknowledged the program’s failures and is reviewing Lander’s recommendations, but has not committed to action. Vulnerable road users remain at risk while the city stalls.
