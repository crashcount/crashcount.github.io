      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Asphalt: City Hall’s Deadly Stalling

      Written June 26, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned citations, ensured all quotes have inline links.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Toll of the Streets

      A cyclist rides home from a community event. She never makes it. In Astoria, Amanda Servedio is struck and killed by a driver fleeing police. She is thrown from her bike, her body coming to rest against a parked car. The chase lasts ten minutes through crowded streets. Her father criticized the police pursuit, saying, “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase. It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood.”

      A 17-year-old rides a moped in Brooklyn. He collides with a car. He dies in the hospital. No arrests. The city moves on.

      In the last twelve months, 245 people have died on New York City streets. 717 were seriously injured. Children, elders, cyclists, pedestrians. The numbers do not stop. They do not care who you are.

      Who Bears the Weight

      Cars and trucks are the main killers. In three and a half years, they have caused 299 deaths and over 26,000 injuries to pedestrians, according to NYC Open Data. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10, bikes 11. The city is a machine that chews through bodies.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      The city talks of Vision Zero. It talks of safer streets. It talks of new designs. It talks. This month, the city removed a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. Cyclists gathered. One said of the mayor, “I don’t think he cares if we die.”

      The Department of Transportation announced new barriers at some intersections. Commissioner Rodriguez claimed, “Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety.” It is a start. It is not enough.

      The Next Step: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another funeral. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected lanes that do not vanish. Demand real change. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and Trucks caused 299 deaths and over 26,000 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 10 deaths and over 1,000 injuries. Bikes caused 11 deaths and 1,452 injuries. Source: NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement can save lives. Every delay means more deaths.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits citywide, build protected bike lanes, redesign intersections, and enforce laws against reckless driving. They can act now or answer for more deaths.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured on NYC streets recently?
      In the last 12 months, 245 people died and 717 were seriously injured in traffic crashes. Source: NYC Open Data

      Complete Streets Bill S3897/A8936 Stalled by Governor Hochul
      ▸

      Albany passed the Complete Streets Funding Bill. Governor Hochul has not signed it. Each day of delay means more lives lost. Advocates rally. Streets remain deadly. The law would let cities build safer roads. The governor holds the pen. The blood toll mounts.

      The Complete Streets Funding Bill (S3897/A8936) passed both the New York State Assembly and Senate in 2022. The bill sits unsigned on Governor Hochul's desk. It aims to make it easier for municipalities to fund and build complete streets—roads designed to slow cars and protect people walking or biking. The bill summary states it would 'make it easier for municipalities across New York State to invest in complete streets projects and save lives.' Sandi Vega, a member of Families for Safe Streets, wrote the opinion and called out the governor for inaction. Vega lost her daughter Brittany to a dangerous road. Advocates, including Vega, will rally outside Hochul’s Midtown office to demand action. Every day the bill is not signed, more New Yorkers risk death on unsafe streets.
