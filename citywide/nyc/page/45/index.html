      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Another Child’s Shoe in the Gutter—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 22, 2025)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      A child’s shoe in the gutter. A bicycle bent in the crosswalk. In the last twelve months, 253 people died on New York City streets. 718 more were seriously injured. Behind every number, a family waits for news that will never be good. See the NYC Open Data.

      Children are not spared. Fourteen under 18 died. Sixty-two were left with injuries that will not heal. The old are not safe. Twenty-five over 75 died. The city’s streets do not forgive mistakes, but the mistakes are not theirs.

      Who Bears the Brunt

      Cars and SUVs kill most. In the past three years, sedans and SUVs together took nearly 300 lives and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers do not lie. The bigger the vehicle, the greater the harm.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      The city talks of Vision Zero. They point to new laws and lower speed limits. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can now set its own speed limits. But the limit is not yet lowered. Speed cameras work, but their future is always in doubt. Each year, Albany must vote to keep them on. Each year, advocates must beg for what should be automatic.

      The city built more bike lanes, redesigned intersections, and claimed progress. But the work is slow. The deaths do not wait. The numbers do not fall fast enough.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by those who decide how wide a street should be and how fast a car can go. The city has the tools. It must use them.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand permanent speed cameras. Demand streets where a child can cross without fear.

      Do not wait for another family to join the count. Take action now.

      In Memoriam: Road Violence Toll Spurs Safety Demands
      ▸

      This year, 140 pedestrians and cyclists died on New York streets. Cars and trucks killed them. Each death was preventable. Streets remain wide, fast, and deadly. Enforcement is weak. Political will is missing. The city fails its most vulnerable. The toll mounts.

      This advocacy piece, published December 26, 2022, highlights the deadly toll of road violence in New York City. The article, titled "In Memoriam: So Many Lives Lost to Road Violence — And So Many Lessons We Could Be Learning," details roughly 140 pedestrian and cyclist deaths this year, nearly all caused by drivers. It exposes failures in enforcement, road design, and political will. The article singles out wide, dangerous streets like Linden Boulevard and Pennsylvania Avenue, noting the lack of turn-calming, pedestrian intervals, and bike lanes. No council member is named; the piece is a call to action, not a legislative bill. It urges stronger enforcement, better design, and accountability. The safety impact is clear: preventable deaths continue because the city has not acted. Vulnerable road users pay the price.

      Complete Streets Bill S3897/A8936 Stalled by Governor Hochul
      ▸

      Albany passed the Complete Streets Funding Bill. Governor Hochul has not signed it. Each day of delay means more lives lost. Advocates rally. Streets remain deadly. The law would let cities build safer roads. The governor holds the pen. The blood toll mounts.

      The Complete Streets Funding Bill (S3897/A8936) passed both the New York State Assembly and Senate in 2022. The bill sits unsigned on Governor Hochul's desk. It aims to make it easier for municipalities to fund and build complete streets—roads designed to slow cars and protect people walking or biking. The bill summary states it would 'make it easier for municipalities across New York State to invest in complete streets projects and save lives.' Sandi Vega, a member of Families for Safe Streets, wrote the opinion and called out the governor for inaction. Vega lost her daughter Brittany to a dangerous road. Advocates, including Vega, will rally outside Hochul’s Midtown office to demand action. Every day the bill is not signed, more New Yorkers risk death on unsafe streets.
