      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Parkway, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed citation links, removed headline/body duplication, deduped and sorted citations.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      Another Child Dead, Another Family Broken

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy tried to cross Eastern Parkway in Crown Heights. A black Honda Pilot hit him. He was dragged from under the SUV. Blood pooled on the street. “I saw the kid being dragged from underneath the car by a woman,” a witness said. The boy’s sister stood nearby. The driver stayed. No arrest. The city moved on.

      He was not the only one. In the last twelve months, 241 people died on New York City streets. 706 were seriously injured. Children, elders, cyclists, walkers—the city’s most vulnerable. The numbers do not stop. They only grow.

      The Toll of Inaction

      The city has seen over 80,000 crashes in the past year. Cars and SUVs killed 299 people. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. Each number is a life. Each crash is a family changed forever. The pain is not abstract. It is a mother’s scream, a father’s silence, a child’s empty bed.

      Residents know the danger. “Yeah, this street is dangerous,” said one neighbor. The city knows it too. But the streets remain the same.

      Leaders Move, But Not Fast Enough

      There are new laws. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. Some streets have changed. Most have not. Red-light cameras have been expanded. But the deaths keep coming. When leaders act, they move slow. When they stall, people die. The mayor removed a protected bike lane in Williamsburg. Cyclists were left exposed. “I don’t think he cares if we die,” said one rider.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand It Now

      No more waiting. No more empty promises. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action for every child, every walker, every rider. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Make them choose life.

      NHTSA Eyes AEB Mandate As Truck Deaths Soar 71 Percent
      ▸

      Truck deaths have surged 71 percent since 2009. NHTSA weighs a rule to require automatic emergency braking on new heavy trucks. Advocates demand broader coverage, including medium-duty vehicles and pedestrian detection. Industry pushes back. Lives hang in the balance.

      Federal regulators are considering a rule to mandate automatic emergency braking (AEB) on all new heavy-duty trucks over 26,000 pounds. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) is reviewing this congressionally mandated rule, spurred by a 71 percent rise in truck deaths between 2009 and 2021. The 2021 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act requires AEB only for the largest trucks, leaving out medium-duty vehicles and not mandating systems that detect pedestrians and cyclists. Advocates, including Zach Cahalan, argue, 'To exclude medium-duty at this time is a big miss.' They highlight that AEB has slashed rear-end crashes in Europe and costs less than $300 per truck. Industry claims about cost and reliability are dismissed by safety experts. The debate centers on whether NHTSA will act boldly to protect vulnerable road users from deadly truck crashes.

      Cycling Census: Bike Lanes, Parking Key for Safety, Access
      ▸

      A new survey shows most New York cyclists fear streets. Lack of protected bike lanes and secure parking keep riders off the road. Riders want more safe routes, better parking, and real investment. The call is clear: build safe space or people stay home.

      On May 1, 2023, Urban Cycling Solutions released the New York Cycling Census, supported by the New York State Energy Research and Development Authority. The report states, 'the lack of bike lanes or safe routes represents the most frequent barrier to cycling, according to 73.6 percent of respondents.' CEO Dan Suraci said, 'we see an overwhelming demand for more protected bike infrastructure and trails.' The census urges lawmakers to expand open streets, improve bike parking, and boost safety education. Over 70 percent of confident cyclists say unsafe streets keep them from riding. Forty-four percent cite poor bike parking. The report warns that focusing only on bike lanes misses other dangers. It calls for funding for parking, training, and maintenance. The message: without safe infrastructure, cycling remains a risk few will take.
