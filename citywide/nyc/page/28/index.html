      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Parkway, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed citation links, removed headline/body duplication, deduped and sorted citations.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      Another Child Dead, Another Family Broken

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy tried to cross Eastern Parkway in Crown Heights. A black Honda Pilot hit him. He was dragged from under the SUV. Blood pooled on the street. “I saw the kid being dragged from underneath the car by a woman,” a witness said. The boy’s sister stood nearby. The driver stayed. No arrest. The city moved on.

      He was not the only one. In the last twelve months, 241 people died on New York City streets. 706 were seriously injured. Children, elders, cyclists, walkers—the city’s most vulnerable. The numbers do not stop. They only grow.

      The Toll of Inaction

      The city has seen over 80,000 crashes in the past year. Cars and SUVs killed 299 people. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. Each number is a life. Each crash is a family changed forever. The pain is not abstract. It is a mother’s scream, a father’s silence, a child’s empty bed.

      Residents know the danger. “Yeah, this street is dangerous,” said one neighbor. The city knows it too. But the streets remain the same.

      Leaders Move, But Not Fast Enough

      There are new laws. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. Some streets have changed. Most have not. Red-light cameras have been expanded. But the deaths keep coming. When leaders act, they move slow. When they stall, people die. The mayor removed a protected bike lane in Williamsburg. Cyclists were left exposed. “I don’t think he cares if we die,” said one rider.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand It Now

      No more waiting. No more empty promises. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action for every child, every walker, every rider. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Make them choose life.

      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. These deaths and injuries are preventable. They happen on streets that could be safer, with lower speeds and better design.

      Berry Street Bike Boulevard: Model for Low-Traffic Streets
      ▸

      Berry Street in Brooklyn is now a two-way bike boulevard. The city flipped traffic, painted intersections, and built barriers. Cars are fewer. Pedestrians and cyclists move safer. Residents see more space. Some confusion remains. Advocates want more streets like this.

      On November 21, 2023, the Department of Transportation (DOT) completed a permanent redesign of Berry Street in north Brooklyn. The project, described as a 'model for other streets,' included flipping one-way traffic, coral paint at intersections, curb extensions, and barriers to keep drivers out. The redesign faced delays from supply chain issues and local opposition. Brent Bovenzi, a volunteer with the North Brooklyn Open Streets Community Coalition, praised the changes, saying, 'Ultimately all of those physical things are what every local street in this city should have.' Residents like David Burgos noted, 'It did lower the traffic.' The street now has two-way bike markings, lower speed limits, and expanded pedestrian space. Advocates urge further improvements and expansion. DOT will monitor and adjust as needed.

      Opinion: Road Violence Victims Demand Action for Safe Streets
      ▸

      Three families lost loved ones to reckless drivers. They march with others, demanding leaders act. Traffic violence kills more New Yorkers than guns or fires. Cyclist deaths surge. The call is clear: build safe streets now, not after more blood is spilled.

      This advocacy statement, published November 17, 2023, is titled 'We Are Victims of Road Violence and We Demand Change.' It is not a council bill, but a public call to action. The statement highlights the deaths of Jayden, Karina, and Tammy, killed by reckless drivers. It notes that 219 New Yorkers have died in traffic violence this year, with cyclist injuries up 35 percent in Queens. The authors demand city and state leaders implement universal daylighting, protected bike lanes, Open Streets, school streets, bike boulevards, and neighborhood-wide safety plans. They urge passage of Sammy's Law and expansion of speed and red-light camera programs. The statement condemns the current piecemeal, reactive approach, calling it 'bad policy, bad government,' and demands systemic change to prevent more deaths.
