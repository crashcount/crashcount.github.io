      Speed Kills. Albany Stalls. Another Family Mourns.

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, deduped and cleaned citations.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      In the last twelve months, 260 people died on New York City streets. More than 51,000 were injured. Seven hundred thirty-four suffered wounds so grave they will never be the same. The dead are not numbers. A 76-year-old woman, struck by an SUV while crossing White Plains Road. A 32-year-old woman, crushed by a Jeep on Marcus Garvey Boulevard. A 79-year-old man, found dead in a parked car on West 77th. Each one leaves a hole that cannot be filled. NYC Open Data

      Most of the killing is done by cars and trucks. In three years, they have taken 298 lives and left 498 with serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10, bikes 11. The rest is steel, speed, and neglect.

      Leadership: Words, Delays, and the Fight for Action

      City leaders say the right things. “Speeding kills, and speed cameras save lives,” said State Senator Andrew Gounardes. But the law that keeps speed cameras running is set to expire. If Albany fails, the cameras go dark. Streets go back to the way they were—fast, brutal, and unforgiving. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez warns, “an extremely reckless few continue to endanger themselves and everyone around them.” Patch

      There is talk of a new law for “super speeders.” There is talk of lowering the speed limit to 20 mph. There is always talk. But families keep burying their dead.

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      The city cracks down on cyclists and e-bike riders, not the drivers who do the killing. Community boards call it out. “Holding cyclists to a far higher standard than motor vehicle drivers is unjust, puts immigrant workers at grave risk, and sends the wrong message about the actual danger on our streets,” said Manhattan Community Board 7. The real threat rolls on four wheels, not two.

      What You Can Do: Make Them Hear You

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand the renewal of speed cameras. Join Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Every day you wait is another day someone dies.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 298 deaths and 498 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 10 deaths and 44 serious injuries. Bikes caused 11 deaths and 52 serious injuries. Other vehicle types caused fewer deaths and injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      They are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement save lives. Policy choices—not fate—decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, renew and expand speed camera programs, redesign streets for safety, and stop policies that punish cyclists and pedestrians instead of dangerous drivers.

      NHTSA Updates NCAP: Pedestrian Safety Ratings Coming 2026
      ▸

      NHTSA will rate cars for how they protect people outside. New rules reward features that spot, warn, and brake for pedestrians. Advocates call it overdue. The move targets rising danger from bigger, deadlier vehicles. Change arrives with the 2026 model year.

      On November 19, 2024, the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) announced an update to the New Car Assessment Program (NCAP). The change, effective with 2026 models, adds safety ratings for how vehicles protect pedestrians and cyclists. The new system scores cars for features like blind spot warnings, lane keeping assist, and pedestrian automatic emergency braking. It also introduces a crashworthiness program to measure injury risk to people struck by a vehicle’s front end. Transportation Secretary Pete Buttigieg praised the move, saying, 'these changes to the 5-Star Safety Ratings will speed up adoption of technologies that reduce the frequency and severity of crashes.' Amy Cohen of Families for Safe Streets called the action 'long overdue and will save lives.' Advocates say the update finally centers the safety of those outside the car, not just those inside.
