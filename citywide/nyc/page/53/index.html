      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      No More Excuses. No More Bodies. Fix Our Streets Now.

      Written June 27, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, deduped citations, and ensured proper citation formatting.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Futures Broken

      In the last twelve months, 244 people died on New York City streets. Nearly 50,000 more were injured. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers—no one is spared. In June, a 17-year-old boy died after his moped collided with a car in Brooklyn. His passenger, 14, was thrown to the pavement and left unconscious. Days earlier, a cyclist named Amanda Servedio was killed in Astoria, struck by a man fleeing police through red lights and bike lanes. She was coming home from a ride. She never made it back. The chase lasted ten minutes. Ten minutes of sirens, speed, and fear.

      The Pattern: Policy Moves, But the Blood Stays

      The city talks safety. The city counts bodies. In June, Mayor Adams ripped out a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. He said it was for safety. Cyclists saw it for what it was. “I don’t think he cares if we die,” said Jeanette Samyn. The city rolled out new intersection designs—mini-protected bike lanes, daylighting, barriers. “Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. But the pace is slow. The danger is fast.

      The Blame: Who Pays, Who Waits

      Most deaths come from cars and trucks. In the last three and a half years, they killed 299 people and injured over 26,000. Bikes killed 11. Mopeds and motorcycles killed 10. The city blames speed, then delays lowering limits. The city blames e-bikes, then stalls on safe storage. The city blames the dead, then moves on.

      The Next Step: No More Waiting

      Every day, someone else is lost. The city has the power to lower speed limits. The city can build real protection at every corner. The city can end the bloodletting. But only if you force their hand. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph limit. Demand hardened intersections. Demand action, not excuses.

      If you wait, the street will take someone you love.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and Trucks killed 299 and injured over 26,000 pedestrians. Motorcycles and Mopeds killed 10 and injured about 1,000. Bikes killed 11 and injured about 1,400. Cars and trucks are the main killers. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement save lives. Delay and neglect kill.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, harden intersections, and enforce laws against reckless drivers. They can act now, or answer for more deaths.
      ▸ What has the city done lately to address traffic violence?
      The city removed a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue, rolled out some intersection redesigns, and talked about safety. But the pace is slow and the danger remains.
      ▸ Who is most at risk on New York City streets?
      Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians are most at risk. Cars and trucks do the most harm.

      NYC Lags Behind: Car-Free School Streets Far Fewer Than Paris, London
      ▸

      New York trails global cities in banning cars near schools. Paris, London, and Tirana have hundreds of car-free school streets. NYC has just 41. Streets near schools remain dangerous. City leaders lack urgency. Children, especially the most vulnerable, pay the price.

      This policy comparison, published August 1, 2022, highlights New York City's failure to match global peers on car-free school streets. The report notes, 'A movement to ban cars from streets near schools is taking off globally, with cities like London, Paris, and Tirana implementing hundreds of car-free school streets to protect children from traffic and air pollution.' In contrast, NYC has only 41 such streets. Advocates like Emily Stutts and Simon Battisti criticize the city’s lack of political will, funding, and urgency. Battisti points to Tirana’s swift removal of parking for safety, while David Belliard in Paris calls for cities to prioritize children and vulnerable people over cars. The report underscores that New York’s program is underfunded, limited, and absent from top officials’ agendas. Streets near schools remain hazardous, especially for children of color and low-income families. The city’s inaction leaves vulnerable road users exposed to daily danger.

      Senate GOP Bill Seeks Repeal of Drunk-Driving Tech Mandate
      ▸

      Three Republican senators want to scrap a law forcing new cars to detect drunk drivers. Their bill would erase a rule meant to stop deadly crashes. Safety advocates warn this move could cost lives. The senators cite privacy. Victims get no say.

      On August 1, 2022, Senators Mike Rounds, Mike Braun, and John Cornyn introduced a bill to repeal a key safety measure. The proposal targets a Bipartisan Infrastructure Law provision that requires automakers to install passive drunk-driving detection systems in all new vehicles by 2024. The bill is not yet assigned to a committee. The senators have not issued public statements, but sources cite concerns over driver privacy. The bill’s summary states it would 'overturn a key provision in the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law that requires automakers to install passive drunk-driving detection mechanisms.' Safety advocates argue the technology could prevent up to 94 percent of alcohol-related crash deaths. They say removing the mandate puts vulnerable road users—pedestrians, cyclists, passengers—at greater risk. The debate pits privacy against public safety, but the cost will be measured in lives lost on the street.
