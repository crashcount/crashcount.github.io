      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Another Body on the Asphalt—How Many More Before City Hall Wakes Up?

      Written June 20, 2025 (Revised June 20, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Toll: Death in Plain Sight

      A man lies still on Woodhaven Boulevard. A bus keeps moving. He will not get up again. In the last twelve months, 252 people died on New York City streets. 718 were seriously injured. The city records the numbers. The families remember the names.

      Children are not spared. Fourteen under 18 lost their lives. Thousands more were hurt. The city counts them: 4,124 injuries for children, 62 serious. The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do. city counts them

      Who Pays the Price

      Cars and SUVs kill the most. In three years, they took 299 lives and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. The city is a machine that chews up the young, the old, the careful, the quick. kill the most

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      The city says it is acting. Mayor Adams calls traffic violence a crime. He says, “Every New Yorker deserves safe streets, and thanks to historic investments in better street designs… our streets are safer than ever.” The city builds barriers at corners, hardens crosswalks, and adds bike lanes. The numbers fall, but not fast enough.

      Parents in Brooklyn beg for more. “There is just this sense of impending doom, it’s an obvious danger and if nothing is being done, another terrible thing will happen,” said Chris Roberti. The city studies, pilots, and debates. The cars keep coming.

      The Fight for Safer Streets

      Some leaders act. The city passed Sammy’s Law, letting it lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit is not citywide. Speed cameras save lives, but Albany drags its feet on renewing them. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      Others work against safety. The mayor removed a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. Cyclists are left exposed. “What the fuck! Where are we supposed to ride?” asked Mel Regalario. There is no answer.

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      Act Now: Demand Action

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand more barriers, more cameras, more safe crossings. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Take action now.

      NYC Lags Behind: Car-Free School Streets Far Fewer Than Paris, London
      ▸

      New York trails global cities in banning cars near schools. Paris, London, and Tirana have hundreds of car-free school streets. NYC has just 41. Streets near schools remain dangerous. City leaders lack urgency. Children, especially the most vulnerable, pay the price.

      This policy comparison, published August 1, 2022, highlights New York City's failure to match global peers on car-free school streets. The report notes, 'A movement to ban cars from streets near schools is taking off globally, with cities like London, Paris, and Tirana implementing hundreds of car-free school streets to protect children from traffic and air pollution.' In contrast, NYC has only 41 such streets. Advocates like Emily Stutts and Simon Battisti criticize the city’s lack of political will, funding, and urgency. Battisti points to Tirana’s swift removal of parking for safety, while David Belliard in Paris calls for cities to prioritize children and vulnerable people over cars. The report underscores that New York’s program is underfunded, limited, and absent from top officials’ agendas. Streets near schools remain hazardous, especially for children of color and low-income families. The city’s inaction leaves vulnerable road users exposed to daily danger.

      Senate GOP Bill Seeks Repeal of Drunk-Driving Tech Mandate
      ▸

      Three Republican senators want to scrap a law forcing new cars to detect drunk drivers. Their bill would erase a rule meant to stop deadly crashes. Safety advocates warn this move could cost lives. The senators cite privacy. Victims get no say.

      On August 1, 2022, Senators Mike Rounds, Mike Braun, and John Cornyn introduced a bill to repeal a key safety measure. The proposal targets a Bipartisan Infrastructure Law provision that requires automakers to install passive drunk-driving detection systems in all new vehicles by 2024. The bill is not yet assigned to a committee. The senators have not issued public statements, but sources cite concerns over driver privacy. The bill’s summary states it would 'overturn a key provision in the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law that requires automakers to install passive drunk-driving detection mechanisms.' Safety advocates argue the technology could prevent up to 94 percent of alcohol-related crash deaths. They say removing the mandate puts vulnerable road users—pedestrians, cyclists, passengers—at greater risk. The debate pits privacy against public safety, but the cost will be measured in lives lost on the street.
