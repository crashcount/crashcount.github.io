      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Another Child’s Shoe in the Gutter—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 21, 2025)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      A child’s shoe in the gutter. A bicycle bent in the crosswalk. In the last twelve months, 253 people died on New York City streets. 718 more were seriously injured. Behind every number, a family waits for news that will never be good. See the NYC Open Data.

      Children are not spared. Fourteen under 18 died. Sixty-two were left with injuries that will not heal. The old are not safe. Twenty-five over 75 died. The city’s streets do not forgive mistakes, but the mistakes are not theirs.

      Who Bears the Brunt

      Cars and SUVs kill most. In the past three years, sedans and SUVs together took nearly 300 lives and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers do not lie. The bigger the vehicle, the greater the harm.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      The city talks of Vision Zero. They point to new laws and lower speed limits. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can now set its own speed limits. But the limit is not yet lowered. Speed cameras work, but their future is always in doubt. Each year, Albany must vote to keep them on. Each year, advocates must beg for what should be automatic.

      The city built more bike lanes, redesigned intersections, and claimed progress. But the work is slow. The deaths do not wait. The numbers do not fall fast enough.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by those who decide how wide a street should be and how fast a car can go. The city has the tools. It must use them.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand permanent speed cameras. Demand streets where a child can cross without fear.

      Do not wait for another family to join the count. Take action now.

      DOT: Protected Bike Lane Approved for Ashland Place, Brooklyn
      ▸

      DOT will build a two-way protected bike lane on Ashland Place and Navy Street. Cyclists and pedestrians face daily danger here. Reckless drivers speed off the BQE. Crashes have injured over 100 people since 2018. The redesign adds refuge islands and narrows crossings.

      On June 20, 2022, the Department of Transportation announced plans for a two-way protected bike lane along Ashland Place and Navy Street in Downtown Brooklyn. The project, presented to Community Board 2's Transportation Committee, targets a 'dangerous, narrow stretch of roadway' connecting the Brooklyn and Manhattan Bridges. DOT will convert Ashland Place to a one-way street northbound, install concrete barriers, and add pedestrian refuge islands. The matter summary states, 'The city will install a two-way protected bike lane along Ashland Place and Navy Street, a dangerous, narrow stretch of roadway.' Community Board 2 members and advocates like Jon Orcutt supported the plan, with only one complaint: it should have come sooner. Jeanette Brown, a local resident, called for more sidewalk space for pedestrians. Since 2018, 297 crashes have caused 111 injuries, including 22 cyclists and 28 pedestrians. The redesign aims to cut these numbers by separating people from traffic.

      Portland Study: Unhoused Pedestrians Face Deadly Streets
      ▸

      Unhoused pedestrians die at alarming rates. Streets kill, not tents. Sweeps push people into danger. Advocates demand safer roads, lower speeds, better lighting, and housing. Systemic neglect, reckless driving, and bad design fuel the crisis. The fix: redesign, not displacement.

      This policy analysis, released June 13, 2022, by Portland State University, exposes the deadly toll of car crashes on unhoused pedestrians. Seventy percent of pedestrians killed in Portland last year lacked permanent housing. The report states: 'The presence of unhoused people does not make our streets unsafe. Rather, poor roadway design, ongoing neglect and deferred maintenance, recklessness in the form of speeding, operating a vehicle while impaired by drugs or alcohol, and other dangerous driving behavior are all well-documented reasons why there is this alarming uptick in deaths.' No council member is named; the report draws on research and advocates’ voices. The analysis condemns sweeps and bans on campsites, arguing these policies force people into more dangerous corridors and away from services. The study calls for ending sweeps, providing housing, redesigning streets, lowering speed limits, improving lighting, and closing high-crash roads to cars. The message: systemic change, not scapegoating, will save lives.

      • How To Save the Lives of Unhoused Pedestrians, Streetsblog NYC, Published 2022-06-13
