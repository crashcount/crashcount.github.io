      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      NYC Streets Run Red—How Many Kids Must Die Before City Hall Acts?

      Written June 29, 2025 (Revised June 29, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      Children, Blood, and Concrete

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy crossed Eastern Parkway with his sister. A black SUV hit him. He died on the street. The driver stayed. Police said, “They were walking, he was crossing the street with his sister” (police said). No arrest. The street is still open. The danger is still there.

      The day before, in the Bronx, a man ran a red light. His car jumped the curb and crushed three people on the sidewalk. One is still fighting for life. The driver ran but police caught him. He faces charges for assault, fleeing, and driving without a license (faces charges).

      In the last 12 months, 244 people have died on New York City streets. 713 were seriously hurt. Thirteen of the dead were children. The city logs over 81,000 crashes a year. Each number is a name, a family, a future gone.

      The Policy That Wasn’t

      Leaders talk. Streets stay the same. The mayor ripped out a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue after crashes involving children. He said he was listening to the community. Cyclists said, “I don’t think he cares if we die” (Cyclists said). The city put back paint. Paint does not stop cars.

      Elsewhere, the Department of Transportation built a new protected bike lane in Queens. A commuter said, “The old street was very dangerous… the new path is definitely safer because he’s no longer competing with trucks and cars for road space” (A commuter said). But these changes come slow. Too slow for the dead.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Cars and trucks killed 299 people. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 21. Bikes killed 11. The city counts 182,570 injuries since 2022. The bodies pile up. The city debates.

      What Now

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action, not words. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is policy.

      Take action now.

      ▸

      On June 23, 2022, a crash on Broadway left several people critically injured when a taxi mounted the curb. The incident reignited calls for pedestrian safety from business improvement districts (BIDs) and advocates. The matter, described as 'renewed calls from BIDs and safe-streets advocates for accelerated pedestrian improvements and full pedestrianization of Broadway,' highlights the slow progress of the city's 'Broadway Vision' project, which began in 2009 and has only fully pedestrianized six blocks. BID leaders James Mettham, Jennifer Falk, and Tom Harris voiced strong support for expanding pedestrian areas and protected bike lanes. Cory Epstein of Transportation Alternatives called pedestrianization a 'win-win.' Mayor Adams, however, showed reluctance, deferring decisions to the transportation commissioner. The Department of Transportation promised more information soon. Advocates and BIDs continue to push for a car-free Broadway, citing proven safety and business benefits.
