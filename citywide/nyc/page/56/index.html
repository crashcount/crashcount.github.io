      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      No Time Left: City Hall Stalls, New Yorkers Die

      Written July 9, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Toll This Week

      The city bleeds. In the last twelve months, 241 people died and 701 suffered serious injuries on New York’s streets. The dead do not get second chances. The injured carry scars no policy can erase. Just days ago, a 90-year-old man lay on the pavement in Brooklyn, his head split open after a masked moped rider hit him and fled. “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open,” said an eyewitness. The rider did not stop. The city keeps moving.

      A day before, Dov Broyde, 70, was crossing Avenue I. A Smart Car driver plowed into him. Broyde died at the hospital. The driver stayed. No charges filed. Fifty-five pedestrians have died this year. The number is lower than last year. It is not low enough. The report confirms it.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      The city has power now. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. New York can set its own speed limits. Some streets have dropped to 20 mph. But the citywide limit remains unchanged. The law sits. The council waits. The mayor waits. Lives do not wait.

      Some leaders fight for safety. Others rip it out. The city tried to remove the Bedford Avenue protected bike lane. DOT warned: “Removing the protected bike lane won’t remove cyclists—it will only make the street less safe” testified DOT. The fight drags on. Cyclists and pedestrians pay the price.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do the most harm. In three years, they killed 301 people and injured 26,959. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers are not just numbers. They are bodies in the street. They are families left waiting for someone who will not come home.

      What Now

      Every day the council delays, someone else dies. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand action. The city belongs to the living, not to the dead.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      From 2022 to July 2025, cars and SUVs killed 301 and injured 26,959 pedestrians. Trucks and buses killed 93 and injured 2,277. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10 and injured 1,074. Bikes killed 11 and injured 1,459. Cars and SUVs are the main killers.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speeds, protected bike lanes, and better street design save lives. Delay costs lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the citywide speed limit to 20 mph, install more protected bike lanes, and keep safety infrastructure in place. They can act now or answer for every life lost.

      DOT: Protected Bike Lane Approved for Ashland Place, Brooklyn
      ▸

      DOT will build a two-way protected bike lane on Ashland Place and Navy Street. Cyclists and pedestrians face daily danger here. Reckless drivers speed off the BQE. Crashes have injured over 100 people since 2018. The redesign adds refuge islands and narrows crossings.

      On June 20, 2022, the Department of Transportation announced plans for a two-way protected bike lane along Ashland Place and Navy Street in Downtown Brooklyn. The project, presented to Community Board 2's Transportation Committee, targets a 'dangerous, narrow stretch of roadway' connecting the Brooklyn and Manhattan Bridges. DOT will convert Ashland Place to a one-way street northbound, install concrete barriers, and add pedestrian refuge islands. The matter summary states, 'The city will install a two-way protected bike lane along Ashland Place and Navy Street, a dangerous, narrow stretch of roadway.' Community Board 2 members and advocates like Jon Orcutt supported the plan, with only one complaint: it should have come sooner. Jeanette Brown, a local resident, called for more sidewalk space for pedestrians. Since 2018, 297 crashes have caused 111 injuries, including 22 cyclists and 28 pedestrians. The redesign aims to cut these numbers by separating people from traffic.
