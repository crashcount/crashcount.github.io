      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Dead Kids, Empty Promises: How Many More Must Die Before the City Acts?

      Written July 2, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 2, 2025

      Children, Cyclists, and the Cost of Delay

      An eight-year-old boy died crossing Eastern Parkway. An SUV hit him at the intersection. His name is not in the data. His age is. The street is busy. The city calls it one of the most dangerous for walkers. The group said, "[Eastern Parkway] acts as a major thoroughfare for drivers, and is the sixth most dangerous street in Brooklyn for pedestrians" (Streetsblog NYC).

      Fourteen people were hurt when a van slammed into a tree in Brooklyn. Twelve of them were adults with special needs, headed to a day program. The driver swerved to avoid another car. A witness said, “It’s sad because they definitely need a lot of attention more than anyone else. I hope that they’re all OK at the end of the day” (ABC7).

      A 17-year-old died riding a moped. A 39-year-old was killed on a motorcycle. The list goes on. In the past 12 months, 244 people have died on city streets. 709 were seriously injured. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers are among them (NYC Open Data).

      Leaders Act—But Not Fast Enough

      The city renewed its speed camera program. Governor Hochul said, “Speed cameras save lives and keep New Yorkers safe. There is no greater priority for me than the safety of New Yorkers” (amNY). Cameras slow some drivers. But the dead keep coming.

      Protected bike lanes work. The city’s own Department of Transportation warned, “Removing the protected bike lane won’t remove cyclists—it will only make the street less safe” (Streetsblog NYC). Still, the mayor ripped out the Bedford Avenue lane. Cyclists are left exposed. The city risks more deaths, more grief.

      The Numbers Are Names

      SUVs and cars kill most. In three years, cars and SUVs killed 300 people and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      Every day of delay is another body on the street. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand that leaders stop tearing out what little safety we have. Do not wait for another child’s name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      From 2022 to July 2025, cars and SUVs killed 300 people and caused over 26,000 injuries. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. Cars and SUVs are the main killers. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speed, street design, and enforcement make the difference. Policies that slow cars and protect walkers save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, keep and expand speed cameras, and redesign streets to protect people, not cars. They can stop removing safety measures.
      ▸ What has the city done lately to improve safety?
      The city renewed its speed camera program and installed some protected bike lanes. But it also removed the Bedford Avenue protected lane, making streets less safe for cyclists and walkers.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured recently?
      In the last 12 months, 244 people died and 709 were seriously injured on New York City streets. NYC Open Data

      Queens Boulevard: Swap Car Lanes for Bus, Bike Safety
      ▸

      Queens Boulevard remains a danger zone. The city’s plan keeps eight car lanes, weak bike protection, and slow buses. Cyclists dodge cars. Bus riders wait. Drivers rule. Real safety demands fewer car lanes, center bus lanes, and true bike barriers.

      This opinion piece, published April 26, 2022, calls out the Department of Transportation’s plan for Queens Boulevard. The plan, part of the 'Great Streets' project, keeps eight car lanes and installs only lightly protected bike lanes with mountable curbs. Samuel Santaella, the author, writes: 'the department has discriminated against thousands of bikers and 13,630 pre-pandemic daily bus riders in order to privilege the needs of 40,000 daily motorists.' Santaella opposes the current DOT plan and urges a redesign: reduce car lanes to four, add center-running bus lanes, fully separated bikeways, and wider sidewalks. He warns that mountable curbs let drivers invade bike lanes, putting cyclists at risk. Bus riders see little benefit, still stuck behind double-parked cars. The piece demands the city stop favoring drivers and start protecting vulnerable road users.

      Study: Asphalt Art Halves Vulnerable Road User Crashes
      ▸

      A Bloomberg study finds asphalt art slashes crashes with pedestrians and cyclists by half. Colorful crosswalks and murals slow drivers. Injury crashes drop. Drivers yield more. Despite proven gains, federal rules threaten a ban. Advocates say art saves lives.

      On April 22, 2022, Bloomberg Philanthropies released a study showing that asphalt art—colorful crosswalks and curb murals—cuts crashes involving vulnerable road users by 50 percent. The report, titled "Asphalt Art Decreases Vulnerable Road User Crashes By 50 Percent," analyzed 17 U.S. sites before and after art installations. Injury crashes fell 37 percent; overall crashes dropped 17 percent. Drivers yielded to pedestrians 27 percent more often. Despite these results, the Manual of Uniform Traffic Control Devices may ban asphalt art, citing concerns about autonomous vehicles and accessibility for low-vision pedestrians. Janette Sadik-Khan called the projects "real results for just pennies on the dollar." Mike Flynn urged more study on accessibility. Advocates argue asphalt art is a cheap, effective tool for safer streets.

      Report: First Quarter 2022 Deadliest Since Vision Zero
      ▸

      Traffic deaths surged 44 percent in early 2022. Fifty-nine killed in three months. Pedestrians, children, seniors hit hardest. Queens and Manhattan saw the worst spikes. Advocates demand urgent action—redesign streets, expand cameras, fund safety. City leaders stall. Streets remain lethal.

      This report, published April 20, 2022, highlights a grim milestone: the first quarter of 2022 marked the deadliest start to any year since Vision Zero began in 2014. Transportation Alternatives found 59 people killed in traffic crashes in just three months, a 44 percent jump from last year. The report states, 'Traffic fatalities are up 44 percent so far this year—the deadliest start to any year since Vision Zero launched.' Danny Harris, executive director of Transportation Alternatives, called on Mayor Adams to include the City Council’s $3.1 billion Streets Master Plan request and to fast-track redesigns of 1,000 intersections. Elke Weiss of Families for Safe Streets urged passage of the Crash Victim Rights and Safety Act, saying, 'Crossing the street should not be a life-or-death risk.' The report urges more car-free space, stronger driver accountability, and expanded speed and red-light camera programs. Despite Vision Zero’s promise, the city’s streets remain deadly for its most vulnerable.
