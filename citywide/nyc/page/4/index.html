      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Red Lights, Dead Pedestrians—Speed Kills, City Stalls

      Written July 13, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Toll This Month

      Two men, Kex Un Chen and Faqiu Lin, crossed Third Avenue in Brooklyn on Friday morning. They did not make it to the other side. A BMW ran the red light, hit them, and kept going. Both died in the street. The driver, 23, was found later and charged with manslaughter and homicide. “He was immediately given a breathalyzer test and had a blood alcohol content of 0.06%, under the legal limit, prosecutors said.” The law draws a line. The bodies do not care.

      In the last twelve months, 261 people have died on New York City streets. Over 52,000 have been injured. 744 suffered serious injuries—the kind that end a career, a childhood, a life as it was. NYC Open Data

      The Patterns That Kill

      The dead are not numbers. They are the old and the young. Eleven children died in the last year. Twenty-six people over 75. The city’s most vulnerable are the first to fall.

      The machines that kill most often are cars and SUVs. 311 deaths in three years. Trucks and buses: 95. Motorcycles and mopeds: 10. Bikes: 11. NYC Open Data

      The intersection at 52nd Street and Third Avenue has seen 59 collisions and 80 injuries in five years. The lines are painted, the signals blink, but the danger does not leave. “Over the past five years, the three-block radius around the site of Friday’s fatal crash has seen 59 collisions resulting in about 80 injuries, data from CrashMapper shows.”

      What Leaders Have Done—And Not Done

      The city has lowered some speed limits. It has renewed speed cameras. It has started to daylight intersections and build more bike lanes. The mayor calls the drop in deaths “historic” and vows to continue using all tools available to drive down traffic violence.

      But the pace is slow. The city has the power to set a 20 mph limit everywhere. It has not used it. The worst drivers keep driving. The streets keep bleeding.

      What You Must Do

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand action against repeat offenders. Every day of delay is another life lost. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Take action now.

      Helicopter Breaks Apart Over Hudson River
      ▸

      Six died when a sightseeing helicopter shattered midair over the Hudson. The fuselage, rotor, and tail tore loose. Loud bangs echoed. The craft plunged. No black box. No warning. Only fragments and silence left behind.

      NY Daily News reported on May 7, 2025, that federal officials released images showing a sightseeing helicopter breaking apart in midair before crashing into the Hudson River, killing six. The National Transportation Safety Board’s preliminary report details how the Bell 206L-4 split into three pieces: 'the fuselage, the main rotor system, and the tail boom.' The aircraft had a prior maintenance issue with its transmission assembly and lacked flight data recorders. The NTSB noted the pilot wore video-capable sunglasses, but they remain missing. The crash highlights gaps in oversight and the risks of repeated tour flights without robust recording or inspection requirements.
