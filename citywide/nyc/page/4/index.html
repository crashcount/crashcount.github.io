      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Parkway, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed citation links, removed headline/body duplication, deduped and sorted citations.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      Another Child Dead, Another Family Broken

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy tried to cross Eastern Parkway in Crown Heights. A black Honda Pilot hit him. He was dragged from under the SUV. Blood pooled on the street. “I saw the kid being dragged from underneath the car by a woman,” a witness said. The boy’s sister stood nearby. The driver stayed. No arrest. The city moved on.

      He was not the only one. In the last twelve months, 241 people died on New York City streets. 706 were seriously injured. Children, elders, cyclists, walkers—the city’s most vulnerable. The numbers do not stop. They only grow.

      The Toll of Inaction

      The city has seen over 80,000 crashes in the past year. Cars and SUVs killed 299 people. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. Each number is a life. Each crash is a family changed forever. The pain is not abstract. It is a mother’s scream, a father’s silence, a child’s empty bed.

      Residents know the danger. “Yeah, this street is dangerous,” said one neighbor. The city knows it too. But the streets remain the same.

      Leaders Move, But Not Fast Enough

      There are new laws. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. Some streets have changed. Most have not. Red-light cameras have been expanded. But the deaths keep coming. When leaders act, they move slow. When they stall, people die. The mayor removed a protected bike lane in Williamsburg. Cyclists were left exposed. “I don’t think he cares if we die,” said one rider.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand It Now

      No more waiting. No more empty promises. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action for every child, every walker, every rider. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Make them choose life.

      The Stop Super Speeders Bill (S7621) is under debate. It would require speed limiter technology for drivers with six or more automated enforcement tickets in a year. The bill is supported by Amber Adler of Families for Safe Streets and Ben Furnas of Transportation Alternatives, who call out the deadly pattern of repeat offenders. Furnas says, 'These results point to a small population with a shocking pattern of recidivism, resistance to traditional deterrents, and disregard for human life.' Adler points to a recent fatal crash by a repeat offender as proof of legislative failure. Assembly Member Michael Novakhov opposes the bill, claiming six tickets is not excessive. The NYPD withholds license plate data, blocking public scrutiny. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program has expired, leaving no replacement. Advocates say the bill is urgent to stop the next tragedy.

      DOT Bike Lane Plan Under Astoria El Targets Deadly Stretch
      ▸

      DOT will install curbside parking-protected bike lanes under Astoria’s elevated tracks. The redesign covers a mile of 31st Street, one of Queens’ most dangerous roads. Painted pedestrian islands and daylighting will shorten crossings. No driving or parking lanes are removed.

      On April 8, 2025, the NYC Department of Transportation announced plans to install protected bike lanes under the elevated subway tracks on 31st Street in Astoria, Queens. The project, not a council bill but a DOT action, targets a stretch ranked in the top 10 percent for severe injuries and deaths. DOT will add eight-foot-wide bike lanes with three-foot buffers, painted pedestrian islands, and daylighting at corners. The plan does not remove driving or parking lanes but reorganizes car storage to align with track columns. DOT planner Rachael Metz called the roadbed chaotic, with frequent illegal double-parking and blocked sight lines. Community board member Nas Magoutas said, 'I feel like I’ve been waiting 30 years for it.' The project follows similar redesigns in the Bronx and will begin after July 1, 2025, taking about two months to complete.
