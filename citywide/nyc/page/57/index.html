      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Death Is Winning on New York’s Streets—Who Will Stop It?

      Written July 7, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Toll Mounts, Week After Week

      A man lies in the street. His son stands over him, waiting for his eyes to open. They never do. Just days ago, Pasqual Tziquin was killed crossing New Utrecht Avenue in Brooklyn. The driver did not stop. “He didn’t stop. He just kept going,” said Henry Tziquin. Eight days later, Pasqual was dead. His family waits for justice.

      Across the city, the violence does not let up. On the Belt Parkway, a BMW vaulted the median and slammed into oncoming cars. Six people were hurt, two left fighting for their lives. Police said, “speed may have been an issue” in the crash. In Crown Heights, an eight-year-old boy was struck and killed by an SUV. The street is a main road, built for speed. The boy is gone.

      The Numbers Behind the Blood

      In the last twelve months, 236 people have died on New York City streets. Another 704 suffered serious injuries. Children, elders, cyclists, pedestrians—no one is spared. SUVs and cars do the most harm: SUVs and cars killed 300 people, seriously injured 509, and hurt over 26,000 more in the last three years. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers do not lie. The bodies pile up.

      Leaders Act—But Not Fast Enough

      Some leaders move. The city has started to lower speed limits, install more speed cameras, and build protected bike lanes. But too often, safety is ripped out as fast as it is built. When the city tried to remove the Bedford Avenue bike lane, the Department of Transportation warned: “Removing the protected bike lane won’t remove cyclists—it will only make the street less safe” testified DOT.

      Others push policies that punish the vulnerable. A 15 mph e-bike speed cap is on the table. Michael Replogle, former DOT official, calls it “an ill-considered idea to improve safety which will be counterproductive” said Replogle. The city debates, the streets bleed.

      Act Now—Or Count the Dead

      Every day of delay is another day of loss. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Tell them to protect bike lanes, not rip them out. Tell them to stop blaming the people on foot and on bikes. The bodies in the street are not statistics. They are your neighbors. They are your children. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      In the last three years, cars and SUVs killed 300 people and seriously injured 509. Trucks and buses killed 93 and seriously injured 103. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10 and seriously injured 44. Bikes killed 11 and seriously injured 54. Cars and SUVs do the most harm.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speeds, protected bike lanes, and safer street design save lives. Every delay means more loss.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits citywide, protect and expand bike lanes, keep speed cameras running, and focus enforcement on dangerous driving—not on people walking or biking.
      ▸ What recent steps has NYC taken to improve street safety?
      NYC has started lowering speed limits, expanded speed cameras, and built new protected bike lanes. But some leaders have also removed safety infrastructure or pushed policies that punish cyclists.

      Sam Schwartz wants a car lane for cyclists on the Henry Hudson Parkway. The Parks Department plans a dangerous detour on Broadway. Advocates say the city ignores cyclist safety. Crash data shows the detour route is unsafe. City Hall faces pressure to act.

      On June 10, 2022, former traffic commissioner Sam Schwartz called for a temporary bike lane on the northbound Henry Hudson Parkway during a planned closure of the Hudson River Greenway. The Parks Department's detour sends cyclists onto Broadway, a route Schwartz and advocates call 'dangerous and congested.' Schwartz said, 'Taking a northbound lane and having a temporary bike path...is far superior and much safer than sending bike riders through the street system of upper Manhattan.' Community Board 12 previously requested a study for this solution, but the city did not act. Critics, including residents and transit advocates, accuse the Parks Department of neglecting cyclists and demand City Hall treat the greenway as essential transportation.       Bill to Slash 60,000 Placards, Enable Civilian Parking Reports
      ▸

      Lincoln Restler targets placard abuse. He introduces two bills. One slashes city-issued parking permits. The other lets civilians report illegal parking. Streets clogged by city workers’ cars. Cyclists, parents, wheelchair users forced into danger. Restler calls it petty corruption.

      On June 2, 2022, Councilmember Lincoln Restler (District 33) introduced two bills to the New York City Council. The bills aim to revoke about 60,000 city-issued parking placards and allow civilians to report illegal parking. The legislation, not yet passed or assigned to a committee, is described as a response to 'petty corruption' that endangers pedestrians, people with disabilities, and cyclists. Restler said, 'It is petty corruption and it makes our streets unsafe for a parent walking with a stroller, for a person with a wheelchair, for a cyclist.' The first bill would sharply restrict city agencies from issuing parking permits for private cars, with narrow exemptions. The second would empower civilians to report cars blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants, creating a $175 fine and a reward for reporters. Restler’s district, including Downtown Brooklyn, suffers from rampant placard abuse. The bills seek to reclaim street space and protect vulnerable road users.

      Council Bill Targets Placard Abuse in Downtown Brooklyn
      ▸

      Placard abuse blocks sidewalks, endangers cyclists, and forces parents and wheelchair users into traffic. Council Member Lincoln Restler pushes a bill to revoke most personal placards and let citizens report abuse. The goal: end corruption and reclaim public space.

      On June 2, 2022, Council Member Lincoln Restler introduced legislation in the New York City Council to crack down on placard abuse. The bill would revoke all placards for personal vehicles without government plates, except for those granted by collective bargaining or disability. Restler’s proposal, echoing a prior bill from Stephen Levin, would also empower citizens to report placard abuse to the Department of Transportation, triggering civil penalties. The matter summary reads: 'Placard abuse forces neighbors using wheelchairs to navigate unsafe sidewalks, cyclists to go up against oncoming traffic, and parents to push strollers into the street.' Restler, representing Downtown Brooklyn, Williamsburg, and Greenpoint, has refused a placard himself. The bill aims to eliminate roughly 60,000 placards and restore safety and access for vulnerable road users.
