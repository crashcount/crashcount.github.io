      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Another Child’s Shoe in the Gutter—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 22, 2025)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      A child’s shoe in the gutter. A bicycle bent in the crosswalk. In the last twelve months, 253 people died on New York City streets. 718 more were seriously injured. Behind every number, a family waits for news that will never be good. See the NYC Open Data.

      Children are not spared. Fourteen under 18 died. Sixty-two were left with injuries that will not heal. The old are not safe. Twenty-five over 75 died. The city’s streets do not forgive mistakes, but the mistakes are not theirs.

      Who Bears the Brunt

      Cars and SUVs kill most. In the past three years, sedans and SUVs together took nearly 300 lives and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers do not lie. The bigger the vehicle, the greater the harm.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      The city talks of Vision Zero. They point to new laws and lower speed limits. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can now set its own speed limits. But the limit is not yet lowered. Speed cameras work, but their future is always in doubt. Each year, Albany must vote to keep them on. Each year, advocates must beg for what should be automatic.

      The city built more bike lanes, redesigned intersections, and claimed progress. But the work is slow. The deaths do not wait. The numbers do not fall fast enough.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by those who decide how wide a street should be and how fast a car can go. The city has the tools. It must use them.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand permanent speed cameras. Demand streets where a child can cross without fear.

      Do not wait for another family to join the count. Take action now.

      NTSB Chair Homendy Rejects 94% Human Error Crash Claim
      ▸

      NTSB Chair Jennifer Homendy calls out the false claim that 94 percent of crashes are human error. She says crashes are not just driver mistakes. Road design, policy, and agency choices matter. She urges a shift to systemic safety. Zero deaths is the goal.

      On January 31, 2022, NTSB Chair Jennifer Homendy issued a federal agency policy statement, challenging the long-cited claim that '94 percent of car crashes are caused by human error.' Homendy said, 'It ain’t 94 percent,' and criticized the statistic for ignoring systemic factors. She called for a 'Safe Systems' approach, stating, 'everybody needs to come to the table to prevent that death.' Homendy praised USDOT’s adoption of this approach and its commitment to zero roadway deaths. She stressed that responsibility is shared among road users, planners, engineers, and regulators. Homendy’s stance rejects blaming individuals alone and demands action from all levels of government to protect vulnerable road users.

      Crash Victim Rights and Safety Act: Albany Advocacy Push Begins
      ▸

      Fourteen groups unite. They demand Albany act. Lower speed limits. Safer street design. Three-foot passing law. Rights for crash victims. Lawmakers hold the power. Streets stay deadly. Families mourn. Advocates press for change. The fight for safety surges again.

      On January 27, 2022, the New York State Safe Streets Coalition launched a campaign for the Crash Victim Rights and Safety Act. The coalition, made up of 14 advocacy groups, called on Albany lawmakers to pass a legislative package including 'Sammy's Law' (S524/A4655), statewide speed limit authority (S2021/A1007), Complete Streets funding and maintenance (S3897/A6454A, S5130/A7782), a three-foot passing law (S4529/A547), a DMV pre-licensing safety course (S1078A/A5084), and a Crash Victim Bill of Rights (S8152). The matter title reads: 'Activists are gathering under a new statewide umbrella... to once again demand that Albany lawmakers make roadways safer, force drivers to slow down, and protect victims.' Sara Lind of Open Plans and Amy Cohen of Families for Safe Streets led the call, demanding proven tools to end traffic violence. The coalition’s push highlights the urgent need for systemic change to protect vulnerable road users.

      USDOT National Roadway Safety Strategy: Vision Zero Commitment
      ▸

      USDOT pledges to end road deaths. Secretary Buttigieg launches a new safety strategy. The plan backs Vision Zero, safer streets, and lower speeds. Critics say it ignores deadly vehicle size and leaves highway funds untouched. Action, not words, will decide lives.

      On January 27, 2022, the United States Department of Transportation announced the National Roadway Safety Strategy. Secretary Pete Buttigieg declared, "Zero is the only acceptable number of deaths and serious injuries on our roadways." The plan, rooted in the Safe System Approach, promises safer people, roads, vehicles, speeds, and post-crash care. It revises speed limit guidelines and launches a Complete Streets Initiative. Buttigieg’s move marks a shift from blaming individuals to fixing systems. But advocates like Beth Osborne warn the plan fails to address the rising danger of large vehicles and does not require all DOT funds to be spent on safety. Most federal money still flows to highways. The plan’s success depends on swift, sweeping action—not just promises.
