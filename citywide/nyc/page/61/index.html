      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Another Child’s Shoe in the Gutter—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 22, 2025)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      A child’s shoe in the gutter. A bicycle bent in the crosswalk. In the last twelve months, 253 people died on New York City streets. 718 more were seriously injured. Behind every number, a family waits for news that will never be good. See the NYC Open Data.

      Children are not spared. Fourteen under 18 died. Sixty-two were left with injuries that will not heal. The old are not safe. Twenty-five over 75 died. The city’s streets do not forgive mistakes, but the mistakes are not theirs.

      Who Bears the Brunt

      Cars and SUVs kill most. In the past three years, sedans and SUVs together took nearly 300 lives and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers do not lie. The bigger the vehicle, the greater the harm.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      The city talks of Vision Zero. They point to new laws and lower speed limits. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can now set its own speed limits. But the limit is not yet lowered. Speed cameras work, but their future is always in doubt. Each year, Albany must vote to keep them on. Each year, advocates must beg for what should be automatic.

      The city built more bike lanes, redesigned intersections, and claimed progress. But the work is slow. The deaths do not wait. The numbers do not fall fast enough.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by those who decide how wide a street should be and how fast a car can go. The city has the tools. It must use them.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand permanent speed cameras. Demand streets where a child can cross without fear.

      Do not wait for another family to join the count. Take action now.

      March Parking Madness 2022: NYPD Precincts Endanger Neighbors
      ▸

      NYPD precincts choke blocks with cars. Cops park on sidewalks, block ramps, erase bike lanes. Streets turn chaotic. Crash rates soar. Pedestrians, cyclists, wheelchair users face daily risk. The NYPD ignores safety, fueling danger for all who walk or ride.

      This media report, published March 7, 2022, by Streetsblog NYC, exposes the dangers caused by NYPD precinct parking practices. The annual 'March (Parking) Madness' tournament highlights how 16 precincts compete for the title of 'Worst Neighbor.' The article states: 'cops commandeer all the parking and leave their personal cars wherever they want, often blocking pedestrian ramps, bus lanes, bus stops, and bike lanes.' No council member is directly involved, but the report draws on public polling and crash data. Crash rates on precinct blocks are up to 232% higher than adjacent blocks, with protected bike lanes vanishing near stationhouses. The chaos is not just unsightly—it creates real, illegal danger for pedestrians, cyclists, and wheelchair users. Solutions like placard reform and residency requirements are suggested, but NYPD action remains lacking.

      Mayor Garcetti Signs 177-Mile Speed Limit Reduction Ordinance
      ▸

      Mayor Garcetti cut speed limits on 177 miles of Los Angeles streets. The law targets deadly roads, especially in Black, Latinx, and low-income areas. The city council and advocates pushed for the change. New limits take effect after signs go up.

      On March 7, 2022, Mayor Eric Garcetti signed an ordinance reducing speed limits on 177 miles of Los Angeles streets. The measure, approved by the City Council Transportation Committee and full Council in February, follows the passage of State Assemblymember Laura Friedman's A.B. 43, which lets cities set safer speed limits. The ordinance states: 'Today, Mayor Garcetti signs Council File action to reduce speed limits in certain streets of Los Angeles.' Councilmembers Mike Bonin and Paul Koretz, along with Friedman, led the push. Garcetti said, 'Far too many Angelenos die in traffic collisions... [which] kill disproportionately in low income, Black, and Latinx communities.' LADOT General Manager Seleta Reynolds promised the new limits will be in place by June. The law aims to slow cars and protect people on foot, bike, and transit.

The plan aligns with the NYC 25X25 initiative to reclaim street space for pedestrians and cyclists. Community leaders and officials support the plaza as a step to prevent future tragedy.
