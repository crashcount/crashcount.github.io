      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      NYC Streets Run Red—How Many Kids Must Die Before City Hall Acts?

      Written June 29, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      Children, Blood, and Concrete

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy crossed Eastern Parkway with his sister. A black SUV hit him. He died on the street. The driver stayed. Police said, “They were walking, he was crossing the street with his sister” (police said). No arrest. The street is still open. The danger is still there.

      The day before, in the Bronx, a man ran a red light. His car jumped the curb and crushed three people on the sidewalk. One is still fighting for life. The driver ran but police caught him. He faces charges for assault, fleeing, and driving without a license (faces charges).

      In the last 12 months, 244 people have died on New York City streets. 713 were seriously hurt. Thirteen of the dead were children. The city logs over 81,000 crashes a year. Each number is a name, a family, a future gone.

      The Policy That Wasn’t

      Leaders talk. Streets stay the same. The mayor ripped out a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue after crashes involving children. He said he was listening to the community. Cyclists said, “I don’t think he cares if we die” (Cyclists said). The city put back paint. Paint does not stop cars.

      Elsewhere, the Department of Transportation built a new protected bike lane in Queens. A commuter said, “The old street was very dangerous… the new path is definitely safer because he’s no longer competing with trucks and cars for road space” (A commuter said). But these changes come slow. Too slow for the dead.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Cars and trucks killed 299 people. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 21. Bikes killed 11. The city counts 182,570 injuries since 2022. The bodies pile up. The city debates.

      What Now

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action, not words. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is policy.

      Take action now.

The NYPD solved more hit-and-runs in white neighborhoods than in communities of color. DOT and City Hall touted small improvements, but the carnage continued. The report demands full, urgent implementation of the NYC Streets Plan to save lives.

      Thousands of Curbside EV Chargers Threaten NYC Sidewalks
      ▸

      City plans to flood sidewalks with electric car chargers. Pedestrian space shrinks. Bike and bus lanes face new barriers. Car dominance grows. Federal money fuels the push. Critics say: put people first, not parking. Sidewalks belong to the vulnerable.

      On January 18, 2023, Christine Berthet published a sharp critique of New York City’s curbside EV charger rollout. The opinion, titled 'Thousands of Chargers for Heavy E-Cars Are Coming to a Sidewalk Near You,' warns that federal grants will fund chargers at the expense of pedestrian, cyclist, and transit space. Berthet writes, 'chargers will enshrine the use of the curb for free parking, and will make it more difficult and expensive to expand sidewalks, bike lanes and bus lanes in the future.' She urges the city to direct incentives toward fast-charging hubs, transit, and Citi Bike, not private car infrastructure. Berthet calls for sidewalk widening, bike lanes, bus lanes, and public review before any curbside charger is installed. Her message: prioritize people, safety, and the environment over car convenience.

      Open Plans Pushes People-First NYC Streets in 2023 Agenda
      ▸

      Open Plans calls for a city built for people, not cars. Their agenda demands less driving, more space for walking, biking, and gathering. They urge lawmakers to end parking mandates, expand micromobility, daylight intersections, and tax heavy vehicles. Streets must serve the vulnerable.

      On January 9, 2023, Open Plans released a sweeping legislative agenda for New York City. The advocacy group’s plan, titled 'Joyful, Sustainable Future for a People-Centered NYC,' urges coordinated action on four fronts: livable streets, curb reform, public space, and parking mandates. Sara Lind, a key voice, states, 'We can’t build a livable city without reclaiming space from cars.' The agenda highlights bills like Intro 500 (placard abuse), S3080A/A516 (e-bike rebates), Intro 708 (truck route redesign), and Intro 854 (intersection daylighting). It calls for dynamic parking pricing, expanded loading zones, and a permanent Open Restaurants program. The plan demands city and state action to reduce car dominance, expand safe alternatives, and protect vulnerable road users. Council members are urged to champion bus lanes and support these reforms.

      Four Ways NHTSA Could Save Pedestrian Lives, 2023 Proposal
      ▸

      NHTSA ignores people on foot. Its five-star ratings protect drivers, not walkers. SUVs and trucks grow deadlier. U.S. pedestrian deaths soar. Professor Shill demands change: count walkers, shrink cars, test for real-world danger. Lives hang in the balance. Reform is overdue.

      On January 4, 2023, University of Iowa professor Gregory Shill published a policy proposal urging the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) to overhaul its safety standards. The article, titled 'Four Ways Automotive Regulators Could Save Pedestrian Lives,' argues NHTSA 'considers the safety of pedestrians—who are third parties rather than consumers—almost completely alien to its mission.' Shill calls for four reforms: include pedestrian safety in NHTSA’s mandate, make cars safer for walkers by addressing size and design, scrutinize high-tech solutions, and make crash tests reflect real-world bodies and conditions. He notes, 'the agency has the capacity to leverage the best evidence and technology to safeguard pedestrians, and should use it.' Shill’s push comes as U.S. pedestrian deaths rise, especially among Black road users, while other nations see declines. The proposal demands systemic change to protect the most vulnerable on American streets.
