      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Parkway, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed citation links, removed headline/body duplication, deduped and sorted citations.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      Another Child Dead, Another Family Broken

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy tried to cross Eastern Parkway in Crown Heights. A black Honda Pilot hit him. He was dragged from under the SUV. Blood pooled on the street. “I saw the kid being dragged from underneath the car by a woman,” a witness said. The boy’s sister stood nearby. The driver stayed. No arrest. The city moved on.

      He was not the only one. In the last twelve months, 241 people died on New York City streets. 706 were seriously injured. Children, elders, cyclists, walkers—the city’s most vulnerable. The numbers do not stop. They only grow.

      The Toll of Inaction

      The city has seen over 80,000 crashes in the past year. Cars and SUVs killed 299 people. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. Each number is a life. Each crash is a family changed forever. The pain is not abstract. It is a mother’s scream, a father’s silence, a child’s empty bed.

      Residents know the danger. “Yeah, this street is dangerous,” said one neighbor. The city knows it too. But the streets remain the same.

      Leaders Move, But Not Fast Enough

      There are new laws. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. Some streets have changed. Most have not. Red-light cameras have been expanded. But the deaths keep coming. When leaders act, they move slow. When they stall, people die. The mayor removed a protected bike lane in Williamsburg. Cyclists were left exposed. “I don’t think he cares if we die,” said one rider.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand It Now

      No more waiting. No more empty promises. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action for every child, every walker, every rider. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Make them choose life.

      Thousands of Curbside EV Chargers Threaten NYC Sidewalks
      ▸

      City plans to flood sidewalks with electric car chargers. Pedestrian space shrinks. Bike and bus lanes face new barriers. Car dominance grows. Federal money fuels the push. Critics say: put people first, not parking. Sidewalks belong to the vulnerable.

      On January 18, 2023, Christine Berthet published a sharp critique of New York City’s curbside EV charger rollout. The opinion, titled 'Thousands of Chargers for Heavy E-Cars Are Coming to a Sidewalk Near You,' warns that federal grants will fund chargers at the expense of pedestrian, cyclist, and transit space. Berthet writes, 'chargers will enshrine the use of the curb for free parking, and will make it more difficult and expensive to expand sidewalks, bike lanes and bus lanes in the future.' She urges the city to direct incentives toward fast-charging hubs, transit, and Citi Bike, not private car infrastructure. Berthet calls for sidewalk widening, bike lanes, bus lanes, and public review before any curbside charger is installed. Her message: prioritize people, safety, and the environment over car convenience.

      Four Ways NHTSA Could Save Pedestrian Lives, 2023 Proposal
      ▸

      NHTSA ignores people on foot. Its five-star ratings protect drivers, not walkers. SUVs and trucks grow deadlier. U.S. pedestrian deaths soar. Professor Shill demands change: count walkers, shrink cars, test for real-world danger. Lives hang in the balance. Reform is overdue.

      On January 4, 2023, University of Iowa professor Gregory Shill published a policy proposal urging the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) to overhaul its safety standards. The article, titled 'Four Ways Automotive Regulators Could Save Pedestrian Lives,' argues NHTSA 'considers the safety of pedestrians—who are third parties rather than consumers—almost completely alien to its mission.' Shill calls for four reforms: include pedestrian safety in NHTSA’s mandate, make cars safer for walkers by addressing size and design, scrutinize high-tech solutions, and make crash tests reflect real-world bodies and conditions. He notes, 'the agency has the capacity to leverage the best evidence and technology to safeguard pedestrians, and should use it.' Shill’s push comes as U.S. pedestrian deaths rise, especially among Black road users, while other nations see declines. The proposal demands systemic change to protect the most vulnerable on American streets.
