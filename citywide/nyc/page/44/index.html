      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      No More Sacrifices: Slow Down or More Kids Die

      Written July 4, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 4, 2025

      The Toll This Summer

      The city bleeds. In the last twelve months, 244 people have died on New York’s streets. 707 were left with serious injuries. Children, elders, cyclists, pedestrians—none spared. Just last week, an 8-year-old boy was killed by an SUV at an intersection in Crown Heights. There was no warning. There was no time. The street was busy, the car was big, and a child did not come home. Streetsblog NYC called it “another child sacrificed to the SUV.”

      Six more people were struck in the Bronx when a Mustang jumped the curb and plowed into a sidewalk shed. Witnesses heard the screams. “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting,” said Vivian Cole. The driver ran. The city moved on.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      The violence is not random. SUVs and cars killed 300 pedestrians in the last three years. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers are cold. The bodies are not. Every crash is a family broken, a street stained.

      What Leaders Have Done—And Not Done

      City Hall claims progress. The first half of 2025 saw a 32% drop in traffic deaths. DOT Commissioner Rodriguez said, “Traffic deaths reached the lowest level in recorded history during the first six months of this year because of Vision Zero’s focus on safer street designs, expanded public education, and targeted traffic enforcement,” according to BKReader.

      But the work is not done. Protected bike lanes are being ripped out. The city drags its feet on lowering speed limits. Policies that sound safe sometimes punish the wrong people. A new rule would cap e-bike speeds at 15 mph. Michael Replogle, a former DOT official, called it “an ill-considered idea to improve safety which will be counterproductive.”

      The Next Step Is Yours

      The city will not save itself. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Tell them to protect bike lanes, not remove them. Tell them to stop blaming the people in the crosswalk. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Act now.

      Federal Grant Funds Vision Zero Plans for Half of U.S.
      ▸

      Washington sends $800 million to 510 communities. Most get planning money, not shovels. Big projects win the rest. Cyclists and walkers see most of the gains. Advocates cheer, but warn: plans alone do not save lives. More cash, more action needed.

      On February 2, 2023, the U.S. Department of Transportation announced the first round of 'Safe Streets and Roads For All' grants. The $800 million, part of a $5 billion, five-year program, will reach 510 communities. Most grants fund local Vision Zero action plans, aiming to cut car crash deaths. Only 37 projects get money for real infrastructure. These receive 60 percent of the funds, with 80 percent benefiting cyclists and 90 percent helping pedestrians. Leah Shahum of the Vision Zero Network called the funding 'an encouraging start,' but warned, 'plans don’t always lead to real results.' Corinne Kisner of NACTO said the program 'aligns transportation investments to the safety, equity, and climate crises.' Still, advocates note the grants are less than 1 percent of federal infrastructure spending. Road deaths remain high, hitting vulnerable users hardest.

      Thousands of Curbside EV Chargers Threaten NYC Sidewalks
      ▸

      City plans to flood sidewalks with electric car chargers. Pedestrian space shrinks. Bike and bus lanes face new barriers. Car dominance grows. Federal money fuels the push. Critics say: put people first, not parking. Sidewalks belong to the vulnerable.

      On January 18, 2023, Christine Berthet published a sharp critique of New York City’s curbside EV charger rollout. The opinion, titled 'Thousands of Chargers for Heavy E-Cars Are Coming to a Sidewalk Near You,' warns that federal grants will fund chargers at the expense of pedestrian, cyclist, and transit space. Berthet writes, 'chargers will enshrine the use of the curb for free parking, and will make it more difficult and expensive to expand sidewalks, bike lanes and bus lanes in the future.' She urges the city to direct incentives toward fast-charging hubs, transit, and Citi Bike, not private car infrastructure. Berthet calls for sidewalk widening, bike lanes, bus lanes, and public review before any curbside charger is installed. Her message: prioritize people, safety, and the environment over car convenience.
