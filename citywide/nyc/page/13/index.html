      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Speed Kills, Leaders Stall—New Yorkers Die

      Written August 10, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 10, 2025

      The Toll of Ordinary Streets

      Every day, the city bleeds. In the last twelve months, 287 people died on New York City streets. 763 were left with serious injuries—bodies broken, lives changed. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the cost of walking, biking, or driving in this city.

      Just last week, a woman was killed crossing 174th Street in the Bronx. The driver fled. Police are still looking for them. Police said they’re looking for the driver of a vehicle who struck and killed a woman as she crossed a Bronx street late Wednesday night and then fled the scene. Another man was dragged fifty feet in Brooklyn, left to die. These are not rare stories. Brooklyn leads the city in pedestrian injuries this year.

      Speed and Silence

      Speed is the quiet killer. On July 19, a stolen car flew off the Manhattan Bridge at over 100 miles per hour. It hit a cyclist and a woman sitting on a bench in Chinatown. Both died. The city promised changes. As we mourn the loss of the victims of this horrific crash, we are taking immediate steps to fortify this intersection while developing a long-term safety redesign for the entire Canal Street corridor, said Transportation Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. But seven have died on Canal Street since 2011. The fixes come after the funerals.

      In another case, a cab driver was killed by an SUV going 77 mph in a 25 mph zone. The driver ran. This defendant was allegedly driving three times the speed limit when her SUV slammed into a livery cab, and she fled the scene without checking on the driver’s condition or waiting for first responders to arrive, said Bronx District Attorney Darcel Clark.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city has tools. Sammy’s Law lets leaders lower the speed limit to 20 mph. They have not done it. Speed cameras are renewed, but the most dangerous drivers—those with dozens of violations—still roam free. The city promises redesigns after the dead are counted. The fixes are slow. The deaths are not.

      Every delay is another name on a slab.

      Act Now

      Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join the fight for safer streets. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in NYC traffic crashes recently?
      In the last 12 months, 287 people died and 763 suffered serious injuries on New York City streets. These numbers come from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and trucks (including SUVs) were involved in 328 pedestrian deaths and 549 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 11 deaths and 45 serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 11 deaths and 58 serious injuries. (2022–2025 data from NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. Speed, reckless driving, and unsafe street design are the main causes. These are choices, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits citywide, redesign dangerous streets, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. Sammy’s Law gives them the power to set a 20 mph limit. They can act now.
      ▸ What has the city done lately to address traffic violence?
      The city renewed speed cameras and promised safety upgrades after deadly crashes. But leaders have not used Sammy’s Law to lower the speed limit citywide, and action against repeat speeders is still lacking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
