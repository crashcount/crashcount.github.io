      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Another Child Dead. How Many More Before City Hall Acts?

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, cleaned citations, and tightened prose.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      The Toll This Month

      The city bleeds. In the last twelve months, 243 people died on New York streets. 708 were left with serious injuries. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers—none are spared. Just last week, an eight-year-old boy was killed crossing Eastern Parkway in Crown Heights. His sister watched. His mother grieves. “He was a boy who loved life,” said his mother.

      Scooter riders, teens on mopeds, and cyclists have all fallen. On June 26, a 39-year-old man was thrown from his scooter and died on 116th Avenue. No arrests. No comfort. The street is unchanged.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In the last three years, they took 299 lives and left thousands more broken. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds, 10. Bikes, 11. The numbers are cold, but the pain is not. Every crash is a family torn open.

      Leaders Act—But Not Fast Enough

      The city has moved, but the pace is slow. Speed cameras in school zones are now extended through 2030. “It’s simple: speed cameras save lives and keep New Yorkers safe,” said Governor Hochul. But cameras alone are not enough. Streets need to be rebuilt. Speed limits must drop. The city has the power to set a 20 mph limit. It has not used it.

      Some leaders speak plain. “At this point, there are no questions, just facts: speeding kills, and speed cameras save lives,” said State Senator Gounardes. But others drag their feet, or worse, rip out bike lanes and leave riders exposed.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand that repeat speeders are stopped. Do not wait for another child’s name to be written in chalk on the street.

      Act now. Or count the dead.

      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or are they just accidents?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Lower speeds, protected bike lanes, and better street design save lives. Policy choices, not fate, decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What have local leaders done to address traffic violence?

      ▸ What can local politicians do right now to make streets safer?

      Manhattan CB6 Demands DOT Open Queensboro Pedestrian Path Now
      ▸

      Manhattan’s Community Board 6 told DOT to open the Queensboro Bridge’s south path to pedestrians now. Cyclists and walkers are crammed into one narrow lane. Drivers get nine. DOT delays keep danger high. The board says: stop stalling, put safety first.

      On October 11, 2024, Manhattan Community Board 6 passed a resolution demanding the Department of Transportation open the Queensboro Bridge’s south outer roadway to pedestrians without delay. The board’s Transportation Committee, led by Vice Chair Barak Friedman and Chair Jason Froimowitz, called the current setup 'really dangerous' and 'horrible for pedestrians and cyclists.' The resolution states opening the path 'must take priority over the convenience of drivers.' DOT has delayed the project repeatedly, citing unrelated upper deck construction. Former Mayor de Blasio promised a fix in 2021, but the Adams administration has not delivered. Cyclists and pedestrians remain forced to share a cramped, unsafe lane while motorists enjoy up to nine. Advocates and board members say the city’s excuses put lives at risk. DOT declined to comment.

      DOT Plan: Close Sixth Ave Bike Lane Gap, Remove Car Lane
      ▸

      DOT will fill a mile-long bike lane gap on Sixth Avenue. One car lane goes. Cyclists get wide, protected paths. Riders must switch sides twice for tunnel traffic and firehouse parking. 469 crashes, 187 hurt in five years. Locals back the change.

      On October 1, 2024, the Department of Transportation announced a plan to close the protected bike lane gap along Soho’s Sixth Avenue. The proposal, discussed with Community Board 2’s Transportation Committee, removes one of four car lanes to install a curbside protected bike lane between Lispenard and W. Eighth streets. DOT project manager Preston Johnson called it 'the last gap between the Financial District and Central Park.' The plan forces cyclists to switch sides twice to accommodate Holland Tunnel traffic and firehouse parking. Over five years, 469 crashes injured 187 people on this stretch. Community Board 2 Transportation Chair Shirley Secunda said, 'Eight years ago we did a resolution asking DOT to reduce Sixth Avenue to three travel lanes from four [so] we’re very happy that it’s finally happening.' The project adds eight-to-ten-foot bike lanes, pedestrianizes a slip lane, and builds bus boarding islands. Local support is strong.
