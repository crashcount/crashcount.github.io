      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Right turn. Crosswalk. Silence.

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 8, 2025

      A driver turned right at 30 St and 39 Ave in Astoria and hit a 38‑year‑old woman in a marked crosswalk on Aug 31. Police recorded failure to yield by the driver and listed her as killed (NYC Open Data).

      This Week

      • Aug 30: a taxi driver going straight on York Ave at E 72 St hit and killed a pedestrian (NYC Open Data).
      • Aug 23: a westbound sedan driver hit and killed a pedestrian on United Nations Ave S (NYC Open Data).

      They are part of a larger count. Since Jan 1, 2022, New York City has recorded 1,119 traffic deaths, with 196,517 people injured and 2,575 listed as seriously hurt (NYC Open Data).

      Across the City, The Numbers Don’t Let Up

      Year to date, the city has logged 195 deaths, down from 209 at this point last year, while serious injuries rose to 528 from 511 (NYC Open Data). Most people on foot are hit by drivers of cars and SUVs; together those drivers account for the largest share of pedestrian deaths and injuries in the period covered (NYC Open Data).

      The Bronx, Queens, Manhattan. Same Story

      In the Bronx, a man was struck and left to die near the Major Deegan and Mosholu Parkway. “The deadly collision happened just after 2 AM on Sunday,” a local report said (ABC7). In Queens, state records show a left‑turning SUV driver killed an e‑bike rider in Hollis Court earlier this summer; Manhattan saw a driver plow onto the Manhattan Bridge walkway in July, killing a cyclist and a pedestrian (both in the city crash log for this period) (NYC Open Data). Different boroughs. The same wound.

      The Worst Drivers Do Outsized Harm

      A small group does the most damage. Research cited by advocates found that 1.5% of motorists are tied to 21% of pedestrian deaths, and vehicles with 16 camera tickets in a year are twice as likely to kill or seriously injure; 30+ tickets multiplies the risk fifty‑fold (Streetsblog). One such driver with 29 prior camera tickets ran a red in Midwood and killed a mother and her two daughters in April (NY Daily News).

      Use the Tools We Have

      New York now has the power to drop speeds on local streets and to rein in repeat speeders. The city can lower the default residential limit to 20 MPH under Sammy’s Law, and Albany has a bill—the Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045C/A2299C)—to require intelligent speed limiters for anyone who racks up 11 DMV points in 18 months or 16 camera tickets in a year (CrashCount: Take Action). Slower streets and governor devices do not bring back the dead. They stop the next call.

      One more right turn. One more crosswalk. We know how this ends unless we choose otherwise. Start here: take action.
