      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Mosholu, 2 AM

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 12, 2025

      Just after 2 AM on Sep 7, a driver in a 2024 Toyota sedan going straight hit and killed a 30‑year‑old man on Mosholu Parkway in the Bronx, with police noting driver distraction (NYC Open Data). “30‑year‑old man struck and killed on Mosholu Parkway,” the NYPD told local TV (CBS New York).

      This was one life in a long count. 1,123 people have been killed on New York City streets since 2022, with 197,252 injured and 2,592 severely hurt in that span (NYC Open Data). Year to date, 199 people have been killed, compared with 212 at this point last year (NYC Open Data).

      This Week

      • Aug 31 in Queens, at 30 St and 39 Ave, a driver in a Ford SUV turned right and hit a 38‑year‑old woman in a marked crosswalk; police recorded failure to yield by the driver (NYC Open Data).
      • Aug 30 in Manhattan, at York Ave and E 72 St, a taxi driver going straight hit and killed a pedestrian (NYC Open Data).
      • Aug 23 in Manhattan, near United Nations Ave S, a driver in a Ford sedan going straight hit and killed a man; police noted driver inattention (NYC Open Data).

      The pattern doesn’t let up

      In the last 12 months, New York City recorded 279 deaths and 52,375 injuries in crashes (NYC Open Data). Most people killed are not in the car that hit them. Pedestrians are struck most often by sedans and SUVs, which together account for hundreds of pedestrian deaths in this period (NYC Open Data).

      Year to date, deaths are down slightly: 199 this year versus 212 last year at the same point (NYC Open Data). The line bends, then snaps back. The funerals don’t stop.

      Corners and corridors keep taking people

      Bronx parkways before dawn. A Queens crosswalk at midday. A Manhattan avenue at 5 AM. The settings change; the outcome stays. Police reports list failure to yield, distraction, unsafe speed. The bodies are counted the same way (NYC Open Data).

      On Mosholu, the record says “going straight ahead.” On 30 St and 39 Ave, “making right turn.” On York and 72nd, “going straight ahead.” These are choices recorded in boxes, after a life ends (NYC Open Data).

      Use the tools we have

      The City can slow cars and save lives. Lower the default speed limit. Stop repeat speeders with intelligent limiters. The steps are clear. The deaths are counted. See how to push these changes and who to call here.

      The man on Mosholu never made it home. That is the start and the end of it. The rest is whether we act, now, before the next name is added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We analyzed NYC’s official Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets for crashes, persons, and vehicles from 2022-01-01 through 2025-09-12, citywide. We used fields including crash date/time, borough, person role (pedestrian, cyclist, occupant), injury severity (killed, serious injury), vehicle type, and contributing factors. Data were accessed September 12, 2025. You can reproduce the base crash pull here.
      ▸ What changed year to date?
      According to the city’s crash database, 199 people have been killed in 2025 year to date, compared with 212 at the same point last year. These figures come from filtering the same datasets by date and injury severity.
      ▸ Which vehicles most often hit pedestrians?
      City records show pedestrians are most often struck by sedans and SUVs, which together account for the largest share of pedestrian deaths and injuries in this period, based on vehicle type fields in the datasets.
      ▸ What can I do right now?
      Push the city to slow speeds and curb repeat speeders. Ask officials to lower the default city speed limit and to mandate intelligent speed limiters for habitual offenders. Get started here.
