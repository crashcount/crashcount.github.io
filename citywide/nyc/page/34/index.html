      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      The Blood Price of City Streets: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      Written June 28, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Bone

      In the last twelve months, 244 people died on New York City streets. Over 49,000 were injured. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The city keeps count, but the numbers do not bleed. A 17-year-old boy died in Brooklyn last week, thrown from a moped after a car turned left in front of him. His passenger, 14, left unconscious in the street. Jhoan later died at the hospital. The driver stayed. The city moved on.

      In Queens, a cyclist was killed by a man fleeing police. She was coming home from a ride. A witness said, “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.” The chase ended. The family waits for justice.

      Leadership: Action and Evasion

      The city has made moves. DOT rolled out new intersection designs with barriers to keep cars from corners. Commissioner Rodriguez said, “Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety.” Some corners will be safer. But the pace is slow. The city removed a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. Cyclists asked, “What the fuck! Where are we supposed to ride?” The mayor listened to a few. The rest ride in fear.

      The Machines That Kill

      Most deaths come from cars and trucks. In the last three and a half years, cars and SUVs killed 299, trucks and buses 93, motorcycles and mopeds 10, bikes 11. The city counts the bodies. The city counts the tickets. Still, the bodies pile up.

      Call to Action: Make Them Hear You

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand hardened intersections, protected bike lanes, and real enforcement. Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      From 2022 to June 2025, cars and SUVs caused 299 deaths, trucks and buses 93, motorcycles and mopeds 10, and bikes 11. Cars and trucks are the main killers. See the data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These deaths are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and real enforcement can save lives. Policy, not fate, decides who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, harden intersections, and enforce laws against reckless driving. They can act now, or wait for more deaths.
      ▸ What has the city done lately to improve street safety?
      The city has started installing barriers at intersections to improve visibility and safety. But progress is slow, and some protected bike lanes have been removed.

      DOT Urged to Replace KSI Metric After 37 UWS Deaths
      ▸

      The city’s KSI metric counts bodies, not danger. Thirty-seven killed on the Upper West Side in a decade. Streets stay deadly. A new, community-centered metric maps risk before blood is spilled. DOT must act before the next crash, not after.

      On August 17, 2023, a Streetsblog NYC policy critique challenged the Department of Transportation’s reliance on the KSI (Killed or Severely Injured) metric. The article, titled 'Beyond KSI: How DOT Can Identify Unsafe Streets Before Tragedy Strikes,' exposes how KSI misses hidden dangers and fails to prevent future deaths. No council bill number is attached; this is a public call to action, not legislation. The critique highlights that, despite 37 road deaths on the Upper West Side in ten years, DOT’s 2023 plan ignored these corridors. The author mapped safety features and hazards, proposing a new, proactive metric based on accessibility, comfort, and livability. The piece urges DOT to shift from counting casualties to preventing them, stating, 'we need a different metric to fix the underlying problem of safety on city streets—one that is community-centered and doesn’t reduce our assessment of a certain street’s needs to grim numbers.'

      On August 10, 2023, the Department of Transportation (DOT) installed the 'Banker's Anchor' pedestrian plaza on N. 15th Street in Greenpoint, Brooklyn.
