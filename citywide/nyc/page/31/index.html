      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      No More Blood for Traffic: NYC Leaders Must Act—Now

      Written July 8, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 8, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Shattered

      In New York City, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, 1,028 people have died on city streets. More than 183,000 have been injured. 2,337 suffered serious injuries—the kind that end careers, break families, and leave scars that never heal. Children, elders, cyclists, and workers are struck down in the crosswalk, on the sidewalk, waiting for the light. The city keeps moving. The blood dries. The next crash comes.

      Just days ago, an eight-year-old boy was killed by an SUV in Crown Heights. The street was wide, the car was heavy, the child was small. The city called it another tragedy. Advocates called it what it is: a failure. Eastern Parkway acts as a major thoroughfare for drivers, and is the sixth most dangerous street in Brooklyn for pedestrians, said one group, demanding more safety infrastructure to stop the killing.

      Policy: Progress and Sabotage

      Some leaders move. Others stall or rip out what works. The Department of Transportation warned: Removing the protected bike lane won’t remove cyclists—it will only make the street less safe. Still, the city tries to erase protection for the most vulnerable. On Review Avenue, a new protected bike lane opened after years of struggle. Cyclists now have a sliver of safety. “The old street was very dangerous,” said a daily rider. The new path is definitely safer because he no longer fights trucks for space.

      But for every step forward, another is lost. The mayor removes bike lanes. The council debates. Children die. The city counts bodies.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. Since 2022, they have caused 301 deaths and over 26,000 injuries to pedestrians and cyclists. Trucks and buses killed 93. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10. Bikes killed 11. The numbers are cold. The pain is not.

      What Now: Demand Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is preventable. The city has the power to lower speed limits, build real protection, and stop repeat offenders. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph limit. Demand protected lanes. Demand enforcement that targets the real danger—drivers, not cyclists or children.

      The city will not save you. Only your voice, raised with others, can force their hand. Do not wait for another child’s name to be written in chalk on the street.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and Trucks: SUVs and cars killed 301 and injured over 19,000. Trucks and buses killed 93 and injured 1,451. Motorcycles and Mopeds: Killed 10, injured 655. Bikes: Killed 11, injured 875. The largest toll comes from cars, SUVs, and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. These deaths and injuries are preventable. Speed, street design, and enforcement choices decide who lives and who dies. Policy—not fate—writes the numbers.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, redesign dangerous streets, and enforce laws against reckless drivers. They can stop removing safety infrastructure and start protecting people, not cars.
      ▸ What recent steps has NYC taken to address traffic violence?
      Some leaders have built new protected bike lanes and warned against removing them. Others have stalled or removed safety measures. Progress is slow and often reversed. Advocates must push for more.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured in NYC traffic since 2022?
      Since 2022, 1,028 people have died and 2,337 have suffered serious injuries on New York City streets.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How can I help make streets safer?
      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and enforcement that targets reckless drivers. Join advocacy groups and show up at meetings. Your voice matters.

      The city scrapped a protected bike lane on Ashland Place. DOT caved to Two Trees, keeping sharrows instead. Cyclists remain exposed. Advocates slam the move. A cyclist died nearby. Cyclist deaths are rising. Corporate interests win. Safety loses.

      On October 6, 2023, the Department of Transportation (DOT) confirmed it would not install a protected bike lane on the southernmost block of Ashland Place. The plan was dropped after pressure from real estate firm Two Trees. DOT will keep a two-way street with bike sharrows, citing 'community feedback and unique block conditions.' The block connects major cycling routes but remains unprotected. Advocates and politicians, including Andrew Matsuoka and Kathy Park Price of Transportation Alternatives, and Jon Orcutt of Bike New York, condemned the decision. Matsuoka said he was 'angry but unsurprised that Mayor Adams would put Two Trees’ interests ahead of safety.' The move follows a recent cyclist fatality and comes as 2023 is set to be the second deadliest year for NYC cyclists. The city’s action leaves vulnerable road users at risk and signals that corporate interests outweigh public safety.
