      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Two deaths in two days. City Hall still drags its feet.

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 7, 2025

      About midday on Aug 31, at 30 St and 39 Ave, the driver of a 2020 Ford SUV made a right turn and hit a 38-year-old woman in a marked crosswalk. Police recorded failure to yield by the driver. She died (NYC Open Data).

      This Week

      • Early morning Aug 30 on York Ave at E 72 St, a taxi driver going straight hit a person. He died (NYC Open Data).
      • Afternoon Aug 23 in Manhattan, a driver going west in a sedan hit a person outside an intersection. He died (NYC Open Data).

      They were three of the 1,119 people killed on New York City streets since 2022 (NYC Open Data). Through Sep 7 this year, deaths stand at 195, down from 209 at this point last year (NYC Open Data). The pace still grinds on.

      Crosswalks are promises we keep breaking

      At 30 St and 39 Ave, the record shows a right turn and a failure to yield. A woman in the crosswalk. Impact to the center front. Fatal on scene (NYC Open Data).

      On York Ave, police logged a driver in a Toyota going straight. Another body in the road before dawn (NYC Open Data). Uptown the week before, a westbound driver in a Ford sedan hit a man outside a crosswalk. He died too (NYC Open Data). Different boroughs. Same ending.

      A small group does outsized harm

      City data and crash cases point to a pattern of repeat dangerous driving. Researchers found that just 1.5% of motorists were tied to 21% of pedestrian deaths; vehicles with 16 camera tickets in a year were twice as likely to kill or seriously injure, and 30+ tickets multiplied the risk fifty‑fold (Streetsblog). In April, one of those drivers, with 29 prior camera tickets, ran a red and killed a mother and her two daughters in Midwood (NY Daily News).

      “Speed cameras have cut speeding by over 60% in locations where installed,” the State Senate has said (NYS Senate). The tools work when we use them.

      The fixes sit on the shelf

      The city now has the power to lower its own speed limits. That includes setting a 20 MPH default on residential streets. It has not done so citywide (CrashCount: Take Action).

      Albany also has a bill ready: the Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045C/A2299C). It would force drivers with 11 DMV points in 18 months or 16 camera tickets in a year to install speed limiters that cap speeds to the limit plus 5 MPH (CrashCount: Take Action). “Families for Safe Streets is one of the most powerful advocacy forces I’ve ever seen in politics,” said former NYC transportation chief Polly Trottenberg, urging action after years of loss (Families for Safe Streets).

      The woman in the crosswalk on Aug 31 is gone. So is the man on York Ave. The policy levers are within reach. Use them. Start here.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, and Vehicles bm4k-52h4) filtered to New York City between 2022-01-01 and 2025-09-07. We counted fatalities citywide and read contributing factors, vehicle movements, and victim types from the corresponding records. Data were accessed Sep 7, 2025. You can view a reproducible crash count query here.
      ▸ What recent crashes does this story reference?
      Three fatal pedestrian crashes: Aug 31, 2025 at 30 St and 39 Ave; Aug 30, 2025 at York Ave and E 72 St; and Aug 23, 2025 in Manhattan with the driver traveling west. All are recorded in NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ What policies could reduce these deaths?
      Two steps are on the table: set a 20 MPH default on residential streets under Sammy’s Law, and pass the Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045C/A2299C) to require speed limiters for repeat offenders. Details and contacts are on our action page at CrashCount: Take Action.
      ▸ Do speed cameras help?
      Yes. “Speed cameras have cut speeding by over 60% in locations where installed,” according to the New York State Senate’s 2019 program findings, which supports keeping and expanding automated enforcement where legal.
