      Speed Kills. Albany Stalls. Another Family Mourns.

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 7, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      In the last twelve months, 260 people died on New York City streets. More than 51,000 were injured. Seven hundred thirty-four suffered wounds so grave they will never be the same. The dead are not numbers. A 76-year-old woman, struck by an SUV while crossing White Plains Road. A 32-year-old woman, crushed by a Jeep on Marcus Garvey Boulevard. A 79-year-old man, found dead in a parked car on West 77th. Each one leaves a hole that cannot be filled. NYC Open Data

      Most of the killing is done by cars and trucks. In three years, they have taken 298 lives and left 498 with serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 10, bikes 11. The rest is steel, speed, and neglect.

      Leadership: Words, Delays, and the Fight for Action

      City leaders say the right things. “Speeding kills, and speed cameras save lives,” said State Senator Andrew Gounardes. But the law that keeps speed cameras running is set to expire. If Albany fails, the cameras go dark. Streets go back to the way they were—fast, brutal, and unforgiving. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez warns, “an extremely reckless few continue to endanger themselves and everyone around them.” Patch

      There is talk of a new law for “super speeders.” There is talk of lowering the speed limit to 20 mph. There is always talk. But families keep burying their dead.

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      The city cracks down on cyclists and e-bike riders, not the drivers who do the killing. Community boards call it out. “Holding cyclists to a far higher standard than motor vehicle drivers is unjust, puts immigrant workers at grave risk, and sends the wrong message about the actual danger on our streets,” said Manhattan Community Board 7. The real threat rolls on four wheels, not two.

      What You Can Do: Make Them Hear You

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand the renewal of speed cameras. Join Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Every day you wait is another day someone dies.

      Streetfilms Exposes Failure of NYC Car-Free School Streets
      ▸

      Children face danger near schools. Streets swarm with cars. Crashes and injuries spike at drop-off. The city’s open streets program falters. Few schools get protection. Most are in wealthy, white areas. Kids in Black and brown neighborhoods face more harm. The system fails the vulnerable.

      On September 8, 2022, Streetfilms released a report highlighting the dangers children face near New York City schools. The film, featuring Streetsblog reporter Jesse Coburn, documents that during the 8 a.m. hour, streets near schools see 57 percent more crashes and 25 percent more injuries per mile than other city streets. The matter summary states, 'School zones don’t have to be danger zones.' The city’s open streets for schools program, meant to keep cars away from children, is failing. Only 38 schools participate, down from 101, and most are in wealthier, whiter neighborhoods. The application process is burdensome, requiring schools to opt in rather than providing universal protection. Former DOT Commissioner Janette Sadik-Khan and StreetopiaUWS Director Carl Mahaney criticize the system. The film exposes how the city’s approach leaves children in Black and brown communities at greater risk, both from crashes and pollution.

      Seagirt Boulevard Redesign: DOT Cuts Lanes, Adds Bike Protection
      ▸

      Seagirt Boulevard in Far Rockaway, once a six-lane speed trap, will shrink to two lanes. DOT will add a parking-protected bike lane and more space for people on foot. The redesign aims to slow drivers and shield those outside cars from harm.

      On September 6, 2022, the Department of Transportation announced a major safety redesign for Seagirt Boulevard, a notorious crash corridor in Far Rockaway. The project, not a council bill but a city action, follows years of neglect and repeated crashes. DOT will cut traffic lanes from six to two, add a parking-protected bike lane, and expand pedestrian space. The plan also includes daylighting at intersections and improved markings. According to DOT, 'the project will improve safety for all users, providing connections to existing bike infrastructure and the reduction of a vehicle lane encouraging safer speeds.' Queens Borough President Donovan Richards praised the move, saying, 'Far too many people are killed and injured on our city’s streets, especially people of color, who are disproportionately impacted by traffic violence.' Laura Shepard of Transportation Alternatives added, 'DOT's move to turn car space into protected bike lanes on Seagirt Boulevard, while shortening crossings and expanding median space, will prevent speeding and make it safer to walk, bike, and take transit.' The redesign targets a stretch with 222 crashes and 87 injuries since 2019, aiming to end the long-standing danger for vulnerable road users.
