      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Astoria morning. Two men at a food cart. A car jumps the curb.

      Written August 27, 2025 (Revised August 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 27, 2025

      Aug 12, 2025, Astoria. Police say an 84‑year‑old Toyota driver went north on 42nd Street at 19th Avenue and hit a food truck. Two men died there. The driver died too. The police told amNY the driver had suffered a stroke two weeks earlier and had been told not to drive. The victims were identified as 41‑year‑old Joaquin Venancio‑Mendez and 70‑year‑old Santiago Baires. amNY reported the car “careened at a high speed into a nearby food truck, ramming into two men.”

      City data logs the crash with three deaths: two pedestrians and one driver. Both pedestrians were listed as “Other Actions in Roadway.” The driver was unconscious at the scene. Four vehicles were involved. NYC Open Data tags it as CrashID 4834594.

      Just after 2:30 AM the next day in Springfield Gardens, a driver struck a 52‑year‑old man at South Conduit Avenue and 155th Street and fled. “The operator of the vehicle fled the scene after hitting the man,” police told ABC7. Cops searched for video, but as of that day there were no arrests, the Daily News wrote.

      Elsewhere, three more people died in separate crashes this month. A 62‑year‑old driver in West Harlem. A 58‑year‑old driver on Henry Street. A pedestrian on the East Side near the UN. City records mark distraction, tailgating, and “Other Actions in Roadway.” All fatal. Official crash data IDs: 4836666, 4836901, 4836979.

      Between Jan 2022 and Aug 27, 2025, cars and trucks have killed 1,108 people citywide and injured 195,048. SUVs and cars alone account for 335 pedestrian deaths recorded in this period. Our dataset shows it.

      The cart, the curb, the flight

      Astoria’s morning rush stopped at 42nd and 19th. A sedan hit a parked SUV, a parked sedan, then the food truck. Two men were crushed. The driver died. That is the shape of what happened. City records list crush injuries for both pedestrians.

      Near JFK the next night, a man crossed at 155th and South Conduit. The driver hit him and ran. “The driver sped off without stopping,” the Daily News said. “There are no arrests,” ABC7 reported.

      On Macombs Road, on Morgan Avenue, on Broadway, the roll call keeps growing. The ledger does not heal. It only counts. NYC Open Data keeps the score.

      The drivers keep going. So do the numbers.

      Year to date, the city counts 53,567 crashes, 184 deaths, and 33,347 injuries. Serious injuries are up over last year. Our YTD stats show a 4.6% rise in serious injuries even as total crashes and injuries fell.

      Pedestrians take the brunt from sedans and SUVs. Since 2022, sedans have injured 11,701 pedestrians and killed 96; SUVs have injured 11,063 and killed 204. Trucks have killed 77 pedestrians. The city rollup lays it out.

      Wrong‑way on the Clearview in 2023, five strikes, two people badly hurt. “I entered the Clearview Expressway in the wrong direction because I wanted to hurt people,” the driver told police, according to amNY. A jury convicted him. A judge gave him eight years.

      The street can change. The policy must catch up.

      Fourteen Street is slated for a redesign next year. “The redesign aims to improve the pedestrian experience,” NY1 reported. The money: $3 million for design. It helps on that corridor. It won’t reach South Conduit or 42nd Street by itself.

      Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can set lower speed limits. Cameras now run 24/7 through 2030. The worst harm comes from a small set of drivers. Vehicles with piles of camera tickets are far more likely to kill or maim. City leaders have tools. Use them. Make 20 MPH the rule on local streets. Require speed limiters for repeat offenders. Then enforce it.

      If you want those steps now, tell them now. Here’s how to take action.
