      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      NYC’s Deadly Streets, Week After Week

      Written August 23, 2025 (Revised August 23, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      This Week on the Street

      On Aug. 12 in Astoria, an 84‑year‑old driver turned and killed two men at a food cart. Police said the sedan “careened at a high speed into a nearby food truck” and named the dead: Joaquin Venancio‑Mendez, 41, and Santiago Baires, 70 (amNY). A TV crew said simply: “Two pedestrians were hit and killed while ordering from a food truck” (CBS New York).

      Around JFK at 2:30 a.m. the next day, a driver hit a 52‑year‑old man and fled. Police said the man died at Jamaica Hospital. Detectives were “looking into the incident” (Gothamist). Hit‑and‑run. One man gone. No car description released.

      On Aug. 11, two moped riders died on the Bronx River Parkway after collisions involving two sedans, per city crash data (NYC Open Data, CrashID 4834345).

      Four deaths in two days. None of them had a chance.

      The Toll, In Cold Numbers

      Since 2022, NYC logged 344,459 crashes, with 1,106 deaths and 194,465 injuries. 2,538 were serious injuries (city stats). Pedestrians are hit most by cars and SUVs: sedans caused 11,663 pedestrian injuries and deaths; SUVs, 11,039. Trucks: 1,485. Bikes: 1,543. Buses and vans add more. The bodies add up.

      Leaders Talk. Streets Wait.

      City Hall says 14th Street will be redesigned “to improve the pedestrian experience” next year (NY1). It is a start. Queens prosecutors did their job in another case: a wrong‑way driver who “terrorized other drivers” was sentenced to eight years, and told police he felt “liberated” by it (amNY). Enforcement comes after the crash. The dead do not hear verdicts.

      What Must Change Now

      • Lower the NYC default speed limit. Slower streets save lives.
      • Force repeat speeders to install speed limiters. Take the speed out of the car.

      Pair design with rules. Make it citywide, not piecemeal. The people at the cart, the man in the crosswalk, the scooter riders on the parkway—they needed a city that planned for their lives, not the after‑action.

      Do One Thing Today

      Tell City Hall and Albany: slow every street and stop repeat speeders. Start here: take action. Do it before the next siren cuts the night.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Cars and Trucks: sedans 11,663; SUVs 11,039; trucks 1,485; buses 614; vans 241; taxis 1,171. Motorcycles and Mopeds: motorcycles 291; mopeds 835. Bikes: 1,543. Figures reflect 2022–Aug. 2025 city data.
      ▸ What does it take to change traffic laws here?
      Real change needs both city and state. Local street fixes need support from your council member, City Council passage, and the mayor’s signature. State tools like automated enforcement must pass the Assembly and Senate and be signed by the Governor. Local officials can push, but they can’t act alone.
