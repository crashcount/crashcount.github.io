      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      Blood on the Asphalt, Silence at City Hall

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, deduped and sorted citations.)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Week the Streets Went Red

      A 90-year-old man tried to cross E. 14th Street in Brooklyn. A blue moped came fast. The rider wore a mask. He hit the man and kept going. The old man died at the hospital. A witness said the rider “hit the guy and he left.” No one has been arrested. The street is quiet again, but the blood is not gone.

      On the Belt Parkway, a BMW lost control. The car hit the median, flew into the air, and landed in oncoming traffic. Two died. Others were thrown from the car. The car caught fire. A survivor described how “they went airborne and into the barrier.” The police are still investigating. The dead do not wait for answers.

      The Numbers That Do Not Lie

      In the last year, 262 people died on New York City streets. Over 52,000 were hurt. More than 750 suffered serious injuries. Children, elders, workers—all caught in the same slow storm. Cars and SUVs killed the most, but trucks, bikes, and mopeds all left bodies behind. The city counts the dead. The families count the days since.

      Leaders Move—But Not Fast Enough

      City Hall lowered speed limits in some places. Speed cameras now run day and night. The city claims the drop in deaths is “historic.” But the work is not done. The law lets leaders set a 20 mph limit citywide. They have not done it. The most dangerous drivers—those with dozens of camera tickets—still drive. The city could stop them. It has not.

      What You Can Do

      Call your Council Member. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit for every street. Demand action against repeat speeders. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      From 2022 to July 2025, cars and trucks (including SUVs) were involved in 27,549 pedestrian injuries and deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 1,099. Bikes were involved in 1,495. Cars and trucks remain the biggest killers. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Crashes are not random. They happen again and again in the same ways, on the same streets. Speed, distraction, and dangerous roads kill. These deaths are preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower the citywide speed limit to 20 mph, crack down on repeat speeders, redesign dangerous streets, and enforce laws that protect people walking and biking. They have the power. They must use it.
      ▸ What has the city done lately to address traffic violence?
      The city has lowered speed limits in some areas, expanded speed cameras to run 24/7, and started redesigning more streets. But the default speed limit is still above 20 mph, and the most dangerous drivers are still on the road.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously hurt in NYC traffic crashes recently?
      In the last 12 months, 262 people died and 754 suffered serious injuries on NYC streets. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      NYC DOT Targets Intersection Danger Zones
      ▸

      City will block cars from corners. Barriers, granite, planters will clear sightlines. DOT acts after half of deaths hit at intersections. Brooklyn gets first fixes. Cyclists and walkers may see drivers before impact. Steel and stone replace painted lines.

      The Brooklyn Paper reported on June 11, 2025, that NYC DOT will redesign intersections to protect cyclists and pedestrians. The plan uses 'hardened daylighting'—physical barriers, granite blocks, and planters—to stop cars from parking near corners. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety.' Nearly half of city traffic deaths happen at intersections, often due to blocked sightlines and turning vehicles. The redesign targets high-crash Brooklyn intersections first, including Ocean Avenue at Church Avenue. The move highlights the city's shift from painted lines to physical infrastructure, aiming to reduce systemic risk for vulnerable road users.
