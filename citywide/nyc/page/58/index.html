      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      The Blood Price of City Streets: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      Written June 28, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Bone

      In the last twelve months, 244 people died on New York City streets. Over 49,000 were injured. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The city keeps count, but the numbers do not bleed. A 17-year-old boy died in Brooklyn last week, thrown from a moped after a car turned left in front of him. His passenger, 14, left unconscious in the street. Jhoan later died at the hospital. The driver stayed. The city moved on.

      In Queens, a cyclist was killed by a man fleeing police. She was coming home from a ride. A witness said, “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force.” The chase ended. The family waits for justice.

      Leadership: Action and Evasion

      The city has made moves. DOT rolled out new intersection designs with barriers to keep cars from corners. Commissioner Rodriguez said, “Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety.” Some corners will be safer. But the pace is slow. The city removed a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. Cyclists asked, “What the fuck! Where are we supposed to ride?” The mayor listened to a few. The rest ride in fear.

      The Machines That Kill

      Most deaths come from cars and trucks. In the last three and a half years, cars and SUVs killed 299, trucks and buses 93, motorcycles and mopeds 10, bikes 11. The city counts the bodies. The city counts the tickets. Still, the bodies pile up.

      Call to Action: Make Them Hear You

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand hardened intersections, protected bike lanes, and real enforcement. Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Feds Report Double-Digit Road Death Surge in 2021
      ▸

      Federal data shows road deaths soared in 2021. Over 42,000 lives lost. Pedestrians and cyclists hit hardest. Urban streets grew deadlier. Officials blame reckless driving and roads built for speed. Advocates demand safer design. The toll keeps rising. No one is safe.

      On May 17, 2022, federal officials released a report showing a double-digit percentage increase in U.S. road fatalities for 2021, the highest since 2005. The summary states, '42,915 people dying in motor vehicle crashes—the most since 2005.' Deaths rose on all road types, especially in cities, with pedestrians and cyclists suffering the most. U.S. Secretary of Transportation Pete Buttigieg responded, 'Zero is the only acceptable number of deaths and serious injuries on our roadways.' Advocates like Beth Osborne and Corinne Kisner called out dangerous road designs and large vehicles as key threats. The Biden Administration and U.S. DOT pledged a 'Safe System' and Vision Zero approach, promising infrastructure fixes and safety grants. The National Association of City Transportation Officials stressed the power of small, targeted interventions—sidewalks, curb extensions, raised crosswalks. The report makes clear: the system, not just drivers, is killing people.

      SB5602: City Control, 24/7 Speed Cameras Backed by Report
      ▸

      A new report shows most NYC road deaths happen when speed cameras are off. Advocates and officials push Albany to let the city run cameras all day, every day. The state stalls. Lives hang in the balance. The data is clear. Action lags.

      Senate Bill 5602, still pending in Albany, would let New York City control and expand speed camera enforcement to 24/7. The Transportation Alternatives report, 'Speeding Doesn’t Sleep,' warns that 59 percent of city road deaths occur when cameras are off. The report urges full-time operation, noting cameras cut speeding by 72 percent where installed. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez calls passage 'very important.' Brooklyn Borough President Antonio Reynoso and Governor Hochul support local control. Danny Harris of Transportation Alternatives says, 'We need Albany to allow New York City to operate speed safety cameras 24/7 so every neighborhood is protected from speeding drivers every hour of the day, every day of the week.' Despite strong public support, the legislature has not acted. The evidence is stark: when cameras are off, people die.
