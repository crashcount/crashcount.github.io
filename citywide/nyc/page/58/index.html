      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in New York City?

      NYC Streets Run Red—How Many Kids Must Die Before City Hall Acts?

      Written June 29, 2025

      New York City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      Children, Blood, and Concrete

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy crossed Eastern Parkway with his sister. A black SUV hit him. He died on the street. The driver stayed. Police said, “They were walking, he was crossing the street with his sister” (police said). No arrest. The street is still open. The danger is still there.

      The day before, in the Bronx, a man ran a red light. His car jumped the curb and crushed three people on the sidewalk. One is still fighting for life. The driver ran but police caught him. He faces charges for assault, fleeing, and driving without a license (faces charges).

      In the last 12 months, 244 people have died on New York City streets. 713 were seriously hurt. Thirteen of the dead were children. The city logs over 81,000 crashes a year. Each number is a name, a family, a future gone.

      The Policy That Wasn’t

      Leaders talk. Streets stay the same. The mayor ripped out a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue after crashes involving children. He said he was listening to the community. Cyclists said, “I don’t think he cares if we die” (Cyclists said). The city put back paint. Paint does not stop cars.

      Elsewhere, the Department of Transportation built a new protected bike lane in Queens. A commuter said, “The old street was very dangerous… the new path is definitely safer because he’s no longer competing with trucks and cars for road space” (A commuter said). But these changes come slow. Too slow for the dead.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Cars and trucks killed 299 people. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 21. Bikes killed 11. The city counts 182,570 injuries since 2022. The bodies pile up. The city debates.

      What Now

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action, not words. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is policy.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in New York City?
      Since 2022, cars and trucks killed 299 people and caused 26,859 injuries to pedestrians. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 16 and injured 1,070. Bikes killed 11 and injured 1,453. The toll is highest from cars and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Every crash is preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and enforcement can stop the killing. These are not acts of fate—they are policy choices.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, build protected bike lanes, daylight intersections, and enforce laws against reckless driving. They can act now or let more people die.
      ▸ How many people have died or been seriously injured on NYC streets recently?
      In the last 12 months, 244 people died and 713 were seriously injured in traffic crashes in New York City.

      Feds Report Double-Digit Road Death Surge in 2021
      ▸

      Federal data shows road deaths soared in 2021. Over 42,000 lives lost. Pedestrians and cyclists hit hardest. Urban streets grew deadlier. Officials blame reckless driving and roads built for speed. Advocates demand safer design. The toll keeps rising. No one is safe.

      On May 17, 2022, federal officials released a report showing a double-digit percentage increase in U.S. road fatalities for 2021, the highest since 2005. The summary states, '42,915 people dying in motor vehicle crashes—the most since 2005.' Deaths rose on all road types, especially in cities, with pedestrians and cyclists suffering the most. U.S. Secretary of Transportation Pete Buttigieg responded, 'Zero is the only acceptable number of deaths and serious injuries on our roadways.' Advocates like Beth Osborne and Corinne Kisner called out dangerous road designs and large vehicles as key threats. The Biden Administration and U.S. DOT pledged a 'Safe System' and Vision Zero approach, promising infrastructure fixes and safety grants. The National Association of City Transportation Officials stressed the power of small, targeted interventions—sidewalks, curb extensions, raised crosswalks. The report makes clear: the system, not just drivers, is killing people.
