      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brownsville?

      Brownsville Bleeds While City Sleeps: Demand 20 MPH Now

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brownsville: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Toll in Brownsville

      Four dead. Over a thousand injured. In the last three and a half years, Brownsville’s streets have not been quiet. The numbers do not flinch: 4 killed, 12 seriously injured, and 1,005 hurt in 1,720 crashes since 2022, according to NYC Open Data. Children are not spared. In the past year alone, 20 kids were injured in crashes. The deadliest wounds are not always visible.

      The faces behind the numbers are not forgotten. In May, Larry Maxwell, 72, was struck and killed on Sutter Avenue. He was on his way to a family cookout. The driver fled. His son, Larnce Vargas, stood at the scene and asked, “There are so many cameras. So why is he still at large?” The city’s eyes are everywhere, but justice is not.

      In February, a Mercedes driver with no license ran a stop sign, slammed into a school bus, and left his passenger, Imani Vance, dying in the front seat. The driver ran. Brooklyn’s District Attorney called it “a shocking disregard for human life and safety, made worse by his attempt to flee the scene instead of helping the victim.”

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      Laws have changed. Streets have not. Albany passed Sammy’s Law, giving New York City the power to lower speed limits. The city can set 20 mph limits on local streets. But in Brownsville, the pace of change is slow. Speed cameras work, but only where they are installed. Enforcement is uneven. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program has lapsed. The driver who killed Larry Maxwell is still free.

      Local leaders have words. They promise safer streets. But the bodies keep coming. The city’s own data shows the carnage is not random. It is routine.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every delay, every loophole, every missing camera is a choice. The city can lower speed limits. It can harden crosswalks. It can demand real accountability for reckless drivers. But it will not move unless pushed.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit on every street. Demand speed cameras on every block. Demand justice for every family left waiting.

      Take action now.

      Senate passes S 4045. Drivers with too many points or camera tickets must install speed assistance devices. Lawmakers move to curb reckless driving. The bill targets repeat offenders. The aim: fewer crashes, fewer deaths. Streets demand action.

      Bill S 4045, titled 'Relates to requiring the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices for repeated violation of maximum speed limits,' advanced in the Senate on May 20, 2025, following a committee vote. The bill requires drivers who rack up eleven or more points in 24 months, or six speed or red-light camera tickets in twelve months, to install intelligent speed assistance devices. Primary sponsor Andrew Gounardes led the push, joined by co-sponsors Samra Brouk, Cordell Cleare, and others. The vote passed with support from senators including Jeremy Cooney, Pete Harckham, and Jessica Ramos. The measure aims to rein in repeat offenders and reduce deadly speeding. The bill’s language is blunt: 'Requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' No safety analyst note was provided.
