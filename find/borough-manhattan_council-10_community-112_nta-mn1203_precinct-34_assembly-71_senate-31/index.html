      Inwood Bleeds While City Hall Talks

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 8, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Inwood: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Inwood: No One Is Safe

      The streets of Inwood do not forgive. In the last twelve months, 101 people were injured in crashes. Two suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. No deaths this year. But three in the last three years.

      A 34-year-old man lost his arm crossing with the signal at West 207th and 10th Avenue. The car did not stop. The street did not care. NYC Open Data

      Two men, ages 31 and 40, were crushed and killed on Sherman Avenue. They were not in the roadway. The cars found them anyway.

      Speed, distraction, and failure to yield—these are not accidents. They are choices. They are patterns. They are the slow violence of daily life in Inwood.

      Leadership: Promises, Policies, and Silence

      City Hall says the right words. “The NYPD’s enforcement efforts must never put the public or the police at undue risk, and pursuits for violations and low-level crimes can be both potentially dangerous and unnecessary,” said Commissioner Jessica Tisch. But the streets do not change with words.

      After a police chase ended in fire and death near Dyckman Street, the department suspended the officers. The family waits for answers. The city waits for action. “The officers are accused of fleeing, leaving him to die in the fiery wreck,” reported the Daily News.

      Vision Zero promises safer streets. But in Inwood, the numbers do not lie. Crashes are up 16% over last year. Injuries are up 58%.

      The Next Step: Demand More Than Words

      Speed kills. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has the power to redesign deadly intersections. It has the power to protect the most vulnerable. But power unused is no power at all.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. Do not wait for another name to become a number. Take action now.

      Officers Flee Scene After Fatal Crash
      ▸

      A stolen SUV crashed and burned at Dyckman Street. Two NYPD officers chased, then left. The driver died in flames. The cops returned to the Bronx, silent. Cameras caught their exit. The city investigates. Policy on police pursuits faces new scrutiny.

      According to NY Daily News (April 3, 2025), two NYPD officers from the 50th Precinct pursued a stolen Honda CR-V into Manhattan. The driver crashed at Dyckman Street and Henry Hudson Parkway. The car burst into flames. The officers left the scene and did not report the crash, later returning to their precinct and finishing their shift. Surveillance footage captured their patrol car leaving. Both officers have been suspended. The article notes, 'Both officers have been suspended as the NYPD's Force Investigation Division investigates their actions.' The crash comes after NYPD restricted police pursuits, limiting them to cases involving felonies or violent misdemeanors. NYPD Commissioner Jessica Tisch said the department now relies on 'advanced tools of modern-day policing' instead of risky chases. The incident highlights ongoing concerns about the dangers of police pursuits and the need for strict adherence to new policies.
