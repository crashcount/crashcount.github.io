      Inwood Bleeds While City Hall Talks

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed duplicate headline in body and cleaned citations list.)

      Inwood: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Inwood: No One Is Safe

      The streets of Inwood do not forgive. In the last twelve months, 101 people were injured in crashes. Two suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. No deaths this year. But three in the last three years.

      A 34-year-old man lost his arm crossing with the signal at West 207th and 10th Avenue. The car did not stop. The street did not care. NYC Open Data

      Two men, ages 31 and 40, were crushed and killed on Sherman Avenue. They were not in the roadway. The cars found them anyway.

      Speed, distraction, and failure to yield—these are not accidents. They are choices. They are patterns. They are the slow violence of daily life in Inwood.

      Leadership: Promises, Policies, and Silence

      City Hall says the right words. “The NYPD’s enforcement efforts must never put the public or the police at undue risk, and pursuits for violations and low-level crimes can be both potentially dangerous and unnecessary,” said Commissioner Jessica Tisch. But the streets do not change with words.

      After a police chase ended in fire and death near Dyckman Street, the department suspended the officers. The family waits for answers. The city waits for action. “The officers are accused of fleeing, leaving him to die in the fiery wreck,” reported the Daily News.

      Vision Zero promises safer streets. But in Inwood, the numbers do not lie. Crashes are up 16% over last year. Injuries are up 58%.

      The Next Step: Demand More Than Words

      Speed kills. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has the power to redesign deadly intersections. It has the power to protect the most vulnerable. But power unused is no power at all.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. Do not wait for another name to become a number. Take action now.

      NYPD Pursuit Ends In Fatal Crash
      ▸

      Francisco Guzman Parra died in a burning car after a police chase in Inwood. Officers left the scene unreported. Family demands answers. Two investigations run. Streets stay dangerous. System failed to protect. The dead remain silent.

      CBS New York reported on April 27, 2025, that Francisco Andres Guzman Parra, 31, died after crashing a stolen vehicle on Dyckman Street in Manhattan following an NYPD pursuit. The article states, "NYPD sources said the two officers in pursuit returned to their stationhouse without reporting the crash." The FDNY later found Guzman Parra dead in the burning car. Patrick Hendry of the Police Benevolent Association claimed officers "lost sight of the car and did not know it crashed," but the family's attorney, Jeremy Feigenbaum, said their investigation "has not corroborated the officers' claim." The officers remain on leave as both the NYPD and the New York attorney general's office investigate. The case raises questions about police pursuit protocols and reporting failures.

      Police Chase Ends In Fiery Death
      ▸

      A stolen car crashed and burned in Manhattan. Two NYPD officers chased it, then left the driver to die in flames. They never called for help. The wreck was total. The city investigates. The street bears the scar.

      According to the New York Post (April 3, 2025), two NYPD officers pursued a stolen Honda CRV from the Bronx into Manhattan. The chase ended near Dyckman Street when the car slammed into a building and caught fire. The officers 'approached the burning SUV but then left without helping the driver or calling for an ambulance.' They did not report the crash. Both have been suspended while the Force Investigation Division and Attorney General review their actions. The incident follows a February NYPD policy change barring pursuits for non-violent crimes. Commissioner Jessica Tisch said, 'The NYPD's enforcement efforts must never put the public or the police at undue risk.' NYPD data shows one in four pursuits last year led to crashes or injuries. The crash exposes the risks of high-speed chases and gaps in police accountability.
