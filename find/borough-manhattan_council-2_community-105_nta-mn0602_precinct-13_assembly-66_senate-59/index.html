      Gramercy Bleeds While City Sleeps

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting.)

      Gramercy: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Gramercy

      A man steps off the curb. A cyclist waits at the light. A child crosses with her mother. In Gramercy, the street does not forgive. One person killed. Six seriously injured. In three and a half years, 297 people have been hurt in 585 crashes. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do. NYC Open Data

      No one walks away untouched. Cyclists crushed by SUVs. Pedestrians knocked down by sedans. A 23-year-old cyclist, dead on East 21st. A 61-year-old, leg shattered at E 23rd. A pedestrian, head smashed at Park Avenue South. The street keeps its own count. The names fade. The pain does not.

      Who Pays the Price

      The young are not spared. Children under 18, injured seven times in the last year. The old, too—four people over 75, hurt in the same span. The street does not care if you are careful. It does not care if you look both ways. It only cares that you are there.

      Bikes, cars, SUVs, taxis. All have left bodies broken. In the last year, bikes caused six pedestrian injuries, two of them serious. Sedans and SUVs together: sixteen injuries, one serious. Taxis, four. The list is long. The street is indifferent.

      Leadership: Action or Delay

      The city says it is acting. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can now set speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit stands. The law waits. The council waits. The mayor waits. Every day of delay is another day for the street to take.

      Speed cameras work. They cut speeding by more than half. But Albany lets the law lapse. The cameras may go dark. The city calls for more enforcement. The street calls their bluff.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. This is the cost of waiting. The cost of silence. The cost of leaders who do not act. The cost is paid in blood and bone.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the count.
