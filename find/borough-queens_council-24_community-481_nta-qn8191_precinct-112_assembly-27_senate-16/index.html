      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Flushing Meadows-Corona Park?

      Expressways cut through Flushing Meadows. People keep dying.

      Written August 27, 2025 (Revised August 27, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Flushing Meadows-Corona Park: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 27, 2025

      Flushing Meadows-Corona Park is wrapped in lanes of steel. The bodies keep coming.

      • Since 2022, this area logged 1,507 crashes, with 7 people killed and 1,243 hurt. Nights hit hardest; midnight to 1 AM saw the most injuries, with deaths spiking again around 5 AM, 10 AM, 11 AM, 4 PM, and 10 PM (NYC Open Data).

      • The worst corridors are the Van Wyck Expressway and the Grand Central Parkway. Together they account for hundreds of injuries and multiple deaths. The Long Island Expressway adds more death and broken bones.

      • Pedestrians took three of the deaths here. Another person on a moped died on College Point Boulevard at Blossom Avenue. Cars and SUVs dominate the harm (NYC Open Data).

      Night, asphalt, sirens

      On Dec 3, 2024, a southbound SUV on the Van Wyck struck a 41-year-old man in the roadway. He died. The data lists distraction. It also lists alcohol. A parked flatbed truck sat nearby. The crash happened just before noon. The man did not go home that day (CrashID 4776236; crash, persons, vehicles).

      On May 12, 2024, just after midnight, an eastbound SUV hit a 33-year-old woman on the Grand Central Parkway. She died at the scene. The record calls it “other actions in roadway.” No further details (CrashID 4723692; crash, persons, vehicles).

      On Mar 6, 2024, at College Point Boulevard and Blossom Avenue, a 63-year-old woman on a moped collided with an SUV. She was ejected and died. The file lists both vehicles “making left turn” (CrashID 4707767).

      Highways ring the park. People walk there anyway.

      The park’s edge is a belt of fast lanes: Van Wyck, Grand Central, L.I.E.. Since 2022, those three corridors alone saw at least five deaths recorded to this NTA. The first hour after midnight is a red zone: 87 injuries and two deaths there alone. Late night and late evening stack more harm (NYC Open Data).

      “Driver Inattention/Distraction” is stamped across fatal files. So is “Unsafe Speed.” Alcohol shows up too. The pattern repeats on the open data rows (NYC Open Data).

      Hit-and-run is not rare. It is routine.

      On Aug 13, 2025, down in Queens South near JFK, a driver hit a 52-year-old man crossing 155th Street and South Conduit Avenue around 2:30 AM and fled. “The operator of the vehicle fled the scene,” police said (ABC7). “The driver sped off without stopping,” the Daily News wrote. No arrests that day (NY Daily News). Gothamist counted 17 traffic deaths this year in Queens South by Aug 10 (Gothamist). Different precinct. Same borough. Same hour.

      What can be fixed now

      • Daylight the crossings around the park. Harden the left turns at College Point Boulevard and along Roosevelt Avenue. Add leading walk signals at conflict points. Target the late‑night spike with focused enforcement on the expressway service roads. These are standard tools; this map shows where the blood pools most: Van Wyck, Grand Central.

      • Repeat offenders drive a big share of the harm. Albany moved a bill to force speed limiters on the worst of them. Senator John Liu voted yes in committee on S 4045 in June 2025 (Open States).

      • City Hall has the power to cut speeds. The safest move is the simplest: lower the default and enforce it. New Yorkers have been told how to push: call, write, and don’t stop until the policy moves (CrashCount: Take Action).

      Voices from the record

      “Joseph Lee terrorized other drivers as he purposefully drove the wrong way on a busy Queens highway and crashed into multiple cars,” Queens DA Melinda Katz said of a 2023 Clearview Expressway spree that left two motorists badly hurt. A judge gave him eight years on Aug 14, 2025 (amNY).

      “At 2:27 a.m., officers responded to reports of a pedestrian struck… The operator of the vehicle fled the scene,” police told reporters after the JFK killing (ABC7).

      “The driver sped off without stopping. No arrests have been made,” the Daily News reported the same day (NY Daily News).

      The ledger

      • Here, in Flushing Meadows-Corona Park, 3 pedestrians are dead, 1 moped rider is dead, and 3 motorists are dead since 2022. The worst hours lean to the dark. The worst places are the highways that slice the park. The numbers sit on the city’s own servers. They do not grieve. They wait for the next entry (NYC Open Data).

      Take one action today. Add your name and voice to slow cars and stop repeat speeders: act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where are the worst crash locations near Flushing Meadows-Corona Park?
      Van Wyck Expressway and Grand Central Parkway lead the harm, with additional deaths on the Long Island Expressway. These corridors account for hundreds of injuries and multiple deaths per NYC Open Data.
      ▸ When are crashes most likely here?
      The late-night hour after midnight shows the highest injuries, with deaths appearing again around early morning and late evening, according to the hourly distribution in NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Who is getting hurt and killed?
      Since 2022, pedestrians account for three deaths, a moped rider for one, and motorists for three. Cars and SUVs dominate the vehicles involved, per NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What policies could reduce repeat dangerous driving?
      The Stop Super Speeders bill (S 4045) advanced in 2025 and would require speed limiters for repeat violators; State Sen. John Liu voted yes in committee, per Open States.

      Int 0857-2024 Gennaro votes yes to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Jun 30 - Council orders swift removal of abandoned, derelict cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. No plates, no stickers, no excuses. Police and sanitation must act. Safer crossings for all who walk, ride, or wait.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council on June 30, 2025. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to vehicles reported as abandoned to the department of sanitation," requires the Department of Sanitation to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours of report. The NYPD must tow cars lacking valid plates or stickers. Prime sponsor Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led, joined by Crystal Hudson, Amanda Farías, Lincoln Restler, and others. The law targets street hazards, clearing blocked sightlines and crosswalks. It aims to cut risks for pedestrians and cyclists by removing abandoned vehicles fast.
