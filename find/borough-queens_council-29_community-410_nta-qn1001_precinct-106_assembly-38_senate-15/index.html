      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      South Conduit, 131st Street, and the cost of looking away

      South Ozone Park: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 21, 2025

      Just after Sep 6 on South Conduit Avenue at 131 Street, a 39-year-old on a moped hit a truck and left with a torn leg. Police logged severe injuries to his lower leg and foot. NYC Open Data

      This Week

      • On Sep 10 at 121 Street and 107 Avenue, a driver on a moped suffered a concussion in a crash with an SUV. NYC Open Data
      • On Sep 6 at 122 Street and 109 Avenue, a 28-year-old motorcycle rider was ejected and badly cut; police recorded alcohol and failure to yield by drivers in the mix. NYC Open Data
      • On Aug 25 at 109 Avenue and 107 Street, a driver in an SUV turned right and hit a 21-year-old crossing with the signal. NYC Open Data

      The count does not stop

      Since Jan 1, 2022, South Ozone Park has recorded 10 deaths, 2,512 injuries, and 3,758 crashes. Forty of those injuries were marked serious. NYC Open Data

      This year to date: 685 crashes, 487 injuries, 3 deaths. Last year by this point: 659 crashes, 482 injuries, 2 deaths. NYC Open Data

      Police keep writing the same causes: failure to yield, distraction, alcohol. The bodies change. The forms do not. NYC Open Data

      Corners that maim

      The Belt Parkway through this area shows one death and 294 injuries. Liberty Avenue shows 77 injuries. Rockaway Boulevard at 110th has four people seriously hurt. These are not secrets. NYC Open Data

      Deaths here often come in the dark: records show fatal crashes at 3 AM, 4 AM, and again at 7–10 PM. Night or day, the hit is the same. NYC Open Data

      A week ago, a worker directing traffic on the Nassau Expressway was run down and left to die. Police later made an arrest. Her name was Isabel Alvarez, 44. NY Daily News

      Tools on the table

      Simple work can save lives at these corners: no parking at the mouth of the crosswalk, early walk signals, hardened turns that force drivers to slow. The City Council has a bill to ban parking within 20 feet of every crosswalk. It has the votes. The Speaker has not brought it to the floor. Streetsblog NYC

      Her office says, “The safety of pedestrians and all street users remains a top priority for Speaker Adams and the council.” AMNY

      Albany is moving on repeat speeders. State Senator James Sanders voted yes in committee for S4045, which would force chronic violators to use speed limiters. Open States

      What must change now

      • Daylight the deadly corners in South Ozone Park. Start with Liberty Avenue, Rockaway Boulevard, and the Belt Parkway access points. Harden turns. Add leading pedestrian intervals. NYC Open Data
      • Pass the city bill to clear sightlines at every crosswalk. Put it to a vote. Streetsblog NYC
      • Enact speed limiters for the worst offenders statewide. Sanders has voted yes; the Assembly can move next. Open States

      The man on the moped, the woman crossing with the light, the flagger on the Expressway. Different days. Same streets. If you want this to end, act. Start here.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4), filtered to South Ozone Park and the period Jan 1, 2022–Sep 21, 2025. We counted records by injury severity and role (killed, serious injury, injury) and summarized totals and locations. Data were last ingested on Sep 20, 2025. You can start from the city’s crash dataset here.
      ▸ Where are the worst spots?
      Open Data tallies point to the Belt Parkway corridor (1 death, 294 injuries), Liberty Avenue (77 injuries), and Rockaway Boulevard near 110th Street (four serious injuries) during the coverage window. These figures come from the filtered NYC Open Data crash records for this neighborhood.
      ▸ When do deaths tend to happen here?
      Neighborhood records include deaths at 3 AM, 4 AM, and again around 7–10 PM during the coverage window, reflecting both late‑night and evening risk in the local data.
      ▸ What policy changes could help right now?
      Three steps: daylight crosswalks and harden turns at the worst corners; bring the City Council’s universal daylighting bill to a vote; and pass the state’s Stop Super Speeders bill (S4045) to force chronic violators to use speed limiters.
