      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Midtown-Times Square: six dead, 1,397 hurt, and the clock keeps ticking

      Midtown-Times Square: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Midtown keeps bleeding. Since 2022, this small slice of Manhattan logged 10 deaths and 1,397 injuries in 2,721 crashes. Pedestrians and cyclists carry the pain. SUVs and cars do most of the harm. The hours blur, but the damage peaks through the day and into the night.

      Crosswalks as crime scenes

      • At 7th Avenue and West 36th, a driver going straight hit a 34‑year‑old man in the crosswalk. He died there. The report lists distraction. The SUV had no listed damage. City data records it.
      • On Avenue of the Americas at West 58th, a Mazda SUV made a left and struck an 83‑year‑old woman crossing with the signal. She died. The crash file shows it.

      Six pedestrians have been killed here since 2022. Another 488 were hurt. The rollup is clear. SUVs are tied to four pedestrian deaths.

      “Demand for curb space in the city is increasing, from trash collection and bike lanes to truck loading and parking.” NYC DOT said. The curb is policy. The curb is also where people die.

      Cyclists hit the metal

      • At Fifth and West 51st, two 67‑year‑old e‑bike riders collided with a parked 2024 SUV and were killed, the file says. See the listing.
      • Taxis keep surfacing in the injuries. Pedestrians were hurt 62 times where taxis are the striking vehicle. The counts are here.

      Three cyclists have been killed since 2022. 361 were hurt. Most by cars and SUVs. The dataset shows it. At West 51st and 8th, a taxi struck a pedestrian this June. Another entry in a long ledger. Crash record logged.

      Corners that don’t forgive

      The worst spots repeat. Avenue of the Americas leads with one death and 59 injuries. West 51st Street shows three deaths. West 42nd and 7th rack up dozens hurt.

      Crashes spike in the afternoon: 2 p.m. brings 109 injuries. The hour of 3 p.m. shows three deaths. The danger does not sleep; 10 p.m. and 11 p.m. still cut. Hourly tallies tell it.

      What the files call the cause

      The city’s labels are cold. “Other” leads this map of harm with 9 deaths and 733 injured. “Vulnerable road user error” takes 2 deaths. Distraction. Failure to yield. Left turns. A bus bumper. A boom lift parked by the curb. All tagged in the data.

      Fix the corners. Slow the cars.

      • Daylight the crossings on 6th, 7th, and 5th. Harden the left turns where they kill, like 6th at 58th and 7th at 36th. Add leading walk signals. These tools match the failure modes listed: left turns, failure to yield, distraction.
      • Work the repeat hotspots weekly: Avenue of the Americas, West 51st, West 42nd, 7th Avenue. Keep counters on the ground until the injury numbers drop.
      • Trucks and buses cause fewer cases than cars here, but their strikes maim. Tighten turns and give buses protected approaches on the worst blocks.

      The citywide brakes we still don’t use

      Albany gave the city the keys to slow streets. The Council and Mayor can set 20 mph on local roads. They haven’t used it. Our own page lays it out and gives you the numbers to call. Act on it here.

      The state is moving on repeat speeders. The Senate advanced the Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045) to force speed limiters on drivers who rack up points or camera tickets. Committee votes passed. The Assembly has a matching push. See A2299. These are the drivers who turn people into names on a sheet.

      “We are taking immediate steps to fortify this intersection,” the city’s traffic chief said of Canal Street after two people were killed by a car going over 100 mph. The quote is here. One street moved after two deaths. Midtown’s list is longer.

      Use the tools

      Lower the default speed. Install limiters on the worst repeat speeders. Daylight and harden every deadly turn. Then check the numbers. If they don’t fall, do more.

      If you want this to stop, start with one call. Ask the city to set 20 mph and back the speed‑limiter bill.
