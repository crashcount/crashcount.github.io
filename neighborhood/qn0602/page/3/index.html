      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Forest Hills?

      Forest Hills Bleeds: Speed Kills, Leaders Stall

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Forest Hills: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Numbers Don’t Lie

      One death. Five serious injuries. 631 people hurt. That is the toll of traffic violence in Forest Hills since 2022. These are not just numbers—they are bodies broken, lives changed. In the last year alone, 212 people were injured here. Four suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again. No one died in the last twelve months, but luck will not hold.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. An 18-year-old was killed by an SUV on the Long Island Expressway. A 16-year-old girl, crossing with the light, was struck by a bus on Yellowstone Boulevard. A cyclist’s face was torn open in a crash with a van on 108th Street. These are not rare events—they are the drumbeat of daily life in Forest Hills. See the data.

      The Vehicles That Wound and Kill

      SUVs, sedans, trucks, buses, bikes, mopeds. In Forest Hills, SUVs and cars caused the only death and most serious injuries. Trucks and buses left two people with life-altering wounds. Bikes and mopeds hurt others. The street does not forgive mistakes, and the biggest machines do the most harm.

      What Has Been Done—And What Hasn’t

      Local leaders talk of Vision Zero. The city touts new speed limits, more cameras, and intersection redesigns. But in Forest Hills, the carnage continues. The numbers do not move fast enough. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not used it here. Cameras catch speeders, but only where they are installed. The rest of the streets are left to chance.

      The Call

      This is not fate. Every injury, every death, is preventable. Demand more. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to lower the speed limit, build real protection for people on foot and bike, and keep the cameras running. Do not wait for another body in the road. Take action now.

      S 1675
      Comrie co-sponsors bill for online pedestrian safety ratings, no major safety change.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 1675 would force carmakers to face the facts. It creates a pedestrian safety rating system for all vehicles. The ratings go public on the DMV website. Lawmakers want drivers to see the danger. The streets demand it.

      Senate bill S 1675 was introduced on January 13, 2025. It sits in the sponsorship stage. The bill, titled 'Creates a pedestrian safety rating system for motor vehicles,' would require the Department of Motor Vehicles to post these ratings online. Primary sponsor Andrew Gounardes leads a slate of co-sponsors: Leroy Comrie, Michael Gianaris, Brad Hoylman-Sigal, Robert Jackson, Liz Krueger, Zellnor Myrie, Gustavo Rivera, Julia Salazar, Luis R. Sepúlveda, and Toby Ann Stavisky. The bill aims to expose the risk cars pose to people on foot. By making safety data public, it puts pressure on automakers and drivers alike. The measure has not yet moved to committee or received a vote.

      Assembly Bill 1077 aims to open streets to all. Sponsors demand safe access for walkers, cyclists, and drivers. The bill calls for complete street design. It stands as a direct answer to traffic violence. The fight for safer roads moves forward.

      Assembly Bill A 1077, sponsored by Jonathan Rivera and over sixty co-sponsors, sits in the sponsorship stage. The bill, introduced January 8, 2025, in the New York State Assembly, is titled: "Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles." Rivera and his colleagues call for streets built for people, not just cars. The bill has not yet reached committee or vote. Its language is clear: every user deserves safe passage. Complete streets mean sidewalks, bike lanes, and crossings that protect the most vulnerable. This is a direct response to the daily toll of crashes.       S 131
      Comrie co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 131 demands complete street design in state- and federally-funded road projects. It forces agencies to consider people on foot and bike, not just cars. The bill orders public guidance on safer street layouts. Sponsors push for streets that protect all.

      Senate bill S 131, introduced on January 1, 2025, is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in the Senate, awaiting committee action. Its summary reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding; authorizes the department to compile and make publicly available guidance and reference material related to consideration of complete street design.' Primary sponsor Sean Ryan (District 61) leads, joined by co-sponsors Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, Samra Brouk, Cordell Cleare, Leroy Comrie, Jeremy Cooney, Nathalia Fernandez, Michael Gianaris, Kristen Gonzalez, Robert Jackson, Rachel May, Kevin S. Parker, Christopher Ryan, and Luis R. Sepúlveda. The bill compels agencies to put vulnerable road users first in every new project that takes public money. It also demands transparency, requiring the department to publish guidance on safer street design for all.

      A 324
      Hevesi co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 324 demands every state- or federally-funded road project weigh complete street design. The bill pushes agencies to publish clear guidance. Streets built for cars alone kill. This bill aims to force change at the blueprint.

      Assembly Bill 324 was introduced on January 1, 2025, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in the New York State Assembly, with Didi Barrett as the primary sponsor and a long list of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Amy Paulin, and others. The bill summary reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding; authorizes the department to compile and make publicly available guidance and reference material related to consideration of complete street design.' This measure would require state and federally funded transportation projects to consider designs that protect all road users, not just drivers. The bill’s broad sponsorship signals growing support for safer, more inclusive streets. No safety analyst note was provided.
