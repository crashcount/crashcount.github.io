      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bedford Park?

      Bedford Park Bleeds—And City Hall Looks Away

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Bedford Park: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Toll in Bedford Park

      No one died in Bedford Park this year. That is the only mercy. But the streets do not forgive. In the last twelve months, 89 people were hurt in 194 crashes. Three suffered serious injuries. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old. Ten children were hurt. One was left with wounds that will not heal soon. Crash data

      Who Gets Hurt, and How

      Cars and SUVs are the main threat. They caused most of the pain. In the last three years, cars and SUVs were involved in 23 serious or moderate injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds added two more. Bikes caused one. The street does not care what you ride or drive. It only takes.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      The stories repeat. A 17-year-old boy on a bike, bleeding from the head, after a crash on East 198th Street. A 27-year-old man, crossing with the signal, struck at Grand Concourse. A 63-year-old woman, ejected from her bike, left with deep cuts. The details change. The pain does not. Crash data

      What Leaders Have Done—And Not Done

      The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has not used it here. Speed cameras work, but their future is always in doubt. The law that keeps them on is set to expire. Local leaders talk about Vision Zero. They talk about safer streets. But the numbers do not lie. The work is not done. The danger is not gone.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by someone in power. Every delay is a risk. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand more cameras. Demand streets that do not bleed. Take action now.

      Int 0746-2024
      Dinowitz votes yes, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety with greener medians.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to turn bare medians green. The city must plant trees or use medians for stormwater. Agencies must report progress. The law targets high-risk areas. Votes showed strong support. The mayor returned it unsigned. Streets will change.

      Int 0746-2024, now Local Law 94 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 12, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the improvement and maintenance of medians through the planting of vegetation or use in stormwater management," requires the city to improve at least one mile of paved medians every two years until 2046. The Department of Transportation, Parks, and Environmental Protection must add planted medians, tree beds, or stormwater infrastructure, focusing on high-priority investment areas. Council Member Shekar Krishnan sponsored the bill, joined by Feliz, Avilés, Nurse, Banks, Restler, Ung, Schulman, Holden, Hudson, and Yeger. The Council passed the bill with broad support, though the mayor returned it unsigned. The law mandates annual progress reports and public tracking of improved medians and responsible agencies.

      Res 0449-2024
      Dinowitz sponsors resolution to raise overnight commercial parking fines, no direct safety impact.
      ▸

      Council calls for higher fines on commercial vehicles parked overnight. Current caps let companies treat tickets as business costs. Streets fill with trucks. Residents lose space. The city wants power to set real deterrents. Albany holds the key.

      Resolution 0449-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced June 6, 2024, it urges the State Legislature to let cities raise the cap on commercial overnight parking fines. The matter title reads: "Resolution calling on the State legislature to increase the cap on commercial overnight fines for cities." Council Members Eric Dinowitz (primary sponsor), Vickie Paladino, and Robert F. Holden back the measure. They argue that capped fines are too low—companies absorb them, streets clog with trucks, and residents pay the price. The Council wants authority to set fines that actually deter illegal parking. The resolution points to nearly 27,000 complaints in 2021 and notes that current enforcement tools are weak. The bill is still in committee, awaiting further action.

      Int 0606-2024
      Dinowitz co-sponsors e-bike and scooter registration bill, reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council wants plates on every e-bike and scooter. The bill sits in committee. Riders face new rules. Lawmakers push for registration. Streets wait. No change yet. The city debates control. Vulnerable users watch. The system holds its breath.

      Int 0606-2024 was introduced on March 7, 2024, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered," would force all e-bikes, e-scooters, and similar vehicles not already covered by DMV rules to register with the Department of Transportation and display a visible plate. Council Member Inna Vernikov led as primary sponsor, joined by Holden, Farías, Riley, Brannan, and many others. The bill was laid over in committee on December 11, 2024. The measure aims to track these vehicles, but its impact on vulnerable road users remains unassessed. The city’s debate continues, with no law yet in force.
