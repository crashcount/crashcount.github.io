      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Park Slope?

      No More Broken Bodies: Flatbush Needs Action, Not Another Funeral

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Park Slope: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Broken Bodies, One Death, No End in Sight

      In Park Slope, the numbers do not lie. One person killed. Seven left with serious injuries. Six hundred sixty-five injured in three and a half years. The dead do not speak. The injured limp, or do not walk at all. The city moves on. The street stays the same.

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. One death, thirty-one moderate injuries, two serious injuries. Taxis killed and maimed. Bikes and motorcycles wounded, but the weight of steel and speed falls hardest from behind a wheel. The old and the young are not spared. In the last year, thirteen children were hurt. Three people suffered injuries so grave they may never heal. No one died, but the cost is not measured only in funerals.

      Leadership: Promises, Plans, and the Waiting

      The city says it will act. The Department of Transportation promises a new Flatbush Avenue. Two car lanes gone. Bus lanes in the center. Pedestrian islands. The plan is bold on paper. “Right now, Flatbush Avenue above Prospect Park doesn’t work for anyone. Almost 70,000 daily bus riders are stuck waiting too long for slow buses, drivers are caught in a mess of traffic, and pedestrians are left crossing intersections clogged with vehicles,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. The city calls it a world-class corridor. The street calls it a delay.

      Since 2019, fifty-five people have been killed or severely injured on Flatbush Avenue alone. The city knows this. “City transportation officials said the changes would also improve street safety along the stretch, noting 55 people have been killed or severely injured in car crashes there since 2019” reported Gothamist.

      The Next Fight: Action, Not Words

      Promises do not stop cars. Plans do not heal broken bones. The city must move faster. Residents must demand it. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them: No more waiting. No more bodies. No more blood on the street.

      Take action now.

      Hanif co-sponsors cool pavement pilot bill with no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council wants DOT to test cool pavement in four heat-vulnerable, disadvantaged neighborhoods. The pilot aims to fight the city’s heat island effect. The bill sits in committee. No direct impact on pedestrian or cyclist safety. Streets stay hot. Danger remains.

      Int 0928-2024, introduced May 23, 2024, sits 'Laid Over in Committee' at the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill orders the Department of Transportation, with Health and Mental Hygiene, to run a pilot project testing cool pavement—materials that lower street temperatures—across at least four neighborhoods facing heat and disadvantage. The official summary reads: 'A Local Law in relation to requiring the department of transportation to conduct a pilot project on the use of cool pavement.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by 29 co-sponsors. The bill requires DOT to report findings to the Mayor and Council. Safety analysts note no direct effect on vulnerable road users: the measure targets heat, not traffic violence. The city’s most dangerous streets remain unchanged.

      Bill A4120/S1981, now before the New York State Legislature, seeks a 20% reduction in vehicle miles traveled (VMT) by 2050. The measure is championed by State Sen. Advocates argue this shift will save lives, cut pollution, and undo harms from car-centric planning.

      Gounardes Opposes Harmful Status Quo Enables Reckless Driving
      ▸

      Drivers rack up speed and red light camera tickets. No points. No suspensions. Gianaris backs a bill to yank registrations after five violations. Victims’ families and DOT demand action. The loophole leaves reckless drivers free to kill.

      Queens Senator Michael Gianaris, with Assemblymember William Magnarelli, introduced a bill to suspend vehicle registrations after five speed or red light camera violations in one year. The measure remains in committee. The bill responds to a fatal flaw: 'We have no mechanism right now, under law, to crack down on extremely reckless drivers,' said Brooklyn State Sen. Andrew Gounardes. The current law lets drivers rack up dozens of camera tickets and keep driving, since no points are added to licenses. Gianaris’s bill aims to close this loophole. DOT and crash victims’ families, including Juliane Williams, whose daughter was killed by a speeding driver, press for stronger penalties and expansion of the red light camera program. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program expired with little effect. The city’s vulnerable road users remain at risk while repeat offenders face few real consequences.
