      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill?

      Deadly Streets, Silent Leaders: Demand Action Before Another Life Is Lost

      Written June 20, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Numbers Don’t Lie

      One death. Twelve serious injuries. In the last three and a half years, the streets of Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill have not spared the vulnerable. Cyclists, pedestrians, the old, the young. In 959 crashes, 376 people were hurt. Two were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. The dead do not speak. The injured carry the story in their bodies.

      The Latest Losses

      On June 18, Salvador Nico Garcia rode his e-bike through Central Park. He collided with a pedestrian in the crosswalk at East Drive and 97th Street. He was thrown from the bike. His head struck the curb. He died at the hospital. The pedestrian walked away with a cut hand. The city counted Garcia as the sixth e-bike rider killed this year. Nico Garcia was thrown from his electric bike by the impact and slammed his head on the curb.
      The NYPD’s Collision Investigation Squad is still reviewing the case. A 43 year-old Bronx resident died on June 18 after flying from an e-bike and striking his head on the curb.

      Who Bears the Cost

      Cars and SUVs caused the most pain. Thirty-two crashes left pedestrians bleeding or broken. Buses and trucks struck two more. Three people were hit by bikes. One by a moped. The numbers are small until you see the faces. A woman crossing with the signal. A cyclist thrown to the pavement. A hand that will never fully heal.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      City leaders talk about Vision Zero. They tout new speed cameras and intersection redesigns. But the carnage continues. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not used it. Cameras that catch speeders are at risk of going dark unless Albany acts. The silence is loud. The clock ticks. Every day without action is another day someone does not come home.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill. Take action now.

      Assembly Bill 803 lands in Albany. It targets drivers who block bike lanes. Cameras will catch violators. Streets choke with cars. Cyclists swerve, risk death. Lawmakers push for clear lanes. The city’s riders wait for action.

      Assembly Bill 803 was introduced on January 6, 2025, in the New York State Assembly. The bill sits in the sponsorship stage. It aims to 'establish in the city of New York a bicycle lane safety program to enforce certain restrictions on the use of bicycle lanes and/or protected bicycle lanes by means of bicycle lane photo devices.' Primary sponsor Zohran Mamdani leads a coalition of fourteen co-sponsors, including Brian Cunningham, Jessica Gonzalez-Rojas, and Harvey Epstein. The bill has not yet advanced to committee or vote. If passed, it would allow automated cameras to ticket drivers who block bike lanes, a move designed to keep lanes clear for cyclists and prevent deadly crashes. Lawmakers back the measure, but the city’s vulnerable road users still face daily danger until enforcement begins.

      Int 0346-2024
      Powers votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.
