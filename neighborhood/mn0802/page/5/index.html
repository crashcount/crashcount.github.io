      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill?

      Deadly Streets, Silent Leaders: Demand Action Before Another Life Is Lost

      Written June 20, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Numbers Don’t Lie

      One death. Twelve serious injuries. In the last three and a half years, the streets of Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill have not spared the vulnerable. Cyclists, pedestrians, the old, the young. In 959 crashes, 376 people were hurt. Two were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. The dead do not speak. The injured carry the story in their bodies.

      The Latest Losses

      On June 18, Salvador Nico Garcia rode his e-bike through Central Park. He collided with a pedestrian in the crosswalk at East Drive and 97th Street. He was thrown from the bike. His head struck the curb. He died at the hospital. The pedestrian walked away with a cut hand. The city counted Garcia as the sixth e-bike rider killed this year. Nico Garcia was thrown from his electric bike by the impact and slammed his head on the curb.
      The NYPD’s Collision Investigation Squad is still reviewing the case. A 43 year-old Bronx resident died on June 18 after flying from an e-bike and striking his head on the curb.

      Who Bears the Cost

      Cars and SUVs caused the most pain. Thirty-two crashes left pedestrians bleeding or broken. Buses and trucks struck two more. Three people were hit by bikes. One by a moped. The numbers are small until you see the faces. A woman crossing with the signal. A cyclist thrown to the pavement. A hand that will never fully heal.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      City leaders talk about Vision Zero. They tout new speed cameras and intersection redesigns. But the carnage continues. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not used it. Cameras that catch speeders are at risk of going dark unless Albany acts. The silence is loud. The clock ticks. Every day without action is another day someone does not come home.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill. Take action now.

      Powers co-sponsors cool pavement pilot bill with no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council wants DOT to test cool pavement in four heat-vulnerable, disadvantaged neighborhoods. The pilot aims to fight the city’s heat island effect. The bill sits in committee. No direct impact on pedestrian or cyclist safety. Streets stay hot. Danger remains.

      Int 0928-2024, introduced May 23, 2024, sits 'Laid Over in Committee' at the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill orders the Department of Transportation, with Health and Mental Hygiene, to run a pilot project testing cool pavement—materials that lower street temperatures—across at least four neighborhoods facing heat and disadvantage. The official summary reads: 'A Local Law in relation to requiring the department of transportation to conduct a pilot project on the use of cool pavement.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by 29 co-sponsors. The bill requires DOT to report findings to the Mayor and Council. Safety analysts note no direct effect on vulnerable road users: the measure targets heat, not traffic violence. The city’s most dangerous streets remain unchanged.

      Int 0177-2024
      Powers co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by targeting fake license plates.
      ▸

      Council moves to outlaw driving with fake or expired plates. The bill sets fines and a short grace period for expired tags. Lawmakers say this targets scofflaws who dodge accountability. The measure now sits with the Public Safety Committee.

      Int 0177-2024, now in the Committee on Public Safety, was introduced on February 28, 2024. The bill reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting the operation of a motor vehicle with fraudulent or expired license plates.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by Feliz, Salamanca, Powers, Restler, Won, Brewer, Schulman, Ung, Marte, Hudson, Avilés, De La Rosa, and the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill makes it unlawful to drive with fake or expired plates, including temporary ones. Civil penalties apply, but drivers with expired plates get a 10-day window to fix the issue. The bill aims to close loopholes that let reckless drivers evade detection and accountability. It awaits further action in committee.

      Bores Supports Safety Boosting License and Registration Bill
      ▸

      Council backs state bills to force moped buyers to show a valid license and register at the point of sale. Unregistered mopeds menace streets. Pedestrians and cyclists pay the price. The measure aims to close loopholes and cut illegal, dangerous riding.

      Resolution 0024-2024 sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced February 8, 2024, it urges Albany to pass A.8052 and S.7703. The resolution calls for 'purchasers of limited use motorcycles present a driver’s license appropriate for the legal operation... and register such limited use motorcycles, prior to completing a purchase.' Council Member Gale A. Brewer leads, joined by Rivera, Schulman, Krishnan, Won, Bottcher, and others. The bills would require dealers to check licenses and register mopeds at sale. The Council notes that illegal, unregistered mopeds endanger pedestrians, cyclists, and riders. NYPD seized over 8,600 illegal mopeds by September 2023. The resolution presses for safeguards to keep unlicensed, unregistered vehicles off city streets.

      Int 0094-2024
      Powers sponsors bill restricting low bass sound permits, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council bill targets deep bass. No more permits for sound devices thumping below 40 hertz. Police commissioner loses power to approve these. Schulman and Powers push the change. Action sits with Public Safety Committee. No direct impact on street safety for walkers or riders.

      Int 0094-2024, introduced February 8, 2024, sits with the Committee on Public Safety. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting the issuance of permits for the use of sound apparatuses to produce low bass sounds,' would stop the police commissioner from granting permits for devices that emit bass at 40 hertz or below for 10 seconds or more. Council Members Keith Powers (primary sponsor, District 4) and Lynn C. Schulman (co-sponsor, District 29) lead the effort. The bill was referred to committee on introduction. The measure amends existing sound permit rules but does not address traffic violence or the safety of pedestrians and cyclists. No safety analyst has found a direct effect on vulnerable road users.
