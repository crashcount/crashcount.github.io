      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Williamsburg?

      Williamsburg Bleeds While City Hall Turns Back

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Williamsburg: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      Blood on the Streets: The Toll in Williamsburg

      A man dies behind the wheel on Lorimer. A cyclist, helmet on, is crushed on Kent Avenue. Two teens on bikes, both sixteen, are thrown and cut open on Driggs. A pedestrian, 26, is struck in the head by a backing sedan on Hope Street. In three and a half years, Williamsburg has seen 3 deaths and 10 serious injuries from crashes. 825 people have been hurt. The numbers do not flinch. They do not heal.

      Broken Promises, Broken Bones

      The city tried to build a shield. They called it a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. It was supposed to keep riders safe. It failed. Crashes piled up. The city tore it out. As CBS New York reported, “The redesign was initially intended to improve safety for cyclists, but recent incidents have prompted city officials to revert to the previous layout.” The shield is gone. The street is bare again.

      Mayor Adams called it a matter of safety. He said he was “back-pedaling on a protected bike lane… citing safety concerns.” The city’s answer to danger is to take away the only barrier between flesh and steel.

      Who Pays the Price?

      The city counts the dead. It counts the injured. It does not count the cost to families, to children, to the ones who walk and ride because they have no other way. Cars and trucks caused the most pain—22 crashes left pedestrians bleeding or worse. Bikes and mopeds, too, but the numbers are smaller. The city’s answer is to wait, to study, to undo what little was done.

      What Now? Take the Fight to City Hall

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to stop removing protections. Tell them to build streets that do not kill. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real barriers, not paint. Demand action before another name is added to the list.

      Take action now.

      A 2299
      Gallagher sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed-limiting tech.
      ▸

      Drivers who rack up points or camera tickets face forced speed control. Assembly bill A 2299 targets repeat violators. The law would require intelligent speed assistance devices. The goal: stop reckless drivers before they kill.

      Assembly Bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 17, 2025, it awaits committee review. The bill summary reads: 'Requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Assembly Member Emily Gallagher leads as primary sponsor, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, Tony Simone, and others. No votes have been recorded yet. The measure aims to curb repeat dangerous driving by forcing speed limiters on offenders. This targets a core threat to pedestrians and cyclists: unchecked speed and repeat lawbreaking.

      A 1077
      Gallagher co-sponsors bill boosting street safety for all users.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 1077 aims to open streets to all. Sponsors demand safe access for walkers, cyclists, and drivers. The bill calls for complete street design. It stands as a direct answer to traffic violence. The fight for safer roads moves forward.

      Assembly Bill A 1077, sponsored by Jonathan Rivera and over sixty co-sponsors, sits in the sponsorship stage. The bill, introduced January 8, 2025, in the New York State Assembly, is titled: "Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles." Rivera and his colleagues call for streets built for people, not just cars. The bill has not yet reached committee or vote. Its language is clear: every user deserves safe passage. Complete streets mean sidewalks, bike lanes, and crossings that protect the most vulnerable. This is a direct response to the daily toll of crashes. The measure signals strong legislative support for systemic change.
