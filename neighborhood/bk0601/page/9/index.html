      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook?

      Five Dead, Hundreds Hurt: City Leaders Stall While Streets Bleed

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      Five dead. Fourteen seriously hurt. Nearly a thousand injured. That’s the cost of traffic violence in Carroll Gardens, Cobble Hill, Gowanus, and Red Hook since 2022. NYC Open Data. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do. Pedestrians, cyclists, children—none are spared. Cars and trucks do most of the damage. Trucks killed. SUVs broke bones. Bikes and mopeds left their own scars, but the big machines do the worst work.

      Just last year, a truck turned and killed an 83-year-old woman crossing Butler and Bond. NYC Open Data. She died on the street. No arrest. No comfort. Only the cold record: “Apparent Death.” The city moves on. The street stays the same.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      Local leaders talk safety. The streets say otherwise. Council Member Shahana Hanif co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks and force the city to clear sightlines at 1,000 intersections a year. NYC Council – Legistar. The bill sits in committee. No vote. No law. Senator Andrew Gounardes sponsored a bill to let speed cameras catch drivers who hide their plates. Open States. It waits in Albany. Another bill to force repeat speeders to slow down is still just talk.

      Advocates push. Leaders promise. The wheels turn slow. “Our elected officials in Albany have failed the livable streets movement again,” said a Streetsblog report.

      The Human Cost

      Every number is a person. In the last 12 months, one person died, four were seriously hurt, and over 300 were injured in crashes here. NYC Open Data. Children, elders, workers. A crash on Atlantic and Court killed an 18-year-old woman. A man died on Hicks Street. The stories pile up. The pain does not fade.

      A witness to one crash said, “I just saw a lot of blood gushing out of his ears, his mouth.” NY Daily News. Another said, “People ran upstairs, got towels, trying to stop the bleeding.” NY Daily News.

      What Next? Demand More

      This is not fate. This is policy. Streets can be made safe. Leaders can act. Call your council member. Demand a vote on daylighting. Call your senator. Demand action on speeders. Join the fight. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, community board Brooklyn CB6, city council district District 39, assembly district AD 52 and state senate district SD 26.

      Cars and Trucks: 93 crashes involving cars and SUVs, 17 involving trucks and buses. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 6 crashes. Bikes: 5 crashes. Most deaths and serious injuries came from cars, SUVs, and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. These crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement can stop the deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?

      ▸ What has been done recently to improve street safety here?

      ▸ How many people have been killed or seriously hurt in this area since 2022?
      Five people have died. Fourteen have been seriously injured. 975 people have been hurt in crashes since 2022.

      Int 1069-2024
      Avilés co-sponsors bill reducing commercial parking time, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1069-2024 slashes legal parking time for tractor-trailers and similar trucks to 90 minutes. Commercial vehicles clogging curbs will face tighter limits. The measure targets streets where hulking rigs linger, blocking sightlines and endangering walkers and riders.

      Int 1069-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on September 26, 2024. The bill reads: "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to reducing the maximum time commercial vehicles may park." It limits parking for tractor-trailer combinations, tractors, truck trailers, and semi-trailers to 90 minutes, down from three hours. Council Member Kevin C. Riley leads as primary sponsor, joined by Nantasha M. Williams, Darlene Mealy, Sandy Nurse, and others. The bill was referred to committee the day it was introduced. No safety analyst has yet assessed the direct impact on vulnerable road users, but the measure aims to keep large trucks from blocking curbs and crosswalks, where they threaten pedestrians and cyclists.

      Int 0346-2024
      Avilés votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.

      Int 0746-2024
      Avilés votes yes, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety with greener medians.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to turn bare medians green. The city must plant trees or use medians for stormwater. Agencies must report progress. The law targets high-risk areas. Votes showed strong support. The mayor returned it unsigned. Streets will change.

      Int 0746-2024, now Local Law 94 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 12, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure.       Int 1039-2024
      Hanif co-sponsors bike share fee cap, boosting cycling safety and access.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1039-2024 would stop bike share operators from charging members more than a subway fare for short rides. The cap covers e-bikes up to one hour and regular bikes up to two hours. The bill sits in committee.

      Int 1039-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on September 12, 2024. The bill reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to setting maximum amounts for certain member usage fees charged by a bike share operator.' Council Member Chi A. Ossé leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Marte, Hudson, Cabán, Brannan, Hanif, Brewer, and Abreu. The bill would prohibit bike share companies from charging members more than the base subway fare for e-bike rides of one hour or less, or regular bike rides of two hours or less. The law would take effect 120 days after passage and apply to new or renewed contracts. This measure aims to keep bike share affordable for New Yorkers, making cycling a more accessible option.
