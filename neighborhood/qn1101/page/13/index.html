      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Auburndale?

      Auburndale Bleeds While City Waits

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Auburndale: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Auburndale

      Five dead. Six seriously hurt. In three and a half years, Auburndale’s streets have claimed lives both young and old. A 75-year-old woman, crossing 192nd Street, was struck and killed by a sedan. A 19-year-old, crossing with the signal on Utopia Parkway, was struck and injured by a car. A 40-year-old woman, a 30-year-old man, a 72-year-old driver—each lost in crashes that left families with empty chairs and silent phones. See the data.

      Nearly 500 injured. The numbers do not flinch. In the last twelve months alone, 165 people were hurt in 265 crashes. No one was spared: children, elders, cyclists, drivers. The pain is spread wide, but it lands hardest on those with the least protection.

      Who Bears the Brunt

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. Sedans and SUVs killed or seriously injured more pedestrians than any other vehicle. Trucks and buses are rare, but when they hit, the damage is deep. Bikes and motorcycles did not kill here, but the threat is always present. The street is a gauntlet, and the odds are not in your favor if you walk or ride.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      The city has tools. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. Speed cameras, proven to cut speeding and injuries, are in place but need reauthorization. Yet the pace is slow. The law is there. The power is there. The will is not always there. Each day of delay is another day of risk.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by those who set the rules and draw the lines. The blood on the street is not washed away by words.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand more cameras. Demand streets that put people first.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Int 0312-2022
      Paladino co-sponsors bill raising fines for excessive vehicle noise.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to hike penalties for loud cars and trucks. The measure targeted blaring horns, illegal claxons, and roaring engines. It died at session’s end. No direct safety gains for people walking or biking. Noise remains. Streets stay loud.

      Int 0312-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety. The bill aimed to amend the city’s administrative code to increase penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles, including those under and over 10,000 pounds, and for unauthorized horns and claxons. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to increasing certain penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles.' Council Member Sandra Ung sponsored the bill, joined by 26 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. There is no evidence the bill would have improved safety for vulnerable road users. The city’s noise problem, and its threat to livable streets, remains unresolved.

      Paladino co-sponsors bill shifting sidewalk repair to city, boosting pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0336-2022 would force the Parks Department to repair sidewalks wrecked by city trees. Homeowners escape blame. The city takes the hit. The bill died in committee. Broken sidewalks stay broken. Cyclists and pedestrians still trip.

      Int 0336-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to shift responsibility for sidewalk damage caused by city-owned trees from homeowners to the Parks Department. The matter title reads: 'requiring the parks department to repair damage caused by trees owned by the city of New York.' Council Member Kalman Yeger sponsored the bill, joined by Holden, Ung, Ayala, Restler, Dinowitz, Williams, Gutiérrez, Riley, Schulman, Brooks-Powers, Lee, Paladino, and Vernikov. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. If passed, it could have led to safer, smoother sidewalks for pedestrians and cyclists, removing a hazard that city government created and left to fester.

      Res 0071-2022
      Ung co-sponsors multilingual transit announcements, boosting public safety for non-English speakers.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to speak to the city. Four languages, not just English. Riders lost in translation miss warnings, miss stops, miss safety. The bill died in committee. The silence continues. Millions still ride in the dark.

      Resolution 0071-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on March 10, 2022. The resolution urged the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make bus and subway announcements in the four most commonly spoken languages in New York City. The official title: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make bus and subway announcements in the four most commonly spoken languages in New York City.' Council Members Shahana K. Hanif (primary sponsor), Sandra Ung, Crystal Hudson, Julie Won, and Tiffany Cabán backed the measure. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. The MTA still makes most announcements in English only.

      Ung Pushes Queens Greenway Funding Despite Harmful Delays
      ▸

      Forty years. No greenway. Queens waits as city stalls. Parks and DOT talk, but no cash. Council Member Sandra Ung vows to fight for funding. Cyclists and families left exposed. The path remains a promise. Danger and delay linger.

      This action concerns the long-delayed Queens Greenway, discussed in a public statement on February 17, 2022. The project, first planned in 1977 and 1988, would connect Flushing Meadows Corona Park to Fort Totten with a safe route for pedestrians and cyclists. The Parks Department and DOT presented a new vision but admitted that none of the $105 million needed is funded. Council Member Sandra Ung, representing parts of the greenway, said, 'I want to finally see it become a reality,' and stressed the need for accessible open spaces. Still, she could not commit any specific funding. Cycling advocates criticized the city for asking residents to pick favorite sub-projects, calling it a tactic to lower expectations. The city offered no timeline. Vulnerable road users remain at risk as the greenway languishes.
