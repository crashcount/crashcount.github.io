      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Arden Heights-Rossville?

      No More Blood on Arden Heights Streets

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Arden Heights-Rossville: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Three dead. Fifty injured. Two left with wounds that will not heal. This is Arden Heights-Rossville since 2022. These numbers are not just statistics. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the sound of sirens at dawn and the empty seat at a table.

      Crashes do not slow down. In the last twelve months, there were 108 crashes. One person died. Fifty were hurt. Children were not spared—seven under eighteen were injured. The old were not spared—three over seventy-five were hurt. The road does not care who you are.

      Who Bears the Brunt

      Pedestrians bleed most. A 69-year-old woman, crossing at Yucca Drive and Dogwood Drive, was struck by an SUV making a left turn. Severe bleeding. A 20-year-old woman, not at an intersection, was hit by a sedan. Severe lacerations. These are not rare stories. They are routine. See the data.

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. In the last three years, SUVs and sedans caused most pedestrian injuries. Trucks and motorcycles are not far behind. Bikes caused none. The danger is heavy, fast, and steel.

      Leadership: Action or Delay?

      The city has the power to lower speed limits. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can set 20 mph limits. But the limit in Arden Heights-Rossville is not yet 20. The city has built more protected bike lanes and redesigned intersections elsewhere. But here, the pace is slow. The silence is loud.

      Speed cameras save lives. But the law that keeps them running is always at risk. Each year, Albany must act or the cameras go dark. Each year, the risk returns.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by leaders who act—or do not. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand more cameras. Demand streets built for people, not just cars.

      Do not wait for another siren.

      Lanza Labels Outerbridge Widening Dangerous Safety Risk
      ▸

      Port Authority will spend $8.3 million to study widening the Outerbridge Crossing. Critics warn more lanes mean more cars, not less congestion. Officials promise to consider a path for cyclists and pedestrians. The bridge remains dangerous for all who cross.

      On September 22, 2023, the Port Authority approved an $8.3 million study to examine widening the Outerbridge Crossing, as required by a 2021 state law. The study, led by HDR, will look at expanding the bridge’s narrow lanes to meet federal standards and possibly adding more lanes. The matter summary states the goal is to 'relieve congestion and improve traffic flow.' State Senator Andrew Lanza pushed for the study, calling the bridge 'very narrow therefore very dangerous.' Port Authority Executive Director Rick Cotton acknowledged induced demand and said the study will consider all options, including a shared-use path for cyclists and pedestrians. Former Traffic Commissioner Sam Schwartz opposed widening, urging investment in public transit instead. The study’s outcome could shape the future safety of vulnerable road users crossing between Staten Island and New Jersey.

      Int 1033-2023
      Borelli co-sponsors bill that could slow street safety improvements citywide.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1033-2023 orders DOT to consult FDNY before approving open streets or bike lanes that cut car lanes or parking. Firehouses get advance notice. The bill aims to keep emergency routes clear but risks slowing safety projects for walkers and riders.

      Int 1033-2023, filed at session’s end in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced May 11, 2023, by Council Member Robert F. Holden with co-sponsors including Ariola, Riley, Yeger, Restler, Brooks-Powers, Hanks, Borelli, Carr, Kagan, Vernikov, and Paladino. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code... requiring the department of transportation to consult with the fire department prior to approving open street applications and certain bicycle lane projects and to notify affected firehouses...” It requires DOT to consult FDNY and notify firehouses before approving open streets, bike lanes, or major transportation projects that remove car or parking lanes. DOT must certify this consultation. The bill does not apply to emergency projects. While it aims to protect emergency access, advocates warn it could delay or block street redesigns that save lives for people walking and biking.

      Int 0872-2022
      Borelli co-sponsors bill to curb illegal curb cuts, boosting pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council moved to target illegal curb cuts. DOT would mark rogue driveways, order fixes, and bill owners for repairs. Community boards would get curb cut permit alerts. The bill stalled. Sidewalks stay exposed. Cars keep cutting through.

      Int 0872-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It was introduced on December 21, 2022, and filed at session’s end on December 31, 2023. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to illegal curb cuts and requiring local community board notification of curb cut applications.” Council Members Crystal Hudson (primary), Joseph C. Borelli, Justin L. Brannan, Robert F. Holden, and Kalman Yeger sponsored. The bill required DOT to investigate illegal curb cut complaints within 30 days, mark illegal cuts with green paint, and force owners to restore or legalize them. If owners failed, DOT would repair and recover costs. Community boards would get notice of new curb cut permits and sixty days to comment. The bill did not pass. No safety analyst reviewed its impact for vulnerable road users.

      Int 0291-2022
      Borelli votes yes, boosting citywide safety with new greenway plan.
      ▸

      The Council passed Int 0291-2022, forcing city agencies to map, plan, and report on greenways. The law demands annual updates and public engagement. It aims to carve out safe, car-free corridors for walkers and cyclists. The mayor returned it unsigned.

      Int 0291-2022, now Local Law 115 of 2022, was enacted by the City Council on November 27, 2022. The bill came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, first introduced April 28, 2022. The law's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to a citywide greenway plan.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers sponsored the bill, joined by dozens of co-sponsors including Rivera, Hudson, and others. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor on October 27, 2022. The law orders the Department of Transportation and Parks to identify, map, and report on greenways, and to consult with community boards. Proposals for new greenway segments or repairs must be presented to affected communities within 60 days. The mayor returned the bill unsigned, but it became law. The measure compels the city to plan and maintain safe routes for non-drivers, with regular public reporting and accountability.
