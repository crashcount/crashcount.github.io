      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Maspeth?

      Maspeth Bleeds While City Hall Sleeps

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Maspeth: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Bodies on the Road

      In Maspeth, the numbers do not lie. Eleven people have died on these streets since 2022. Six more suffered injuries so grave they were marked as serious. 768 people have been hurt—bodies broken, lives changed. The dead do not speak. The wounded carry the story in scars and limps. NYC crash data

      The Pattern of Harm

      Pedestrians, cyclists, and riders of small vehicles pay the highest price. Trucks and SUVs strike hardest. In one crash, a 75-year-old woman was killed crossing with the signal, hit by a turning pickup truck. A cyclist crushed by a box truck. A moped rider thrown and left bleeding. The pattern repeats. The names change. The pain does not.

      Leadership: Progress and Silence

      City leaders have the power to act. Albany passed Sammy’s Law, giving New York City the right to lower speed limits. The city can set 20 mph limits on local streets. But in Maspeth, the pace of change is slow. No record of bold action from local officials. No public push for safer crossings or protected bike lanes. The silence is its own answer.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every day without action is a day closer to the next siren, the next family shattered. The tools exist: lower speed limits, redesign dangerous intersections, enforce the laws that save lives. But tools unused are as useless as promises unkept.

      Call your council member. Demand safer streets. Do not wait for another obituary. Take Action

      Holden Backs Misguided E-Bike Registration and Crackdowns
      ▸

      Council Member Holden’s bill demands license plates for e-bikes and scooters. Applause for deliveristas turns to boos. The alliance calls for crackdowns, fines, and reclassification. The crowd fears e-vehicles, but city data shows cars injure more.

      On September 28, 2023, Queens Council Member Bob Holden introduced Intro. 758 at an E-Vehicle Safety Alliance (E-VSA) meeting. The bill, now backed by a Council majority, would require all legal e-bikes and scooters to be registered and display license plates. The meeting opened with a standing ovation for Holden, but tensions rose when NYPD Deputy Inspector Erik Worobey praised deliveristas for safer riding. The crowd booed, voicing fear of fast e-vehicles. The E-VSA’s nine-point plan calls for reclassifying e-bikes as mopeds, ending a park e-bike pilot, and NYPD confiscation of e-vehicles on sidewalks, with a $500 retrieval fine. The group frames itself as victims of 'rogue' riders, though city statistics show drivers of larger vehicles cause far more injuries. The bill does not address mopeds or provide funding, and critics say it ignores the dangers faced by delivery workers, 26 of whom died between 2020 and 2022.

      Int 1173-2023
      Holden co-sponsors bill on emergency vehicle access, no clear safety impact.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to keep open streets open for fire trucks and ambulances. No more moving barriers by hand. DOT must warn walkers and riders when sirens come. The bill died at session’s end. Streets stay risky. Safety waits.

      Int 1173-2023 was introduced in the City Council on September 14, 2023, and referred to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill’s title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to ensuring emergency vehicle access to open streets.' Council Members Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor, District 44) and Robert F. Holden (co-sponsor, District 30) pushed for rules so emergency vehicles could always reach open streets—no more manual barrier moves. The Department of Transportation would also have to alert pedestrians and cyclists when vehicles enter. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. Vulnerable road users remain exposed while the city delays action.

      Int 1151-2023
      Holden co-sponsors solar crosswalk bill, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council pushed for 500 solar-lit crosswalks. The bill demanded action—100 new devices each year. It called for a hard look at results. But the session ended. The bill died. Streets wait. Pedestrians keep crossing in the dark.

      Int 1151-2023, introduced August 3, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to require the Department of Transportation to install at least 100 illuminated, solar-powered traffic control devices at crosswalks each year for five years—a total of 500. The bill also ordered a study comparing these devices to unlit signs, probing their power to deter traffic violations and mapping out logistical hurdles. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the installation of solar-powered crosswalks.' Council Member Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Menin, Brooks-Powers, Rivera, and over thirty others. The bill was filed at session’s end on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The city’s crosswalks remain unchanged. Vulnerable road users remain exposed.
