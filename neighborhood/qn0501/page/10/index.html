      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Maspeth?

      Maspeth Bleeds While City Hall Sleeps

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Maspeth: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Bodies on the Road

      In Maspeth, the numbers do not lie. Eleven people have died on these streets since 2022. Six more suffered injuries so grave they were marked as serious. 768 people have been hurt—bodies broken, lives changed. The dead do not speak. The wounded carry the story in scars and limps. NYC crash data

      The Pattern of Harm

      Pedestrians, cyclists, and riders of small vehicles pay the highest price. Trucks and SUVs strike hardest. In one crash, a 75-year-old woman was killed crossing with the signal, hit by a turning pickup truck. A cyclist crushed by a box truck. A moped rider thrown and left bleeding. The pattern repeats. The names change. The pain does not.

      Leadership: Progress and Silence

      City leaders have the power to act. Albany passed Sammy’s Law, giving New York City the right to lower speed limits. The city can set 20 mph limits on local streets. But in Maspeth, the pace of change is slow. No record of bold action from local officials. No public push for safer crossings or protected bike lanes. The silence is its own answer.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every day without action is a day closer to the next siren, the next family shattered. The tools exist: lower speed limits, redesign dangerous intersections, enforce the laws that save lives. But tools unused are as useless as promises unkept.

      Call your council member. Demand safer streets. Do not wait for another obituary. Take Action

      Councilmember Holden pushes tougher penalties for fake and obscured plates. NYPD tickets rise, but repeat offenders dodge tolls and cameras. Advocates warn: weak enforcement means more cheating, less safety, and crowded streets. The city cracks down, but loopholes remain.

      Int 1259-2023
      Holden co-sponsors helmet mandate bill that undermines overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council Member Holden pushed a bill to force all cyclists to wear helmets. The measure threatened $50 fines for bare heads. The council filed it at session’s end. No new protections for riders. The city’s danger stays the same.

      Int 1259-2023, introduced by Council Member Robert F. Holden, aimed to require every bicyclist in New York City to wear protective headgear. The bill was referred to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on December 6, 2023, and filed without passage at the end of the session on December 31, 2023. The measure stated: 'No person shall ride upon or operate a bicycle without wearing protective headgear of good fit fastened securely upon the head with the headgear straps.' Violators faced civil penalties up to $50. Holden sponsored the bill. The law would have exempted those already covered by other helmet laws. The council took no further action. No safety analyst reviewed the bill’s impact on vulnerable road users.

      On December 6, 2023, Councilmember Robert F. Holden (District 30) led a push for his bill requiring license plates and registration for all e-bikes, e-scooters, and similar vehicles. The debate follows Mayor Adams' pilot for public e-bike charging stations.

      Holden Supports Misguided E-Bike Licensing Bill Safety Risks
      ▸

      Council debates Intro 758, a bill to license and register all e-bikes and e-scooters. Cyclists push back. Advocates warn it will hurt riders, not reckless drivers. Supporters say chaos rules the streets. The fight centers on who pays the price.

      Intro 758, debated in the City Council on November 17, 2023, would require all legal e-bikes and e-scooters in New York City to be licensed and registered with the Department of Transportation. The bill, sponsored by Council Member Robert Holden, claims to restore 'civility' to chaotic streets. Council Member Rita Joseph, district 40, is mentioned in the debate. The bill drew a bipartisan supermajority of co-sponsors, but two have since withdrawn. Cycling groups, including Transportation Alternatives and Los Deliveristas Unidos, call the bill 'ineffective, dangerous, expensive, short-sighted, and bureaucratically complicated.' They warn it will discourage e-bike use, push people toward cars, and lead to racially targeted police enforcement. Supporters cite rising injuries and deaths involving e-vehicles. The debate exposes a rift: safety for whom, and at what cost?
