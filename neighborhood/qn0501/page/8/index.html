      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Maspeth?

      Maspeth Bleeds While City Hall Sleeps

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Maspeth: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Bodies on the Road

      In Maspeth, the numbers do not lie. Eleven people have died on these streets since 2022. Six more suffered injuries so grave they were marked as serious. 768 people have been hurt—bodies broken, lives changed. The dead do not speak. The wounded carry the story in scars and limps. NYC crash data

      The Pattern of Harm

      Pedestrians, cyclists, and riders of small vehicles pay the highest price. Trucks and SUVs strike hardest. In one crash, a 75-year-old woman was killed crossing with the signal, hit by a turning pickup truck. A cyclist crushed by a box truck. A moped rider thrown and left bleeding. The pattern repeats. The names change. The pain does not.

      Leadership: Progress and Silence

      City leaders have the power to act. Albany passed Sammy’s Law, giving New York City the right to lower speed limits. The city can set 20 mph limits on local streets. But in Maspeth, the pace of change is slow. No record of bold action from local officials. No public push for safer crossings or protected bike lanes. The silence is its own answer.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every day without action is a day closer to the next siren, the next family shattered. The tools exist: lower speed limits, redesign dangerous intersections, enforce the laws that save lives. But tools unused are as useless as promises unkept.

      Call your council member. Demand safer streets. Do not wait for another obituary. Take Action

      Int 0781-2022
      Holden votes no on vehicle stop reporting bill with neutral safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing NYPD to spell out why they stop cars. Each stop now gets logged with the reason, time, and outcome. No names. Just facts. The city posts these reports online. A move for sunlight. No more secrets.

      Int 0781-2022, now law, came through the Committee on Public Safety. The Council passed it on January 20, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the police department to report on the justification for the stop in vehicle encounter reports," demands the NYPD document every vehicle stop. Reports must include the reason for the stop, the level of offense, and demographic details, but no personal identifiers. Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Lincoln Restler (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, and others led the charge. The law aims to expose patterns in police stops and holds officers to account. Reports go online, open to all. This is a step toward transparency, not just for drivers, but for every New Yorker who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.

      Holden Supports Stricter Penalties for Ghost Plate Toll Dodgers
      ▸

      Councilmember Holden pushes tougher penalties for fake and obscured plates. NYPD tickets rise, but repeat offenders dodge tolls and cameras. Advocates warn: weak enforcement means more cheating, less safety, and crowded streets. The city cracks down, but loopholes remain.

      Bill action centers on license plate enforcement as congestion pricing approaches. On December 11, 2023, Councilmember Robert F. Holden (District 30) supported raising fines for drivers using fake or obscured plates. The matter, reported by Gothamist, highlights a surge in 'ghost cars'—vehicles with covered, defaced, or phony plates—dodging tolls and speed cameras. Holden stated, "If somebody can save a lot of money by obscuring their plates, they'll do it." The NYPD has increased enforcement, but many offenders remain undeterred. The city has banned camera-blocking covers and encourages public reporting, but unresolved complaints and repeat violations persist. Holden's push aims to make penalties outweigh the savings from toll evasion, seeking legislative action to protect the program's integrity.
