      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Bedford-Stuyvesant (West)?

      Paint Won’t Stop the Killing

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Bedford-Stuyvesant (West): Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      A man, 63, crossing with the light at Bedford and Flushing. Crushed by a turning truck. Dead before sunrise. A woman, 79, crossing with the signal at New York and Herkimer. Two SUVs. She never made it across. A 48-year-old man, struck by an SUV on Fulton. Gone. In three and a half years, six people killed on these streets. Seventeen left with serious injuries. More than 1,200 injured. The numbers are blunt. The pain is not.

      The Blame Game and the Bike Lane

      Children run from between parked cars. Cyclists swerve. Drivers park where they want. The city built a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue. It was meant to shield the vulnerable. But after a child darted from a double-parked car and collided with an e-bike, the city caved. Mayor Adams said he was “listening to community concerns” and ordered the lane ripped out. “We decided to adjust the current design to better reflect community feedback,” said Adams.

      Council Member Lincoln Restler called it what it is: “This is just pure politics. That is not how safety decisions on our streets should be made” Restler told CBS. The city will paint a line where there was once a barrier. The cars will keep parking. The children will keep running. The blood will keep flowing.

      Who Gets Protected?

      SUVs and trucks did most of the killing. The city blames the victims, not the drivers or the street. The protected lane is gone. The illegal parking stays. The mayor calls it safety. The council calls it politics. The dead stay dead.

      What Next? Demand More Than Paint

      Contact your council member. Demand real protection. Tell the mayor: paint is not a barrier. Tell the DOT: children need more than prayers. Tell your neighbors: silence is complicity. Take action now.

      Res 0008-2022
      Ossé co-sponsors fare-free holiday transit, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council pushed MTA to drop fares on subways and buses for major holidays. The move aimed to open transit, cut car trips, and ease city movement. The resolution gathered broad support but stalled, filed at session’s end. No action. No change.

      Resolution 0008-2022, filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, called on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority (MTA) to make subways and buses fare-free on major holidays. Introduced February 10, 2022, and filed December 31, 2023, the measure read: "Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority (MTA) to make subways and buses fare-free on major holidays." Council Member Darlene Mealy led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Menin, Louis, Yeger, Hanif, Joseph, Ossé, Nurse, Gutiérrez, Avilés, Cabán, Richardson Jordan, and Brooks-Powers. The bill cited global fare-free transit efforts and past local fare suspensions. Despite wide co-sponsorship, the resolution ended the session without passage or impact. No safety analyst reviewed its effect on vulnerable road users.

      Ossé Supports Safety Boosting Vision Zero and Bike Lanes
      ▸

      Five neighborhoods bleed under car wheels. East New York, East Flatbush, Bedford-Stuyvesant, Williamsburg, Jamaica. Council members speak. Some call for bike lanes, street redesigns, real safety. Others hesitate. The toll mounts. Streets remain hostile. Vision Zero falters.

      This council action, highlighted in a January 3, 2022 Streetsblog NYC article, spotlights deadly streets in East New York, East Flatbush, Bedford-Stuyvesant, Williamsburg, and Jamaica. The matter, 'Vision Zero Epicenters: 5 Dangerous Neighborhoods that Should Be on Eric Adams’s Agenda,' draws statements from council members. Charles Barron (East New York) showed initial reluctance but later acknowledged the need for safety and equity. Rita Joseph (East Flatbush) demanded urgent improvements, saying, 'We can and must do better as a city.' Chi Ossé (Bedford-Stuyvesant) called for expanded safe corridors. Lincoln Restler (Williamsburg) pushed for 'Vision Zero on steroids' and a network of protected bike lanes. Nantasha Williams (Jamaica) recognized the need for redesigns to curb speeding. The council’s stance: most support protected bike lanes, street redesigns, and Vision Zero policy, but action lags as deaths and injuries climb.
