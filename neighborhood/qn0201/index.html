      Blood on the Crosswalks, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      qn0201: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      No one walks these streets untouched. In the past three years, qn0201 has seen lives ended and bodies broken. A man, 58, crossing Jackson Avenue, struck dead at the intersection. A 43-year-old e-bike rider, crushed by a bus at Queens Boulevard. A 30-year-old, thrown from his moped, left lifeless on 44 Drive. The numbers are cold, but the loss is not. Every crash leaves a hole—a family waiting for a voice that will not come home.

      Broken Crossings, Broken Promises

      The danger is not random. Intersections like Jackson Avenue and Queens Boulevard are killing fields. Pedestrians and cyclists are hit where the city should protect them most. The data shows the pattern: failure to yield, unsafe speed, distraction. The city knows these truths. Still, the bodies pile up.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Council Member Julie Won has backed real change. She helped secure funding for the Queens Boulevard redesign, which brought a 68% drop in deaths and a 35% fall in injuries since 2015. The redesign added protected bike lanes and pedestrian islands. “Queens Boulevard is not the Boulevard of Death anymore, but the Boulevard of Life,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez in November 2024.

      Won also voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed the dead for their own deaths. She pushed for daylighting—banning parking near crosswalks to clear sightlines. She stood with advocates for the Blissville Greenway, a bike lane where cyclists had died before, as reported by Streetsblog NYC.

      But the work is not done. The crossings are still dangerous. The city moves slow. Too many bills sit in committee. Too many families wait for justice.

      The Call

      This is not fate. Every crash is a choice made by leaders who delay, who settle for half-measures, who let drivers rule the road. Call your council member. Demand more protected lanes, more daylighting, more action. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.
