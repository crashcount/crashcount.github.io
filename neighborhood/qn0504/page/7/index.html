      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Middle Village?

      Middle Village Bleeds While Leaders Hide

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Middle Village: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      Death at the Crossroads

      A man on a bicycle was crushed beneath the wheels of an FDNY truck turning onto Juniper Boulevard. He died on the street. The police said only, “The bicyclist was an adult male who was pronounced dead at the scene” (ABC7).

      In the last twelve months, four people have died in Middle Village traffic. Over 100 more were injured. One was a cyclist, two were pedestrians, one rode a motorcycle. The numbers do not bleed, but the families do.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Since 2022, six people have died on these streets. 377 have been injured. Two suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Cars and trucks did most of the harm. Motorcycles killed. Bikes did not kill anyone. The numbers are here, cold and unyielding.

      Leadership: Words and Silence

      The city talks of Vision Zero and safer streets. But in Middle Village, the carnage continues. After the firetruck killed the cyclist, a witness said, “One of them seemed concerned, like shaken, like shocked” (New York Post). The NYPD investigates. The leaders wait. No new laws. No redesigns. No public statements from the council or board. The silence is heavy.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day without change is a choice. Call your council member. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand slower speeds. Demand action before another name becomes a number.

      Do not wait for another family to grieve. Take action now.

      Holden Backs Misguided E-Bike Registration and Crackdowns
      ▸

      Council Member Holden’s bill demands license plates for e-bikes and scooters. Applause for deliveristas turns to boos. The alliance calls for crackdowns, fines, and reclassification. The crowd fears e-vehicles, but city data shows cars injure more.

      On September 28, 2023, Queens Council Member Bob Holden introduced Intro. 758 at an E-Vehicle Safety Alliance (E-VSA) meeting. The bill, now backed by a Council majority, would require all legal e-bikes and scooters to be registered and display license plates. The meeting opened with a standing ovation for Holden, but tensions rose when NYPD Deputy Inspector Erik Worobey praised deliveristas for safer riding. The crowd booed, voicing fear of fast e-vehicles. The E-VSA’s nine-point plan calls for reclassifying e-bikes as mopeds, ending a park e-bike pilot, and NYPD confiscation of e-vehicles on sidewalks, with a $500 retrieval fine. The group frames itself as victims of 'rogue' riders, though city statistics show drivers of larger vehicles cause far more injuries. The bill does not address mopeds or provide funding, and critics say it ignores the dangers faced by delivery workers, 26 of whom died between 2020 and 2022.

      Int 1173-2023
      Holden co-sponsors bill on emergency vehicle access, no clear safety impact.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to keep open streets open for fire trucks and ambulances. No more moving barriers by hand. DOT must warn walkers and riders when sirens come. The bill died at session’s end. Streets stay risky. Safety waits.

      Int 1173-2023 was introduced in the City Council on September 14, 2023, and referred to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill’s title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to ensuring emergency vehicle access to open streets.' Council Members Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor, District 44) and Robert F. Holden (co-sponsor, District 30) pushed for rules so emergency vehicles could always reach open streets—no more manual barrier moves. The Department of Transportation would also have to alert pedestrians and cyclists when vehicles enter. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. Vulnerable road users remain exposed while the city delays action.

No safety analyst note is available, but the bill’s intent is clear: rein in drivers who endanger others again and again.

      Int 1151-2023
      Holden co-sponsors solar crosswalk bill, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council pushed for 500 solar-lit crosswalks. The bill demanded action—100 new devices each year. It called for a hard look at results. But the session ended. The bill died. Streets wait. Pedestrians keep crossing in the dark.

      Int 1151-2023, introduced August 3, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to require the Department of Transportation to install at least 100 illuminated, solar-powered traffic control devices at crosswalks each year for five years—a total of 500. The bill also ordered a study comparing these devices to unlit signs, probing their power to deter traffic violations and mapping out logistical hurdles. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the installation of solar-powered crosswalks.' Council Member Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Menin, Brooks-Powers, Rivera, and over thirty others. The bill was filed at session’s end on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The city’s crosswalks remain unchanged. Vulnerable road users remain exposed.

      Int 1033-2023
      Holden sponsors bill that could slow street safety improvements and harm safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1033-2023 orders DOT to consult FDNY before approving open streets or bike lanes that cut car lanes or parking. Firehouses get advance notice. The bill aims to keep emergency routes clear but risks slowing safety projects for walkers and riders.

      Int 1033-2023, filed at session’s end in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced May 11, 2023, by Council Member Robert F. Holden with co-sponsors including Ariola, Riley, Yeger, Restler, Brooks-Powers, Hanks, Borelli, Carr, Kagan, Vernikov, and Paladino. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code... requiring the department of transportation to consult with the fire department prior to approving open street applications and certain bicycle lane projects and to notify affected firehouses...” It requires DOT to consult FDNY and notify firehouses before approving open streets, bike lanes, or major transportation projects that remove car or parking lanes. DOT must certify this consultation. The bill does not apply to emergency projects. While it aims to protect emergency access, advocates warn it could delay or block street redesigns that save lives for people walking and biking.
