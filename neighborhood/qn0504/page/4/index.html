      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Middle Village?

      Middle Village Bleeds While Leaders Hide

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Middle Village: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      Death at the Crossroads

      A man on a bicycle was crushed beneath the wheels of an FDNY truck turning onto Juniper Boulevard. He died on the street. The police said only, “The bicyclist was an adult male who was pronounced dead at the scene” (ABC7).

      In the last twelve months, four people have died in Middle Village traffic. Over 100 more were injured. One was a cyclist, two were pedestrians, one rode a motorcycle. The numbers do not bleed, but the families do.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Since 2022, six people have died on these streets. 377 have been injured. Two suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Cars and trucks did most of the harm. Motorcycles killed. Bikes did not kill anyone. The numbers are here, cold and unyielding.

      Leadership: Words and Silence

      The city talks of Vision Zero and safer streets. But in Middle Village, the carnage continues. After the firetruck killed the cyclist, a witness said, “One of them seemed concerned, like shaken, like shocked” (New York Post). The NYPD investigates. The leaders wait. No new laws. No redesigns. No public statements from the council or board. The silence is heavy.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day without change is a choice. Call your council member. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand slower speeds. Demand action before another name becomes a number.

      Do not wait for another family to grieve. Take action now.

      Hevesi Supports Safety Boosting Congestion Pricing and MTA Independence
      ▸

      Lawmakers say Hochul broke the law. They filed a brief. They argue the governor has no right to halt congestion pricing. The MTA Board, not Albany, holds the power. The pause sows confusion. Streets stay clogged. Vulnerable New Yorkers pay the price.

      On August 23, 2024, a group of New York State legislators—Assembly Members Phil Steck and Andrew Hevesi, State Senator Julia Salazar, and former Assembly Member Dick Gottfried—filed a legal brief against Governor Hochul’s decision to pause congestion pricing. The matter, as described: “the legislature never gave her or any governor the power to do away with the traffic toll.” The brief cites the 2019 Traffic Mobility Act, arguing only the Traffic Mobility Review Board and the MTA can make such decisions. The lawmakers warn that letting the governor override the MTA would “make impossibly opaque the actual responsibility for MTA decisions.” Their action supports the MTA Board’s independence and opposes executive interference. No safety analyst has assessed the direct impact on vulnerable road users, but the legislators’ stance highlights the risk of political meddling in life-and-death street policy.

The project targets a corridor marked by high injury and death rates, aiming to shield vulnerable road users from relentless traffic.

      Res 0467-2024
      Holden co-sponsors bill boosting penalties for blocked plates, improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council backs tougher fines for drivers who hide plates. Obscured tags let reckless motorists dodge cameras and tickets. The problem grows. Lawmakers want stiffer penalties to stop the evasion and protect people on city streets.

      Resolution 0467-2024 sits in the Committee on Public Safety. Introduced June 20, 2024, it urges Albany to pass S.2447/A.5234. The measure’s title: 'increase the penalties for purposefully obstructed license plates.' Council Members Justin L. Brannan (primary sponsor, District 47) and Robert F. The resolution calls for higher fines, confiscation of plate coverings, and registration suspensions. Lawmakers say this crackdown is needed to keep dangerous drivers from slipping through the cracks and to make streets safer for everyone.

      Res 0451-2024
      Holden co-sponsors resolution to increase penalties for improper vehicle registration.
      ▸

      Council Member Holden pushes Albany to hit drivers who dodge New York registration. Out-of-state plates show up in deadly crashes. The bill seeks stiffer penalties. The aim: stop the shell game, make streets safer for those on foot and bike.

      Resolution 0451-2024 sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced June 6, 2024, by Council Member Robert F. Holden, the measure urges the State Legislature and Governor to 'introduce and pass... legislation increasing penalties on drivers who improperly register vehicles in another state.' The resolution cites data: 11,000 injuries and 62 deaths in 2021 crashes involving out-of-state cars. Out-of-state vehicles made up 19% of city crashes that year. Holden’s action targets drivers who skirt taxes, fees, and insurance, shifting costs and risks to New Yorkers. The bill is a call to close loopholes and hold reckless drivers accountable. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Int 0921-2024
      Holden co-sponsors bill speeding utility pole removal, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Old poles rot. Wires sag. This bill sets a clock. Sixty days to clear abandoned poles and wires. Immediate action for hazards. Thirty days to shift gear to new poles. Streets get less clutter. Danger drops for those on foot and bike.

      Int 0921-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced May 23, 2024, it amends the city code to force utility owners to remove abandoned poles and wires within 60 days, and to act immediately if danger looms. Appurtenances must move to new poles within 30 days. The bill’s title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the timelines for the removal of abandoned or unsafe utility poles, wires, and appurtenances, and the transfer of appurtenances to newly erected poles.' Council Members Brannan (primary), Holden, Vernikov, Marmorato, and Paladino sponsor. The measure targets street hazards that trip, block, or injure pedestrians and cyclists. It aims to clear the path and cut risk for all vulnerable road users.

      Int 0880-2024
      Holden co-sponsors bill that would slow or block street safety improvements.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0880-2024 would force DOT to notify and present plans for any bus or bike lane changes to local boards. Boards get sixty days to respond. Sponsors want quarterly updates online. The bill sits in committee. No safety review yet.

      Int 0880-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on May 16, 2024, by Council Members Joann Ariola (District 32, primary sponsor) and Robert F. Holden (District 30, co-sponsor). The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to community notification of proposed major transportation projects,' redefines any bus lane, busway, or bike lane construction or removal as a major project, regardless of length. It requires DOT to offer presentations to affected community boards and gives those boards sixty days to comment. DOT must also post quarterly progress reports online. The bill was referred to committee and has not yet been voted on. No safety analyst has assessed its impact on vulnerable road users.
