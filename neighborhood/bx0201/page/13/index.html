      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Hunts Point?

      Hunts Point Bleeds: Trucks Kill, Leaders Stall, Families Mourn

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised August 24, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Hunts Point: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      Blood on the Streets: The Toll in Hunts Point

      Two dead. Three left with injuries that will never fully heal. In the last twelve months, Hunts Point saw 466 crashes. Two people died. Three were seriously hurt. These are not just numbers. They are bodies in the street, families waiting at hospital doors.

      Just weeks ago, a 61-year-old man was crushed by a box truck on Oak Point Avenue. He died where he stood. A year before, a 29-year-old cyclist was killed by a dump truck turning right on Tiffany Street. No helmet, no right of way, no chance. The street did not forgive him.

      Patterns of Harm: Who Pays the Price

      Cars and trucks do most of the killing. In this period, SUVs and sedans caused the most pedestrian injuries and deaths. Trucks followed close behind. Not a single death from a bicycle. The violence is not random. It is heavy, fast, and always moving forward.

      The pain is not spread evenly. Children, the elderly, the young—all are hit. In the last year, 11 children were hurt. One person aged 18–24 died. Another in their late fifties. The old and the young, both broken by speed and steel.

      Leadership: Action and Silence

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Assembly Member Amanda Septimo co-sponsored a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed-limiting tech in their cars (A2299). State Senator Jose Serrano voted yes on a similar bill in the Senate (S4045). Council Member Rafael Salamanca Jr. has called for more accountability from DOT and supported crackdowns on fake plates (supported crackdowns). But the streets remain the same. The trucks keep rolling. The bodies keep falling.

      A witness once described the aftermath: “I saw one lady was out on the ground. They was giving her medical attention, checking her body. She was laid out.” The words are plain. The horror is not.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day without change is another day someone does not come home. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real redesigns. Demand that repeat speeders lose their keys. Do not wait for another body in the road.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Hunts Point

      S 1078
      Septimo votes yes, boosting driver education and improving street safety.
      ▸

      Senate and Assembly passed S 1078. New drivers must now learn how to avoid hitting people on foot or bike. Law aims to cut crashes at the root—before drivers get the keys.

      Bill S 1078, introduced in the Senate on April 27, 2021, requires pedestrian and bicyclist safety instruction in the drivers pre-licensing course. The bill moved through committee and passed the Senate and Assembly, with key votes on May 20, 2021, February 1, 2022, May 16, 2022, and May 23, 2022. The matter summary reads: 'Requires instruction in pedestrian and bicyclist safety as part of the drivers pre-licensing course.' Primary sponsor: Senator Gounardes, joined by Bailey, Biaggi, Brisport, Cleare, Comrie, and others. The measure targets driver ignorance, a root cause of deadly crashes, by putting vulnerable road users at the center of driver education.

      S 5130
      Serrano votes yes, boosting street safety and access for everyone.
      ▸

      Senate passed S 5130. The bill pushes complete street design. It aims for safe access for all. Pedestrians and cyclists get a shot at safer roads. The vote was split, but the bill moved forward.

      Senate bill S 5130, titled 'Enables safe access to public roads for all users by utilizing complete street design principles,' passed committee votes on March 2 and May 16, 2022. Senator Kennedy sponsored the bill. Support came from members like Jeremy Cooney, Anna Kaplan, and John Liu. Dissent came from Patrick Gallivan and Susan Serino. The bill pushes cities to design roads for everyone—pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers. It marks a shift from car-first planning. The measure aims to cut risk for people outside cars. Full vote details and text are at the New York Senate website.
