      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Hunts Point?

      Hunts Point Bleeds While City Hall Sleeps

      Written June 29, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Hunts Point: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      Blood on the Sidewalks: Recent Crashes in Hunts Point

      The streets of Hunts Point do not forgive. In the last twelve months, two people have died and 292 have been injured in 430 crashes (NYC Open Data). Most were on foot or in cars. Some were children. Some were old. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie.

      Just days ago, a driver ran a red light at Hunts Point Avenue and Bruckner Boulevard. His car jumped the curb. Three men on the sidewalk were hit. One is still fighting for his life. The driver ran but did not get far. Police charged him with vehicular assault and reckless endangerment. “Jenkins’ vehicle struck three pedestrians on the sidewalk, then hit a Chevy Equinox,” police said. The street was left marked by blood and broken glass.

      This is not rare. In April, a box truck killed a 61-year-old man on Oak Point Avenue. Last summer, a 29-year-old cyclist was crushed by a turning dump truck on Barry Street. The dead do not speak. Their absence is the only proof left.

      Who Pays, Who Acts

      Most injuries to pedestrians here come from cars, SUVs, and trucks. In three years, these vehicles killed two and hurt over 80 more. Not one death came from a bike or moped (NYC Open Data).

      Local leaders have taken some steps. Council Member Rafael Salamanca, Jr. co-sponsored bills to fix NYCHA sidewalks and tow unregistered vehicles—measures that could help. He also voted to legalize jaywalking, erasing a law used to blame the dead instead of the driver. But too often, action is slow. Bills to tow unregistered cars and target fake plates have stalled. The streets wait. People keep dying.

      The Cost of Delay

      Every crash is preventable. Every delay is a choice. “The unlicensed 28-year-old driver is facing multiple counts of vehicular assault following the 9:45 p.m. crash,” police said. The law caught up, but not before the car did.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand safer streets. Ask for lower speed limits, protected crossings, and real enforcement. Do not let another name become a number. The dead cannot wait. Neither should you.

      Int 0363-2022
      Salamanca co-sponsors bill for NYPD response time reporting, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0363-2022 would force NYPD to reveal how fast cops show up after minor crashes. Reports must break down times by precinct and flag if pedestrians are hit. The bill died in committee. Data stays hidden. Streets stay risky.

      Int 0363-2022, introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety, aimed to require the NYPD to report monthly on response times to minor or non-critical traffic crashes. The bill’s summary reads: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring reports on police department response times to minor traffic crashes.” Council Members Rita C. Joseph (primary sponsor), Rafael Salamanca, Jr., and Diana I. Ayala backed the measure. The bill demanded data on whether pedestrians were involved and if injuries occurred, with details by precinct, borough, and zip code. It also called for annual NYPD recommendations to cut response times. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and never became law. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Int 0203-2022
      Salamanca co-sponsors bill requiring luxury limos show safety inspection proof.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force luxury limo companies to show safety inspection proof at booking. No loopholes. No excuses. Passengers would see if a car passed inspection before stepping in. The bill died at session’s end. Danger remains.

      Bill Int 0203-2022 was introduced on April 14, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill would have amended city law to require luxury limousine services to provide proof of vehicle safety inspections at the time of contract. The matter summary reads: 'This bill would require for-hire luxury limousine companies to provide proof of safety inspections for its licensed vehicles at the time a person contracts with the company for the transportation of passengers.' Council Members Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor, District 44) and Rafael Salamanca, Jr. (co-sponsor, District 17) backed the measure. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided. The bill’s failure leaves passengers and other road users exposed to unchecked vehicles.
