      bk0201: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Toll: Broken Bodies, Silent Streets

      No one was killed in bk0201 in the past year. But the numbers do not comfort. The streets still bleed. In the last three years, people have been crushed, trapped, and left with broken limbs. A 76-year-old woman died in the back seat of an SUV on Furman Street. The driver was speeding. She was trapped, incoherent, and then gone. The man behind the wheel survived, but not unscathed. The street did not forgive either of them. NYC Open Data

      A 27-year-old woman crossing with the signal at Tillary and Cadman Plaza West was hit by a turning sedan. She walked with the light. The car turned anyway. Her knee and leg were crushed. She lived. The pain stays.

      A 23-year-old man, not at an intersection, was struck by a sedan on Court Street. He was emerging from behind a parked car. The city calls it an abrasion. The city does not count the fear.

      Leadership: Promises, Bills, and the Waiting

      Local leaders have written bills. Council Member Restler has sponsored laws to speed up protected bike lanes, ban parking near crosswalks, and mandate speed limiters for repeat offenders. The bills sit in committee. The SAFE Streets Act waits for a vote. The city promises daylight at crossings, but cars still block the view. The law says to wait. The street does not wait.

      State Senator Gounardes has pushed for speed cameras and speed limiters. The Stop Super Speeders Act would force the worst drivers to slow down. It has not passed. The cameras watch, but the dead do not rise.

      The Human Cost: Names Lost, Grief That Stays

      “She was a phenomenon, a force to be reckoned with. She was determined. She was smart. She was funny, and she had everything to look forward to. I’m gonna tell you it’s been awful, but there isn’t a word to describe how awful it’s been. She’s been part of my DNA, and it’s like losing part of me. I lost part of me.” said Kim Harris after her daughter was killed by a drunk driver in Brooklyn. The loss is not just a number. It is a hole in the world.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      The disaster is slow, but it is not silent. The laws are written. The votes are not cast. The streets are not safe. Call your council member. Demand a vote. Demand daylight at every crossing. Demand speed limiters for repeat offenders. Demand protected bike lanes that do not wait for another body.

      Do not wait for the next name.

      Gounardes Supports Safety Boosting Speed Limiter Mandate
      ▸

      State Sen. Gounardes and Assembly Member Gallagher push a bill to force repeat speeders to install limiters. The tech would cap speed, targeting drivers with six or more tickets. Advocates say it could cut deaths. Reckless drivers face real consequences.

      On August 2, 2023, State Senator Andrew Gounardes and Assembly Member Emily Gallagher announced new legislation targeting recidivist reckless drivers. The bill, introduced in the New York State Legislature, would require drivers with at least six speeding tickets in a single year, or 11 points in 18 months, to install speed limiter devices in their vehicles. The matter summary states: "We are going to literally force you to slow down by requiring you to install a speed limiter on your car." Gounardes led the announcement at the Atlantic Avenue intersection where a driver killed Katherine Harris. Gallagher, co-sponsor, said, "Cars and trucks can act as weapons when used recklessly." The bill aims to bypass bureaucratic hurdles and act automatically, modeled after ignition interlock devices for drunk drivers. Advocates and city officials joined the call, citing data that speed limiters can reduce traffic deaths by 37 percent. The bill targets the most dangerous drivers, seeking to end impunity for repeat offenders.

      On August 1, 2023, lawmakers introduced a bill requiring speed-limiting devices for repeat reckless drivers. The bill, sponsored by State Sen. Andrew Gounardes and Assemblymember Emily Gallagher, targets drivers with six or more automated speed or red light tickets, or eleven license points in 18 months. The measure would force these drivers to install devices that cap speed at five miles per hour above the limit. This new bill seeks to close those gaps and directly slow down the most dangerous drivers on city streets.

      ▸

      Brooklyn officials demand equal tolls on all Manhattan crossings. They warn free bridges funnel traffic into certain neighborhoods. Their letter calls for fairness. The MTA stays silent. The Traffic Mobility Review Board will decide. Streets hang in the balance.

      On July 18, 2023, Council Member Shahana Hanif and other Brooklyn officials called for 'equalized tolling on all crossings into Manhattan' as congestion pricing nears. Their letter to the Traffic Mobility Review Board states: 'No one community should disproportionately bear the burden of the cars and trucks passing through their neighborhood.'       Int 1115-2023
      Restler co-sponsors stricter alternate side parking enforcement, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1115-2023 targets repeat alternate side parking violators. First offense: $65. More violations: $100 each. Three strikes in a year, and the car gets towed. Filed at session’s end. Bottcher and Restler sponsor. No direct safety impact noted.

      Int 1115-2023 was introduced on June 22, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill sought to amend city code by setting penalties for alternate side parking violations at $65 for a first offense and $100 for each subsequent offense within a 12-month period. After three violations in a year, the vehicle would be towed if found in violation again. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to establishing penalties for alternate side parking violations and requiring towing of certain vehicles in violation of alternate side parking rules.' Council Members Erik D. Bottcher (primary sponsor) and Lincoln Restler (co-sponsor) led the effort. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst assessment was provided, and no direct impact on vulnerable road users was noted.
