      Stop the Bleeding: Streets Won’t Wait for Lawmakers to Act

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      bk0201: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Toll: Broken Bodies, Silent Streets

      No one was killed in bk0201 in the past year. But the numbers do not comfort. The streets still bleed. In the last three years, people have been crushed, trapped, and left with broken limbs. A 76-year-old woman died in the back seat of an SUV on Furman Street. The driver was speeding. She was trapped, incoherent, and then gone. The man behind the wheel survived, but not unscathed. The street did not forgive either of them. NYC Open Data

      A 27-year-old woman crossing with the signal at Tillary and Cadman Plaza West was hit by a turning sedan. She walked with the light. The car turned anyway. Her knee and leg were crushed. She lived. The pain stays.

      A 23-year-old man, not at an intersection, was struck by a sedan on Court Street. He was emerging from behind a parked car. The city calls it an abrasion. The city does not count the fear.

      Leadership: Promises, Bills, and the Waiting

      Local leaders have written bills. Council Member Restler has sponsored laws to speed up protected bike lanes, ban parking near crosswalks, and mandate speed limiters for repeat offenders. The bills sit in committee. The SAFE Streets Act waits for a vote. The city promises daylight at crossings, but cars still block the view. The law says to wait. The street does not wait.

      State Senator Gounardes has pushed for speed cameras and speed limiters. The Stop Super Speeders Act would force the worst drivers to slow down. It has not passed. The cameras watch, but the dead do not rise.

      The Human Cost: Names Lost, Grief That Stays

      “She was a phenomenon, a force to be reckoned with. She was determined. She was smart. She was funny, and she had everything to look forward to. I’m gonna tell you it’s been awful, but there isn’t a word to describe how awful it’s been. She’s been part of my DNA, and it’s like losing part of me. I lost part of me.” said Kim Harris after her daughter was killed by a drunk driver in Brooklyn. The loss is not just a number. It is a hole in the world.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      The disaster is slow, but it is not silent. The laws are written. The votes are not cast. The streets are not safe. Call your council member. Demand a vote. Demand daylight at every crossing. Demand speed limiters for repeat offenders. Demand protected bike lanes that do not wait for another body.

      Do not wait for the next name.

      Int 0080-2024
      Restler co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by deterring hazardous vehicle obstruction.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants within half a mile of schools. Fines set at $175. Civilians can report violations. DOT must act on complaints. Council aims to clear paths for people, not cars.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after its introduction on February 8, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations," creates a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of a school. The penalty is $175 per violation. The Department of Transportation must launch a civilian reporting program. If DOT prosecutes a case using civilian evidence, the complainant gets 25% of the proceeds. Council Member Carlina Rivera leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Joseph, Menin, Nurse, Hudson, Brannan, Farías, Brewer, Salaam, Hanif, Avilés, Won, Bottcher, Krishnan, Gutiérrez, Marte, and the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill aims to keep streets clear for vulnerable road users, especially near schools.

The bill aims to halt the cycle of road expansion, rising traffic, and mounting deaths.
