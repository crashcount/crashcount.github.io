      Brooklyn Heights: Streets Run Red While Lawmakers Stall

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      Brooklyn Heights: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      Blood on the Pavement

      Two dead. Three seriously hurt. In Brooklyn Heights, the numbers do not tell the whole story, but they do not lie. Since 2022, traffic violence has left 2 people dead and 3 with life-changing injuries. In the last year alone, 75 people were hurt in 207 crashes. No one under 18 died, but the young are not spared. One child was injured. The streets do not forgive.

      Cars and SUVs do most of the damage. They broke bones and ended lives. Trucks and vans followed close behind. A sedan hit a 23-year-old man crossing near Court Street. He walked away with an abrasion. He was lucky. Others were not. On Furman Street, a Cadillac SUV slammed into a car. A 76-year-old woman died. The driver was trapped, incoherent, and survived. The city moved on. The street stayed the same.

      The Cost of Delay

      Speed kills. In February 2025, a drunk driver tore through Atlantic Avenue at 72 mph. He ran a red light and killed Katherine Harris, 31, steps from her home. Her mother said, “I lost part of me.” The driver got three to nine years. The street got nothing.

      Leaders: Action and Silence

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has pushed for change. He co-sponsored bills to speed up protected bike lanes, ban parking near crosswalks, and mandate speed limiters for repeat offenders. State Senator Andrew Gounardes led the charge to expand speed cameras and crack down on drivers who hide their plates. Assembly Member Jo Anne Simon backed bills to fine bike lane blockers and force speed-limiting tech on repeat speeders. But most bills are still waiting. The SAFE Streets Act sits in committee. The dead keep waiting too.

      What Next? The Work Is Not Done

      Every delay is another risk. The city could lower speed limits to 20 mph. It could pass the Stop Super Speeders Act. It could daylight every crosswalk. It could build real protection for people on foot and bike. But it has not. Not yet.

      Call your council member. Call your senator. Demand action. The next victim is only a green light away. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn Heights sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, community board Brooklyn CB2, city council district District 33, assembly district AD 52 and state senate district SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn Heights?
      Cars and SUVs were responsible for the vast majority of pedestrian injuries and deaths, with 32 incidents. Trucks and vans caused 8 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 1 injury. Bikes were involved in 1 incident. The danger comes mostly from large vehicles.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. They follow patterns—speed, failure to yield, distraction. Every serious crash in Brooklyn Heights could have been prevented by safer street design, lower speeds, and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, daylight crosswalks, build protected bike lanes, and require speed limiters for repeat offenders. They can fund real changes, not just studies.
      ▸ What has Council Member Lincoln Restler done about traffic violence?
      Restler has co-sponsored bills to speed up protected bike lanes, ban parking near crosswalks, and require speed limiters for repeat offenders. But many of these bills are still waiting for a vote.

      Int 1215-2023
      Restler sponsors bill increasing sidewalk riding penalties, likely reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1215-2023 orders new signs. Bicycles, e-bikes, and scooters get clear warnings: stay off sidewalks, park paths, and boardwalks. Fines and penalties spelled out. Filed at session’s end. No change yet for street safety.

      Int 1215-2023 was introduced in the City Council on October 5, 2023, and referred to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the posting of signs notifying operators of bicycles, bicycles with electric assist, and electric scooters of the prohibition against operating such devices on sidewalks, park walkways, and boardwalks, and of related fines and penalties," would require the Department of Transportation and the Department of Parks and Recreation to post signs warning riders of these restrictions and the consequences. Council Member Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Hudson, Hanif, Brewer, Ung, Abreu, Brooks-Powers, Gutiérrez, and Schulman. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No direct safety analysis was provided. The measure aims for clarity, not infrastructure change.

      Res 0792-2023
      Restler co-sponsors bill for scramble crosswalks, boosting student pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council calls for scramble crosswalks at school zones. The move aims to cut deadly conflict between cars and kids. Filed at session’s end, the resolution targets peak arrival and dismissal. Sponsors push Albany to act. Streets remain risky.

      Resolution 0792-2023 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on September 28, 2023, and closed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. The resolution urges the New York State Legislature and Governor to pass A.5001-A/S.2515-B, which would require scramble crosswalks at intersections near schools during student arrival and dismissal. The matter title reads: 'Resolution calling on the New York State Legislature to pass, and the Governor to sign, A.5001-A/S.2515-B, in relation to establishing scramble crosswalks leading to and from school buildings during times of student arrival and dismissal.' Council Members Shahana K. Hanif (primary), Selvena N. Brooks-Powers, Lincoln Restler, and Kevin C. Riley sponsored the measure. The bill responds to persistent crashes near schools and the loss of hundreds of crossing guards. Scramble crosswalks, also called Barnes Dances, stop all traffic so pedestrians cross in every direction. The council’s action highlights the ongoing danger children face on city streets.

      Int 0906-2023
      Restler votes yes to move trucks off residential streets, boosting safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to force off-street parking for tractor trailers. No more big rigs clogging residential blocks. By 2025, each borough must have a site. The city must act or explain delays. Streets may breathe. Danger shifts from curbs.

      Int 0906-2023, now Local Law 142 of 2023, was enacted by the City Council on October 29, 2023, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill’s title: 'A Local Law in relation to parking for tractor trailers and other commercial trucks.' It requires the city to identify at least one feasible off-street parking location for tractor trailers in each borough by the end of 2023, and to establish those sites by December 31, 2025. No location can be on a residential street. Council Member Kamillah Hanks sponsored the bill, joined by Brannan, Riley, Dinowitz, Ung, Brooks-Powers, and others. The law aims to clear residential streets of oversized trucks, shifting their presence to designated lots. The council’s action targets the chronic hazard of trucks parked where people walk, bike, and live.

      Gounardes Opposes DOT Ignoring Temporary Bike Lane Law
      ▸

      DOT stripped protected bike lanes from Brooklyn’s Fourth Avenue. Cyclists now dodge cars and illegal parking. Elected officials and advocates demand action. DOT cites traffic, but danger grows. Pedestrians lose safe crossings. The agency stays silent. Streets stay deadly.

The matter, described as 'DOT continues to ignore dangers it created on Brooklyn’s Fourth Ave,' centers on DOT’s decision to replace bike lanes with a second car lane, violating a city law that requires temporary bike lanes during such work. Cyclists and pedestrians remain at risk.
