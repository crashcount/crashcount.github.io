      Brooklyn Heights Bleeds: Demand 20 MPH Before More Lives Are Lost

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      Brooklyn Heights: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Brooklyn Heights

      A man steps into the street. A car keeps going. The numbers pile up. In the last twelve months, 206 crashes. Seventy-five injured. One left with life-changing wounds. No deaths this year, but two in the last three. The lucky ones limp away. The rest do not.

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the worst of it. A 27-year-old woman, crossing with the signal at Tillary and Cadman Plaza West, crushed by a turning sedan. A 23-year-old man, hit by a car on Court Street, left with torn skin and a memory that will not heal. A 26-year-old cyclist, arm split open by an SUV on Atlantic Avenue. The street does not care who you are. It only counts the bodies.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They say every life matters. They point to new laws. Sammy’s Law passed. The city can now lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit stands. The clock ticks. The cars do not slow.

      Speed cameras work, but they are always at risk. Albany holds the power to keep them running. Each year, the law faces expiration. Each year, advocates beg for renewal. The city builds more bike lanes, redesigns intersections, but the pace is slow. The street does not wait.

      The Human Cost

      The numbers do not bleed. People do. “She was a phenomenon, a force to be reckoned with… I’m gonna tell you it’s been awful, but there isn’t a word to describe how awful it’s been. She’s been part of my DNA, and it’s like losing part of me. I lost part of me,” said Kim Harris after her daughter was killed by a speeding drunk driver.

      No law brings her back. No camera erases the blood. The only thing that changes is what we do next.

      Call to Action: Demand More, Now

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name to become a number. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where does Brooklyn Heights sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, community board Brooklyn CB2, city council district District 33, assembly district AD 52 and state senate district SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in Brooklyn Heights?
      Cars and Trucks were responsible for the most injuries and all deaths. Bikes caused one moderate injury. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused no reported injuries or deaths in this period. Source: NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speed, failure to yield, distraction. These are preventable. They are not random acts of fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits to 20 mph, keep speed cameras running, redesign dangerous intersections, and fund more protected bike lanes. They can act now, not later.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How many people were seriously injured or killed in Brooklyn Heights in the last year?
      In the last twelve months, there were 75 injuries and 1 serious injury. No deaths were reported in this period, but two deaths occurred in the last three years. Source: NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can residents do right now?
      Call your council member, the mayor, and the DOT. Demand a 20 mph speed limit, permanent speed cameras, and safer street design. Join advocacy groups and show up at community board meetings.
