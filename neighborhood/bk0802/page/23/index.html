      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Crown Heights (North)?

      Another Child Dead, Another Excuse—How Many More?

      Written June 29, 2025

      Crown Heights (North): Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy tried to cross Eastern Parkway with his sister. A black SUV struck him. He died in the street. The driver stayed. No arrest. The boy’s name was Mordica Keller. His sister stood by. A neighbor described, “They were walking, he was crossing the street with his sister. She’s 17-years-old.” The street is wide. The cars are fast. The city moves on.

      The Numbers Behind the Names

      In the last twelve months, 282 people have been injured and one killed in Crown Heights (North). Three suffered serious injuries. Children are not spared—fifteen injured, none killed, but the numbers change with every week. SUVs and cars do most of the harm. Trucks, too. In three years, eight people have died. Over 1,200 have been hurt. The numbers do not grieve. They only grow.

      Local Leaders: Bills and Broken Promises

      Council Member Chi Ossé and Crystal Hudson have co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and speed up protected bike lanes. The bills sit in committee. No law. No change. Ossé and Hudson called for a redesign of Atlantic Avenue. The avenue waits. State Senator Zellnor Myrie rode a bike with a reporter and said, “We should be making this as easy as possible and as safe as possible for as many people as possible.” The street did not change.

      The Cost of Delay

      Every day without action is another day of risk. The city studies. The council debates. The children walk to school. The cars do not slow. A neighbor warned, “Yeah, this street is dangerous.” The city knows. The city waits.

      Act Now: Demand Change

      Call your council member. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand protected crossings. Demand that the bills move, not just the bodies. The dead do not call. The living must.

      Cars and SUVs: 1 death, 201 injuries. Trucks and buses: 1 death, 13 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 0 deaths, 7 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 6 injuries. Most harm comes from cars, SUVs, and trucks.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The same streets see the same harm, year after year. These deaths and injuries are preventable with better design, enforcement, and political will.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and fund laws to ban parking near crosswalks, build protected bike lanes, redesign dangerous streets, and demand daylight at every intersection. They can move bills out of committee and into law.
      ▸ What has been done lately to improve street safety here?
      Council Members Ossé and Hudson have co-sponsored bills for safer crossings and bike lanes, but most are stalled in committee. Atlantic Avenue redesign has been called for, but not delivered.
      ▸ How many people have been killed or injured in Crown Heights (North) recently?

      Int 0291-2022
      Hudson votes yes, boosting citywide safety with new greenway plan.
      ▸

      The Council passed Int 0291-2022, forcing city agencies to map, plan, and report on greenways. The law demands annual updates and public engagement. It aims to carve out safe, car-free corridors for walkers and cyclists. The mayor returned it unsigned.

      Int 0291-2022, now Local Law 115 of 2022, was enacted by the City Council on November 27, 2022. The bill came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, first introduced April 28, 2022. The law's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to a citywide greenway plan.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers sponsored the bill, joined by dozens of co-sponsors including Rivera, Hudson, and others. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor on October 27, 2022. The law orders the Department of Transportation and Parks to identify, map, and report on greenways, and to consult with community boards. Proposals for new greenway segments or repairs must be presented to affected communities within 60 days. The mayor returned the bill unsigned, but it became law. The measure compels the city to plan and maintain safe routes for non-drivers, with regular public reporting and accountability.

      Crystal Hudson Opposes Misguided Scaling Back of Safety Plan
      ▸

      DOT shrank plans for Apolline’s Garden. The city dropped slow-street changes and neckdowns. Parking won out over safety. A plaza remains, but the boldest protections are gone. Council Member Hudson was left out. The street stays dangerous for now.

      On October 12, 2022, the Department of Transportation (DOT) announced it would scale back its Apolline’s Garden project, a response to a fatal crash that killed a 3-month-old girl. The original plan would have closed part of Gates Avenue to cars and added traffic-calming features. Instead, DOT scrapped the one-way conversion and neckdowns, citing 'community feedback' and resource limits. DOT spokesman Vin Barone said, 'the project honoring Baby Apolline still creates new public space and safer streets,' but for now, only the Gates Plaza will move ahead. Council Member Crystal Hudson said she was not informed in advance and focused on advocating for the garden. The rollback means fewer protections for pedestrians and leaves the street’s dangers largely unaddressed.

      Int 0758-2022
      Ossé co-sponsors e-bike licensing bill, likely reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council tried to force plates on e-bikes and scooters. The bill died in committee. Riders faced new rules, more scrutiny. The city’s cars kept killing. The council moved on. Vulnerable road users stayed exposed. The system failed to shift blame from drivers.

      Int 0758-2022 was introduced in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on October 12, 2022. The bill aimed to require every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter, and other legal motorized vehicle to be registered with the Department of Transportation and display a visible license plate. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered.' Council Member Inna Vernikov sponsored the bill, joined by Robert F. Holden and 29 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. No safety analyst assessed the impact on vulnerable road users. The bill’s failure left the city’s most exposed—cyclists, pedestrians, passengers—without new protections. The streets stayed dangerous. The cars kept their edge.

      Int 0721-2022
      Hudson co-sponsors truck route GPS study, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to study truck route compliance and GPS mapping. The measure called for tracking trucks off-route, web-based maps, and new street designs. Avilés led, with Gutiérrez and others co-sponsoring. The session ended before action. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0721-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on September 29, 2022. The bill required the Department of Transportation to study truck drivers’ compliance with city truck routes and to assess integrating truck maps with GPS technology. The matter’s title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to a truck route GPS study.' Alexa Avilés sponsored the bill, joined by Gutiérrez, Hanif, Restler, Brooks-Powers, Hudson, Krishnan, and the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill called for measures like converting two-way streets to one-way, posting truck route signs, and outreach to the trucking industry. The study’s results were to be posted online and sent to the council by January 1, 2023. The bill was filed at session’s end, with no further action. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Res 0308-2022
      Hudson co-sponsors federal helicopter noise limits, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council members push Congress to curb helicopter tours. The resolution demands limits on low, loud flights over New York. Helicopter noise and risk plague city streets. The act would set stricter rules, aiming to shield millions from harm and stress.

      Res 0308-2022, filed by the Committee on State and Federal Legislation, urges Congress and the president to pass H.R. 389, the Safe and Quiet Skies Act of 2021. Introduced on September 14, 2022, and filed at session's end on December 31, 2023, the resolution states: 'calls on the United States Congress to pass, and the president to sign, H.R. 389.' Council Members Shahana K. Hanif (primary sponsor), Gale A. Brewer, Crystal Hudson, and Rita C. Joseph co-sponsored the measure. The act would ban commercial helicopter tours below 1,500 feet, cap noise at 55 decibels, and let local governments set even tougher rules. The council’s action responds to a surge in helicopter noise complaints and the dangers posed by low-flying tours over dense city neighborhoods.
