      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Crown Heights (North)?

      Eight Dead, Countless Broken—Still No Action on Crown Heights’ Killing Streets

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Crown Heights (North): Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A four-year-old boy. A 39-year-old man crossing Atlantic Avenue. A cyclist, age 37, thrown from his bike. In Crown Heights (North), the names fade but the numbers do not. Eight people have died on these streets since 2022. More than 1,200 have been hurt. Twelve suffered injuries so grave they may never walk the same again. NYC Open Data

      Children are not spared. In the last year, a child died. Fifteen others were injured. The youngest bleed and break alongside the old. No one is safe here.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and trucks do most of the harm. They killed two. They left 53 with injuries that do not heal. Motorcycles and mopeds struck two more. Not a single cyclist killed anyone, but one cyclist died. The street is a gauntlet. The odds are not in your favor.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      The city talks of Vision Zero. They count deaths and promise less. They passed Sammy’s Law. They say they will lower the speed limit to 20 mph. But the limit stands. The clock runs. Promises do not slow a car.

      Speed cameras work. They cut speeding by more than half where installed. But the law that keeps them running is always at risk. Albany debates. The city waits. The dead do not wait.

      The Path Forward

      This is not fate. Every crash is a choice made by a city that could do more. Lower the speed limit. Build real protection for people on foot and bike. Keep the cameras on. Do not let another child die while leaders talk.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. Take action

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      A 3180
      Cunningham co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 3180 demands every state- or federally-funded road project weigh complete street design. It pushes agencies to publish clear guidance. The bill aims to force planners to see people, not just cars. Streets must serve all. No more blind spots.

      Assembly Bill A 3180 was introduced on February 3, 2023, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in committee, awaiting further action. Its matter title reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding.' Didi Barrett leads as primary sponsor, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Amy Paulin, Carrie Woerner, and many others as co-sponsors. The bill compels agencies to consider complete street design—meaning streets built for pedestrians, cyclists, and transit users, not just drivers. It also requires the department to publish guidance and reference materials for public use. This measure, if enforced, could reshape how New York builds and rebuilds its roads, putting vulnerable road users at the center of every project.

      Myrie Opposes NYPD Safety Undermining Excessive Force Ruling
      ▸

      NYPD cleared Officer Michael Kovalik after he threatened Senator Zellnor Myrie with pepper spray at a 2020 protest. Despite video and a civilian board’s finding of abuse, an NYPD judge found no misconduct. Myrie calls the system rotten. No officers face charges.

      On January 31, 2023, the NYPD declined to charge Officer Michael Kovalik for threatening Brooklyn State Senator Zellnor Myrie with pepper spray during a 2020 George Floyd protest. The case, reviewed internally by Assistant Trial Commissioner Josh Kleiman, ended with Kovalik cleared of wrongdoing. The Civilian Complaint Review Board had found Kovalik abused his authority, but Kleiman ruled, 'The evidence fails to support either of the specifications with which Respondent is charged.' NYPD Commissioner Keechant Sewell signed off on the decision. Senator Myrie, who testified about being shoved and threatened, condemned the ruling: 'This ruling is EXACTLY why New Yorkers have zero faith in the system.' Myrie and then-Assembly Member Diana Richardson, both detained at the protest, have filed a federal lawsuit. The NYPD’s internal process shielded officers from accountability, leaving vulnerable protesters exposed to unchecked force.

      Int 0879-2023
      Hudson co-sponsors bill requiring bollards, boosting pedestrian safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0879-2023 would force DOT to install bollards at rebuilt sidewalks, curb extensions, and ramps. The law targets spots where cars strike. It demands a study, annual reports, and clear rules. Filed at session’s end. No action yet.

      Int 0879-2023 was introduced on January 19, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the installation of bollards at reconstructed sidewalks, curb extensions and pedestrian ramps,” was sponsored by Council Members Brooks-Powers, Krishnan (primary), Stevens, Hanif, Hudson, Richardson Jordan, Riley, and the Manhattan Borough President. The bill requires the Department of Transportation to install bollards at reconstructed sidewalks, curb extensions, and pedestrian ramps, especially where accessibility upgrades are made. It mandates a study on bollard effectiveness in high pedestrian areas and demands annual reporting on installations and requests. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, with no enactment. The measure aims to harden pedestrian space, forcing the city to act where cars threaten walkers and wheelchair users.

Lawmakers are under pressure to act.

      Int 0872-2022
      Hudson sponsors bill to curb illegal curb cuts, boosting pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council moved to target illegal curb cuts. DOT would mark rogue driveways, order fixes, and bill owners for repairs. Community boards would get curb cut permit alerts. The bill stalled. Sidewalks stay exposed. Cars keep cutting through.

      Int 0872-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It was introduced on December 21, 2022, and filed at session’s end on December 31, 2023. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to illegal curb cuts and requiring local community board notification of curb cut applications.” Council Members Crystal Hudson (primary), Joseph C. Borelli, Justin L. Brannan, Robert F. Holden, and Kalman Yeger sponsored. The bill required DOT to investigate illegal curb cut complaints within 30 days, mark illegal cuts with green paint, and force owners to restore or legalize them. If owners failed, DOT would repair and recover costs. Community boards would get notice of new curb cut permits and sixty days to comment. The bill did not pass. No safety analyst reviewed its impact for vulnerable road users.
