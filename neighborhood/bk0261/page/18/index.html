      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Brooklyn Navy Yard?

      Blood on Kent Avenue: City Inaction Costs Lives

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Brooklyn Navy Yard: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      One person dead. Two seriously hurt. In three and a half years, the Brooklyn Navy Yard has seen 102 crashes. Fifty-four people have been injured. One did not go home. Two were left with wounds that do not heal fast. These are not numbers. They are lives, broken on the road. NYC crash data

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the worst of it. A 59-year-old woman was killed crossing Kent Avenue. The SUV kept going. A cyclist, 24, was thrown from his bike after a car cut him off. Another cyclist, 39, hit by a sedan, left bleeding in the street. A 53-year-old woman, walking, hit by a car at the crosswalk. The pattern is plain. The most vulnerable pay the price.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They say every life matters. They say they are redesigning intersections and lowering speed limits. But in the Navy Yard, the pace is slow. The crashes keep coming. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not used it here. Speed cameras work, but only where they are turned on. Laws lapse. Programs expire. The street stays the same.

      What Comes Next

      No one should die crossing the street. The city can act. The council can demand lower speed limits. The mayor can order more cameras and redesigns. Albany can keep the cameras running. But nothing changes unless people demand it.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand safer speeds. Demand real protection for people who walk and bike.

      Take action now.

      Council Member Lincoln Restler calls for swift federal approval of congestion pricing. He says the plan will cut traffic, clean the air, and fund transit. Restler warns delay will worsen danger on the BQE and keep car culture entrenched.

      On September 28, 2022, Council Member Lincoln Restler (District 33) published an op-ed titled, "Congestion pricing must be approved so it can help our community." The piece urges the Federal Highway Administration to approve the MTA's Central Business District Tolling Program. Restler writes, "Congestion pricing will reduce through-traffic in our neighborhood, improve air quality and quality of life for residents, and allow for substantial investments in a public transportation system that all New Yorkers rely on." He highlights the risk of further delay for the Brooklyn Queens Expressway, a corridor plagued by crashes and pollution. Restler supports reducing car dependence and warns that restoring more lanes to the BQE would fuel more danger and cost.       Restler Supports Safety Boosting Interim Fixes McGuinness Boulevard
      ▸

      DOT will daylight intersections, ban left turns, and add pedestrian space on McGuinness Boulevard. Eleven pedestrians and four cyclists have died here since 1995. Advocates demand a full redesign. The city promises more action after these quick fixes.

      On August 31, 2022, the Department of Transportation announced interim safety improvements for McGuinness Boulevard in Greenpoint. The street has seen 1,594 crashes, 44 cyclist injuries, 67 pedestrian injuries, and three deaths since 2013. Assembly Member Emily Gallagher announced the changes, which include daylighting intersections, banning left turns at key avenues, and adding painted pedestrian extensions. Gallagher said, 'Quick, iterative incremental changes will be a great way to reduce injuries and fatalities rather than waiting for an entire overhaul.' Council Member Lincoln Restler praised the upgrades as 'a step in the right direction.' Bronwyn Breitner of Make McGuinness Safe Coalition called for a full-scale redesign, reallocating space from cars to people. The city will revisit the corridor plan this winter after installing these measures in the fall.

      ▸

      On July 7, 2022, the NYC Department of Transportation presented three redesign options for McGuinness Boulevard, a notorious crash corridor in Brooklyn. The proposals, discussed at Community Board 1’s Transportation Committee, included: keeping all travel lanes with added bike lanes (Plan A), removing a lane for protected bike lanes and pedestrian islands (Plan B), and a two-way bike lane with a buffer (Plan C). DOT has not made a final decision and promised more community engagement.
