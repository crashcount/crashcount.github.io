      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Douglaston-Little Neck?

      No More Bodies for the Spreadsheet: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Douglaston-Little Neck: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Numbers Hide the Hurt

      One death. Three serious injuries. Over 320 hurt. These are not numbers. They are lives changed in Douglaston-Little Neck since 2022. The pain does not make the news. It sits in hospital rooms and quiet kitchens. In the last twelve months, 101 people were injured in 195 crashes. No one died this year. But the luck will not hold.

      The Most Vulnerable Still Bleed

      Pedestrians and children are not spared. In the last year, eight kids were hurt in crashes here. A 68-year-old man crossing at Westmoreland Street was struck by an SUV. He left the scene with a bleeding head. Data shows SUVs, sedans, and trucks all played their part. The street does not care who you are. It only cares that you are in the way.

      Leadership: Progress or Delay?

      The city talks about Vision Zero. They count intersection redesigns and new speed limits. But in Douglaston-Little Neck, the danger remains. No new local laws. No bold moves. The council and community board have not led. They have not fought for more cameras, lower speeds, or safer crossings. The silence is loud. Each day without action is another day of risk.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Crashes are not weather. They are the result of choices. Choices made by leaders, or not made at all. If you want change, you must demand it. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Call the DOT. Tell them to lower the speed limit, add cameras, and fix the streets. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Act now. Demand safer streets before another family pays the price.

      Int 0291-2022
      Lee votes yes, boosting citywide safety with new greenway plan.
      ▸

      The Council passed Int 0291-2022, forcing city agencies to map, plan, and report on greenways. The law demands annual updates and public engagement. It aims to carve out safe, car-free corridors for walkers and cyclists. The mayor returned it unsigned.

      Int 0291-2022, now Local Law 115 of 2022, was enacted by the City Council on November 27, 2022. The bill came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, first introduced April 28, 2022. The law's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to a citywide greenway plan.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers sponsored the bill, joined by dozens of co-sponsors including Rivera, Hudson, and others. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor on October 27, 2022. The law orders the Department of Transportation and Parks to identify, map, and report on greenways, and to consult with community boards. Proposals for new greenway segments or repairs must be presented to affected communities within 60 days. The mayor returned the bill unsigned, but it became law. The measure compels the city to plan and maintain safe routes for non-drivers, with regular public reporting and accountability.

      Int 0758-2022
      Lee co-sponsors e-bike licensing bill, likely reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council tried to force plates on e-bikes and scooters. The bill died in committee. Riders faced new rules, more scrutiny. The city’s cars kept killing. The council moved on. Vulnerable road users stayed exposed. The system failed to shift blame from drivers.

      Int 0758-2022 was introduced in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on October 12, 2022. The bill aimed to require every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter, and other legal motorized vehicle to be registered with the Department of Transportation and display a visible license plate. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered.' Council Member Inna Vernikov sponsored the bill, joined by Robert F. Holden and 29 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. No safety analyst assessed the impact on vulnerable road users. The bill’s failure left the city’s most exposed—cyclists, pedestrians, passengers—without new protections. The streets stayed dangerous. The cars kept their edge.

      Res 0235-2022
      Lee co-sponsors MTA lead cleanup resolution, boosting street safety and public health.
      ▸

      Paint chips rain down from old elevated tracks. Lead coats the streets. The council calls on the MTA to clean up. Riders, walkers, and children breathe poison. The city waits. The bill is filed. The danger lingers.

      Res 0235-2022 was introduced on June 16, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution calls on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to remediate any transportation structure, primarily elevated train lines, with extremely high levels of lead. Council Member Sandy Nurse led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Cabán, Hanif, Abreu, Louis, Holden, Gutiérrez, Joseph, Won, Brewer, and Lee. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. The resolution cites studies showing lead levels in paint chips from elevated tracks far above legal limits. The council demands action to protect New Yorkers from toxic exposure. The MTA, a state-run agency, holds the power to act. The bill’s filing leaves the hazard unresolved.

      Linda Lee Warns Against Harmful Speed Camera Measures
      ▸

      Council passed Albany’s speed camera expansion. Cameras now run all day, every day. Seven members opposed. Supporters cited lives lost when cameras slept. Opponents called it a tax. The vote followed a deadly year. Danger stalks city streets, especially near schools.

      On May 26, 2022, the New York City Council voted 43-7 to approve Albany’s extension and expansion of the city’s speed camera program. The measure, handled by the Transportation Committee, allows speed cameras to operate 24/7. The matter summary: 'The City Council approved Albany's extension and expansion of New York's speed camera program, allowing cameras to operate 24/7, but not before several council members voiced opposition.' Chairwoman Selvena Brooks-Powers urged action, saying, 'We must use all the tools at our disposal.' Council Members Joan Ariola, Vickie Paladino, and David Carr opposed, calling cameras a financial burden. Eric Dinowitz defended the program: 'If you don't want a speeding ticket, don't speed.' The vote came after a year of high road deaths, with special risk near schools.

      Int 0312-2022
      Lee co-sponsors bill raising fines for excessive vehicle noise.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to hike penalties for loud cars and trucks. The measure targeted blaring horns, illegal claxons, and roaring engines. It died at session’s end. No direct safety gains for people walking or biking. Noise remains. Streets stay loud.

      Int 0312-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety. The bill aimed to amend the city’s administrative code to increase penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles, including those under and over 10,000 pounds, and for unauthorized horns and claxons. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to increasing certain penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles.' Council Member Sandra Ung sponsored the bill, joined by 26 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. There is no evidence the bill would have improved safety for vulnerable road users. The city’s noise problem, and its threat to livable streets, remains unresolved.

      Lee co-sponsors bill shifting sidewalk repair to city, boosting pedestrian safety.

      Int 0053-2022
      Paladino co-sponsors bill restricting overnight commercial parking, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban commercial trucks from sleeping on quiet streets. The bill died. Utility and oil trucks kept their curb. Pedestrians and families still dodge hulking vehicles at dawn. The city blinked. The danger stayed.

      Int 0053-2022, introduced on February 24, 2022, sought to amend city law to bar commercial vehicles from parking overnight on residential streets, between 9 p.m. and 5 a.m. The Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure handled the bill. The measure removed exemptions for gas, oil, and utility trucks. The matter summary reads: 'This bill would prohibit the parking of commercial vehicles owned or operated by gas or oil heat suppliers, gas or oil heat systems maintenance companies or any public utility in residential streets overnight.' Council Members Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor), Robert F. Holden, Vickie Paladino, and Kevin C. Riley backed the bill. The council filed it at session’s end, on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst weighed in, but the bill’s failure means residential streets remain lined with large trucks, leaving vulnerable road users exposed.
