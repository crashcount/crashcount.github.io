      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Co-Op City?

      Co-Op City Bleeds While City Hall Sleeps

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Co-Op City: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Toll in Co-Op City: Flesh and Bone, Not Numbers

      A man steps into the street. A truck turns left. The man does not come home. In three and a half years, one person died and 242 were injured in traffic crashes in Co-Op City. Seven were left with serious injuries. The dead do not speak. The wounded carry scars the city cannot count.

      Pedestrians are struck by trucks, taxis, SUVs. A 76-year-old woman was killed by a dump truck at an intersection. A 59-year-old woman was left bleeding in the road after a taxi hit her. Teenagers, faces broken, after a police pursuit ended in a crash. No one is spared.

      Road Rage, Gunfire, and the Price of Inaction

      On a June night, a minor crash. Words. Then gunfire. Keino Campbell, 27, a Navy veteran, shot dead in his car. His mother, Suzette Thomas, said, “I want people to remember my son as a humble person, one to do the best for the world.” His father pleaded, “Stop jumping out of car and shooting people, guys. Use your insurance. That’s what it’s for.”

      This is not just a story of cars and speed. It is a story of lives ended for nothing. Of families left with empty chairs and words that cannot be unsaid.

      Leadership: Promises and the Waiting

      The city talks of Vision Zero. Of lower speed limits. Of cameras that catch the reckless. But in Co-Op City, the grind continues. No new laws or redesigns have come to these corners. The numbers do not lie: 477 crashes, 1 death, 242 injuries. The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has the power to harden crosswalks and protect the old and the young. It has not used it here.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings. Demand action, not words.

      Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home. The city will not save you unless you force its hand. Take action now.

      Int 0479-2022
      Riley co-sponsors bill boosting pedestrian safety near schools and hospitals.
      ▸

      Council pushed a bill to force early walk signals at crossings near hospitals, schools, libraries, and senior centers. Four hundred intersections per year. The bill died in committee. No law. No change. Streets stay dangerous for the city’s most vulnerable.

      Int 0479-2022 was introduced on June 2, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to require leading pedestrian interval signals at intersections next to hospitals, libraries, schools, and senior centers. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring leading pedestrian interval signals at intersections adjacent to hospitals, libraries, schools, and senior centers.' Council Member Lynn C. Schulman sponsored the bill, joined by Kagan, Hudson, Hanif, Abreu, Restler, Riley, and Sanchez. The bill would have forced the city to install these signals at no fewer than 400 intersections each year. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No law passed. The city’s most vulnerable—kids, elders, patients—remain at risk at crossings meant to protect them.

      Int 0251-2022
      Riley co-sponsors bill that could slow rollout of street safety projects.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0251-2022 expands what counts as a major transportation project. Bike lanes, busways, and bus lanes now trigger public notice. The Department of Transportation must alert community boards, offer presentations, and post updates online. The bill is filed, not law.

      Int 0251-2022, introduced April 28, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, sought to amend city law on community notification for major transportation projects. The bill, sponsored by Kalman Yeger (primary), with Inna Vernikov, Joseph C. Borelli, Kevin C. Riley, Shaun Abreu, and Robert F. Holden as co-sponsors, was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. DOT would be required to notify and present plans to community boards, consider feedback, and post quarterly progress reports online. The bill aimed to increase transparency and community input on street changes that affect vulnerable road users, but did not become law.

      Int 0261-2022
      Riley co-sponsors curb extension bill, boosting pedestrian safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0261-2022 would force the city to build curb extensions at deadly crossings. Five intersections per borough, every year. The aim: block cars from parking near crosswalks, boost pedestrian sightlines, and cut crashes where people walk.

      Int 0261-2022 was introduced on April 28, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring curb extensions at certain dangerous intersections,' was sponsored by Council Members Hanif (primary), Menin, Hudson, Stevens, Ayala, Abreu, Krishnan, Nurse, and Riley. The bill required the Department of Transportation to identify intersections with high pedestrian crash rates and install curb extensions—prohibiting parking within 15 feet of crosswalks—at a minimum of five such intersections in each borough per year. The measure was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. Curb extensions are designed to increase pedestrian visibility and reduce deadly conflicts at crossings, directly targeting systemic dangers faced by people on foot.
