      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Westerleigh-Castleton Corners?

      No More Blood on Staten Island’s Streets

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Westerleigh-Castleton Corners: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      Death and Injury on Familiar Streets

      A man steps into the crosswalk. An SUV turns left. The man does not make it home. In the last three and a half years, four people have died in traffic crashes in Westerleigh-Castleton Corners. Seven more suffered serious injuries. The numbers do not flinch: 637 injured, 1089 crashes since 2022. Each number is a body, a family, a life changed or ended. NYC Open Data

      Children are not spared. In the last year, one child suffered a serious injury. Another was hit crossing Clove Road. The old are not spared. A 65-year-old man was killed at an intersection. The young, the old, the careful, the distracted—it does not matter. The street takes them all.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs are the main weapon. Of the deaths and serious injuries to pedestrians, most came from sedans and SUVs. Trucks and buses added more. Bikes caused injuries, but not deaths. The pattern is clear. The bigger the vehicle, the greater the harm.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city talks about Vision Zero. The state passed Sammy’s Law, letting New York City lower speed limits. But in Westerleigh-Castleton Corners, the pace of change is slow. No local leader has made this neighborhood a model for safety. The deaths keep coming. The silence is loud.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. Lower the speed limit. Build real protection for people walking and biking. Install more speed cameras and keep them on. Do not wait for the next siren.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand action. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home. Take action

      Int 0448-2024
      Hanks sponsors bill creating crossing guard advisory board, no direct safety impact.
      ▸

      Council moves to form an advisory board on school crossing guard deployment. NYPD, DOT, and DOE must join. The board will send reports twice a year to city leaders. The aim: track, review, and recommend guard placement. Streets near schools stay dangerous.

      Bill Int 0448-2024 sits in the Committee on Public Safety. Introduced February 28, 2024, it calls for an advisory board on school crossing guard deployment. The board would include the NYPD, Department of Transportation, and Department of Education. According to the bill summary: 'Such advisory board would be responsible for submitting biannual reports, relating to recommended deployment of school crossing guards, to the Mayor, the City Council Speaker and the Police Commissioner.' Council Member Kamillah Hanks leads as primary sponsor, joined by Stevens, Schulman, Salaam, Won, Cabán, Riley, Farías, Restler, Williams, Narcisse, Banks, Louis, Brooks-Powers, Marmorato, and the Bronx Borough President. The bill demands city agencies work together, but it does not guarantee more guards or safer crossings. The danger for children at city intersections remains.

      Int 0179-2024
      Hanks sponsors bill expanding tow pound capacity, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council wants NYPD tow pounds big enough to tow more cars. The bill demands public reports on towing and pound use. Lawmakers say this deters illegal driving. The committee stalled the bill. Streets stay risky for walkers and riders.

      Int 0179-2024 sits with the Committee on Public Safety. It was introduced on February 28, 2024, and laid over in committee on April 28, 2025. The bill reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to police department tow pound capacity.' Council Members Kamillah Hanks (primary sponsor), Oswald Feliz, Robert F. Holden, Farah N. Louis, Crystal Hudson, Lincoln Restler, Mercedes Narcisse, and Amanda Farías co-sponsored. The bill would force the NYPD to run tow pounds with enough space to tow cars at a rate that deters illegal conduct. It also requires public reports on tow pound capacity and towing in response to traffic violations. The committee has not advanced the bill. No safety analyst has assessed the impact on vulnerable road users.

      Hanks sponsors bill to increase transparency on crossing guard deployment.

      Int 0099-2024
      Carr co-sponsors bill increasing overnight truck parking, reducing pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council bill would carve out curbside overnight truck parking in city industrial zones. Only commercial vehicles allowed, 8 p.m. to 6 a.m. DOT must consult locals and report yearly. Bill sits in committee. Streets stay risky for those on foot or bike.

      Int 0099-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, would require the Department of Transportation to create curbside overnight parking for commercial vehicles in every Industrial Business Zone. The bill, introduced February 8, 2024, mandates that only commercial vehicles may park in these zones between 8:00 p.m. and 6:00 a.m. The matter title states: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the creation of curbside overnight truck parking sections in Industrial Business Zones.' Council Members Althea V. Stevens (primary sponsor), Nantasha M. Williams, Lincoln Restler, Farah N. Louis, Kevin C. Riley, and others back the measure. DOT must consult community boards and report annually on effectiveness. No safety analysis for vulnerable road users is available. The bill remains in committee, with no vote scheduled.

      Carr Opposes Congestion Pricing Blocking Safety Improvements
      ▸

      Eighteen lawmakers, including Joseph Borelli, sued to stop New York’s $15 congestion pricing. They claim the toll shifts pollution, burdens drivers, and fails communities with poor transit. The MTA defends the plan, saying it funds safer, less crowded streets.

      On February 4, 2024, Council Member Joseph C. Borelli (District 51) joined seventeen other lawmakers in a federal lawsuit to block New York City’s $15 congestion pricing toll for Midtown Manhattan. The suit, supported by both Democrats and Republicans, argues the toll 'is a detriment to those that will be affected by this toll, environmentally and financially,' and claims it will shift traffic and pollution to other neighborhoods. Other plaintiffs include State Senators James Skoufis, Jessica Scarcella-Spanton, Iwen Chu, Monica Martinez, and Assemblymembers Aileen Gunther, Jamie Williams, and David Weprin. The MTA, backed by Governor Hochul, says the toll will raise $1 billion yearly for transit upgrades, promising safer, less congested streets and better transit for the majority who rely on public transportation. The case highlights the political and environmental battle over how to fund and shape New York’s streets.

      Int 1011-2023
      Hanks votes yes on fare evasion info bill, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing police to hand out flyers on reduced fare programs to anyone ticketed or arrested for subway fare evasion. The flyers list eligibility, discounts, and how to apply. The law aims to inform, not punish. It is now in effect.

      Int 1011-2023, now enacted as Local Law 30 of 2024, moved through the Committee on Public Safety and passed the City Council on January 20, 2024. The bill requires police officers patrolling subway stations to carry and distribute flyers about city-run reduced fare programs to anyone arrested or summoned for fare evasion.       Int 0781-2022
      Hanks votes yes on vehicle stop reporting bill, safety impact neutral.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing NYPD to spell out why they stop cars. Each stop now gets logged with the reason, time, and outcome. No names. Just facts. The city posts these reports online. A move for sunlight. No more secrets.

      Int 0781-2022, now law, came through the Committee on Public Safety. The Council passed it on January 20, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the police department to report on the justification for the stop in vehicle encounter reports," demands the NYPD document every vehicle stop. Reports must include the reason for the stop, the level of offense, and demographic details, but no personal identifiers. Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Lincoln Restler (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, and others led the charge. The law aims to expose patterns in police stops and holds officers to account. Reports go online, open to all. This is a step toward transparency, not just for drivers, but for every New Yorker who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.

      Int 0417-2022
      Carr votes no on notice requirements bill, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to streamline how the city notifies communities about big street changes. Bike lanes now follow the same rules as other major projects. The city can act faster after feedback. The old bike lane notice law is gone.

      Int 0417-2022, now enacted, amends the city code to create a single, uniform process for notifying council members and community boards about major transportation projects—including bike lanes. The bill, handled by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on May 19, 2022, and passed on January 6, 2024. The official title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to notice requirements for certain transportation projects and the repeal of section 19-187 in relation thereto.' Council Member Shekar Krishnan led as primary sponsor, joined by dozens of colleagues. The law repeals the old, separate bike lane notice process and folds it into the broader project notice rules. Now, after a set notice period and community feedback, the Department of Transportation can move ahead without delay. This change aims to cut red tape and speed up street redesigns that affect vulnerable road users.
