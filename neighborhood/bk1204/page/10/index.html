      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Mapleton-Midwood (West)?

      No More Dead Kids: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written June 27, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Mapleton-Midwood (West): Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A boy rides a moped on East 8th Street. He is seventeen. A car turns left. The moped slams into the car. The boy is thrown. He dies at the hospital. His passenger, fourteen, survives with broken bones. There are no arrests. The street is quiet again, but the blood is not washed away. Jhoan later died at the hospital.

      A few days before, another moped rider is killed. The driver who hit him is drunk and unlicensed. The victim’s brother remembers his brother: “He never stopped working.” The driver walks free, awaiting trial. The street forgets. The family does not.

      In the last twelve months, three people have died here. One was a child. Five more suffered injuries so severe they may never heal.

      The Numbers Are Not Just Numbers

      Since 2022, there have been 908 crashes in Mapleton-Midwood (West). Four dead. Twelve with serious injuries.

      Most of the pain comes from cars and trucks. SUVs and sedans killed one, injured over 100. Trucks left three with serious wounds. Mopeds and bikes are not spared, but they do not kill. They are the ones who bleed.

      Leaders Talk. Streets Stay Deadly.

      The council member, Simcha Felder, has a record. He votes against speed cameras. He votes against safer truck routes. He votes no on greenways, on daylighting, on bills that would make crossing less deadly. He is silent after deaths. He does not show up at funerals.

      Assembly Member Simcha Eichenstein votes against renewing speed cameras. He stands with those who call them a burden.

      There are bills to slow cars, to protect people. Some are filed, some die in committee. The streets do not wait.

      What Now? The Blood Is Still Fresh

      Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Tell them you are tired of funerals. Tell them you want speed cameras, daylighting, raised crosswalks, and real enforcement.

      Do not let them hide behind process. Do not let them blame the dead.

      The next name could be yours. Act now.

      Felder Supports Safety Boosting 24 Hour Speed Cameras
      ▸

      State Senate passed bill to run speed cameras all day, every day. The vote was 51 to 12. Cameras now catch speeders at night and on weekends. Most deaths happen off-hours. Expansion aims to cut deadly crashes citywide.

      On May 31, 2022, the State Senate approved a bill to extend and expand New York City's speed camera program. The measure passed 51-12 and now moves to the Assembly. The bill allows cameras to operate 24/7 in nearly 2,000 locations across 750 school zones until July 1, 2025. The matter summary states, 'The proposal to extend New York City's speed camera program for another three years and expand it to run every day and around the clock easily passed.' Senator Andrew Gounardes sponsored the bill, saying, 'We are going to see a marked reduction in traffic violence on our streets at a time when traffic deaths are at their highest in a decade.' Simcha Felder, Democrat from District 44, voted against. The expansion targets off-hours, which account for 59% of traffic deaths. Speeding dropped 72% and deaths fell 55% where cameras operated. The city pushed for this as fatalities rose under Mayor Adams. Some penalties for repeat speeders were removed during negotiations.

      Felder Opposes Protected Bike Lanes and Safety Measures
      ▸

      A sanitation truck killed a cyclist in Borough Park. The driver fled, then got summonses but no arrest. MTA workers kept parking illegally at the scene. Local politicians block safer streets. The road still lacks protection. Cyclists remain exposed. Danger lingers.

The crash site remained clogged with illegally parked cars, many marked with MTA vests and NYPD placards. These blockages forced buses to detour and left cyclists at risk. The MTA stated employees are not exempt from parking rules. The street remains unsafe for cyclists and pedestrians.

      Int 0172-2022, filed by the NYC Council and handled by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to amend city law on open streets. The bill was filed at the end of session, leaving open streets policy unchanged.
