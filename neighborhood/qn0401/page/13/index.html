      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Elmhurst?

      Eight Dead in Elmhurst—How Many More Before City Hall Acts?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Elmhurst: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Elmhurst, the numbers do not lie. Eight people killed. Over one thousand injured. These are not just numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. In the last twelve months alone, one person died and 297 were hurt in 566 crashes. One was a child. One was someone’s parent. The street does not care who you are.

      Pedestrians take the worst of it. Trucks, SUVs, bikes, mopeds—each has left bodies broken or dead. A 43-year-old woman, crossing with the light, was killed by a turning dump truck on 80th Street. A 75-year-old man died after a bike hit him at Broadway and Roosevelt. A 78-year-old woman was struck by a moped in a crosswalk. A man was crushed by an SUV on Broadway. The list goes on. See the data.

      Promises and Delays

      Local leaders talk about Vision Zero. They say one death is too many. But the deaths keep coming. The city has new powers under Sammy’s Law to lower speed limits to 20 mph. The law is on the books. The streets are not yet safer. Speed cameras work, but their future is always in doubt. Each delay is another risk, another family left to mourn.

      Who Pays the Price?

      The most vulnerable pay first. In Elmhurst, the old and the young are hit hardest. Cars and trucks killed and maimed. Bikes and mopeds, too. The street is a gauntlet. The city counts the bodies. The politicians count the votes.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. Call your council member. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand more cameras. Demand streets that put people first.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Take action now.

      Int 0781-2022
      Holden votes no on vehicle stop reporting bill with neutral safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing NYPD to spell out why they stop cars. Each stop now gets logged with the reason, time, and outcome. No names. Just facts. The city posts these reports online. A move for sunlight. No more secrets.

      Int 0781-2022, now law, came through the Committee on Public Safety. The Council passed it on January 20, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the police department to report on the justification for the stop in vehicle encounter reports," demands the NYPD document every vehicle stop. Reports must include the reason for the stop, the level of offense, and demographic details, but no personal identifiers. Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Lincoln Restler (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, and others led the charge. The law aims to expose patterns in police stops and holds officers to account. Reports go online, open to all. This is a step toward transparency, not just for drivers, but for every New Yorker who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.

      Robert F Holden Opposes Misguided E-Bike Licensing Bill
      ▸

      Council Member Holden’s push for e-bike licensing draws fire. Critics say it targets delivery workers, mostly immigrants and people of color. The bill would hand police more power to stop, fine, and harass riders. The city’s most vulnerable would pay the price.

      On December 20, 2023, Council Member Robert F. Holden (District 30) published an op-ed opposing his own bill to require registration and licensing for e-bikes and e-scooters. The bill, introduced in the New York City Council, would impact the city’s 65,000 app-based delivery workers. The op-ed warns: 'What might sound benign at first would actually lead to a substantial increase in state surveillance and regulation of New York City's 65,000 app-based delivery workers.' Holden’s action—introducing the bill—has drawn sharp criticism for enabling more NYPD stops and fines, especially against Black, Latine, and immigrant riders. The piece details how similar enforcement has led to racial profiling and financial hardship for those least able to bear it. No safety analyst assessment was provided, but the op-ed makes clear: more policing means more danger for the city’s most vulnerable road users.

      Holden Supports Stricter Penalties for Ghost Plate Toll Dodgers
      ▸

      Councilmember Holden pushes tougher penalties for fake and obscured plates. NYPD tickets rise, but repeat offenders dodge tolls and cameras. Advocates warn: weak enforcement means more cheating, less safety, and crowded streets. The city cracks down, but loopholes remain.

      Bill action centers on license plate enforcement as congestion pricing approaches. On December 11, 2023, Councilmember Robert F. Holden (District 30) supported raising fines for drivers using fake or obscured plates. The matter, reported by Gothamist, highlights a surge in 'ghost cars'—vehicles with covered, defaced, or phony plates—dodging tolls and speed cameras. Holden stated, "If somebody can save a lot of money by obscuring their plates, they'll do it." The NYPD has increased enforcement, but many offenders remain undeterred. Advocates and experts, including Elizabeth Adams of Transportation Alternatives, warn that without strict enforcement, congestion pricing goals and street safety will suffer. The city has banned camera-blocking covers and encourages public reporting, but unresolved complaints and repeat violations persist. Holden's push aims to make penalties outweigh the savings from toll evasion, seeking legislative action to protect the program's integrity.

      Int 1259-2023
      Holden co-sponsors helmet mandate bill that undermines overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council Member Holden pushed a bill to force all cyclists to wear helmets. The measure threatened $50 fines for bare heads. The council filed it at session’s end. No new protections for riders. The city’s danger stays the same.

      Int 1259-2023, introduced by Council Member Robert F. Holden, aimed to require every bicyclist in New York City to wear protective headgear. The bill was referred to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on December 6, 2023, and filed without passage at the end of the session on December 31, 2023. The measure stated: 'No person shall ride upon or operate a bicycle without wearing protective headgear of good fit fastened securely upon the head with the headgear straps.' Violators faced civil penalties up to $50. Holden sponsored the bill. The law would have exempted those already covered by other helmet laws. The council took no further action. No safety analyst reviewed the bill’s impact on vulnerable road users.
