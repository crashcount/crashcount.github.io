      Four Dead, Two Children Gone—Gravesend’s Streets Are Killing Fields

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Gravesend (East)-Homecrest: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Four people killed. Six seriously injured. In the last twelve months, 400 crashes tore through Gravesend (East)-Homecrest. Nearly 300 neighbors hurt. Two children dead before their lives began. These are not just numbers. They are empty chairs at dinner, shoes left by the door, a mother’s hand forever reaching for a child who won’t come home.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      On Ocean Parkway, a mother and her two daughters were killed in a crosswalk. The driver had a suspended license and over 93 traffic violations. She was still behind the wheel. NYPD Commissioner Jessica Tisch described the crash: “Miriam Yarimi, 32, a mother of one, was driving her Audi on a suspended license… when she got into a collision… and careened into and killed a mother and two children who were leaving Shabbat services.” The car had just sped through a school zone weeks before. The city let it happen.

      Elsewhere, a 24-year-old drove drunk through Coney Island Avenue at 2 a.m., slamming into a Volvo, then an NYPD cruiser. Two officers hospitalized. A passenger thrown from the car, left in critical condition. Police reported that “one police officer suffered a broken pelvis and arm, as well as head trauma, while another suffered a broken hip.” The driver refused a blood test. He was charged with DWI and reckless endangerment.

      Leadership: Action or Excuses?

      Local leaders have the power. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit still stands at 25. Dangerous Vehicle Abatement lapsed. Cameras that catch speeders face expiration. The city talks about Vision Zero, but the dead keep piling up.

      Mayor Adams called for a full investigation after the Ocean Parkway killings. He commented on the tragedy, emphasizing the importance of investigating the incident fully. But investigations don’t bring back the dead. Lower speeds do. Cameras do. Real enforcement does.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Demand the city take the keys from repeat offenders. Don’t wait for another child’s name in the news. Take action now.

      Vernikov Urges Traffic Studies and Signals to Boost Safety
      ▸

      Injuries from crashes surge across New York. Pedestrians hit hardest. Council members and advocates sound alarms. Brooklyn South and the Bronx suffer most. Calls mount for street redesign, tougher enforcement, and urgent city action. Lower-income neighborhoods bear the brunt.

      On March 10, 2022, council members and advocates responded to a spike in road violence, as reported by Streetsblog NYC. NYPD statistics show injury-causing collisions up 15.4 percent citywide, with pedestrian injuries up 47.2 percent. The matter is described as a 'street safety crisis.' Council Members Charles Barron, Rita Joseph, and Inna Vernikov called for urgent action, including street redesign, more traffic studies, and stronger enforcement. Elizabeth Adams of Transportation Alternatives demanded passage of the Crash Victim Rights and Safety Act and expansion of automated enforcement. The Department of Transportation confirmed the rise in injuries and highlighted new safety initiatives. The impact falls hardest on lower-income and communities of color. The council’s response underscores the urgent need for systemic change to protect vulnerable road users.

      Int 0075-2022
      Yeger co-sponsors moving billboard ban, boosting street safety for all.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban moving billboards. The bill would have stopped cars from carrying giant ads through city streets. Sponsors wanted to codify an existing rule. The session ended. The bill died. The danger rolls on.

      Int 0075-2022 was introduced on March 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to ban moving billboards, echoing the matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to banning moving billboards.' Council Members Gale A. Brewer (primary), Justin L. Brannan, Erik D. Bottcher, Kalman Yeger, and Lincoln Restler sponsored the measure. The bill sought to codify an existing city rule (34 RCNY 4-12(j)), making it illegal to operate, stand, or park vehicles for commercial advertising. Violators would face a $500 penalty. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Int 0053-2022
      Yeger sponsors bill to restrict overnight commercial parking, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban commercial trucks from sleeping on quiet streets. The bill died. Utility and oil trucks kept their curb. Pedestrians and families still dodge hulking vehicles at dawn. The city blinked. The danger stayed.

      Int 0053-2022, introduced on February 24, 2022, sought to amend city law to bar commercial vehicles from parking overnight on residential streets, between 9 p.m. and 5 a.m. The Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure handled the bill. The measure removed exemptions for gas, oil, and utility trucks. The matter summary reads: 'This bill would prohibit the parking of commercial vehicles owned or operated by gas or oil heat suppliers, gas or oil heat systems maintenance companies or any public utility in residential streets overnight.' Council Members Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor), Robert F. Holden, Vickie Paladino, and Kevin C. Riley backed the bill. The council filed it at session’s end, on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst weighed in, but the bill’s failure means residential streets remain lined with large trucks, leaving vulnerable road users exposed.

      Res 0009-2022
      Yeger co-sponsors resolution for accessible subways, improving safety for vulnerable riders.
      ▸

      Resolution 0009-2022 was introduced on February 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It called on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to ensure any subway station undergoing enhancement or renovation becomes fully accessible to people with disabilities. The matter title reads: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make any subway stations undergoing enhancement or renovation fully accessible to people with disabilities.' Council Member Darlene Mealy sponsored the resolution, joined by Brannan, Menin, Louis, Yeger, Hanif, Hudson, Marte, Joseph, Riley, and Brooks-Powers. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. Only 117 out of 493 subway stations are accessible. The Council’s action highlights the city’s failure to guarantee safe, equal passage for all riders. Elevators and upgrades are overdue. The bill’s filing leaves vulnerable New Yorkers waiting.
