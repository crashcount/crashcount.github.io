      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Gravesend (East)-Homecrest?

      Four Dead, Two Children Gone—Gravesend’s Streets Are Killing Fields

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Gravesend (East)-Homecrest: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Four people killed. Six seriously injured. In the last twelve months, 400 crashes tore through Gravesend (East)-Homecrest. Nearly 300 neighbors hurt. Two children dead before their lives began. These are not just numbers. They are empty chairs at dinner, shoes left by the door, a mother’s hand forever reaching for a child who won’t come home.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      On Ocean Parkway, a mother and her two daughters were killed in a crosswalk. The driver had a suspended license and over 93 traffic violations. She was still behind the wheel. NYPD Commissioner Jessica Tisch described the crash: “Miriam Yarimi, 32, a mother of one, was driving her Audi on a suspended license… when she got into a collision… and careened into and killed a mother and two children who were leaving Shabbat services.” The car had just sped through a school zone weeks before. The city let it happen.

      Elsewhere, a 24-year-old drove drunk through Coney Island Avenue at 2 a.m., slamming into a Volvo, then an NYPD cruiser. Two officers hospitalized. A passenger thrown from the car, left in critical condition. Police reported that “one police officer suffered a broken pelvis and arm, as well as head trauma, while another suffered a broken hip.” The driver refused a blood test. He was charged with DWI and reckless endangerment.

      Leadership: Action or Excuses?

      Local leaders have the power. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit still stands at 25. Dangerous Vehicle Abatement lapsed. Cameras that catch speeders face expiration. The city talks about Vision Zero, but the dead keep piling up.

      Mayor Adams called for a full investigation after the Ocean Parkway killings. He commented on the tragedy, emphasizing the importance of investigating the incident fully. But investigations don’t bring back the dead. Lower speeds do. Cameras do. Real enforcement does.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Demand the city take the keys from repeat offenders. Don’t wait for another child’s name in the news. Take action now.

      Felder Supports Safety Boosting Automated Parking Enforcement Cameras
      ▸

      Lawmakers push for cameras to ticket double parkers. Streets choke with blocked bike lanes and chaos. Manual enforcement fails. DOT backs automation. Some lawmakers resist, call for cops. Vulnerable road users left dodging danger as debate drags.

      On March 3, 2025, a legislative proposal surfaced to deploy automated parking enforcement cameras across New York City. Assemblymember Steven Raga leads the push for a $35 million pilot, aiming to install 150 cameras targeting double parking and illegal stops. The bill, not yet assigned a committee or number, seeks to automate enforcement where manual efforts fall short. Raga writes, 'manual enforcement has not been able to meet the demand to combat double parking and illegal parking.' State Senator Simcha Felder of District 44 opposes the measure, arguing for traditional police summonses instead. The Department of Transportation supports the expansion, stating, 'Automated enforcement has proven to change driver behavior and make our streets safer for everyone.' The debate centers on whether automation or police presence best protects pedestrians and cyclists from blocked lanes and traffic hazards.

      A 324
      Novakhov co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 324 demands every state- or federally-funded road project weigh complete street design. The bill pushes agencies to publish clear guidance. Streets built for cars alone kill. This bill aims to force change at the blueprint.

      Assembly Bill 324 was introduced on January 1, 2025, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in the New York State Assembly, with Didi Barrett as the primary sponsor and a long list of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Amy Paulin, and others. The bill summary reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding; authorizes the department to compile and make publicly available guidance and reference material related to consideration of complete street design.' This measure would require state and federally funded transportation projects to consider designs that protect all road users, not just drivers. The bill’s broad sponsorship signals growing support for safer, more inclusive streets. No safety analyst note was provided.

      A 116
      Novakhov co-sponsors e-scooter bill that likely reduces street safety.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 116 would force e-scooter riders in New York City to get a license, read a safety manual, and buy insurance. The bill blocks sales to unlicensed buyers. It aims to regulate e-scooters but leaves questions for those on foot and bike.

      Assembly Bill 116, now in sponsorship, sits in the Assembly. Filed January 1, 2025, it seeks to 'establish a bicycle and electric scooter operator's safety manual; provide for the issuance of a license for residents who successfully read and comprehend the operator's safety manual; require residents who operate an electric scooter in New York City to have a license to operate such devices; require instruction in electric scooter safety as part of the drivers pre-licensing course; require liability insurance for electric scooters in cities having a population of one million or more; prohibit the sale of an electric scooter to a purchaser unless the purchaser has a license to operate the electric scooter and has insurance in a city having a population of one million or more.' Assemblymember John Zaccaro Jr. leads sponsorship, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Michael Benedetto, Deborah Glick, David McDonough, and Michael Novakhov. The bill targets e-scooter operators, not drivers of larger vehicles. No safety analyst has assessed its impact on pedestrians or cyclists.
