      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Gravesend (East)-Homecrest?

      Four Dead, Two Children Gone—Gravesend’s Streets Are Killing Fields

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Gravesend (East)-Homecrest: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Four people killed. Six seriously injured. In the last twelve months, 400 crashes tore through Gravesend (East)-Homecrest. Nearly 300 neighbors hurt. Two children dead before their lives began. These are not just numbers. They are empty chairs at dinner, shoes left by the door, a mother’s hand forever reaching for a child who won’t come home.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      On Ocean Parkway, a mother and her two daughters were killed in a crosswalk. The driver had a suspended license and over 93 traffic violations. She was still behind the wheel. NYPD Commissioner Jessica Tisch described the crash: “Miriam Yarimi, 32, a mother of one, was driving her Audi on a suspended license… when she got into a collision… and careened into and killed a mother and two children who were leaving Shabbat services.” The car had just sped through a school zone weeks before. The city let it happen.

      Elsewhere, a 24-year-old drove drunk through Coney Island Avenue at 2 a.m., slamming into a Volvo, then an NYPD cruiser. Two officers hospitalized. A passenger thrown from the car, left in critical condition. Police reported that “one police officer suffered a broken pelvis and arm, as well as head trauma, while another suffered a broken hip.” The driver refused a blood test. He was charged with DWI and reckless endangerment.

      Leadership: Action or Excuses?

      Local leaders have the power. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit still stands at 25. Dangerous Vehicle Abatement lapsed. Cameras that catch speeders face expiration. The city talks about Vision Zero, but the dead keep piling up.

      Mayor Adams called for a full investigation after the Ocean Parkway killings. He commented on the tragedy, emphasizing the importance of investigating the incident fully. But investigations don’t bring back the dead. Lower speeds do. Cameras do. Real enforcement does.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Demand the city take the keys from repeat offenders. Don’t wait for another child’s name in the news. Take action now.

      Int 0746-2024
      Vernikov votes no, opposing safer medians and improved pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to turn bare medians green. The city must plant trees or use medians for stormwater. Agencies must report progress. The law targets high-risk areas. Votes showed strong support. The mayor returned it unsigned. Streets will change.

      Int 0746-2024, now Local Law 94 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 12, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the improvement and maintenance of medians through the planting of vegetation or use in stormwater management," requires the city to improve at least one mile of paved medians every two years until 2046. The Department of Transportation, Parks, and Environmental Protection must add planted medians, tree beds, or stormwater infrastructure, focusing on high-priority investment areas. Council Member Shekar Krishnan sponsored the bill, joined by Feliz, Avilés, Nurse, Banks, Restler, Ung, Schulman, Holden, Hudson, and Yeger. The Council passed the bill with broad support, though the mayor returned it unsigned. The law mandates annual progress reports and public tracking of improved medians and responsible agencies.

      Bill A 7652, titled "Establishes a school speed zone camera demonstration program in the city of Schenectady," advanced with a committee vote in the Assembly on June 7, 2024. No formal safety analyst note was provided, but the measure targets driver speed in zones where children walk, bike, and cross.

      Int 0921-2024
      Vernikov co-sponsors bill speeding utility pole removal, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Old poles rot. Wires sag. This bill sets a clock. Sixty days to clear abandoned poles and wires. Immediate action for hazards. Thirty days to shift gear to new poles. Streets get less clutter. Danger drops for those on foot and bike.

      Int 0921-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced May 23, 2024, it amends the city code to force utility owners to remove abandoned poles and wires within 60 days, and to act immediately if danger looms. Appurtenances must move to new poles within 30 days. The bill’s title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the timelines for the removal of abandoned or unsafe utility poles, wires, and appurtenances, and the transfer of appurtenances to newly erected poles.' Council Members Brannan (primary), Holden, Vernikov, Marmorato, and Paladino sponsor. The measure targets street hazards that trip, block, or injure pedestrians and cyclists. It aims to clear the path and cut risk for all vulnerable road users.
