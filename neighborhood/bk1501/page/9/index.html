      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Gravesend (East)-Homecrest?

      Four Dead, Two Children Gone—Gravesend’s Streets Are Killing Fields

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Gravesend (East)-Homecrest: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Four people killed. Six seriously injured. In the last twelve months, 400 crashes tore through Gravesend (East)-Homecrest. Nearly 300 neighbors hurt. Two children dead before their lives began. These are not just numbers. They are empty chairs at dinner, shoes left by the door, a mother’s hand forever reaching for a child who won’t come home.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      On Ocean Parkway, a mother and her two daughters were killed in a crosswalk. The driver had a suspended license and over 93 traffic violations. She was still behind the wheel. NYPD Commissioner Jessica Tisch described the crash: “Miriam Yarimi, 32, a mother of one, was driving her Audi on a suspended license… when she got into a collision… and careened into and killed a mother and two children who were leaving Shabbat services.” The car had just sped through a school zone weeks before. The city let it happen.

      Elsewhere, a 24-year-old drove drunk through Coney Island Avenue at 2 a.m., slamming into a Volvo, then an NYPD cruiser. Two officers hospitalized. A passenger thrown from the car, left in critical condition. Police reported that “one police officer suffered a broken pelvis and arm, as well as head trauma, while another suffered a broken hip.” The driver refused a blood test. He was charged with DWI and reckless endangerment.

      Leadership: Action or Excuses?

      Local leaders have the power. Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. But the limit still stands at 25. Dangerous Vehicle Abatement lapsed. Cameras that catch speeders face expiration. The city talks about Vision Zero, but the dead keep piling up.

      Mayor Adams called for a full investigation after the Ocean Parkway killings. He commented on the tragedy, emphasizing the importance of investigating the incident fully. But investigations don’t bring back the dead. Lower speeds do. Cameras do. Real enforcement does.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Demand the city take the keys from repeat offenders. Don’t wait for another child’s name in the news. Take action now.

      Int 0906-2023
      Yeger absent as committee approves bill improving street safety from truck parking.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to force off-street parking for tractor trailers. No more big rigs clogging residential blocks. By 2025, each borough must have a site. The city must act or explain delays. Streets may breathe. Danger shifts from curbs.

      Int 0906-2023, now Local Law 142 of 2023, was enacted by the City Council on October 29, 2023, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill’s title: 'A Local Law in relation to parking for tractor trailers and other commercial trucks.' It requires the city to identify at least one feasible off-street parking location for tractor trailers in each borough by the end of 2023, and to establish those sites by December 31, 2025. No location can be on a residential street. Council Member Kamillah Hanks sponsored the bill, joined by Brannan, Riley, Dinowitz, Ung, Brooks-Powers, and others. The law aims to clear residential streets of oversized trucks, shifting their presence to designated lots. The council’s action targets the chronic hazard of trucks parked where people walk, bike, and live.

      Int 1173-2023
      Yeger sponsors bill on emergency vehicle access, neutral for street safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to keep open streets open for fire trucks and ambulances. No more moving barriers by hand. DOT must warn walkers and riders when sirens come. The bill died at session’s end. Streets stay risky. Safety waits.

      Int 1173-2023 was introduced in the City Council on September 14, 2023, and referred to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill’s title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to ensuring emergency vehicle access to open streets.' Council Members Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor, District 44) and Robert F. Holden (co-sponsor, District 30) pushed for rules so emergency vehicles could always reach open streets—no more manual barrier moves. The Department of Transportation would also have to alert pedestrians and cyclists when vehicles enter. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. Vulnerable road users remain exposed while the city delays action.

      Int 1143-2023
      Vernikov co-sponsors bill raising sidewalk riding fines, worsening overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to raise fines for riding bikes, e-bikes, or scooters on sidewalks. The measure targets repeat offenders and cases with physical contact. The bill died in committee. Streets remain unchanged. Vulnerable users still face car danger.

      Int 1143-2023 was introduced on August 3, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill sought to amend the city code by 'increasing civil penalties for operating a bicycle, bicycle with electric assist, or electric scooter on the sidewalk.' The measure would have raised fines up to $510 for endangering others, with double penalties for repeat violations and extra penalties for physical contact. Council Member Gale A. Brewer sponsored the bill, joined by Gennaro, Lee, Brannan, Yeger, Kagan, Vernikov, and Ariola. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst assessed its impact on vulnerable road users. The bill focused on sidewalk riding, but left the core danger—cars on city streets—untouched.

      Int 0289-2022
      Yeger absent as committee advances neutral-impact bike infrastructure mapping bill.
      ▸

      The Council passed a law forcing DOT to map every bike lane, hazard, and obstruction. Cyclists and pedestrians get a clear look at danger. The searchable map must show blocked lanes, crash sites, and repairs. No more hiding unsafe streets.

      Int 0289-2022, now Local Law 124 of 2023, was enacted by the City Council on September 3, 2023, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The law requires the Department of Transportation to publish a monthly-updated, searchable map of all city bike lanes. The map must show obstructions, construction, crash patterns, shared paths, conflict points, resurfacing, parking, bike share docks, open streets, repair shops, and reporting tools. The bill’s matter title reads: 'requiring the department of transportation to develop a map of bicycle infrastructure conditions.' Council Member Crystal Hudson sponsored the bill, joined by Rivera, Sanchez, Stevens, Yeger, Restler, Avilés, and others. The law brings sunlight to hidden hazards, giving vulnerable road users the facts they need to see the city’s dangers in plain sight.

      Int 1120-2023
      Yeger co-sponsors bill to boost safety with raised speed reducers.
      ▸

      Council pushed DOT to study raised speed bumps at speed camera spots. If feasible, bumps would go in within a year. Annual reports would track danger and say if cameras are still needed. The bill died at session’s end. No action. No change.

      Bill Int 1120-2023, filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to force the Department of Transportation to assess at least 100 speed camera locations per year for raised speed reducer feasibility. Introduced July 13, 2023, and sponsored by Council Members Farah N. Louis (primary), Kevin C. Riley, Kalman Yeger, Amanda Farías, and David M. Carr, the measure required DOT to install raised speed reducers within a year where feasible, then report annually on danger indicators and recommend if cameras should remain. The official summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring a raised speed reducer feasibility assessment at speed camera locations.' The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No raised speed reducers will be installed under this law. Vulnerable road users remain exposed at these crossings.
