      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Ridgewood?

      Ridgewood Bleeds While Leaders Stall: Demand Safer Streets Now

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Ridgewood: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Ridgewood

      No one died on Ridgewood’s streets in the last year. But the blood did not stop. In twelve months, 199 people were hurt in 363 crashes. Three were left with serious injuries. Children were among the wounded—twenty under eighteen, their lives changed by a driver’s mistake (NYC Open Data).

      The wounds are not rare. Since 2022, Ridgewood has seen 1,399 crashes. Two people died. Seven suffered injuries so grave they may never heal. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old. They do not care if you are careful.

      The Shape of Danger

      Cars and SUVs do most of the harm. They killed one person and left twenty-two with moderate or serious injuries. Trucks, motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes all left their mark. No one is safe when the street is built for speed and steel.

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay the price. A 74-year-old man was killed crossing Myrtle Avenue. A 64-year-old woman was crushed at Fresh Pond Road and 68th Avenue. A 23-year-old cyclist was thrown and battered on Gates Avenue. The stories repeat. The pain does not fade.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      City leaders talk of Vision Zero. They say one death is too many. They point to new speed cameras, intersection redesigns, and the power to lower speed limits. But the work is slow. The streets do not wait (NYC Open Data).

      The law now allows a 20 mph limit. Yet Ridgewood waits. Each day without action is a day someone else may not come home.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by planners, by leaders who delay.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand more cameras. Demand streets that put people first.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Take action.

      Int 0312-2022
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors bill raising fines for excessive vehicle noise.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to hike penalties for loud cars and trucks. The measure targeted blaring horns, illegal claxons, and roaring engines. It died at session’s end. No direct safety gains for people walking or biking. Noise remains. Streets stay loud.

      Int 0312-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety. The bill aimed to amend the city’s administrative code to increase penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles, including those under and over 10,000 pounds, and for unauthorized horns and claxons. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to increasing certain penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles.' Council Member Sandra Ung sponsored the bill, joined by 26 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. There is no evidence the bill would have improved safety for vulnerable road users. The city’s noise problem, and its threat to livable streets, remains unresolved.

      Gutiérrez co-sponsors bill shifting sidewalk repair to city, boosting pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0336-2022 would force the Parks Department to repair sidewalks wrecked by city trees. Homeowners escape blame. The city takes the hit. The bill died in committee. Broken sidewalks stay broken. Cyclists and pedestrians still trip.

      Int 0336-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to shift responsibility for sidewalk damage caused by city-owned trees from homeowners to the Parks Department. The matter title reads: 'requiring the parks department to repair damage caused by trees owned by the city of New York.' Council Member Kalman Yeger sponsored the bill, joined by Holden, Ung, Ayala, Restler, Dinowitz, Williams, Gutiérrez, Riley, Schulman, Brooks-Powers, Lee, Paladino, and Vernikov. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. If passed, it could have led to safer, smoother sidewalks for pedestrians and cyclists, removing a hazard that city government created and left to fester.

      Int 0334-2022
      Holden co-sponsors bill to speed repairs, boosting street and sidewalk safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0334-2022 would cut the city’s repair window for street and sidewalk hazards from fifteen days to seven. If the city fails to fix dangers after notice, injured people can sue. The bill stalled. Streets stay risky.

      Int 0334-2022, introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to amend New York City’s administrative code. The bill’s summary reads: “Requirements for maintenance of a civil action against the city for damages or injuries sustained in consequence of unsafe conditions on streets, sidewalks or similar public spaces.” Council Members Julie Won, Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor), and Robert F. Holden sponsored the measure. The bill would have forced the city to fix reported street and sidewalk hazards within seven days, down from fifteen. If the city failed, injured pedestrians or cyclists could sue. The bill was filed at session’s end on December 31, 2023. No new protections reached the street.

      Moped Strikes 4-Year-Old Pedestrian Crossing
      ▸

      A moped traveling south on Fresh Pond Road hit a 4-year-old boy crossing with the signal. The child suffered an upper arm contusion and bruise. Police cited the moped driver for failure to yield right-of-way. The boy remained conscious.

      Int 0251-2022
      Holden co-sponsors bill that could slow rollout of street safety projects.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0251-2022 expands what counts as a major transportation project. Bike lanes, busways, and bus lanes now trigger public notice. The Department of Transportation must alert community boards, offer presentations, and post updates online. The bill is filed, not law.

      Int 0251-2022, introduced April 28, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, sought to amend city law on community notification for major transportation projects. The bill, sponsored by Kalman Yeger (primary), with Inna Vernikov, Joseph C. Borelli, Kevin C. Riley, Shaun Abreu, and Robert F. Holden as co-sponsors, was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. The bill summary states: 'This bill would change the community board notification process for major transportation projects by defining the construction or removal of a bus lane, busway, or bike lane as a major transportation project, regardless of the number of consecutive blocks or consecutive feet of street that the project alters.' DOT would be required to notify and present plans to community boards, consider feedback, and post quarterly progress reports online. The bill aimed to increase transparency and community input on street changes that affect vulnerable road users, but did not become law.

      Int 0253-2022
      Holden co-sponsors school bus parking ban, boosting street safety and visibility.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban school buses from parking on city streets overnight and weekends. The bill died in committee. Buses still line curbs after dark. Streets remain cluttered. Danger for walkers and riders lingers. No change. Status quo wins.

      Int 0253-2022 was introduced in the New York City Council on April 28, 2022. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting school bus parking on city streets overnight and on weekends,' aimed to stop school buses from parking on city streets between 5:00 p.m. and 5:00 a.m. on weekdays and all weekend. The measure was referred to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Sponsors included Justin L. Brannan (primary), Robert F. Holden, Joann Ariola, Sandy Nurse, and Marjorie Velázquez. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, without a vote or enactment. School buses continue to occupy curb space overnight, leaving visibility and access issues for pedestrians and cyclists unresolved.

      Res 0108-2022
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors Atlantic Ticket expansion, boosting transit use and street safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to expand and lock in the Atlantic Ticket. They want Far Rockaway and more LIRR stations in the fare program. The move aims to cut commute times and boost transit use. The bill is filed, not enacted.

      Res 0108-2022 was introduced on April 14, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution urges the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to 'expand and make permanent the Atlantic Ticket pilot in order to include Far Rockaway, and other stations along the LIRR corridor, in the fare affordability program.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Yeger, Stevens, Hanif, Bottcher, Krishnan, and many others. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. The Atlantic Ticket pilot, launched in 2018, gave Southeast Queens and Brooklyn riders cheaper LIRR fares. Advocates say expanding it citywide could mean faster, safer commutes and more transit riders. The council’s action highlights the need for affordable, accessible transit—especially for neighborhoods with long, punishing commutes.

      Resolution 0109-2022, filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, urges continued exploration and feasibility evaluation of the proposed Interborough Express (IBX).       Gutiérrez co-sponsors resolution boosting subway service, improving overall transit safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to run more peak-hour trains from distant subway terminals. Riders in transit deserts face long waits, missed trains, and crowded platforms. The resolution highlights gaps in service that strand working New Yorkers far from Manhattan.

      Res 0110-2022 was filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution, introduced April 14, 2022, and filed at session's end on December 31, 2023, urges the MTA to 'adjust schedules for distant subway terminal lines to include more peak-direction rush hour trains for commuters to travel to central economic hubs.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Bottcher, and others. The bill responds to service gaps hitting outer boroughs, end-of-line stations, and transit deserts—areas where 600,000 New Yorkers live with poor transit access. The council points to pandemic-era staffing shortages and train cancellations that left riders stranded. The measure aims to push the MTA to close these gaps and connect more New Yorkers to jobs and opportunity.

      Int 0178-2022
      Holden sponsors bill clarifying sidewalk maintenance rules, with no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to cap sidewalk maintenance for property owners at thirty feet from the property line. The city keeps liability for the rest. The measure died in committee. Pedestrians still face broken walks and shifting blame.

      Int 0178-2022 was introduced on April 14, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill sought to limit the sidewalk area that residential or commercial property owners must maintain to thirty feet from the property line. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to limiting the amount of sidewalk area that private property owners are responsible for maintaining.' Council Members Robert F. Holden (primary sponsor), Kalman Yeger, and Francisco P. Moya backed the bill. The measure was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, without passage. The bill would have shifted some sidewalk responsibility away from private owners, but left gaps in accountability. The city retains liability for areas beyond the thirty-foot limit, but the fractured system remains. Vulnerable road users—pedestrians, wheelchair users—still face hazards from uneven, neglected sidewalks.

      Gutiérrez Supports Participatory Budgeting Expanding Community Budget Input
      ▸

      Councilmember Rita Joseph opens the door. Residents in District 40 and four other Brooklyn districts can vote on local projects. Ballots close April 10. The process funds parks, schools, and public spaces. The city lets people decide. Streets may change.

      Participatory Budgeting voting opened in Brooklyn District 40 on April 7, 2022. Councilmember Rita Joseph announced the cycle, which runs until April 10. The process lets residents as young as 11 vote on capital and expense projects. The official matter title: 'Voting for Participatory Budgeting is open in five Brooklyn districts until April 10.' Joseph, along with councilmembers Jennifer Gutiérrez (34), Chi Ossé (36), Alexa Avilés (38), and Shahana Hanif (39), offers in-person and online voting. Projects range from park upgrades to school improvements. Final results will be announced the week of April 17. Participatory Budgeting shapes how public money is spent, but the safety impact for vulnerable road users depends on which projects win. No direct safety assessment was provided.
