      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Ridgewood?

      Ridgewood Bleeds While Leaders Stall: Demand Safer Streets Now

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Ridgewood: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Ridgewood

      No one died on Ridgewood’s streets in the last year. But the blood did not stop. In twelve months, 199 people were hurt in 363 crashes. Three were left with serious injuries. Children were among the wounded—twenty under eighteen, their lives changed by a driver’s mistake (NYC Open Data).

      The wounds are not rare. Since 2022, Ridgewood has seen 1,399 crashes. Two people died. Seven suffered injuries so grave they may never heal. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old. They do not care if you are careful.

      The Shape of Danger

      Cars and SUVs do most of the harm. They killed one person and left twenty-two with moderate or serious injuries. Trucks, motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes all left their mark. No one is safe when the street is built for speed and steel.

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay the price. A 74-year-old man was killed crossing Myrtle Avenue. A 64-year-old woman was crushed at Fresh Pond Road and 68th Avenue. A 23-year-old cyclist was thrown and battered on Gates Avenue. The stories repeat. The pain does not fade.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      City leaders talk of Vision Zero. They say one death is too many. They point to new speed cameras, intersection redesigns, and the power to lower speed limits. But the work is slow. The streets do not wait (NYC Open Data).

      The law now allows a 20 mph limit. Yet Ridgewood waits. Each day without action is a day someone else may not come home.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by planners, by leaders who delay.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand more cameras. Demand streets that put people first.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Take action.

      Int 0781-2022
      Holden votes no on vehicle stop reporting bill with neutral safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing NYPD to spell out why they stop cars. Each stop now gets logged with the reason, time, and outcome. No names. Just facts. The city posts these reports online. A move for sunlight. No more secrets.

      Int 0781-2022, now law, came through the Committee on Public Safety. The Council passed it on January 20, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the police department to report on the justification for the stop in vehicle encounter reports," demands the NYPD document every vehicle stop. Reports must include the reason for the stop, the level of offense, and demographic details, but no personal identifiers. Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Lincoln Restler (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, and others led the charge. The law aims to expose patterns in police stops and holds officers to account. Reports go online, open to all. This is a step toward transparency, not just for drivers, but for every New Yorker who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.

      Holden Supports Stricter Penalties for Ghost Plate Toll Dodgers
      ▸

      Councilmember Holden pushes tougher penalties for fake and obscured plates. NYPD tickets rise, but repeat offenders dodge tolls and cameras. Advocates warn: weak enforcement means more cheating, less safety, and crowded streets. The city cracks down, but loopholes remain.

      Bill action centers on license plate enforcement as congestion pricing approaches. On December 11, 2023, Councilmember Robert F. Holden (District 30) supported raising fines for drivers using fake or obscured plates. The matter, reported by Gothamist, highlights a surge in 'ghost cars'—vehicles with covered, defaced, or phony plates—dodging tolls and speed cameras. The city has banned camera-blocking covers and encourages public reporting, but unresolved complaints and repeat violations persist. Holden's push aims to make penalties outweigh the savings from toll evasion, seeking legislative action to protect the program's integrity.

      Int 1259-2023
      Holden co-sponsors helmet mandate bill that undermines overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council Member Holden pushed a bill to force all cyclists to wear helmets. The measure threatened $50 fines for bare heads. The council filed it at session’s end. No new protections for riders. The city’s danger stays the same.

      Int 1259-2023, introduced by Council Member Robert F. Holden, aimed to require every bicyclist in New York City to wear protective headgear.
