      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Sunset Park (West)?

      Sunset Park Bleeds—City Stalls. Demand Action Before More Die.

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Sunset Park (West): Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Nine dead. Fourteen left with serious injuries. In Sunset Park (West), from 2022 to June 2025, the street keeps taking. More than 1,400 people hurt in 2,282 crashes. The dead do not speak. The injured carry scars the rest of us do not see.

      In the last year, three lives were lost. This year, so far, none. But the wounds keep coming. 218 people injured since January. Three more with injuries that will not heal.

      Who Pays the Price?

      The young and the old. Eighteen children hurt in the last year. Sixty-five people aged 18 to 24. The street does not care who you are. It does not care if you are walking, riding, or waiting for the light to change.

      Cars and trucks did the most harm. Two deaths, twenty-five moderate injuries, four serious. Bikes and mopeds hurt too, but the big machines do the killing. See the data.

      Leadership: Words and Silence

      City Hall says, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” said Mayor Adams. The city has lowered speed limits, built bike lanes, and put up cameras. But the work is not done. The law that forces repeat dangerous drivers to take safety courses has lapsed. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph, but has not done it everywhere. Cameras that catch speeding may go dark if Albany does not act.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day without action is a day someone else bleeds. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city use its new power to lower speed limits. Demand Albany keep the cameras on. Join the fight. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      Avilés Opposes DOT Delay on Safety Boosting Truck Report
      ▸

      DOT failed to deliver its truck route redesign report on time. Streets choke on freight. Pedestrians and cyclists face danger. Community leaders demand action. The law required urgency. DOT delayed, citing more public input. Vulnerable New Yorkers wait. Trucks keep rolling.

      Council bill (2023) mandated DOT to publish a truck route network redesign report by September 15, 2024. The Transportation Committee oversees the law. Council Member Alexa Avilés sponsored the bill, citing freight traffic’s toll on her district. The law’s summary: DOT must report on redesign, using over 2,000 public comments. Many comments—36 percent—flagged dangerous conflicts between trucks, pedestrians, and cyclists. DOT missed the deadline, blaming an extended comment period. Elizabeth Yeampierre (UPROSE) demanded fewer trucks on residential streets and routes away from schools and parks. Maria Fernanda Pulido-Velosa (El Puente) called for real engagement and clear communication if delays continue. DOT now promises more outreach and a microhubs pilot for last-mile deliveries. But the overdue report leaves vulnerable road users exposed to daily risk.

      Int 0746-2024
      Avilés votes yes, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety with greener medians.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to turn bare medians green. The city must plant trees or use medians for stormwater. Agencies must report progress. The law targets high-risk areas. Votes showed strong support. The mayor returned it unsigned. Streets will change.

      Int 0746-2024, now Local Law 94 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 12, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the improvement and maintenance of medians through the planting of vegetation or use in stormwater management," requires the city to improve at least one mile of paved medians every two years until 2046. The Department of Transportation, Parks, and Environmental Protection must add planted medians, tree beds, or stormwater infrastructure, focusing on high-priority investment areas. Council Member Shekar Krishnan sponsored the bill, joined by Feliz, Avilés, Nurse, Banks, Restler, Ung, Schulman, Holden, Hudson, and Yeger. The Council passed the bill with broad support, though the mayor returned it unsigned. The law mandates annual progress reports and public tracking of improved medians and responsible agencies.
