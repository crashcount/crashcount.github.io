      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Sunset Park (West)?

      Sunset Park Bleeds—City Stalls. Demand Action Before More Die.

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 1, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Sunset Park (West): Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Nine dead. Fourteen left with serious injuries. In Sunset Park (West), from 2022 to June 2025, the street keeps taking. More than 1,400 people hurt in 2,282 crashes. The dead do not speak. The injured carry scars the rest of us do not see.

      In the last year, three lives were lost. This year, so far, none. But the wounds keep coming. 218 people injured since January. Three more with injuries that will not heal.

      Who Pays the Price?

      The young and the old. Eighteen children hurt in the last year. Sixty-five people aged 18 to 24. The street does not care who you are. It does not care if you are walking, riding, or waiting for the light to change.

      Cars and trucks did the most harm. Two deaths, twenty-five moderate injuries, four serious. Bikes and mopeds hurt too, but the big machines do the killing. See the data.

      Leadership: Words and Silence

      City Hall says, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” said Mayor Adams. The city has lowered speed limits, built bike lanes, and put up cameras. But the work is not done. The law that forces repeat dangerous drivers to take safety courses has lapsed. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph, but has not done it everywhere. Cameras that catch speeding may go dark if Albany does not act.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day without action is a day someone else bleeds. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city use its new power to lower speed limits. Demand Albany keep the cameras on. Join the fight. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      Int 0501-2022
      Avilés co-sponsors bill boosting civilian reporting to improve street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants near schools. Civilians can report violators. Each offense draws a $175 fine. The city pays whistleblowers a cut. The bill stalled. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0501-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on June 2, 2022. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations,' aimed to create a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 1,320 feet of a school. The penalty: $175 per violation, enforced through the Office of Administrative Trials and Hearings. Civilians, except city employees, could file complaints and receive 25% of collected fines. The Department of Transportation would support this with a phased-in reporting program and annual public reports. Council Member Carlina Rivera led as primary sponsor, joined by over two dozen co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Int 0500-2022
      Avilés co-sponsors bill to revoke private parking permits, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Int 0500-2022 was introduced on June 2, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill sought to "prohibit the issuance of private vehicle parking permits and revoke such existing permits," with exceptions for people with disabilities and collective bargaining agreements. Council Member Christopher Marte led as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Brewer, Joseph, Nurse, Cabán, Avilés, Bottcher, Won, Ossé, and Richardson Jordan, plus the Brooklyn Borough President.
