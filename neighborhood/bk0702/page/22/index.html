      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Sunset Park (West)?

      Sunset Park Bleeds—City Stalls. Demand Action Before More Die.

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Sunset Park (West): Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Nine dead. Fourteen left with serious injuries. In Sunset Park (West), from 2022 to June 2025, the street keeps taking. More than 1,400 people hurt in 2,282 crashes. The dead do not speak. The injured carry scars the rest of us do not see.

      In the last year, three lives were lost. This year, so far, none. But the wounds keep coming. 218 people injured since January. Three more with injuries that will not heal.

      Who Pays the Price?

      The young and the old. Eighteen children hurt in the last year. Sixty-five people aged 18 to 24. The street does not care who you are. It does not care if you are walking, riding, or waiting for the light to change.

      Cars and trucks did the most harm. Two deaths, twenty-five moderate injuries, four serious. Bikes and mopeds hurt too, but the big machines do the killing. See the data.

      Leadership: Words and Silence

      City Hall says, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” said Mayor Adams. The city has lowered speed limits, built bike lanes, and put up cameras. But the work is not done. The law that forces repeat dangerous drivers to take safety courses has lapsed. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph, but has not done it everywhere. Cameras that catch speeding may go dark if Albany does not act.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day without action is a day someone else bleeds. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city use its new power to lower speed limits. Demand Albany keep the cameras on. Join the fight. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      Councilmember Alexa Avilés pushes new rules to rein in e-commerce warehouses. The bill would block massive facilities near homes, schools, and parks. It demands public review and stricter permits. The aim: less truck traffic, cleaner air, safer streets.

Council members and advocates, including Elizabeth Adams and Jehiah Czebotar, backed the move.       Int 0604-2022
      Avilés sponsors bill prioritizing NYCHA sidewalk repairs, boosting pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council moved to put NYCHA sidewalks first in line for repairs. Seniors get top priority. Non-NYCHA emergencies still jump the queue. The bill died at session’s end. Broken walks remain. Vulnerable tenants wait. Streets stay rough. Danger lingers.

      Int 0604-2022 was introduced on August 11, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to 'establish priority for sidewalk repairs at developments operated by the New York City Housing Authority,' giving first priority to senior-only NYCHA buildings, then to other NYCHA sites. Emergencies at non-NYCHA properties could override this order, with required notification to local officials. The bill required DOT to report on completed and pending NYCHA sidewalk repairs by June 30, 2023. Council Member Alexa Avilés sponsored the bill, joined by Abreu, Brooks-Powers, Restler, Hanif, Won, Nurse, Gutiérrez, and Sanchez. The measure was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, without enactment. Sidewalk hazards at NYCHA developments persist, leaving vulnerable residents exposed.

      Andrew Gounardes Backs Safety Boosting 24/7 Speed Cameras
      ▸

      Speed cameras now run all day, every day in New York City. The law ends the blackout. No more breaks for speeders. State Senator Andrew Gounardes led the charge. The city’s streets stay watched. Vulnerable lives get a fighting chance.

      On August 1, 2022, a new law took effect expanding New York City’s speed camera program to operate 24/7. The bill, sponsored in the State Senate by Andrew Gounardes, passed in Albany and was signed by Governor Hochul in June. The measure extends the program for three years and ends the old rule that shut cameras off overnight and on weekends. The Department of Transportation has installed about 2,000 cameras in 750 school zones. The bill’s summary: 'expands the safety technology around the clock.' Gounardes said, 'Your right to speed does not supersede anyone’s rights to live on the streets safely.' Mayor Eric Adams joined advocates to celebrate, stating, 'A city that never sleeps deserves a camera system that won’t take a nap.' DOT data shows 59% of traffic deaths happened during off hours. The expansion aims to close that deadly gap.

      Gounardes Backs Safety Boosting Driver Education Mandate

      On July 15, 2022, Governor Kathy Hochul signed a bill mandating that new drivers in New York be tested on pedestrian and cyclist safety awareness. The measure, championed by State Senator Andrew Gounardes, requires pre-licensing courses and written exams to include instruction on the dangers faced by cyclists and pedestrians.       On July 15, 2022, Governor Hochul signed a law requiring cyclist and pedestrian safety awareness in New York's driver licensing process.
