      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Sunset Park (West)?

      Sunset Park Bleeds—City Stalls. Demand Action Before More Die.

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Sunset Park (West): Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Nine dead. Fourteen left with serious injuries. In Sunset Park (West), from 2022 to June 2025, the street keeps taking. More than 1,400 people hurt in 2,282 crashes. The dead do not speak. The injured carry scars the rest of us do not see.

      In the last year, three lives were lost. This year, so far, none. But the wounds keep coming. 218 people injured since January. Three more with injuries that will not heal.

      Who Pays the Price?

      The young and the old. Eighteen children hurt in the last year. Sixty-five people aged 18 to 24. The street does not care who you are. It does not care if you are walking, riding, or waiting for the light to change.

      Cars and trucks did the most harm. Two deaths, twenty-five moderate injuries, four serious. Bikes and mopeds hurt too, but the big machines do the killing. See the data.

      Leadership: Words and Silence

      City Hall says, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” said Mayor Adams. The city has lowered speed limits, built bike lanes, and put up cameras. But the work is not done. The law that forces repeat dangerous drivers to take safety courses has lapsed. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph, but has not done it everywhere. Cameras that catch speeding may go dark if Albany does not act.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day without action is a day someone else bleeds. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city use its new power to lower speed limits. Demand Albany keep the cameras on. Join the fight. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      Avilés Urges Safety Boosting Protected Bike Lane Installation
      ▸

      DOT refused a protected bike lane on Brooklyn’s Fourth Avenue during construction. Cyclists face danger. The city law demands protection. DOT chose traffic flow over safety. Advocates and officials condemned the move. Illegally parked cars block the shared lane. Cyclists remain exposed.

      On October 20, 2022, the Department of Transportation (DOT) announced it would not install a temporary protected bike lane on Brooklyn’s Fourth Avenue during major construction, despite Local Law 124 requiring such measures when bike lanes are blocked. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez, who once supported the law as a council member, now claims a protected lane would worsen traffic and turning conflicts. Brooklyn Borough President Antonio Reynoso and Council Member Alexa Aviles criticized the decision, with Reynoso stating, 'protected bike lanes are essential,' and Aviles urging the city to 'install an alternative, fully protected bike lane where the road can accommodate one.' Advocates argue the shared lane is unsafe and often blocked by cars. The DOT’s move prioritizes vehicle flow over cyclist safety, leaving vulnerable road users at risk.

      Avilés Supports Safety Boosting Last Mile Trucking Regulation
      ▸

      Council Member Alexa Avilés pushes new rules for last-mile trucking. Trucks choke Red Hook and Sunset Park. Narrow streets shake. Residents breathe fumes. The bill demands safer, smarter routes. Data and daylighting aim to protect people, not just freight.

      On September 30, 2022, Council Member Alexa Avilés (District 38) introduced a package of bills targeting last-mile trucking regulation. The measures, revived in committee, seek to redesign truck routes and gather data on facilities run by Amazon, FedEx, and UPS. The matter aims to 'reduce congestion and emissions, improve safety and increase visibility,' especially in overburdened neighborhoods like Red Hook and Sunset Park. Avilés, the lead sponsor, calls for systematic changes: 'We really need to look systematically at more improved routes to ensure people are safe.' The bills would require the Department of Transportation to daylight intersections and the Department of Environmental Protection to install air monitors on heavy-use roads. Councilwoman Jennifer Gutiérrez sponsors a related bill for public truck-route data. The legislation draws support from industry and advocates, all seeking safer streets and cleaner air for vulnerable New Yorkers.

      Avilés Backs Safety Boosting E Commerce Warehouse Restrictions
      ▸

      Councilmember Alexa Avilés pushes new rules to rein in e-commerce warehouses. The bill would block massive facilities near homes, schools, and parks. It demands public review and stricter permits. The aim: less truck traffic, cleaner air, safer streets.

      On September 8, 2022, Councilmember Alexa Avilés (District 38) supported a proposal to tighten zoning and permitting for e-commerce warehouses in New York City. The measure, not yet introduced as a formal bill, seeks to amend city zoning rules to require special permits for warehouses over 50,000 square feet and ban them within 1,000 feet of schools, parks, nursing homes, public housing, or other warehouses. Community groups and environmental advocates say the change is urgent for neighborhoods already burdened by traffic and pollution.

      Avilés Supports Safety-Boosting Zoning for Last-Mile Warehouses
      ▸

      Council Member Alexa Aviles and advocates push to rein in last-mile warehouses. Trucks choke streets in poor neighborhoods. The plan demands distance from schools, parks, homes. It urges bikes, boats, and rails over diesel. The city’s old zoning lets danger spread.

      On September 8, 2022, Council Member Alexa Aviles and the NYC Environmental Justice Alliance proposed new zoning rules for last-mile trucking facilities. The measure, not yet in committee, would require a special permit for new warehouses. The proposal states: 'new last-mile warehouses must be at least 1,000 feet from schools, parks, nursing homes, public housing, and other such facilities.' It also pushes for 80% of deliveries in maritime areas to use marine transport, and encourages deliveries by foot, bike, and rail. Aviles and advocates demand a full land-use review (ULURP) for new sites. Existing warehouses are exempt. Kevin Garcia, an advocate, said, 'We want to reduce the number of cars and trucks that are leaving the facility.' The plan aims to cut truck traffic and emissions, which endanger vulnerable road users in low-income neighborhoods. No formal safety analyst note was provided.
