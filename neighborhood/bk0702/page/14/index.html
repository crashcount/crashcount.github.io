      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Sunset Park (West)?

      Sunset Park Bleeds—City Stalls. Demand Action Before More Die.

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Sunset Park (West): Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Nine dead. Fourteen left with serious injuries. In Sunset Park (West), from 2022 to June 2025, the street keeps taking. More than 1,400 people hurt in 2,282 crashes. The dead do not speak. The injured carry scars the rest of us do not see.

      In the last year, three lives were lost. This year, so far, none. But the wounds keep coming. 218 people injured since January. Three more with injuries that will not heal.

      Who Pays the Price?

      The young and the old. Eighteen children hurt in the last year. Sixty-five people aged 18 to 24. The street does not care who you are. It does not care if you are walking, riding, or waiting for the light to change.

      Cars and trucks did the most harm. Two deaths, twenty-five moderate injuries, four serious. Bikes and mopeds hurt too, but the big machines do the killing. See the data.

      Leadership: Words and Silence

      City Hall says, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” said Mayor Adams. The city has lowered speed limits, built bike lanes, and put up cameras. But the work is not done. The law that forces repeat dangerous drivers to take safety courses has lapsed. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph, but has not done it everywhere. Cameras that catch speeding may go dark if Albany does not act.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day without action is a day someone else bleeds. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the city use its new power to lower speed limits. Demand Albany keep the cameras on. Join the fight. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

The bill, now before the City Council, would require the Economic Development Corporation to tie port access to shore power use and to manage cruise-related traffic. Avilés declared, 'No cruise company should be allowed in our city unless they respect the communities and quality of life of our residents.' Bottcher compared ship emissions to 34,000 idling trucks. Residents, including Hart Valentine, blame cruise pollution for asthma and ruined lives. The bill responds to years of extractive deals that left neighborhoods sick and ignored.

      Avilés Demands Safety Boosting Protected Bike Lanes Restored
      ▸

      DOT stripped protected bike lanes from Brooklyn’s Fourth Avenue. Cyclists now dodge cars and illegal parking. Elected officials and advocates demand action. DOT cites traffic, but danger grows. Pedestrians lose safe crossings. The agency stays silent. Streets stay deadly.

      On September 18, 2023, a coalition of elected officials and advocates called out the Department of Transportation for removing protected bike lanes on Brooklyn’s Fourth Avenue during construction. The matter, described as 'DOT continues to ignore dangers it created on Brooklyn’s Fourth Ave,' centers on DOT’s decision to replace bike lanes with a second car lane, violating a city law that requires temporary bike lanes during such work. Council Members Lincoln Restler, Alexa Aviles, Shahana Hanif, Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, State Senator Andrew Gounardes, Assembly Member Jo Anne Simon, Borough President Antonio Reynoso, and groups like Bike New York and Transportation Alternatives sent a letter demanding the bike lane’s return and physical barriers to stop illegal parking on pedestrian islands. The letter urges DOT to 'ensure curb lanes be preserved for safe cyclist passage' and to 'deploy quick-build physical elements' for pedestrian safety. DOT has not responded. Cyclists and pedestrians remain at risk.

      On September 18, 2023, the City Council reviewed the Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program (DVAP), which is set to expire on October 26.
