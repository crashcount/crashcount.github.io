      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Washington Heights (North)?

      Washington Heights: Five Dead, Hundreds Hurt. Still No Safe Streets.

      Written June 6, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Washington Heights (North): Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 6, 2025

      Blood on the Streets

      A boy, 15, thrown from his moped. A cyclist, 37, crushed by a truck. A man, 46, dead on a parkway. In the last three years, five people have died in traffic crashes in Washington Heights (North). Fourteen more suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Nearly 600 people have been hurt since 2022. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie. NYC crash data

      The Machines That Maim

      SUVs and sedans hit hardest. They account for the most injuries and serious wounds. Trucks and buses add to the toll. Motorcycles and mopeds leave bodies broken. Even bikes draw blood. The street does not care who you are. It only cares who is bigger, faster, heavier.

      Leadership: Progress or Delay?

      The city talks of Vision Zero. They promise safer streets, lower speed limits, more cameras. But in these blocks, the pain keeps coming. One death, then another. Laws pass in Albany, but the wait drags on for real change. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not. Cameras that catch speeders could go dark if Albany stalls. Each delay is a risk. Each risk is a name lost.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. Every crash is a choice made by someone, or a choice not made by leaders. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand the 20 mph limit. Demand cameras stay on. Demand streets that do not bleed.

      Take action now.

      Int 1011-2023
      De La Rosa votes yes on fare evasion info bill, no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing police to hand out flyers on reduced fare programs to anyone ticketed or arrested for subway fare evasion. The flyers list eligibility, discounts, and how to apply. The law aims to inform, not punish. It is now in effect.

      Int 1011-2023, now enacted as Local Law 30 of 2024, moved through the Committee on Public Safety and passed the City Council on January 20, 2024. The bill requires police officers patrolling subway stations to carry and distribute flyers about city-run reduced fare programs to anyone arrested or summoned for fare evasion. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to providing information on the reduced fare programs to persons issued a summons for fare evasion.' Council Member Farah N. Louis sponsored the bill, joined by Brooks-Powers, Brewer, Dinowitz, Barron, Krishnan, and others. The flyers must explain eligibility, discount details, and application steps. The law took effect 120 days after passage. No direct safety impact for vulnerable road users was noted.

      Int 0781-2022
      De La Rosa votes yes on vehicle stop reporting bill, safety impact neutral.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing NYPD to spell out why they stop cars. Each stop now gets logged with the reason, time, and outcome. No names. Just facts. The city posts these reports online. A move for sunlight. No more secrets.

      Int 0781-2022, now law, came through the Committee on Public Safety. The Council passed it on January 20, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the police department to report on the justification for the stop in vehicle encounter reports," demands the NYPD document every vehicle stop. Reports must include the reason for the stop, the level of offense, and demographic details, but no personal identifiers. Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Lincoln Restler (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, and others led the charge. The law aims to expose patterns in police stops and holds officers to account. Reports go online, open to all. This is a step toward transparency, not just for drivers, but for every New Yorker who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.

      Rodriguez Supports Safety Boosting Physical Infrastructure for Daylighting
      ▸

      Mayor Adams will ban parking near 1,000 intersections each year. The city aims to clear corners, boost sightlines, and protect people on foot. Advocates pushed for this. The plan outpaces current law but leaves thousands of corners untouched for decades.

      On December 1, 2023, Mayor Eric Adams announced a new executive policy: New York City will remove car parking near 1,000 intersections annually, far exceeding the 100 intersections required by recent Council law. The effort, known as daylighting, aims to improve visibility and pedestrian safety. Adams said, 'Protecting New Yorkers is my most sacred responsibility as mayor.' Council Member Selvena Brooks-Powers praised daylighting as 'a proven safety measure.' DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez stressed that daylighting must include physical infrastructure to prevent reckless turns. The Department of Transportation will also add raised crosswalks, extended sidewalks, and leading pedestrian signals at 1,000 intersections next year. The city will expand speed restriction technology in its fleet and increase data transparency. Advocates and community boards have long called for these changes. With nearly 47,000 intersections citywide, the plan will take decades to reach every corner.
