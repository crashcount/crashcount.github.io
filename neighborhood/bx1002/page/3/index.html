      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Throgs Neck-Schuylerville?

      Bronx Streets Bleed While Politicians Stall—Who’s Next?

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Throgs Neck-Schuylerville: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Wounds That Do Not Heal

      In Throgs Neck-Schuylerville, the numbers do not tell the whole story, but they do not lie. In the last twelve months, 188 people were injured in crashes. Two were seriously hurt. No one lost their life. The dead do not speak. The injured carry scars the rest of us cannot see.

      A man tries to stop a thief from taking his car. He is dragged down the street. His leg is torn away. He may never walk again. “The victim suffered head trauma and an amputated left leg and remains in critical condition at Jacobi hospital,” reported ABC7.

      A neighbor stands on the sidewalk and says, “This has absolutely happened before,” told the Daily News. The violence is not new. It is routine.

      The Numbers Behind the Pain

      Since 2022, 527 people have been injured here. Two have died. Six suffered serious injuries. The young are not spared. In the last year, 18 children were hurt. Cars and SUVs do most of the damage. Trucks and motorcycles add to the toll. No one is immune.

      Pedestrians are struck at intersections and in the street. Cyclists are hit. Drivers and passengers are crushed in metal and glass. The numbers rise and fall, but the pain stays.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      The city passed new laws. Speed cameras now run day and night. The speed limit can be lowered. But in Throgs Neck-Schuylerville, the danger remains. The Bronx saw a 4% rise in car thefts this year, even as rates fell citywide reported ABC7. Local leaders talk about safety. The streets do not change. The blood dries and the news moves on.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand lower speed limits. Demand more cameras. Demand streets built for people, not just cars. Do not wait for another neighbor to bleed in the gutter. Take action now.

      A 116
      Benedetto co-sponsors e-scooter bill that likely reduces street safety.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 116 would force e-scooter riders in New York City to get a license, read a safety manual, and buy insurance. The bill blocks sales to unlicensed buyers. It aims to regulate e-scooters but leaves questions for those on foot and bike.

      Assembly Bill 116, now in sponsorship, sits in the Assembly. Filed January 1, 2025, it seeks to 'establish a bicycle and electric scooter operator's safety manual; provide for the issuance of a license for residents who successfully read and comprehend the operator's safety manual; require residents who operate an electric scooter in New York City to have a license to operate such devices; require instruction in electric scooter safety as part of the drivers pre-licensing course; require liability insurance for electric scooters in cities having a population of one million or more; prohibit the sale of an electric scooter to a purchaser unless the purchaser has a license to operate the electric scooter and has insurance in a city having a population of one million or more.' Assemblymember John Zaccaro Jr. leads sponsorship, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Michael Benedetto, Deborah Glick, David McDonough, and Michael Novakhov. The bill targets e-scooter operators, not drivers of larger vehicles. No safety analyst has assessed its impact on pedestrians or cyclists.

      S 131
      Fernandez co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 131 demands complete street design in state- and federally-funded road projects. It forces agencies to consider people on foot and bike, not just cars. The bill orders public guidance on safer street layouts. Sponsors push for streets that protect all.

      Senate bill S 131, introduced on January 1, 2025, is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in the Senate, awaiting committee action. Its summary reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding; authorizes the department to compile and make publicly available guidance and reference material related to consideration of complete street design.' Primary sponsor Sean Ryan (District 61) leads, joined by co-sponsors Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, Samra Brouk, Cordell Cleare, Leroy Comrie, Jeremy Cooney, Nathalia Fernandez, Michael Gianaris, Kristen Gonzalez, Robert Jackson, Rachel May, Kevin S.       Int 1142-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 19, 2024. Riley is the primary sponsor, joined by Ariola, Joseph, Moya, Won, Banks, Narcisse, Paladino, and Marmorato. Their action: referral to committee. The city continues to rely on signage, not street redesign, to address systemic danger.
