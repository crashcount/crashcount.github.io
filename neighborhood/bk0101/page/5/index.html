      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Greenpoint?

      Another Body, Same Silence: Greenpoint Bleeds While City Hall Sleeps

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Greenpoint: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Bodies on the Street

      In Greenpoint, the numbers do not lie. Four dead. Seven left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In the last twelve months, two people died just trying to cross the street. Ninety-three more were injured. The dead do not get a second chance. The living carry scars.

      Luis Cruz, 49, stepped out of his car on India Street. An e-bike delivery worker ran the stop sign and hit him. “He died basically on the spot,” said witness Jack Collins. The rider stayed. No arrest. No justice. Collins did not mince words: “It’s not a unicorn incident. It’s happened a lot. I’ve seen several people get swiped.” He knows the street.

      A woman, also 49, was killed in a crosswalk at Sutton and Nassau. A pickup truck turned left and took her life. No headline. Just another number in the city’s ledger.

      The Vehicles That Kill

      Trucks, bikes, cars, mopeds. In Greenpoint, trucks killed one pedestrian. E-bikes killed one. Cars and SUVs left a trail of broken bodies—twelve moderate injuries, none fatal this year, but the threat is constant. Motorcycles and mopeds add to the toll. The street does not care what you drive. It only takes.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Failed to Do

      The city has tools. Sammy’s Law lets New York lower speed limits to 20 mph. The council could act. They have not. Speed cameras work, but their future is always in doubt. The mayor says, “one life lost to traffic violence is one life too many.” The numbers say otherwise. The silence from local leaders is louder than any promise.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day of delay is another chance for death. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras that never go dark. Demand streets that put people first.

      Do not wait for another body in the road.

      S 131
      Gonzalez co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Senate bill S 131 demands complete street design in state- and federally-funded road projects. It forces agencies to consider people on foot and bike, not just cars. The bill orders public guidance on safer street layouts. Sponsors push for streets that protect all.

      Senate bill S 131, introduced on January 1, 2025, is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in the Senate, awaiting committee action. Its summary reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding; authorizes the department to compile and make publicly available guidance and reference material related to consideration of complete street design.' Primary sponsor Sean Ryan (District 61) leads, joined by co-sponsors Jamaal Bailey, Jabari Brisport, Samra Brouk, Cordell Cleare, Leroy Comrie, Jeremy Cooney, Nathalia Fernandez, Michael Gianaris, Kristen Gonzalez, Robert Jackson, Rachel May, Kevin S. It also demands transparency, requiring the department to publish guidance on safer street design for all.
