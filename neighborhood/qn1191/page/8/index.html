      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Alley Pond Park?

      Alley Pond Park: 21 Kids Hurt, Zero Action

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Alley Pond Park: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Toll in Alley Pond Park

      The crashes do not stop. In the last twelve months, 111 people were injured in 181 crashes in Alley Pond Park. Two suffered serious injuries. No one died, but the wounds run deep. Children are not spared—21 kids hurt in a single year. These are not just numbers. They are broken legs, shattered faces, and families waiting in hospital halls.

      SUVs hit hardest. In the past three years, SUVs caused three pedestrian injuries, including one serious. No bikes, no buses. Just heavy metal and flesh. The parkway is a river of steel. The young and the old are swept up the same.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city says it is acting. They point to new laws and cameras. “In order to make New York City the best place to raise a family, we need to be safer at every level — including on our streets,” said Mayor Adams. But the crashes keep coming. The city lowered speed limits in some places. Cameras catch speeders, but only where they are placed. The rest is luck.

      Police say they are watching. “NYPD officers will be highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action,” said Commissioner Caban. But in Alley Pond Park, the numbers do not move. The pain stays the same.

      The Work Left Undone

      No deaths this year. But the injuries pile up. The city claims progress, but the park is still a danger zone. The council and community board have not done enough. The roads are wide. The crossings are long. The cars are fast. The children are small.

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Demand lower speed limits and more cameras. Demand streets that protect walkers, not just drivers.

      Do not wait for the first death.

      Take action now.

      Res 0419-2022
      Paladino co-sponsors resolution that could undermine congestion pricing safety benefits.
      ▸

      Council members want voters to decide on the MTA’s congestion pricing plan. The resolution calls for a statewide ballot. Sponsors cite risks to outer-borough communities and doubt traffic will drop. The measure is filed, not enacted. Streets stay dangerous.

      Resolution 0419-2022, filed by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, urges the New York State Legislature to require a statewide ballot proposal before the Metropolitan Transportation Authority’s congestion pricing plan—known as the Central Business District Tolling Program—can proceed. Introduced December 7, 2022, and filed at session’s end, the resolution states: “calls upon the New York State Legislature to amend state law to make implementation of the Metropolitan Transportation Authority’s congestion pricing plan... subject to a statewide ballot proposal.” Council Member Kamillah Hanks led sponsorship, joined by Borelli, Carr, Louis, Yeger, Ariola, and Paladino. The sponsors raise concerns about pollution, health, and traffic impacts on outer-borough communities. The measure questions whether congestion pricing will cut traffic or fund transit improvements. No direct safety impact for vulnerable road users is assessed. The bill remains filed, with no further action.

      Int 0291-2022
      Lee votes yes, boosting citywide safety with new greenway plan.
      ▸

      The Council passed Int 0291-2022, forcing city agencies to map, plan, and report on greenways. The law demands annual updates and public engagement. It aims to carve out safe, car-free corridors for walkers and cyclists. The mayor returned it unsigned.

      Int 0291-2022, now Local Law 115 of 2022, was enacted by the City Council on November 27, 2022. The bill came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, first introduced April 28, 2022. The law's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to a citywide greenway plan.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers sponsored the bill, joined by dozens of co-sponsors including Rivera, Hudson, and others. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor on October 27, 2022. The law orders the Department of Transportation and Parks to identify, map, and report on greenways, and to consult with community boards. Proposals for new greenway segments or repairs must be presented to affected communities within 60 days. The mayor returned the bill unsigned, but it became law. The measure compels the city to plan and maintain safe routes for non-drivers, with regular public reporting and accountability.

      Int 0758-2022
      Lee co-sponsors e-bike licensing bill, likely reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council tried to force plates on e-bikes and scooters. The bill died in committee. Riders faced new rules, more scrutiny. The city’s cars kept killing. The council moved on. Vulnerable road users stayed exposed. The system failed to shift blame from drivers.

      Int 0758-2022 was introduced in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on October 12, 2022. The bill aimed to require every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter, and other legal motorized vehicle to be registered with the Department of Transportation and display a visible license plate. The streets stayed dangerous. The cars kept their edge.

      Res 0235-2022
      Lee co-sponsors MTA lead cleanup resolution, boosting street safety and public health.
      ▸

      Paint chips rain down from old elevated tracks. Lead coats the streets. The council calls on the MTA to clean up. Riders, walkers, and children breathe poison. The city waits. The bill is filed. The danger lingers.

      Res 0235-2022 was introduced on June 16, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The resolution calls on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to remediate any transportation structure, primarily elevated train lines, with extremely high levels of lead. Council Member Sandy Nurse led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Cabán, Hanif, Abreu, Louis, Holden, Gutiérrez, Joseph, Won, Brewer, and Lee. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. The resolution cites studies showing lead levels in paint chips from elevated tracks far above legal limits. The council demands action to protect New Yorkers from toxic exposure. The MTA, a state-run agency, holds the power to act. The bill’s filing leaves the hazard unresolved.

      Linda Lee Warns Against Harmful Speed Camera Measures
      ▸

      Council passed Albany’s speed camera expansion. Cameras now run all day, every day. Seven members opposed. Supporters cited lives lost when cameras slept. Opponents called it a tax. The vote followed a deadly year. Danger stalks city streets, especially near schools.

      On May 26, 2022, the New York City Council voted 43-7 to approve Albany’s extension and expansion of the city’s speed camera program. The measure, handled by the Transportation Committee, allows speed cameras to operate 24/7. The matter summary: 'The City Council approved Albany's extension and expansion of New York's speed camera program, allowing cameras to operate 24/7, but not before several council members voiced opposition.' Chairwoman Selvena Brooks-Powers urged action, saying, 'We must use all the tools at our disposal.' Council Members Joan Ariola, Vickie Paladino, and David Carr opposed, calling cameras a financial burden. Eric Dinowitz defended the program: 'If you don't want a speeding ticket, don't speed.' The vote came after a year of high road deaths, with special risk near schools.

      Int 0312-2022
      Lee co-sponsors bill raising fines for excessive vehicle noise.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to hike penalties for loud cars and trucks. The measure targeted blaring horns, illegal claxons, and roaring engines. It died at session’s end. No direct safety gains for people walking or biking. Noise remains. Streets stay loud.

      Int 0312-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety. The bill aimed to amend the city’s administrative code to increase penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles, including those under and over 10,000 pounds, and for unauthorized horns and claxons. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to increasing certain penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles.' Council Member Sandra Ung sponsored the bill, joined by 26 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. There is no evidence the bill would have improved safety for vulnerable road users. The city’s noise problem, and its threat to livable streets, remains unresolved.

      Int 0336-2022
      Lee co-sponsors bill shifting sidewalk repair to city, boosting pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0336-2022 would force the Parks Department to repair sidewalks wrecked by city trees. Homeowners escape blame. The city takes the hit. The bill died in committee. Broken sidewalks stay broken. Cyclists and pedestrians still trip.

      Int 0336-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to shift responsibility for sidewalk damage caused by city-owned trees from homeowners to the Parks Department. The matter title reads: 'requiring the parks department to repair damage caused by trees owned by the city of New York.' Council Member Kalman Yeger sponsored the bill, joined by Holden, Ung, Ayala, Restler, Dinowitz, Williams, Gutiérrez, Riley, Schulman, Brooks-Powers, Lee, Paladino, and Vernikov. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. If passed, it could have led to safer, smoother sidewalks for pedestrians and cyclists, removing a hazard that city government created and left to fester.
