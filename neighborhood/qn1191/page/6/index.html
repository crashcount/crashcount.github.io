      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Alley Pond Park?

      Alley Pond Park: 21 Kids Hurt, Zero Action

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      Alley Pond Park: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Toll in Alley Pond Park

      The crashes do not stop. In the last twelve months, 111 people were injured in 181 crashes in Alley Pond Park. Two suffered serious injuries. No one died, but the wounds run deep. Children are not spared—21 kids hurt in a single year. These are not just numbers. They are broken legs, shattered faces, and families waiting in hospital halls.

      SUVs hit hardest. In the past three years, SUVs caused three pedestrian injuries, including one serious. No bikes, no buses. Just heavy metal and flesh. The parkway is a river of steel. The young and the old are swept up the same.

      Leadership: Promises and Silence

      The city says it is acting. They point to new laws and cameras. “In order to make New York City the best place to raise a family, we need to be safer at every level — including on our streets,” said Mayor Adams. But the crashes keep coming. The city lowered speed limits in some places. Cameras catch speeders, but only where they are placed. The rest is luck.

      Police say they are watching. “NYPD officers will be highly visible on New York City roadways… to deter unsafe driving and, when necessary, to take appropriate enforcement action,” said Commissioner Caban. But in Alley Pond Park, the numbers do not move. The pain stays the same.

      The Work Left Undone

      No deaths this year. But the injuries pile up. The city claims progress, but the park is still a danger zone. The council and community board have not done enough. The roads are wide. The crossings are long. The cars are fast. The children are small.

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Demand lower speed limits and more cameras. Demand streets that protect walkers, not just drivers.

      Do not wait for the first death.

      Take action now.

      Int 0708-2022
      Lee votes yes to redesign truck routes, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to overhaul city truck routes. The bill orders daylighting at intersections and new signage. It aims to cut crashes, boost visibility, and shrink truck miles. Sponsors demand safer streets for people on foot and bike. Change is now law.

      Int 0708-2022, now Local Law 171 of 2023, was enacted by the City Council on December 16, 2023, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The law, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to redesigning the city truck route network," requires the Department of Transportation to redesign truck routes to "improve safety, increase visibility, reduce traffic congestion, and reduce vehicle miles traveled." Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by dozens of co-sponsors. The law mandates daylighting—removing parking near intersections—to clear sight lines for drivers and protect people crossing. DOT must consult with safety groups, residents, and businesses, and replace truck route signage for better clarity. The law sets deadlines: initial report by November 2023, implementation by June 2024, and new signage by July 2024. This overhaul targets the deadly risk trucks pose to vulnerable road users at city intersections.

      Int 0712-2022
      Lee votes yes on license plate reporting bill with no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to report cars with unreadable plates dodging cameras. The bill targets drivers hiding their plates from red light and speed cameras. Reports must show where, when, and how plates go unseen. Data goes public, every quarter.

      Int 0712-2022, now enacted as Local Law 155 of 2023, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and the full Council on October 19, 2023. The law, sponsored by Gale A. Brewer with co-sponsors including Erik D. The law’s title states it is 'in relation to reporting on motor vehicles with license plates that are unreadable by photo violation monitoring systems.' DOT must post quarterly reports showing the time, location, and reasons plates evade cameras—whether by concealment, distortion, or missing tags. The law aims to expose drivers who dodge automated enforcement, shining light on a loophole that puts pedestrians and cyclists at risk. The reports must be public and sent to the Mayor and Council Speaker.

      Int 1164-2023
      Lee votes yes, advancing safer, more equitable streets for vulnerable users.
      ▸

      Council passed Int 1164-2023. The law forces DOT to map out where street safety money goes. It tracks past investment, crash rates, and who gets left behind. The city must show its work, district by district. No more hiding the gaps.

      Int 1164-2023, now enacted, amends the city code to require an 'investment roadmap' in every Streets Master Plan starting December 1, 2026. The bill moved through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passing on October 19, 2023, and became law on November 19, 2023. The law states: 'The department shall prioritize and promote...the safety of all street users...and improving equity in infrastructure investment.' Sponsored by Kevin C. Riley (primary), with Brooks-Powers, Narcisse, Hanif, Brewer, Restler, and Rivera as co-sponsors, the bill demands DOT report, by neighborhood, on safety infrastructure investment, crash rates, and demographics. It shines a light on which districts get safety upgrades and which are left exposed. The roadmap aims to close the gap for underserved areas, forcing transparency and accountability for every dollar spent on street safety.

      Int 0906-2023
      Lee votes yes to move trucks off residential streets, boosting safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to force off-street parking for tractor trailers. No more big rigs clogging residential blocks. By 2025, each borough must have a site. The city must act or explain delays. Streets may breathe. Danger shifts from curbs.

      Int 0906-2023, now Local Law 142 of 2023, was enacted by the City Council on October 29, 2023, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill’s title: 'A Local Law in relation to parking for tractor trailers and other commercial trucks.' It requires the city to identify at least one feasible off-street parking location for tractor trailers in each borough by the end of 2023, and to establish those sites by December 31, 2025. No location can be on a residential street. Council Member Kamillah Hanks sponsored the bill, joined by Brannan, Riley, Dinowitz, Ung, Brooks-Powers, and others. The law aims to clear residential streets of oversized trucks, shifting their presence to designated lots. The council’s action targets the chronic hazard of trucks parked where people walk, bike, and live.

      Int 1033-2023
      Paladino co-sponsors bill that could slow street safety improvements citywide.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1033-2023 orders DOT to consult FDNY before approving open streets or bike lanes that cut car lanes or parking. Firehouses get advance notice. The bill aims to keep emergency routes clear but risks slowing safety projects for walkers and riders.

      Int 1033-2023, filed at session’s end in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced May 11, 2023, by Council Member Robert F. Holden with co-sponsors including Ariola, Riley, Yeger, Restler, Brooks-Powers, Hanks, Borelli, Carr, Kagan, Vernikov, and Paladino. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code... requiring the department of transportation to consult with the fire department prior to approving open street applications and certain bicycle lane projects and to notify affected firehouses...” It requires DOT to consult FDNY and notify firehouses before approving open streets, bike lanes, or major transportation projects that remove car or parking lanes. DOT must certify this consultation. The bill does not apply to emergency projects. While it aims to protect emergency access, advocates warn it could delay or block street redesigns that save lives for people walking and biking.
