      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square?

      Midtown Bleeds While Leaders Dither

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      Blood on the Streets

      A man’s leg crushed by a taxi. A cyclist thrown bleeding on the curb. A pedestrian’s life ended by a turning SUV. In Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, the violence is constant. In the last twelve months, 228 people were injured and 12 suffered serious injuries. One person died.

      The numbers do not flinch. Since 2022, there have been 681 injuries, 20 serious injuries, and 3 deaths in this district. Most of the wounded are not drivers. They are people on foot, on bikes, on mopeds. They are the ones who bleed first.

      The Human Toll

      A cyclist in his fifties, memory wiped, bones broken, after a driver ran a red light and fled. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” he said. He woke up in the ambulance. The driver was caught, but the street stayed the same.

      A woman, 24, lost her life at Union Square station. She tried to climb back to the platform. She could not. “Witnesses said the woman stepped onto the tracks shortly before the collision, but then attempted to climb back onto the platform,” reported West Side Spirit.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have talked of Vision Zero. They have backed more speed cameras, lower speed limits, and intersection redesigns. The city now has the power to set 20 mph limits under Sammy’s Law. But the pace is slow. Cameras need Albany’s blessing to keep running. Promised barriers in the subway have not arrived. The blood dries before the next press release.

      Who Gets Hurt, and Who Gets Heard

      Taxis, SUVs, trucks, bikes, mopeds—all have left people broken. But the deadliest wounds come from speed and inattention. Policies that blame the walker or the cyclist miss the point. The street is the weapon. The silence of leaders is the accomplice.

      Act Now: Demand Change

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras that never go dark. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name to become a number. Take action today.

      Bottcher co-sponsors dynamic parking zones bill, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0474-2024 would force DOT to set up demand-based parking zones in each borough. Rates rise or fall with congestion. Special permit holders dodge the fees. Sponsors say it targets traffic, but the city’s most vulnerable stay exposed.

      Bill Int 0474-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced February 28, 2024, it orders the Department of Transportation to create at least one dynamic parking zone per borough. Parking rates in these zones would shift with real-time demand. The bill’s summary reads: 'Dynamic-parking zones are areas of high traffic congestion where a demand-based parking fee would be implemented.' Council Members Julie Won (primary sponsor), Williams, Restler, Salaam, Bottcher, Riley, Brewer, Farías, and the Brooklyn Borough President back the measure. Vehicles with special permits remain exempt from these fees. The bill aims to cut congestion, but offers no direct safety gains for pedestrians or cyclists. Vulnerable road users still face the same risks from traffic and curbside chaos.

      Int 0080-2024
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by deterring hazardous vehicle obstruction.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants within half a mile of schools. Fines set at $175. Civilians can report violations. DOT must act on complaints. Council aims to clear paths for people, not cars.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after its introduction on February 8, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations," creates a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of a school. The penalty is $175 per violation. The Department of Transportation must launch a civilian reporting program. If DOT prosecutes a case using civilian evidence, the complainant gets 25% of the proceeds. Council Member Carlina Rivera leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Joseph, Menin, Nurse, Hudson, Brannan, Farías, Brewer, Salaam, Hanif, Avilés, Won, Bottcher, Krishnan, Gutiérrez, Marte, and the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill aims to keep streets clear for vulnerable road users, especially near schools.

      Bottcher Highlights Cars as Main Danger in Safety Campaign
      ▸

      Electeds launched 'Bike Safe' to urge cyclists to respect pedestrians. They named cars as the main killers. Delivery workers face pressure from apps. The campaign puts blame where it belongs: on reckless drivers and the systems that push workers to risk lives.

      On January 19, 2024, Senator Brad Hoylman-Sigal and Council Member Erik Bottcher launched the 'Bike Safe' public safety campaign in Council District 47. The campaign, titled 'Slow Your Roll, Respect The Stroll,' urges cyclists to stop at red lights and avoid sidewalks, but Bottcher stressed, 'the vast majority of deaths and injuries are caused by cars.' Bottcher and Hoylman-Sigal both called out reckless drivers as the real threat. Assembly Member Tony Simone joined, pushing for statewide laws to curb dangerous delivery app practices. Ligia Guallpa of the Workers Justice Project highlighted how apps force delivery workers into risky behavior, but noted that new minimum pay rates help slow things down. The campaign focuses on education, not punishment, and centers the voices of workers and vulnerable road users. No direct safety impact assessment was provided.
