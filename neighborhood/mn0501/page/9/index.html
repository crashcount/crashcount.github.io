      The Toll: Blood on the Asphalt

      One death. Twelve left with life-altering wounds. In the last year, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square saw 230 people injured and 12 seriously hurt in 350 crashes. One never made it home. These are not just numbers. They are bodies in crosswalks, bikes twisted on the curb, families waiting in hospital halls.

      A cyclist was hit at Park Avenue South and East 27th. He was 23. He died. The car turned left. The bike went straight. The street stayed the same. NYC Open Data

      On West 29th, a woman was killed walking. She was not in the roadway. The SUV made a left. She did not get up. The city moved on.

      Voices in the Noise: What the People Say

      The pain is not silent. After a hit-and-run in the West Village, the victim described the aftermath: “All my body was blood and the sweater.”

      A witness recalled, “The guy’s bike goes in one way. The guy flies in the other direction. It was pretty crazy.”

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      Local leaders talk of Vision Zero and safer streets. In the last three years, the city redesigned intersections and added protected bike lanes. The speed limit can now be set at 20 mph, thanks to Sammy’s Law. But the limit is not yet lowered. The cameras that catch speeders could go dark if Albany does not act. The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program lapsed. The city claims, “One life lost to traffic violence is one life too many,” but the streets tell another story.

      Community boards debate busways and car bans. Some call for more studies. Others demand action. The buses crawl. The cars do not wait.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day leaders delay, another person risks not coming home. Call your council member. Demand the 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras stay on. Join the fight. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. Take action now.
