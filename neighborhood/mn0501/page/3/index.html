      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square?

      Midtown Bleeds While Leaders Dither

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      Blood on the Streets

      A man’s leg crushed by a taxi. A cyclist thrown bleeding on the curb. A pedestrian’s life ended by a turning SUV. In Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, the violence is constant. In the last twelve months, 228 people were injured and 12 suffered serious injuries. One person died.

      The numbers do not flinch. Since 2022, there have been 681 injuries, 20 serious injuries, and 3 deaths in this district. Most of the wounded are not drivers. They are people on foot, on bikes, on mopeds. They are the ones who bleed first.

      The Human Toll

      A cyclist in his fifties, memory wiped, bones broken, after a driver ran a red light and fled. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” he said. He woke up in the ambulance. The driver was caught, but the street stayed the same.

      A woman, 24, lost her life at Union Square station. She tried to climb back to the platform. She could not. “Witnesses said the woman stepped onto the tracks shortly before the collision, but then attempted to climb back onto the platform,” reported West Side Spirit.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have talked of Vision Zero. They have backed more speed cameras, lower speed limits, and intersection redesigns. The city now has the power to set 20 mph limits under Sammy’s Law. But the pace is slow. Cameras need Albany’s blessing to keep running. Promised barriers in the subway have not arrived. The blood dries before the next press release.

      Who Gets Hurt, and Who Gets Heard

      Taxis, SUVs, trucks, bikes, mopeds—all have left people broken. But the deadliest wounds come from speed and inattention. Policies that blame the walker or the cyclist miss the point. The street is the weapon. The silence of leaders is the accomplice.

      Act Now: Demand Change

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras that never go dark. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name to become a number. Take action today.

      Bottcher Supports Safety Boosting Crackdown on Lingering Scaffolding
      ▸

      City Council and Mayor slash shed permits, force faster repairs. New laws demand brighter lights, stricter deadlines, and design upgrades. Council Members Powers and Bottcher drive the push. The city aims to reclaim sidewalks from decades of dark, lingering scaffolding.

      On April 18, 2025, the City Council passed and Mayor Eric Adams signed a five-bill package to overhaul sidewalk shed and scaffolding rules. The bills—Intro. 393-A, 391-A, 394-A (sponsored by Council Member Keith Powers), and Intro. 660-A, 661-A (sponsored by Council Member Erik Bottcher)—cut shed permit durations from one year to three months, double required lighting, and impose new penalties for delays. The matter summary states: 'New laws are set to finally tackle the scourge of unsightly sidewalk construction sheds and scaffolding structures around the city.' Powers said, 'This package of legislation puts forward reforms to fundamentally change our approach to scaffolding while ensuring safety is still the top priority.' Bottcher added, 'It’s time we cut back the endless sidewalk sheds that block our light.' The reforms target over 400 miles of lingering scaffolding, aiming to restore light and space to city sidewalks.

      Int 1233-2025
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill for vegetated bike lane medians, boosting cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council bill orders new medians between bike lanes and car traffic to hold trees and plants. Parks and Transportation must decide what’s possible, then plant. The law aims to shield cyclists, break up the street, and cool the city’s hard edges.

      Int 1233-2025, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on April 10, 2025. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the planting of vegetation on new medians separating bicycle lanes from motorized vehicle traffic.” Council Member Shekar Krishnan leads as primary sponsor, joined by Bottcher, Ossé, Brannan, Hanif, and Brooks-Powers. Their action: referral to committee. The bill requires the Department of Transportation to build new medians between bike lanes and car lanes that can support trees and other vegetation, subject to feasibility. Parks and Recreation must plant or allow planting in these medians. The move promises a harder barrier between cyclists and cars, with green infrastructure as a shield.

      Assembly bill A 6225 was introduced on February 28, 2025. It sits in the sponsorship stage.
