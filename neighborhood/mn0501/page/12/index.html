      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square?

      Midtown Bleeds While Leaders Dither

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      Blood on the Streets

      A man’s leg crushed by a taxi. A cyclist thrown bleeding on the curb. A pedestrian’s life ended by a turning SUV. In Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, the violence is constant. In the last twelve months, 228 people were injured and 12 suffered serious injuries. One person died.

      The numbers do not flinch. Since 2022, there have been 681 injuries, 20 serious injuries, and 3 deaths in this district. Most of the wounded are not drivers. They are people on foot, on bikes, on mopeds. They are the ones who bleed first.

      The Human Toll

      A cyclist in his fifties, memory wiped, bones broken, after a driver ran a red light and fled. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” he said. He woke up in the ambulance. The driver was caught, but the street stayed the same.

      A woman, 24, lost her life at Union Square station. She tried to climb back to the platform. She could not. “Witnesses said the woman stepped onto the tracks shortly before the collision, but then attempted to climb back onto the platform,” reported West Side Spirit.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have talked of Vision Zero. They have backed more speed cameras, lower speed limits, and intersection redesigns. The city now has the power to set 20 mph limits under Sammy’s Law. But the pace is slow. Cameras need Albany’s blessing to keep running. Promised barriers in the subway have not arrived. The blood dries before the next press release.

      Who Gets Hurt, and Who Gets Heard

      Taxis, SUVs, trucks, bikes, mopeds—all have left people broken. But the deadliest wounds come from speed and inattention. Policies that blame the walker or the cyclist miss the point. The street is the weapon. The silence of leaders is the accomplice.

      Act Now: Demand Change

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras that never go dark. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name to become a number. Take action today.

      Bottcher Supports Safety Boosting Bill Against Cruise Ship Pollution
      ▸

      Cruise ships choke Red Hook. Streets clog with cars and trucks. Buses stall. Residents gasp for air. Council Members Avilés and Bottcher push a bill. They demand shore power and traffic plans. The city drags its feet. Danger grows for all outside a car.

      On September 21, 2023, Council Members Alexa Avilés and Erik Bottcher introduced a bill targeting cruise ship traffic and pollution in Red Hook. The proposal, discussed at a City Council press conference, would require the Economic Development Corporation (EDC) to mandate shore power use and enforce traffic mitigation plans for cruise ships. Avilés declared, 'Our residents are suffocating and the city must act.' The bill responds to chaos caused by ships like the MSC Meraviglia, which brings thousands of passengers and vehicles, blocking streets and the B61 bus. Unlike other lines, MSC offers no shuttle, forcing more cars onto local roads. The EDC claims traffic has improved, but local counts and residents say otherwise. Businesses see little benefit. The bill aims to cut pollution and congestion, but the city has yet to act.

      Bottcher Compares Cruise Ship Emissions to Idling Trucks
      ▸

      Councilmembers Avilés and Bottcher want cruise ships to plug into shore power, not spew fumes. Their bill targets pollution choking Red Hook and Manhattan. Residents breathe the cost. The city’s cruise deal brings sickness, not safety. Lawmakers demand action.

      Intro 1050, introduced September 19, 2023, by Councilmembers Alexa Avilés (District 38) and Erik Bottcher, aims to force cruise ships at NYC ports to connect to shore power instead of burning fossil fuels. The bill, now before the City Council, would require the Economic Development Corporation to tie port access to shore power use and to manage cruise-related traffic. The matter summary reads: 'require cruise ships docking at New York City ports to connect to the shore's energy grid, rather than using fossil fuels, in order to reduce emissions.' Avilés declared, 'No cruise company should be allowed in our city unless they respect the communities and quality of life of our residents.' Bottcher compared ship emissions to 34,000 idling trucks. Residents, including Hart Valentine, blame cruise pollution for asthma and ruined lives. The bill responds to years of extractive deals that left neighborhoods sick and ignored.

      Int 1151-2023
      Bottcher co-sponsors solar crosswalk bill, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council pushed for 500 solar-lit crosswalks. The bill demanded action—100 new devices each year. It called for a hard look at results. But the session ended. The bill died. Streets wait. Pedestrians keep crossing in the dark.

      Int 1151-2023, introduced August 3, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to require the Department of Transportation to install at least 100 illuminated, solar-powered traffic control devices at crosswalks each year for five years—a total of 500. The bill also ordered a study comparing these devices to unlit signs, probing their power to deter traffic violations and mapping out logistical hurdles. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the installation of solar-powered crosswalks.' Council Member Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Menin, Brooks-Powers, Rivera, and over thirty others. The bill was filed at session’s end on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The city’s crosswalks remain unchanged. Vulnerable road users remain exposed.

      Krueger Supports Misguided EV Discount Undermining Street Safety
      ▸

      Manhattan lawmakers want electric cars to pay less under congestion pricing. They argue EVs cut smog, so drivers deserve a break. Critics warn this move keeps streets clogged. Fewer cars mean fewer crashes. The fight pits clean air against crowded roads.

      On July 31, 2023, a group of Manhattan elected officials—including Assembly Members Alex Bores, Eddie Gibbs, Deborah Glick, Harvey Epstein, Rebecca Seawright; State Senators Brad Hoylman-Sigal, Liz Krueger, Kristen Gonzalez; and Borough President Mark Levine—sent a letter to the Traffic Mobility Review Board. They urged a congestion pricing discount for electric vehicles, claiming EVs 'do not contribute significantly to the smog and pollution of the Central Business District.' Assembly Member Bores led the effort, stating, 'when it comes to the environmental case, an electric vehicle is just a different profile than a gas-guzzling one.' The officials oppose credits for bridge and tunnel crossings. Congestion pricing advocates and environmental groups counter that EV discounts undermine the core goal: fewer cars, less traffic, safer streets. They note other cities are ending such breaks. The proposal highlights a tension—cleaner air versus safer, less crowded roads for all.

      Int 1115-2023
      Bottcher sponsors bill boosting alternate side parking enforcement, improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1115-2023 targets repeat alternate side parking violators. First offense: $65. More violations: $100 each. Three strikes in a year, and the car gets towed. Filed at session’s end. Bottcher and Restler sponsor. No direct safety impact noted.

      Int 1115-2023 was introduced on June 22, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill sought to amend city code by setting penalties for alternate side parking violations at $65 for a first offense and $100 for each subsequent offense within a 12-month period. After three violations in a year, the vehicle would be towed if found in violation again. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to establishing penalties for alternate side parking violations and requiring towing of certain vehicles in violation of alternate side parking rules.' Council Members Erik D. Bottcher (primary sponsor) and Lincoln Restler (co-sponsor) led the effort. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst assessment was provided, and no direct impact on vulnerable road users was noted.
