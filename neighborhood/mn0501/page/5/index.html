      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square?

      Midtown Bleeds While Leaders Dither

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      Blood on the Streets

      A man’s leg crushed by a taxi. A cyclist thrown bleeding on the curb. A pedestrian’s life ended by a turning SUV. In Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, the violence is constant. In the last twelve months, 228 people were injured and 12 suffered serious injuries. One person died.

      The numbers do not flinch. Since 2022, there have been 681 injuries, 20 serious injuries, and 3 deaths in this district. Most of the wounded are not drivers. They are people on foot, on bikes, on mopeds. They are the ones who bleed first.

      The Human Toll

      A cyclist in his fifties, memory wiped, bones broken, after a driver ran a red light and fled. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” he said. He woke up in the ambulance. The driver was caught, but the street stayed the same.

      A woman, 24, lost her life at Union Square station. She tried to climb back to the platform. She could not. “Witnesses said the woman stepped onto the tracks shortly before the collision, but then attempted to climb back onto the platform,” reported West Side Spirit.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have talked of Vision Zero. They have backed more speed cameras, lower speed limits, and intersection redesigns. The city now has the power to set 20 mph limits under Sammy’s Law. But the pace is slow. Cameras need Albany’s blessing to keep running. Promised barriers in the subway have not arrived. The blood dries before the next press release.

      Who Gets Hurt, and Who Gets Heard

      Taxis, SUVs, trucks, bikes, mopeds—all have left people broken. But the deadliest wounds come from speed and inattention. Policies that blame the walker or the cyclist miss the point. The street is the weapon. The silence of leaders is the accomplice.

      Act Now: Demand Change

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras that never go dark. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name to become a number. Take action today.

      Int 0746-2024
      Bottcher votes yes, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety with greener medians.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to turn bare medians green. The city must plant trees or use medians for stormwater. Agencies must report progress. The law targets high-risk areas. Votes showed strong support. The mayor returned it unsigned. Streets will change.

      Int 0746-2024, now Local Law 94 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 12, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the improvement and maintenance of medians through the planting of vegetation or use in stormwater management," requires the city to improve at least one mile of paved medians every two years until 2046. The Department of Transportation, Parks, and Environmental Protection must add planted medians, tree beds, or stormwater infrastructure, focusing on high-priority investment areas. Council Member Shekar Krishnan sponsored the bill, joined by Feliz, Avilés, Nurse, Banks, Restler, Ung, Schulman, Holden, Hudson, and Yeger. The Council passed the bill with broad support, though the mayor returned it unsigned. The law mandates annual progress reports and public tracking of improved medians and responsible agencies.

      Bottcher Supports Safety Boosting Plaza33 Pedestrian Transformation

      City leaders cut the ribbon on Plaza33. Once a car-choked block, now a pedestrian haven. Trees, tables, and open space replace exhaust and noise. Years of work. A new chapter for Midtown. People walk, sit, and breathe. Cars banished. Safety reclaimed.

      On June 27, 2024, city officials and civic leaders marked the completion of Plaza33, a pedestrian plaza on 33rd Street by Penn Station. The event capped years of transformation, turning a block once 'full of cars and trucks' into a car-free space. Manhattan Borough President Mark Levine, Assembly Member Tony Simone, and Council Member Erik Bottcher attended the ribbon-cutting. Levine recalled, 'I remember the battle over transforming this space and all the people who said that it would clog up traffic all over Midtown if we turned this street over to people and here we are and it's gorgeous.' Vornado Realty Trust funded the $65-million project with the Department of Transportation. The plaza, now permanent, offers 16,000 square feet for pedestrians, with trees, seating, and public programming. The change removes cars from a busy Midtown block, giving vulnerable road users safe, open ground.
