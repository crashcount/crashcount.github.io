      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square?

      Midtown Bleeds While Leaders Dither

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 18, 2025

      Blood on the Streets

      A man’s leg crushed by a taxi. A cyclist thrown bleeding on the curb. A pedestrian’s life ended by a turning SUV. In Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, the violence is constant. In the last twelve months, 228 people were injured and 12 suffered serious injuries. One person died.

      The numbers do not flinch. Since 2022, there have been 681 injuries, 20 serious injuries, and 3 deaths in this district. Most of the wounded are not drivers. They are people on foot, on bikes, on mopeds. They are the ones who bleed first.

      The Human Toll

      A cyclist in his fifties, memory wiped, bones broken, after a driver ran a red light and fled. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” he said. He woke up in the ambulance. The driver was caught, but the street stayed the same.

      A woman, 24, lost her life at Union Square station. She tried to climb back to the platform. She could not. “Witnesses said the woman stepped onto the tracks shortly before the collision, but then attempted to climb back onto the platform,” reported West Side Spirit.

      What Leaders Have Done—and Not Done

      Local leaders have talked of Vision Zero. They have backed more speed cameras, lower speed limits, and intersection redesigns. The city now has the power to set 20 mph limits under Sammy’s Law. But the pace is slow. Cameras need Albany’s blessing to keep running. Promised barriers in the subway have not arrived. The blood dries before the next press release.

      Who Gets Hurt, and Who Gets Heard

      Taxis, SUVs, trucks, bikes, mopeds—all have left people broken. But the deadliest wounds come from speed and inattention. Policies that blame the walker or the cyclist miss the point. The street is the weapon. The silence of leaders is the accomplice.

      Act Now: Demand Change

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras that never go dark. Demand streets that do not kill.

      Do not wait for another name to become a number. Take action today.

      A 324
      Simone co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring complete street design.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 324 demands every state- or federally-funded road project weigh complete street design. The bill pushes agencies to publish clear guidance. Streets built for cars alone kill. This bill aims to force change at the blueprint.

      Assembly Bill 324 was introduced on January 1, 2025, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill sits in the New York State Assembly, with Didi Barrett as the primary sponsor and a long list of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Amy Paulin, and others. The bill summary reads: 'Requires the consideration of complete street design for certain transportation projects which receive federal or state funding; authorizes the department to compile and make publicly available guidance and reference material related to consideration of complete street design.' This measure would require state and federally funded transportation projects to consider designs that protect all road users, not just drivers. The bill’s broad sponsorship signals growing support for safer, more inclusive streets. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Int 1138-2024
      Bottcher sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1138-2024 would ban parking and standing within 20 feet of crosswalks. It forces the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections each year. The bill sits in committee. Sponsors include Bottcher, Won, and the Public Advocate.

      Int 1138-2024, introduced December 5, 2024, is under review by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill is 'Laid Over in Committee.' Its title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.' Council Member Erik D. Bottcher is the primary sponsor, joined by Julie Won, Jumaane Williams, and others. The bill prohibits standing or parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and mandates the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections per year. The law also requires citywide outreach and reporting. This measure aims to keep sightlines clear at crossings, a known danger zone for people on foot and bike. The bill has not yet received a vote.

      ▸

      On November 18, 2024, the City Council approved a bill regulating sidewalk newsracks. The measure, sponsored by Council Member Erik Bottcher (District 3), passed through committee and aims to address neglected, broken, and obstructive newspaper boxes.
