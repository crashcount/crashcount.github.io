      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Astoria (East)-Woodside (North)?

      Blood on the Crosswalk: How Many More Before They Act?

      Written June 25, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      Astoria (East)-Woodside (North): Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Toll: Flesh and Bone on Asphalt

      A cyclist leaves a ride and never comes home. Amanda Servedio, 36, was hit and killed at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. The truck that struck her was fleeing police. She “went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. Her father spoke for every parent who has waited for justice: “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood, and we were very upset that that’s what actually happened” he told the Daily News.

      In the last twelve months, 154 people were injured and 3 suffered serious injuries in this district. One person died. The numbers are not just numbers. They are broken legs, split skulls, and families left with silence.

      Children and Sidewalks: No Safe Harbor

      A 7-year-old girl, a 14-year-old, and a man were hit outside a school on 35th Avenue. The driver had no license. The younger girl left with a broken femur and a head injury. The car did not care about the school bell or the crossing guard. The street did not forgive. Police charged the driver with reckless endangerment and reckless driving.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Local leaders have not been silent. Council Member Tiffany Cabán has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and legalize jaywalking—measures that put people before cars. State Senator Michael Gianaris voted for “complete streets” design and called for stronger penalties for repeat reckless drivers. But the pace is slow. The streets do not wait.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand Now

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every injury, every death, is a choice made by those in power or those who look away. Contact your council member, your senator, your assembly member. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted crosswalks, and real enforcement against reckless drivers.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Int 1071-2023
      Cabán sponsors bill speeding up removal of hazardous utility poles, boosting safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force quick removal of abandoned or dangerous utility poles and wires. Owners would have sixty days to clear junk, or act at once if hazards loom. The bill died in committee. Streets stay cluttered. Danger lingers.

      Int 1071-2023 was introduced on June 8, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to require owners to remove disused or abandoned utility poles, wires, and appurtenances within 60 days, and to act immediately if conditions were dangerous. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the timelines for the removal of abandoned or unsafe utility poles, wires, and appurtenances, and the transfer of appurtenances to newly erected poles.' Council Member Tiffany Cabán sponsored the bill, joined by Kevin C. Riley, Jennifer Gutiérrez, Lincoln Restler, Kristin Richardson Jordan, Amanda Farías, and Robert F. Holden. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The bill’s failure leaves hazardous poles and wires on city streets, a threat to those on foot and bike.

      Gianaris Backs Misguided Residential Parking Permit Proposal
      ▸

      Albany’s push for residential parking permits risks locking in car dominance. Critics say low fees fuel car ownership, crowding out transit, bikes, and green space. Experts warn it’s a subsidy for drivers, not a fix for congestion or emissions.

      On March 16, 2023, a Senate proposal surfaced to create a Residential Parking Permit system in New York City. The plan, now under City Council review, would charge drivers up to $30 per month for curbside parking, with revenue aimed at funding the MTA. The proposal leaves key details—like reserving 20% of spaces for non-residents—to the Council. The bill’s summary claims it will reduce emissions and support transit. Senator Michael Gianaris supports the measure, but critics are blunt. Bike New York’s Jon Orcutt calls it a 'big nothingburger.' Donald Shoup says the $30 fee is a subsidy for cars. Elizabeth Adams of Transportation Alternatives argues, 'public space should be used for transit, bike lanes, and green space, not car storage.' Studies show similar programs in other cities have failed to cut congestion or emissions, and may even increase car ownership. The Department of Transportation has testified against the plan, warning it entrenches private car storage at the expense of vulnerable road users.
