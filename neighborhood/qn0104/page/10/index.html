      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in Astoria (East)-Woodside (North)?

      No More Broken Kids: Streets Are Killing, Leaders Are Stalling

      Written June 25, 2025 (Revised June 24, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, improved citation formatting, and cleaned citations.)

      Astoria (East)-Woodside (North): Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 25, 2025

      The Toll: Broken Bodies, Shattered Lives

      In Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), the violence comes steady. One person killed. Six left with serious injuries. Over 500 hurt since 2022. The numbers are cold, but the pain is not. A 7-year-old girl, her femur snapped and head bleeding, after a car jumped the curb outside her school. A 14-year-old girl and a man, both struck on the same sidewalk. The driver had no license. She was charged with reckless endangerment and reckless driving. Police said she lost control. The street did not forgive.

      A cyclist, Amanda Servedio, was hit and killed at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. She was coming home from a ride. The driver was fleeing police, running red lights, barreling through a neighborhood where children walk and play. “She went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said a witness. The chase lasted ten minutes. The street was crowded. The cost was a life.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      Local leaders have not been silent. Council Member Tiffany Cabán has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, legalize jaywalking, and speed up protected bike lanes. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act and pushed for daylighting and better lighting at crossings. State Senator Michael Gianaris voted for complete street design and called for stronger penalties for repeat reckless drivers. But laws move slow. Promises pile up. The streets stay the same.

      When the city delayed opening a safe path on the Queensboro Bridge, Gianaris said the wait dragged on: “After years of advocacy, many of us were excited to see [the city’s Department of Transportation] complete the work to open new, dedicated pedestrian paths on the Queensboro Bridge.” Cyclists and walkers kept sharing a narrow, dangerous lane.

      The Pattern: Cars Rule, Kids Pay

      Most injuries come from cars and trucks. SUVs, sedans, and pickups do the damage. The victims are young and old. The sidewalk is not safe. The crosswalk is not safe. The bike lane is not safe. The law is slow. The car is fast.

      What Now: Demand More Than Words

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call your state senator. Tell them the numbers. Tell them the names. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted corners, and real penalties for reckless drivers. Do not wait for another child to bleed on the sidewalk.

      Take action now.

      Int 0291-2022
      Cabán votes yes, boosting citywide safety with new greenway plan.
      ▸

      The Council passed Int 0291-2022, forcing city agencies to map, plan, and report on greenways. The law demands annual updates and public engagement. It aims to carve out safe, car-free corridors for walkers and cyclists. The mayor returned it unsigned.

      Int 0291-2022, now Local Law 115 of 2022, was enacted by the City Council on November 27, 2022. The bill came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, first introduced April 28, 2022. The law's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to a citywide greenway plan.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers sponsored the bill, joined by dozens of co-sponsors including Rivera, Hudson, and others. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor on October 27, 2022. The law orders the Department of Transportation and Parks to identify, map, and report on greenways, and to consult with community boards. Proposals for new greenway segments or repairs must be presented to affected communities within 60 days. The mayor returned the bill unsigned, but it became law. The measure compels the city to plan and maintain safe routes for non-drivers, with regular public reporting and accountability.

      Int 0578-2022
      Cabán co-sponsors bill boosting pedestrian crossing safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force DOT to study deadly intersections and add signals that give walkers a head start or full crossing time. The bill died at session’s end. Streets stay risky. Pedestrians wait for action.

      Bill Int 0578-2022, introduced July 14, 2022, aimed to amend city law to improve safety at pedestrian crossings. The Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure reviewed it, but the bill was filed without passage at the end of session on December 31, 2023. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to improving safety at pedestrian crossings in the city.' Council Members Robert F. Holden (primary sponsor), Ari Kagan, Justin L. Brannan, Jennifer Gutiérrez, Sandy Nurse, Kalman Yeger, Rita C. Joseph, and Tiffany Cabán sponsored the bill. It would have required DOT to audit the city’s deadliest intersections and consider installing leading pedestrian intervals and exclusive pedestrian phases—signals proven to protect people on foot. The bill’s failure leaves dangerous crossings unchanged.
