      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in College Point?

      College Point Bleeds While Leaders Stall—Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      College Point: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Toll in Flesh and Bone

      In College Point, the numbers do not bleed, but people do. Four dead. Nine left with serious injuries. In three and a half years, 940 crashes. 419 hurt.

      A 58-year-old man, struck and killed while walking. A 62-year-old cyclist, dead on College Point Boulevard. A 43-year-old man, thrown from his car and left to die on the Whitestone Expressway. The driver who hit him ran. Police hunted him for a year. His sister waited. When the arrest came, she expressed relief at the arrest after 12 months.

      No child should have to cross a street in fear. No family should wait a year for justice.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. In the last year alone, 140 injured, one seriously. The dead are not numbers. They are fathers, sisters, sons. The street does not care if you are young or old. A sedan, an SUV, a truck—they all hit the same. Cars and trucks caused every pedestrian death and injury here.

      What Has Changed? What Hasn’t?

      Local leaders talk of Vision Zero. They tout new laws, lower speed limits, more cameras. But the blood dries slow. No public statement from local council or board after the last deaths. No new protected bike lanes. No redesigns for the deadliest crossings. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. They have not used it here.

      What Next? Who Will Act?

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by someone in power. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph limit. Demand real protection for people who walk and bike.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Int 0312-2022
      Paladino co-sponsors bill raising fines for excessive vehicle noise.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to hike penalties for loud cars and trucks. The measure targeted blaring horns, illegal claxons, and roaring engines. It died at session’s end. No direct safety gains for people walking or biking. Noise remains. Streets stay loud.

      Int 0312-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Public Safety. The bill aimed to amend the city’s administrative code to increase penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles, including those under and over 10,000 pounds, and for unauthorized horns and claxons. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to increasing certain penalties for excessive noise from motor vehicles.' Council Member Sandra Ung sponsored the bill, joined by 26 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of the session on December 31, 2023, and did not advance. There is no evidence the bill would have improved safety for vulnerable road users. The city’s noise problem, and its threat to livable streets, remains unresolved.

      Int 0336-2022
      Paladino co-sponsors bill shifting sidewalk repair to city, boosting pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0336-2022 would force the Parks Department to repair sidewalks wrecked by city trees. Homeowners escape blame. The city takes the hit. The bill died in committee. Broken sidewalks stay broken. Cyclists and pedestrians still trip.

      Int 0336-2022 was introduced on May 5, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill aimed to shift responsibility for sidewalk damage caused by city-owned trees from homeowners to the Parks Department. The matter title reads: 'requiring the parks department to repair damage caused by trees owned by the city of New York.' Council Member Kalman Yeger sponsored the bill, joined by Holden, Ung, Ayala, Restler, Dinowitz, Williams, Gutiérrez, Riley, Schulman, Brooks-Powers, Lee, Paladino, and Vernikov. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. If passed, it could have led to safer, smoother sidewalks for pedestrians and cyclists, removing a hazard that city government created and left to fester.

      Int 0053-2022
      Paladino co-sponsors bill restricting overnight commercial parking, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council tried to ban commercial trucks from sleeping on quiet streets. The bill died. Utility and oil trucks kept their curb. Pedestrians and families still dodge hulking vehicles at dawn. The city blinked. The danger stayed.

      Int 0053-2022, introduced on February 24, 2022, sought to amend city law to bar commercial vehicles from parking overnight on residential streets, between 9 p.m. and 5 a.m. The Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure handled the bill. The measure removed exemptions for gas, oil, and utility trucks. The matter summary reads: 'This bill would prohibit the parking of commercial vehicles owned or operated by gas or oil heat suppliers, gas or oil heat systems maintenance companies or any public utility in residential streets overnight.' Council Members Kalman Yeger (primary sponsor), Robert F. Holden, Vickie Paladino, and Kevin C. Riley backed the bill. The council filed it at session’s end, on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst weighed in, but the bill’s failure means residential streets remain lined with large trucks, leaving vulnerable road users exposed.
