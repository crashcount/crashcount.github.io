      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in East Flatbush-Erasmus?

      East Flatbush Bleeds While City Stalls: Demand 20 MPH Now

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      East Flatbush-Erasmus: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Numbers Don’t Lie: Broken Bodies, Empty Promises

      In East Flatbush-Erasmus, the street is a gauntlet. Since 2022, two people have died and 832 have been injured in 1,338 crashes. Seven suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In the last year alone, 232 people were hurt. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. The numbers keep coming. The pain does not stop.

      SUVs and sedans hit hardest. Trucks killed. Bikes and mopeds left riders bleeding. The street does not care who you are. It only takes.

      The Human Cost: Names Lost, Families Shattered

      A woman steps out of her car after a crash. She is run down and dragged. The driver flees. Her family waits for news that will never be good. Police say a maroon Chevy hit the victim’s car from behind, and when she got out to approach the vehicle she was run over and dragged.

      A pregnant woman is killed in a hit-and-run. Her child dies with her. The driver does not stop. A woman killed in a hit-and-run in Brooklyn on Saturday night was pregnant, her family says.

      A school bus jumps the curb. An eight-year-old boy’s arm breaks. His mother’s neck and hip are hurt. They survive, but the street leaves its mark. No charges were immediately filed.

      Leadership: Barriers, Promises, and the Waiting

      Local leaders talk of action. The city promises new intersection designs—granite blocks, planters, barriers to keep corners clear. “Redesigning our streets under Vision Zero has helped us reduce traffic deaths to historic lows but we are always looking for new ways to keep people safe,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. The plans are real. The rollout is slow. The dead do not wait.

      The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not used it. Speed cameras save lives, but Albany lets the law hang by a thread. Each day of delay is a risk paid in blood.

      What Now: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand intersection redesigns now, not next year. Demand that speed cameras stay on. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Take action now.

      Myrie Supports Safety Boosting Universal Daylighting with Barriers
      ▸

      Seven Brooklyn officials urge DOT to clear cars from corners. They want boulders, planters, and bike corrals—not just paint. Their call follows deadly crashes. They press the city to use state law and federal funds. DOT promises review. Advocates back the push.

      On January 17, 2024, seven Brooklyn elected officials—including Council Member Shahana Hanif, Borough President Antonio Reynoso, Assembly Members Marcela Mitaynes, Jo Anne Simon, Robert Carroll, and State Senators Zellnor Myrie and Andrew Gounardes—issued a joint letter to the NYC Department of Transportation. They called for 'universal daylighting with hardened materials such as boulders, planters, and bike corrals' at intersections. The officials cited recent fatal crashes and urged the city to opt into a state law banning parking within 20 feet of corners. They want federal funds used for these changes. The group opposes DOT’s slow pace and reliance on paint, demanding physical barriers. Community board leaders and advocates support the move. DOT says it will review the letter and remains committed to evidence-based daylighting.

      Int 0781-2022
      Joseph votes yes on vehicle stop reporting bill, safety impact neutral.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing NYPD to spell out why they stop cars. Each stop now gets logged with the reason, time, and outcome. No names. Just facts. The city posts these reports online. A move for sunlight. No more secrets.

      Int 0781-2022, now law, came through the Committee on Public Safety. The Council passed it on January 20, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the police department to report on the justification for the stop in vehicle encounter reports," demands the NYPD document every vehicle stop. Reports must include the reason for the stop, the level of offense, and demographic details, but no personal identifiers. Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Lincoln Restler (primary sponsor), Amanda Farías, and others led the charge. The law aims to expose patterns in police stops and holds officers to account. Reports go online, open to all. This is a step toward transparency, not just for drivers, but for every New Yorker who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.
