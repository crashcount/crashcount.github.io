      East Flatbush Bleeds While City Stalls: Demand 20 MPH Now

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      East Flatbush-Erasmus: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Numbers Don’t Lie: Broken Bodies, Empty Promises

      In East Flatbush-Erasmus, the street is a gauntlet. Since 2022, two people have died and 832 have been injured in 1,338 crashes. Seven suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In the last year alone, 232 people were hurt. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. The numbers keep coming. The pain does not stop.

      SUVs and sedans hit hardest. Trucks killed. Bikes and mopeds left riders bleeding. The street does not care who you are. It only takes.

      The Human Cost: Names Lost, Families Shattered

      A woman steps out of her car after a crash. She is run down and dragged. The driver flees. Her family waits for news that will never be good. Police say a maroon Chevy hit the victim’s car from behind, and when she got out to approach the vehicle she was run over and dragged.

      A pregnant woman is killed in a hit-and-run. Her child dies with her. The driver does not stop. A woman killed in a hit-and-run in Brooklyn on Saturday night was pregnant, her family says.

      A school bus jumps the curb. An eight-year-old boy’s arm breaks. His mother’s neck and hip are hurt. They survive, but the street leaves its mark. No charges were immediately filed.

      Leadership: Barriers, Promises, and the Waiting

      Local leaders talk of action. The city promises new intersection designs—granite blocks, planters, barriers to keep corners clear. “Redesigning our streets under Vision Zero has helped us reduce traffic deaths to historic lows but we are always looking for new ways to keep people safe,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. The plans are real. The rollout is slow. The dead do not wait.

      The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not used it. Speed cameras save lives, but Albany lets the law hang by a thread. Each day of delay is a risk paid in blood.

      What Now: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand intersection redesigns now, not next year. Demand that speed cameras stay on. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Take action now.

      Int 0758-2022
      Louis co-sponsors e-bike licensing bill, likely reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council tried to force plates on e-bikes and scooters. The bill died in committee. Riders faced new rules, more scrutiny. The city’s cars kept killing. The council moved on. Vulnerable road users stayed exposed. The system failed to shift blame from drivers.

      Int 0758-2022 was introduced in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on October 12, 2022. The bill aimed to require every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter, and other legal motorized vehicle to be registered with the Department of Transportation and display a visible license plate. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered.' Council Member Inna Vernikov sponsored the bill, joined by Robert F. Holden and 29 co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, without a vote. No safety analyst assessed the impact on vulnerable road users. The bill’s failure left the city’s most exposed—cyclists, pedestrians, passengers—without new protections. The streets stayed dangerous. The cars kept their edge.

      Int 0662-2022
      Joseph co-sponsors bill to create parking permit enforcement, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to create a parking permit enforcement unit in DOT. The unit would target misuse of city-issued permits. The measure died at session’s end. Streets stay clogged. Danger lingers for those on foot and bike.

      Int 0662-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on September 14, 2022. The bill sought to amend city law by establishing a parking permit enforcement unit within the Department of Transportation. Its summary reads: 'This bill would require the Department of Transportation to create a parking permit enforcement unit that would be dedicated to the enforcement of laws and rules relating to misuse of city-issued parking permits.' The primary sponsor was Kristin Richardson Jordan, joined by Amanda Farías, Selvena N. Brooks-Powers, Julie Won, Rita C. Joseph, Crystal Hudson, Farah N. Louis, and Lincoln Restler. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, with no further action. No safety analyst assessed its impact on vulnerable road users. The city’s permit abuse problem remains unaddressed.

      Res 0308-2022
      Joseph co-sponsors federal helicopter noise limits, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council members push Congress to curb helicopter tours. The resolution demands limits on low, loud flights over New York. Helicopter noise and risk plague city streets. The act would set stricter rules, aiming to shield millions from harm and stress.

      Res 0308-2022, filed by the Committee on State and Federal Legislation, urges Congress and the president to pass H.R. 389, the Safe and Quiet Skies Act of 2021. Introduced on September 14, 2022, and filed at session's end on December 31, 2023, the resolution states: 'calls on the United States Congress to pass, and the president to sign, H.R. 389.' Council Members Shahana K. Hanif (primary sponsor), Gale A. Brewer, Crystal Hudson, and Rita C. Joseph co-sponsored the measure. The act would ban commercial helicopter tours below 1,500 feet, cap noise at 55 decibels, and let local governments set even tougher rules. The council’s action responds to a surge in helicopter noise complaints and the dangers posed by low-flying tours over dense city neighborhoods.
