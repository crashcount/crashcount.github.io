      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 42?

      Flatbush to Midwood: Cross at Your Own Risk

      Written August 24, 2025 (Revised August 24, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 42: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 24, 2025

      Another driver. Same ending.

      • A woman, 74, crossed with the signal at Avenue D and New York Avenue. A 2023 Honda sedan turned left and hit her. She died. Police coded it as failure to yield. NYC Open Data crash 4717810.
      • A dump truck turned right at Tilden and Bedford and struck a 58‑year‑old woman who had the signal. She died. The record lists “Failure to Yield” and “Oversized Vehicle.” NYC Open Data crash 4707890.
      • An Audi sedan on Flatbush near Caton. A 64‑year‑old pedestrian. Apparent death. NYC Open Data crash 4783016.

      On Ditmas and E. 21st, a 50‑year‑old woman died after an SUV hit her in the crosswalk. NYC Open Data crash 4606531.

      A school bus took the corner at Bedford and Farragut, jumped the curb, and broke an 8‑year‑old boy’s arm. His mother went to the hospital. The driver said he “hit the gas instead of the brakes.” ABC7. A councilmember said, “We do know we need more traffic safety in this area.” CBS New York.

      Three corners. One fix.

      Flatbush Avenue leads the harm: 1 death, 143 injuries, 6 serious. NYC Open Data. Nostrand Avenue adds 82 injuries; Foster Avenue adds 32 injuries and a death; New York Avenue, 44 injuries and two deaths; Tilden Avenue, 14 injuries and five serious. NYC Open Data.

      DOT says nearly half of city traffic deaths happen in intersections. The agency will harden corners with concrete, granite blocks, and planters and start at high‑crash Brooklyn sites. “Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other.” — DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. amNY/The Brooklyn Paper. Advocates call it a start and want faster rollouts. Streetsblog NYC.

      When the day turns deadly.

      The harm spikes at the edges of the workday. Deaths cluster at 5 p.m., 6 p.m., and especially 6 p.m. to 7 p.m., with three deaths at 6 p.m. Injuries mount from late morning through the evening rush. NYC Open Data.

      Who gets hit.

      Since 2022, pedestrians took the worst of it: 7 killed and 500 injured here. Cyclists: 2 killed and 245 injured. Crashes involving occupants also killed one person and injured 1,425. NYC Open Data.

      Causes on paper.

      “Failure to Yield” shows up in the deaths at Avenue D and at Tilden. Intersections loom over the injury map. Trucks and buses are few but hit hard: trucks and buses account for a share of pedestrian deaths, including the right‑turning dump truck and multiple bus crashes in the district record. NYC Open Data.

      Officials know what works — do they?

      DOT is adding hard barriers to corners now. “We look forward to rolling out the design at high‑crash locations starting this year.” — Commissioner Rodriguez. amNY/The Brooklyn Paper. “Anything with real (not plastic) infrastructure in street corners is good news,” an advocate said, urging faster, broader use. Streetsblog NYC.

      Three steps for these streets.

      • Harden daylighting at Flatbush, Nostrand, New York, Foster, and Tilden. Use concrete, not paint. amNY/The Brooklyn Paper • Streetsblog NYC
      • Protect turns and slow entries with barriers and tighter radii at the crash corners in the dataset. NYC Open Data
      • Target right‑turning trucks and left‑turning sedans at signalized intersections named in fatal files 4707890 and 4717810. NYC Open Data

      Citywide fixes that stop the bleed.

      Albany extended and cleaned up school speed‑zone law this June. S 8344. DOT is moving to clear corners. amNY/The Brooklyn Paper • Streetsblog NYC

      The next steps are plain: lower speeds on more streets, stop repeat speeders with technology. Advocates are pushing both. See how to press your officials and join the fight in our Take Action guide.
