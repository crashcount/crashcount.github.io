      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 44?

      AD 44: The corners that keep breaking people

      Written August 24, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 44: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 24, 2025

      Another driver. Same ending.

      • A 16‑year‑old on an e‑bike died at Ditmas and Coney Island Avenue. A box truck turned right. He was ejected. He died at the scene (NYC Open Data crash 4749410).
      • A 71‑year‑old cyclist was killed on West Drive at Center Drive in Prospect Park. The record lists “lost consciousness” (NYC Open Data crash 4824644).
      • A pedestrian, 46, was struck at Fort Hamilton Parkway and 37th Street. The log says “driver inattention/distraction.” He died (NYC Open Data crash 4678904).

      These are not outliers. In this district since 2022, there have been 3,421 crashes, with 10 people killed and 1,978 injured (NYC Open Data rollup). Bikes and pedestrians take a heavy share: bicyclists had 337 injuries and 2 deaths; pedestrians had 365 injuries and 3 deaths. SUVs and cars led the harm to pedestrians, with trucks next (period stats).

      Peak pain comes in the afternoon and evening. Injuries stack up at 2 p.m., 3 p.m., 6 p.m., and 8 p.m. Deaths strike at 1 a.m., 10 a.m., noon, 1 p.m., 3 p.m., 6 p.m., 9 p.m., and 10 p.m. The clock does not save you (hourly distribution).

      Three corners. One fix.

      • Atlantic Avenue bleeds. It leads this list with 88 injuries and one death (top intersections).
      • Caton Avenue has three deaths recorded. Thirty‑seven injuries. Same strip, same outcome (top intersections).
      • Eastern Parkway shows 30 injuries and three serious injuries. Flatbush at No. 450 shows three serious injuries. Coney Island Avenue racks up 42 injuries and two serious ones (top intersections).

      The patterns are blunt. “Other” factors lead the body count here, with two deaths tied to that bucket and 658 injuries. “Vulnerable road user error” appears in two deaths. Distraction is logged. Alcohol is logged. A red light blown is logged. These are the inputs the city collects; the outcomes are on the street (contributing factors).

      Trucks and turns.

      A 2017 Hino box truck turned right at Ditmas and Coney Island Ave. The teen on the e‑bike was crushed. The truck showed “no damage” in the report. The rider did not survive (crash 4749410). On Atlantic at Clinton, a tractor‑trailer and a Jeep collided; an 88‑year‑old passenger died (crash 4681770).

      Who gets hit, when.

      This year to date, crashes in AD 44 are up 25% over last year’s same stretch. Deaths tripled, from 1 to 3. Injuries jumped 47% (period comparison). People 35 to 44 and 45 to 54 bear the worst of it this year, each with a recorded death. Seniors 65 to 74 also show a death in the ledger (period stats by age).

      Three moves the city can make now.

      • Daylight the corners with hard materials so people can see and be seen. Seven Brooklyn officials, including Council Member Shahana Hanif and Assembly Member Robert Carroll, already urged “universal daylighting with hardened materials” at intersections (their letter).
      • Harden turns and slow right turns on Coney Island Avenue and Caton Avenue where trucks cut in and people die.
      • Target repeat hotspots like Atlantic Avenue with concrete, not paint, and enforce distracted driving where deaths are logged.

      Officials know what works — do they?

      Albany extended the 24‑hour school speed safety program through 2030, a technical bill that kept protections alive (S 8344). Another bill would force chronic speeders to slow down with intelligent speed assistance. The proposal, framed as the Stop Super Speeders Act, would require devices for drivers with heavy point totals or camera tickets (policy file). Advocates say a tiny pool of drivers does outsized harm and linked high camera‑ticket counts to fatal risk (analysis and coverage).

      Lower the speed. Save a life.

      New York City has the power to set safer speeds under Sammy’s Law, and groups are pushing for a default 20 mph limit and controls on repeat speeders. The fight is laid out here: Take Action.
