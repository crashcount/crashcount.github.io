      York Avenue Kills—Policy Lets It Happen

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 7, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      AD 76: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll on York Avenue

      A 90-year-old woman tried to cross York Avenue at 72nd Street. A yellow taxi made a U-turn and hit her. Seconds later, an SUV struck her again. She died at the hospital. Both drivers stayed. No charges. The street was cleared. Her name was Frances Rickard. She lived on the Upper West Side. She did not make it home. Frances Rickard was crossing at York Avenue and East 72nd Street around 5:40 p.m. when the 68-year-old man driving the cab made a U-turn and hit her, authorities said.

      A day earlier, a Tesla tore down the FDR. It flipped. It burned. The driver died. A witness described the speed and destruction: “At least 120, 130 [mph]. At least. The damage was just so much that it split in half. And that just started going on fire.”

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the last year, 3 people died and 250 were injured in crashes in AD 76. Five suffered serious injuries. The dead are mostly the old and the vulnerable. Two were over 75. One was a woman in her thirties. Most were pedestrians. The machines that killed: SUVs, taxis, trucks, sedans. The numbers do not show the faces. But they do not let you look away.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Assembly Member Rebecca Seawright has signed on to bills that could help. She co-sponsored a law to force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed-limiting tech A 7979. She voted yes to bring speed cameras to more school zones A 7652. These are steps in the right direction. But she also backs bills that shift the burden to cyclists and e-scooter riders, demanding licenses and insurance for the smallest vehicles A 116. These laws do not stop the SUVs and trucks that kill. They do not slow the traffic. They do not protect the old woman crossing the street.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Streets can be made safe. Speed can be tamed. Cameras can stay on. But only if you demand it. Call your council member. Call Seawright. Tell them: Lower the speed limit. Keep the cameras. Build streets for people, not cars. Do not wait for another name in the news. Take action now.
