      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 76?

      York Avenue keeps taking lives. The fixes sit on the shelf.

      Written August 24, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 76: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 24, 2025

      Another driver. Same ending.

      • A 90‑year‑old woman tried to cross York and E 72nd just before dusk. A yellow cab made a U‑turn and hit her. Moments later, a second car hit her again. She died at the hospital, police said (NYC Open Data; New York Post).
      • A 71‑year‑old woman had the walk at York and E 68th. An SUV turned right and struck her. She died there (NYC Open Data).
      • On the FDR by E 70th, a Tesla flipped, burned, and threw both people out. The driver died. “At least 120, 130 [mph],” a witness said. Sixty firefighters fought the battery fire (NY Daily News; New York Post).

      The roll call is long in AD 76: 10 people dead, 979 injured since 2022, with pedestrians and cyclists taking hundreds of those hits (NYC Open Data).

      The corners that keep bleeding

      The worst locations are not secrets. FDR Drive leads with deaths and injuries tied to high‑energy crashes. On the avenues, 2nd Avenue and 1st Avenue rack up injuries. York Avenue shows two separate death entries in the state logs since 2022 (NYC Open Data).

      Night brings more pain. The tall spikes come around 4–6 a.m., and again at the evening rush. Deaths peak at 4, 5, 6, 17, 18, and 21 hours on the clock in this district’s record (NYC Open Data).

      SUVs hit people hardest here. They are tied to the largest share of pedestrian deaths and serious injuries in the district’s rollup. Trucks and buses add more bodies than their small numbers should allow (NYC Open Data).

      What the record says, over and over

      The patterns repeat. “Driver Inattention/Distraction” shows up in fatal turns. “Unsafe Speed” shows up on the FDR. “Disregarded traffic control” is there too. The data marks “other” factors most often, but the bodies are real: pedestrians killed at the crosswalk, at the corner, and mid‑block on York (NYC Open Data).

      A city bridge nearby finally split space for people on foot and on bikes. The Queensboro Bridge now runs one outer roadway for bikes, one for walkers. Advocates fought years for it (amNY).

      Three corners. One fix.

      • York at E 68th and York at E 72nd: Harden the turns. Daylight the corners. Give a leading pedestrian interval. Ticket U‑turns at the crosswalk. These two corners have names in the death ledger (NYC Open Data; New York Post).
      • Second Avenue at E 86th and along First Avenue: Protect the crossings where injuries pile up. Paint, posts, and signal time are cheap. The injury lists are not (NYC Open Data).
      • FDR by the 70s: Speed is killing. Targeted enforcement in the peaks. Crash history says when the sirens wail (NYC Open Data).

      Officials know what works — do they?

      Albany extended school‑zone rules this June. Assembly Member Rebecca Seawright voted yes on a bill to keep protections in place near schools (S 8344). She also co‑sponsors a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed‑limiting tech (A 2299).

      The city already has tools. Advocates won separate paths on the Queensboro. Cameras run around the clock near schools through 2030. The data still shows spikes at dawn and dusk, and bodies at York and the FDR (amNY; NYC Open Data).

      Slow it everywhere. Stop the worst.

      • Lower the default speed where people live and walk. Slower streets cut deaths.
      • Pass mandatory speed limiters for chronic offenders. The bill sits in Albany now (A 2299).

      The names fade fast. The corners don’t. York Avenue keeps the score. The FDR hums. We know what breaks bones here. We also know what slows cars. Act now. Take action.
