      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 65?

      Who Dies Next? Streets Bleed While Leaders Stall

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed link formatting, removed duplicate headline, cleaned citations)

      AD 65: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Toll in Plain Sight

      In Assembly District 65, the numbers do not flinch. Twelve people killed. Twenty-nine left with serious injuries. Over 1,700 hurt since 2022. The dead do not speak, but the silence is heavy. A cyclist, age 44, killed at Centre and Broome. Three pedestrians crushed on Jackson Street. An 81-year-old man, struck crossing Delancey, never made it home. The youngest victim was eleven. The oldest, eighty-one. No one is spared.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and trucks do most of the damage. SUVs and sedans alone took three lives and wounded hundreds. Trucks killed four. Bikes and mopeds, too, have left scars—one dead, dozens hurt. But the weight of steel and speed falls hardest on those outside the car. The streets are not safe for the old, the young, or anyone on foot or bike. The numbers are not just numbers. They are bodies, broken and gone.

      Leadership: Votes and Silence

      Assembly Member Grace Lee has voted to extend and expand school speed cameras—“boosting safety for children” and “improving child pedestrian safety” (speed camera legislation). She co-sponsored bills for safer street design and bus lane enforcement. But she also voted to weaken bus rules, a move that “increases pedestrian and cyclist risk”. The record is mixed. The work is not done.

      The System Fails, Again and Again

      The NYPD has started issuing criminal summonses to cyclists for low-level violations. “Under the new policy, a person issued a criminal summons must turn up in person in criminal court” (NYPD summons policy). The city cracks down on those with the least power. The drivers who kill walk away. The families do not.

      Call to Action: Make Them Hear You

      Call Assembly Member Grace Lee. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people outside cars. Demand an end to laws that punish the vulnerable and let the reckless drive on.

      Every day you wait, another life is at risk.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents the people of New York in state government.
      ▸ Where does AD 65 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 1 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 65?
      It includes the Financial District-Battery Park City, Soho-Little Italy-Hudson Square, Chinatown-Two Bridges, Lower East Side, and Manhattan CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 1 and District 2, and State Senate District SD 27.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 65?
      Cars and Trucks: 3 deaths, 343 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 26 injuries. Bikes: 1 death, 43 injuries. Most deaths and injuries come from cars, SUVs, and trucks. NYC Open Data crash records
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and stronger enforcement save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, fund safer street designs, and pass laws that protect people outside cars instead of punishing them.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Helicopter Breaks Apart Over Hudson River
      ▸

      Six died when a sightseeing helicopter shattered midair over the Hudson. The fuselage, rotor, and tail tore loose. Loud bangs echoed. The craft plunged. No black box. No warning. Only fragments and silence left behind.

      NY Daily News reported on May 7, 2025, that federal officials released images showing a sightseeing helicopter breaking apart in midair before crashing into the Hudson River, killing six. The National Transportation Safety Board’s preliminary report details how the Bell 206L-4 split into three pieces: 'the fuselage, the main rotor system, and the tail boom.' Witnesses heard 'several loud 'bangs'' before the breakup. The helicopter had flown eight tours that day, all with the same pilot, who had less than 50 hours in this model. The aircraft had a prior maintenance issue with its transmission assembly and lacked flight data recorders. The NTSB noted the pilot wore video-capable sunglasses, but they remain missing. The crash highlights gaps in oversight and the risks of repeated tour flights without robust recording or inspection requirements.
