      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 65?

      Canal Street Kills—City Stalls

      Written July 24, 2025

      AD 65: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 24, 2025

      The Toll on Canal and Bowery

      Just days ago, a stolen car tore through Bowery and Canal. Two lives ended. May Kwok, 63, sat on a bench. Kevin Cruickshank, 55, rode his bike. The car jumped the curb. The driver ran. Police found an open bottle of tequila and two pistols in the wreck. The street was left stained and silent. “They then tried to flee the scene followed by multiple eyewitnesses who told them to stop and come back because they had just killed two people.”

      This was not the first time. It will not be the last. Canal Street is a funnel for speeding, chaos, and death. Advocates have begged for change. “City Hall knows that Canal Street is one of the most dangerous in our city, but they’ve stalled any improvements over and over again. Spinelessly ignoring a public health emergency puts every New Yorker at risk.”

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the last 12 months, 2 people died and 12 were seriously injured in crashes in AD 65. There were 594 injuries and 1,207 crashes. Most victims were walking or riding. Cars and trucks did the most harm, but bikes and motorcycles left scars too. The dead do not care about the vehicle type. They are just gone.

      Leadership: Votes and Silence

      Assembly Member Grace Lee has voted to extend and expand school speed cameras, and co-sponsored bills for safer streets. She backed bus lane enforcement and “complete streets” design. But she also voted to weaken bus rules, making it easier for some to break the law. The blood on Canal Street is not just from one crash. It is from years of delay, half-measures, and compromise.

      What Now

      No more vigils. No more empty words. Call Assembly Member Grace Lee. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and pedestrian space on Canal. Demand action, not studies. The next name could be yours.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents local districts like AD 65.
      ▸ Where does AD 65 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 1 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 65?
      It includes the Financial District-Battery Park City, Soho-Little Italy-Hudson Square, Chinatown-Two Bridges, Lower East Side, and Manhattan CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 1 and District 2, and State Senate District SD 27.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 65?
      Cars and trucks caused the most deaths and injuries to pedestrians in AD 65, with 3 deaths and 354 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 29 crashes, and bikes in 47. All types left people hurt or dead. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and enforcement can stop the next death.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes and wider sidewalks, and hold reckless drivers accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      NYPD Issues Criminal Summonses To Cyclists
      ▸

      Police now hand out criminal summonses to cyclists for traffic violations. Fines become court dates. Advocates warn of escalation. Lawmakers push for e-bike registration after a fatal crash. Streets grow tense. Riders and pedestrians caught in the crossfire.

      West Side Spirit reported on May 13, 2025, that NYPD has begun issuing criminal summonses, not just traffic tickets, to cyclists and e-bike riders for violations like running red lights or riding on sidewalks. The change means accused riders must appear in criminal court, not just pay a fine. The move follows the death of Priscilla Loke, struck by an e-bike in 2023. Advocates like Transportation Alternatives call the new policy 'a dangerous escalation.' The NYC E-Vehicle Safety Alliance urges lawmakers to require license plates for e-bikes and scooters. The article notes, 'Under the new policy, a person issued a criminal summons must turn up in person in criminal court.' The shift highlights growing tension over enforcement and the push for stricter regulation after high-profile crashes.

      Helicopter Breaks Apart Over Hudson River
      ▸

      Six died when a sightseeing helicopter shattered midair over the Hudson. The fuselage, rotor, and tail tore loose. Loud bangs echoed. The craft plunged. No black box. No warning. Only fragments and silence left behind.

      NY Daily News reported on May 7, 2025, that federal officials released images showing a sightseeing helicopter breaking apart in midair before crashing into the Hudson River, killing six. The National Transportation Safety Board’s preliminary report details how the Bell 206L-4 split into three pieces: 'the fuselage, the main rotor system, and the tail boom.' Witnesses heard 'several loud 'bangs'' before the breakup. The helicopter had flown eight tours that day, all with the same pilot, who had less than 50 hours in this model. The aircraft had a prior maintenance issue with its transmission assembly and lacked flight data recorders. The NTSB noted the pilot wore video-capable sunglasses, but they remain missing. The crash highlights gaps in oversight and the risks of repeated tour flights without robust recording or inspection requirements.
