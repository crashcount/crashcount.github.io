      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 28?

      Juniper and Queens Blvd: Four dead, and the clock keeps running

      Written August 25, 2025 (Revised August 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      Bodies keep turning up on our streets. The hours keep ticking. The promises keep slipping.

      80th Street turns. A man doesn’t.

      On April 19, 2025, the driver of an FDNY truck turned from 80th Street onto Juniper Boulevard North. He hit a cyclist. The man died there in Middle Village. Police said the truck was turning when the crash happened, and the NYPD is investigating ABC7. A witness said firefighters on scene looked shaken: “One of them seemed concerned, like shocked” New York Post. Open data lists the same hour and corner. A truck making a right. A 54‑year‑old cyclist killed. The box truck’s right front met the bike’s left side NYC Open Data.

      Three deaths stack up at “80 ST.” It is one of this district’s worst spots, by the numbers NYC Open Data.

      Queens Boulevard takes another life

      On March 19, 2025, a 23‑year‑old cyclist was hit near Queens Boulevard and 63rd Drive. He died. The crash record lists two eastbound cars and a northbound bike. Impact to the bike’s left side. “Apparent death,” the city database says NYC Open Data. Queens Boulevard remains a top injury corridor here NYC Open Data.

      The worst hours hit late afternoon

      From 2 to 5 p.m., injuries spike. At 2 p.m., 125 people hurt. At 3 p.m., 113 injuries and one death. At 4 p.m., 116 injuries and three deaths. At 5 p.m., 102 injuries and two deaths. Daylight. Crowded streets. People trying to get home NYC Open Data.

      Trucks and buses do real harm

      In this district, SUVs and cars injure the most pedestrians, but trucks and buses leave a deeper mark per crash. Among pedestrian injuries traced to vehicle type: SUVs 102, sedans 107, trucks 10, buses 8. Serious harm shows up with the heavy ones too NYC Open Data. The FDNY turn at Juniper was not an outlier; heavy vehicles loom large in the worst outcomes here.

      What the numbers say, without mercy

      Since 2022, AD 28 logged 3,089 crashes, 1,620 injuries, and six deaths. In the last 12 months alone: 1,015 crashes, 567 injured, five dead. Year‑to‑date injuries are up more than half from last year’s pace NYC Open Data. Cyclists paid dearly: four killed, 118 injured. Pedestrians: 284 injured, four seriously NYC Open Data.

      Contributing factors stack up as “other,” distraction, failure to yield, and blown signals. “Other” alone covers 483 injuries. Failure to yield hurt five more. Signals ignored, six. Each line is a body, not a theory NYC Open Data.

      Repeat hotspots, simple fixes

      The district’s worst corridors are named in the data: Jackie Robinson Parkway, Queens Boulevard, Woodhaven Boulevard, and Metropolitan Avenue. The turn at 80th and Juniper needs hard edges: daylight every corner, harden the turn, set a leading walk signal. On truck routes, add protected space for the bike and the walk. Give big vehicles fewer chances to sweep across people.

      The thin line between speed and ash

      A BMW driver in Queens rammed a motorcyclist after a minor collision, prosecutors say. The bike burst into flames. The rider died. “Our roadways are not the place to settle disputes,” Queens DA Melinda Katz said NY Daily News. The paper reported the driver had past summonses for speeding and wrong‑way driving NY Daily News.

      The state has a bill to force repeat violators to slow down. Assembly Member Emily Gallagher’s measure would make any driver with 11 points in 24 months, or 6 camera tickets in a year, install an intelligent speed limiter Open States A 2299. Another version set the trigger at 11 points in 18 months, or 6 tickets in a year Open States A 7979. Lawmakers also extended protections in school speed zones this June; the bill passed both chambers Open States S 8344.

      Slow it all down

      Speeds kill. Albany renewed 24‑hour school‑zone cameras through 2030, but city streets outside those zones still run hot. New York City now has the power to lower speed limits on local streets. Advocates say use it. Make 20 miles per hour the rule and back it with cameras and limiters. The tools exist. The bodies are here.

      Take one step now. Tell City Hall to act: take action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where is this happening?
      Assembly District 28 in Queens, covering Glendale, Middle Village, Rego Park, Forest Hills, Kew Gardens, Forest Park, and nearby areas.
      ▸ What times are most dangerous here?
      Late afternoons. From 2–5 p.m. injuries soar: 125 at 2 p.m., 113 at 3 p.m., 116 at 4 p.m., and 102 at 5 p.m., with multiple deaths recorded in those hours.
      ▸ What streets and corners are the worst?
      Jackie Robinson Parkway, Queens Boulevard, Woodhaven Boulevard, and Metropolitan Avenue. The 80th St and Juniper Blvd N area has multiple deaths in the data.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ What should be fixed first?
      Daylight corners and harden turns at 80th St/Juniper Blvd N. Add LPIs and protected space where trucks turn. On Queens Blvd and Woodhaven, tighten turns and separate bikes from traffic.

      On August 23, 2024, a group of New York State legislators—Assembly Members Phil Steck and Andrew Hevesi, State Senator Julia Salazar, and former Assembly Member Dick Gottfried—filed a legal brief against Governor Hochul’s decision to pause congestion pricing. The matter, as described: “the legislature never gave her or any governor the power to do away with the traffic toll.” The brief cites the 2019 Traffic Mobility Act, arguing only the Traffic Mobility Review Board and the MTA can make such decisions. The lawmakers warn that letting the governor override the MTA would “make impossibly opaque the actual responsibility for MTA decisions.” Their action supports the MTA Board’s independence and opposes executive interference. No safety analyst has assessed the direct impact on vulnerable road users, but the legislators’ stance highlights the risk of political meddling in life-and-death street policy.
