      Five Dead, Hundreds Hurt: Bronx Streets Run Red on Benedetto’s Watch

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 82: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Toll in the Bronx: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      In Assembly District 82, the road does not forgive. Five people have died in crashes here in the last year. Six hundred fifty-five more were injured. Seven suffered wounds so deep they will never be the same. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie.

      Just days ago, a 71-year-old woman died in Co-op City. A 79-year-old driver crashed into two cars and a pole. “I saw one lady was out on the ground. They was giving her medical attention, checking her body. She was laid out,” said Samuel Cherry. Seven others were hurt. The street was quiet after, except for the sirens. Police said the cause was not known. No one was arrested. The dead woman’s name was Stella Nyarko-Dei.

      A week before, an 18-year-old woman was thrown from a moped on the Hutchinson River Parkway. The driver sped off. She was left fighting for her life. “The Honda Pilot then collided with the Razor Scooter, causing the 24-year-old man operating the scooter and an 18-year-old woman to be ejected,” reported ABC7. Six others were sent to the hospital.

      Benedetto: Votes, Silence, and the Cost of Delay

      Assembly Member Michael Benedetto has voted to extend school speed zones and expand speed cameras, moves that help protect children and walkers. He co-sponsored a bill to require safer street designs for all users. But he also backed bills that weaken bus rules, putting pedestrians and cyclists at greater risk. He supported a donor’s bid to keep polluting buses idling in the Bronx, against the advice of health advocates. Each vote has a cost. Each delay means another family waits for the call that never should come.

      The Road Ahead: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. Every crash is a policy failure. Every injury is a warning. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. The state can pass laws to stop repeat speeders. Residents can demand more from leaders who claim to care.

      Call Assembly Member Benedetto. Call your council member. Demand safer speeds, stronger enforcement, and streets built for people, not just cars.

      Do not wait for the next siren. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees state policy, and represents local districts like AD 82.
      ▸ Where does AD 82 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 13 and state senate district SD 34.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 82?
      It includes the Westchester Square, Throgs Neck-Schuylerville, Pelham Bay-Country Club-City Island, Co-Op City, Hart Island, Ferry Point Park-St. Raymond Cemetery, Morris Park, Hutchinson Metro Center, Pelham Bay Park, and Bronx CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 12 and District 13, and State Senate Districts SD 34 and SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 82?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 82 were caused by cars and trucks (2 deaths, 163 minor injuries, 47 moderate injuries, 7 serious injuries), followed by motorcycles and mopeds (no deaths, 9 minor injuries, 1 moderate injury), and bikes (no deaths, 3 minor injuries). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Every crash is preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. These are policy failures, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws to stop repeat dangerous drivers. They can also refuse to support bills that put vulnerable road users at risk.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
