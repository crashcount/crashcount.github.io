      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 82?

      No More Blood on Bartow: Demand Real Safety Now

      Written July 13, 2025

      AD 82: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Blood on Bartow Avenue

      A woman lay on the ground. Paramedics checked her body. The sound of the crash still hung in the air. “It was a terrible sound – it was a terrible incident that happened,” a neighbor said after the three-car pileup in Co-op City. She did not get up. She died at 71. Six others were hurt. The driver was 79. The street was Bartow Avenue. The date was July 11, 2025. The next day, the news reported a passenger dead after a car slammed into two others and a pole.

      This is not rare. In the last year, five people died in crashes in AD 82. 655 were injured. Seven were left with serious, life-changing wounds. Children, elders, men, women. The numbers do not stop. The pain does not fade.

      The Pattern: No End in Sight

      The streets do not forgive. In June, a scooter crash on the Hutchinson River Parkway left an 18-year-old woman fighting for her life. The driver fled. She was left on the asphalt, alone. “One lady was out on the ground. They was giving her medical attention, checking her body. She was laid out,” a witness said at the scene.

      In the last twelve months, the district saw 923 crashes. The dead and injured are not numbers. They are neighbors. They are children and elders. They are the people who walk, ride, and drive these streets.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Assembly Member Michael Benedetto voted to keep speed cameras near schools and extend enforcement. He co-sponsored a bill to make streets safer for all users. He also backed a bill to require e-scooter licenses and insurance—a move that burdens riders, not drivers. He supported an exemption for a campaign donor to keep polluting buses idling in the Bronx. Some votes help. Some votes hurt. The record is mixed. The streets are not safe.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. The city has the power to lower speed limits. The state can rein in repeat speeders. The Assembly Member can lead or stand aside. Call Michael Benedetto. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat offenders. Demand streets where no one is left bleeding on the asphalt.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, votes on bills, and represents local districts like AD 82.
      ▸ Where does AD 82 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 13 and state senate district SD 34.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 82?
      It includes the Westchester Square, Throgs Neck-Schuylerville, Pelham Bay-Country Club-City Island, Co-Op City, Hart Island, Ferry Point Park-St. Raymond Cemetery, Morris Park, Hutchinson Metro Center, Pelham Bay Park, and Bronx CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 12 and District 13, and State Senate Districts SD 34 and SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 82?
      Cars and Trucks: 3 deaths, 179 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 10 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 3 injuries. Most harm comes from cars and trucks. Source: NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Every crash is preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and stronger enforcement. Calling them ‘accidents’ hides the truth.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws to rein in repeat dangerous drivers. They can also reject bills that shift blame to cyclists and pedestrians.
