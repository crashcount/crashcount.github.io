      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 66?

      AD 66: Crosswalk deaths, soft turns, hard silence

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      At West 10th and Greenwich Ave, a left‑turning sedan hit a 76‑year‑old woman who was crossing with the signal. She died. The police report lists “Failure to Yield Right‑of‑Way.” The impact was front‑center. The turn was left. The date was Oct. 3, 2023. City data names it all.

      At Spring and Crosby, a Jeep SUV turned left and struck a 54‑year‑old woman in the crosswalk. She was crossing with the signal. She died. The vehicle was a 2015 SUV, New Jersey plates. The point of impact was the right front bumper. The cause again: failure to yield. The date was Oct. 28, 2024. The city’s crash file says so.

      On Cornelia Street, a garbage truck backed up and crushed a 35‑year‑old man. He died in the roadway. The report lists “Backing Unsafely.” The date was May 16, 2024. It’s in the record.

      By the clock, injuries stack up across the day. Noon hurts. So do the commute hours. Fatal spikes hit at 5 a.m., 10 a.m., 1 p.m., 4 p.m., 7 p.m., 8 p.m., and 11 p.m. in this district’s record. The hourly grid is cold and clear.

      Where the street bleeds

      Lafayette Street, Sixth Avenue, Hudson, West Houston. These names repeat in the logs. Lafayette shows 27 injuries and three serious. Sixth Avenue has 72 injuries. Hudson and West Houston add more. The city maps these hotspots.

      Pedestrians take the harshest blow: six dead, 417 injured since 2022. Cyclists: 496 injured. No cyclist deaths in this span, but bones and faces don’t count as luck. The mode split is plain.

      The killers are familiar: failure to yield, unsafe backing, and “other.” Speed shows up less on the form here, but when a sedan hit fast on Seventh Avenue this March, a pedestrian’s pelvis shattered. Each field has a name.

      Left turns, heavy fronts

      The turn is the pattern. Both West 10th and Spring had left‑turning cars striking people who had the signal. Front‑center. Right front. A body against the grille. The reports match.

      Trucks and buses are fewer, but weight wins. A 2020 garbage truck, backing, ended a life on a narrow block. That’s in the log.

      At Bowery and East 4th, a taxi going straight hit a 79‑year‑old woman. The report says “Traffic Control Disregarded.” She died. The city file states it.

      The numbers don’t blink

      From 2022 to now, this district logged 3,868 crashes, nine deaths, and 1,649 injuries. Pedestrians were killed by sedans, SUVs, taxis, and a truck. The roll‑up shows it.

      This year to date, crashes are up 26% over last year’s pace; injuries up 48%; serious injuries up 150%. Deaths are zero so far in 2025, but the curve is climbing. The period stats are public.

      Fix the corners that kill

      Make the turns tight. Harden them with posts. Give people a head start at the signal. Daylight the crosswalks at Lafayette and at Sixth, and on Spring. Protect Cornelia from trucks backing blind. Route heavy vehicles off the small blocks.

      Repeat the fixes where injuries stack. The dataset flags Lafayette Street and Avenue of the Americas. Start there, then move.

      Citywide levers, right now

      Albany renewed New York City’s 24/7 school‑zone speed cameras through 2030. “Speed cameras have cut speeding by over 60% in locations where installed,” sponsors said when pushing earlier renewals. Assembly Member Deborah Glick backed this year’s extension. AMNY reported the renewal and Open States lists the bill.

      The state also passed S.8344 to extend and clean up school speed‑zone rules; Glick voted yes. The record shows the vote. In 2022, the city’s cameras went 24/7. “The city isn’t in the car with you… If you don’t want to get a ticket, don’t speed,” Glick said then. Gothamist captured that line.

      Now use the tools. Lower speeds save lives. New Yorkers can also press Albany to rein in the worst repeat speeders with intelligent limiters; bills to expand camera enforcement are on file. See A7997.

      One ask

      This district’s dead include a woman in a crosswalk on a bright corner. A man pinned by a truck in a quiet lane. Names missing from mailboxes.

      Act. Push the city to slow the default speed and the state to stop repeat speeders. Start with a call. Here’s how.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where are the worst locations in AD 66 right now?
      The city’s dataset shows injuries concentrated on Lafayette Street (27 injuries, 3 serious) and on Avenue of the Americas (72 injuries). Hudson Street and West Houston Street also rank high.
      ▸ What times are most dangerous?
      Injuries stack at midday and the commute hours. Fatal spikes in this record occur at 5 a.m., 10 a.m., 1 p.m., 4 p.m., 7 p.m., 8 p.m., and 11 p.m., per the hourly distribution.
      ▸ How are pedestrians and cyclists faring?
      Since 2022, pedestrians account for six deaths and 417 injuries; cyclists suffered 496 injuries, according to NYC Open Data for this area.
      ▸ What policies moved recently?
      Albany extended 24/7 NYC school‑zone speed cameras through 2030 and passed S.8344 to maintain and clean up school speed‑zone rules. Deborah Glick supported these efforts.
