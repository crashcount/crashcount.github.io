      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 66?

      No More Excuses: Lower the Speed Limit or Count More Dead

      Written August 5, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      AD 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 5, 2025

      The Slow March of Loss

      In Assembly District 66, the numbers do not lie. Nine people killed. Twenty-four left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. In just the last twelve months, one person died and twelve suffered serious injuries on these streets (NYC Open Data). The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors. A 76-year-old woman, crossing with the signal at Greenwich Avenue and West 10th, struck down by a turning sedan (crash record). A 35-year-old man, crushed by a backing garbage truck on Cornelia Street (crash record). A 79-year-old woman, killed by a taxi at Bowery and East 4th. The city moves on. Their families do not.

      The Vehicles That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did the most damage. Five deaths, hundreds of injuries. Trucks and buses killed one, left dozens hurt. Bikes, mopeds, and motorcycles injured many, but did not kill (NYC Open Data). The pattern is clear. The larger the vehicle, the greater the harm. The street does not forgive.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Assembly Member Deborah Glick has acted. She sponsored and voted to extend the city’s school speed camera program through 2030, a move praised as an “extra boost” for automated enforcement. She pushed for bills to expand camera enforcement and keep speeders from dodging the law. When the city’s cameras went 24/7, Glick dismissed critics: “The city isn’t in the car with you… If you don’t want to get a ticket, don’t speed.” But loopholes remain. The worst repeat offenders still slip through. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not. The blood dries, but the danger stays.

      The Next Fight

      Every day of delay is another day of risk. The law now lets New York set safer speeds. The cameras are watching, but the cars keep coming. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees state agencies, and represents local districts like AD 66. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Where does AD 66 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 2 and state senate district SD 27. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 66?
      It includes the Tribeca-Civic Center, Soho-Little Italy-Hudson Square, Greenwich Village, West Village, East Village, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 1, District 2, and District 3, and State Senate Districts SD 27 and SD 47. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 66?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most deaths and injuries: 5 deaths and over 300 injuries. Trucks and buses killed one, left dozens hurt. Bikes, mopeds, and motorcycles injured many, but did not kill. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The patterns repeat: same streets, same vehicles, same deadly results. These deaths and injuries can be prevented with safer street design and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, close loopholes for repeat offenders, and fund safer street redesigns. They can act now, not after the next death.

      A 2299
      Glick co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 2299 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets in a year triggers forced speed control tech. Lawmakers move to curb repeat speeders. Streets demand fewer deadly risks.

      Assembly bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 16, 2025, the bill 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Primary sponsor Emily Gallagher leads a bloc of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, and others. The measure aims to clamp down on repeat speeders with mandatory speed-limiting tech. No safety analyst note was provided.
