      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 66?

      One Dead, Hundreds Hurt: Glick’s District Bleeds While Albany Waits

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 29, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 18, 2025

      Blood on the Streets

      A woman steps onto the tracks at Union Square. She cannot climb back. The train does not stop. She dies there, under the city, as witnesses watch. The L train is silent for three hours. Her name is not in the news. Only her age: 24.

      In the West Village, a cyclist is struck by a driver who runs a red light. The man is thrown. His bike goes one way, his body another. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” he says. He wakes up in an ambulance. He cannot remember the crash. The driver flees. The city keeps moving.

      In the last twelve months, AD 66 saw 967 crashes, 475 injuries, and 10 serious injuries. One person died. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers—no one is spared. Cars and trucks did most of the damage. Taxis, SUVs, and sedans left bodies broken. Bikes and mopeds hurt too, but the numbers are smaller. The street does not care who you are.

      What Has Deborah Glick Done?

      Assembly Member Deborah Glick has moved on some fronts. She co-sponsored a bill to raise fines for drivers who block bike lanes. She backed speed cameras in school zones, pushing for 24/7 enforcement. She has signed on to bills for safer street design and speed limiters. But the pace is slow. Cameras catch speeders, but loopholes remain. Fines rise, but the dead stay dead.

      Some bills shift the focus to plate covers and enforcement gadgets, not the drivers who kill. Others call for more studies, more pilots, more waiting. Every delay means another family waits for news that will break them.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by the city, the state, and the people in charge. The street can be made safe. The tools exist. The laws can change. But only if you demand it.

      Call Deborah Glick. Call your council member. Tell them: Enough. Lower the speed limit. Build real protection for people, not cars. End the waiting.

      Take action now.

      Glick Supports Safety Boosting West Side Highway Bike Lane
      ▸

      Manhattan Borough President Mark Levine called on state DOT to take a lane from cars on the West Side Highway. He wants a two-way protected bike lane. The Greenway is packed. Delivery workers need space. Local boards and lawmakers back the move.

      On August 1, 2022, Manhattan Borough President Mark Levine demanded the state Department of Transportation repurpose a lane on the West Side Highway for a two-way protected bike lane. Levine wrote, 'The Hudson River Greenway is one of the most heavily used bike paths in the country... congestion has worsened significantly.' He sent his letter to state DOT Commissioner Therese Dominguez and city DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez. Local elected officials and Community Board 1, which voted 41-3 in May 2020 to support the change, also back the plan. State senators, assembly members, and council members signed a letter urging the same. Levine and supporters argue the new lane would ease crowding and give e-bike delivery workers—many of them immigrants—a safe, legal route. The state DOT has resisted, citing car congestion. Advocates say more space for cyclists is overdue.
