      Still Bleeding: NYC Streets Demand More Than Promises

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, tightened prose, ensured correct citation order and deduplication.)

      AD 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Broken Bodies, Empty Promises

      A cyclist’s bike flies one way. His body the other. In the West Village, a driver runs a red, hits Myung Jin Chung, and flees. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” Chung said from his hospital bed. “All my memory is gone. When I woke up I was in the ambulance” (Chung said from his hospital bed).

      In the last twelve months, 473 people were injured and 10 suffered serious injuries in Assembly District 66. One person died. Most were walking or biking. The numbers do not flinch. They do not heal.

      A 79-year-old woman was killed by a taxi on Bowery. A 35-year-old man crushed by a truck on Cornelia Street. A 76-year-old woman struck dead crossing with the signal at Greenwich Avenue. The street does not care if you have the light. The street does not care if you are old.

      Leadership: Bills, Votes, and the Waiting

      Assembly Member Deborah Glick has signed her name to safety. She co-sponsored bills for bike lane protection, speed limiters, and complete street design. She backed 24/7 speed cameras after the city lost children to speeding cars. “72 percent of fatalities have been happening when the cameras were off,” Mayor Adams said, as Glick and others pushed for the law.

      But the blood keeps coming. Some bills stall. Some pass and wait for enforcement. Cameras catch plates, but drivers hide them. Glick’s latest bill would let cameras ticket for covered plates, but the city still waits for full accountability (Glick’s latest bill).

      What Next: The Work Is Not Done

      No law brings back the dead. No camera mends a shattered leg. But every delay is a choice. Every loophole is a wound. Call your Assembly Member. Call your Council Member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement. Demand streets where a child can cross and come home alive.

      The street does not wait. Neither should you.

      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees state agencies, and represents districts like AD 66. NYC Open Data

      It belongs to borough Manhattan and city council district District 2. NYC Open Data

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 66?
      Cars and Trucks: 4 deaths, 116 moderate injuries, 6 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 8 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 0 deaths, 26 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      They are preventable. Most deaths and injuries happen because of speeding, failure to yield, and unsafe street design—not fate. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, more protected bike lanes, and stricter penalties for reckless driving. They can also push for better street design and full use of speed and red-light cameras. NYC Open Data
