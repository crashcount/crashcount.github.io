      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 66?

      Eight Dead, Hundreds Hurt—Albany’s Delay is a Death Sentence

      Written July 3, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 3, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      The streets of Assembly District 66 do not forgive. Since 2022, eight people have died in crashes here. One was a 76-year-old woman, struck while crossing with the signal at Greenwich Avenue and West 10th. She bled out on the street. The driver failed to yield. She never made it home. NYC Open Data

      In the last year, 455 people have been hurt in crashes. Ten were seriously injured. No one under 18 died, but eighteen children were hurt. The numbers do not stop. They only grow.

      The Vehicles That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm. Four deaths, 140 minor injuries, 119 moderate, six serious. Trucks and buses killed one, left sixteen with minor wounds, fourteen with worse. Bikes and e-bikes hurt fifty-three. No one is safe. NYC Open Data

      Leadership: Progress and Gaps

      Assembly Member Deborah Glick has backed speed cameras and higher fines for repeat offenders. She co-sponsored bills to keep bike lanes clear and to require safer street design. She voted yes on more speed cameras near schools. She sponsored a bill to crack down on drivers who hide their plates from cameras. NYC Open Data

      But Albany failed to pass the bills that matter most. “Our elected officials in Albany have failed the livable streets movement again,” wrote Streetsblog NYC. The only big win was speed camera renewal. The rest died in committee. The status quo remains. The dead stay dead.

      The Call: Demand More

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by leaders who delay, water down, or ignore the laws that save lives. Call Assembly Member Glick. Call your council member. Tell them: No more waiting. No more deaths. Pass the bills. Build the lanes. Lower the speed.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature, responsible for passing laws and representing local districts. Members propose, debate, and vote on legislation that affects all New Yorkers.
      ▸ Where does AD 66 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 2 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 66?
      It includes the Tribeca-Civic Center, Soho-Little Italy-Hudson Square, Greenwich Village, West Village, East Village, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 1, District 2, and District 3, and State Senate Districts SD 27 and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 66?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm, with 4 deaths, 140 minor injuries, 119 moderate injuries, and 6 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 1 death, 16 minor injuries, 14 moderate injuries, and 1 serious injury. Bikes and e-bikes caused 53 injuries but no deaths.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speeds, and strong enforcement. They are not random—they are the result of policy choices.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, more protected bike lanes, daylighting intersections, and stronger penalties for reckless drivers. They can also push for funding and accountability to make these changes real.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      A 1236
      Glick co-sponsors bill increasing penalties, improving cyclist safety in bike lanes.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 1236 targets drivers who block bike lanes. It adds a mandatory surcharge for parking violations. The money goes to the court that finds the driver liable. Lawmakers push to keep bike lanes clear. Streets stay safer for cyclists.

      Assembly bill A 1236 was introduced on January 10, 2025. The bill is in the sponsorship stage. It is titled: 'Relates to violations and a mandatory surcharge for parking in a bicycle lane.' The bill would require drivers who park in bike lanes to pay a mandatory surcharge. The surcharge is paid to the clerk of the court or administrative tribunal that determines liability. Assembly Member Jo Anne Simon (District 52) is the primary sponsor. Deborah Glick (District 66) and Tony Simone (District 75) co-sponsor. The bill aims to deter illegal parking in bike lanes and keep routes clear for cyclists. No safety analyst note was provided.
