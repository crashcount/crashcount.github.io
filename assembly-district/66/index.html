      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 66?

      AD 66: Crosswalk deaths, broken turns, and the clock that won’t stop

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Pedestrians keep getting hit on these blocks. Since 2022, six pedestrians are dead here, alongside three people inside vehicles. Hundreds are hurt. The city logs 3,868 crashes, 1,649 injuries, and 25 serious injuries in this district’s span of years we track. SUVs and sedans do most of the harm to people on foot. Trucks and buses add their share. The toll does not pause.

      • Pedestrian injuries tied to sedans: 112, with two deaths. SUVs: 101, with two deaths. Trucks: 25, with one death. Taxis: 51, with one death. Bikes strike too: 58 pedestrian injuries, three serious, no deaths. The record is here in the numbers we pulled from the city’s crash files. Source.

      Jeep turns left. Woman with the signal. Dead.

      • On October 3, 2023, a 76‑year‑old woman crossing with the signal at Greenwich Ave and W 10th was killed by a left‑turning Chevy sedan. NYPD coded “failure to yield.” City data places her at the intersection; the car turned left. CrashID 4667744 lists severe head trauma and death.

      • On May 16, 2024, a 35‑year‑old man on Cornelia Street was crushed by a backing garbage truck. The city marked “backing unsafely.” He died there. CrashID 4725065.

      • On June 23, 2024, at Bowery and E 4th, a taxi going straight hit a 79‑year‑old woman at the intersection. She died. The file shows “traffic control disregarded.” CrashID 4735570.

      • On October 28, 2024, at Spring and Crosby, a left‑turning Jeep SUV struck a 54‑year‑old woman crossing with the signal. She died. The code again: “failure to yield.” CrashID 4767502.

      These are not outliers. They are the pattern.

      Where the street bites

      The police files say when and where people get hurt. Injuries peak at lunch and the late afternoon into the evening. From noon to 8 p.m., the injuries mount hour by hour. Deaths show up at 10 a.m., 1 p.m., 4 p.m., 7 p.m., 8 p.m., 11 p.m., and even 5 a.m. The pain does not keep regular hours. District curve.

      The worst corridors include Lafayette Street, Avenue of the Americas, Hudson Street, and West Houston. Lafayette logs 27 injuries and three serious injuries. Sixth Avenue sees 72 injuries. Hudson has 27. West Houston, 23. These are not numbers on a page. They are places you walk. Hotspots.

      The city blames “other” most often. After that come failure to yield, inattention, and unsafe speed. But when a driver turns left into a person with the light, the code reads the same: failure to yield. Contributing factors.

      Turn, strike, repeat

      Left turns keep killing. The fatal files here read like a loop: left‑turning sedan at Greenwich and W 10th; left‑turning SUV at Spring and Crosby. Center‑front impacts. People in the crosswalk with the signal. The driver’s bumper meets a human body. Then the paperwork. 4667744. 4767502.

      Large vehicles kill when they back up. The Cornelia Street death is a line in a spreadsheet: “Garbage or Refuse,” “Backing,” “Crush Injuries.” It is also a body in an alley. 4725065.

      Inside cars, people die too. A December 2023 crash at Sixth Ave and Bleecker lists a 40‑year‑old driver dead after an SUV hit a parked taxi. 4688160.

      The fixes are known

      This district bleeds at left‑turns and tight lanes. The remedies are simple and local:

      • Hardened left turns and longer walk head starts at known danger corners like Greenwich and W 10th and Spring and Crosby.
      • Daylight the corners on Lafayette, Sixth Avenue, and West Houston. Pull back parking so people can see and be seen.
      • Ban or tightly manage backing movements on narrow blocks like Cornelia during peak hours. Separate truck routes from the foot traffic that fills these streets. City data.

      Politics moves. The street still waits.

      Albany renewed 24/7 school‑zone speed cameras through 2030. Assembly Member Deborah Glick sponsored and backed the bill. AMNY reports, and the Senate file confirms the extension. S 8344. Glick also sponsored the Assembly version. A 8787. Cameras slow some streets. Children walk with a bit more cover.

      But the killing keeps its pace outside school zones and outside camera sights. We know who pays: pedestrians most of all, then cyclists, then the rest. The city’s own rollup shows it. Open Data.

      Two more tools stand ready. A lower default speed limit across the city. And speed limiters for the drivers who rack up violation after violation. The case is laid out by state bills and advocates. One proposal would force repeat speeders to install intelligent speed control. Survivors and families say it will save lives. See our take action guide, with sources and bills linked.

      Fourteen blocks of promise

      The city says 14th Street will be redesigned, with a goal to “improve the pedestrian experience.” Money is set: $3 million in design, with the city and two BIDs paying. Work begins next year, the report says. It will help where it lands. The rest of the map still waits its turn. NY1.

      The record is the record. Since 2022, nine dead here. Pedestrians take the brunt. Left turns. Backing trucks. Center‑front impacts. Noon to night. The names are not in the files we cite. The dates and the corners are. The corners are yours.

      Bold steps sit on paper. The street asks for steel and paint. Slower speeds everywhere. Limiters on the worst drivers. Harden the turns. Clear the corners. Do it before the next line gets added.

      Act now. One call, one email, one push. Start here: take action.
