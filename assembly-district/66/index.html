      Steel Rules, Bodies Break—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      AD 66: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      A man on a bike, crushed in the West Village. A delivery worker, doored in Soho and thrown under a truck. A 76-year-old woman, struck dead while crossing with the light. In the last twelve months, 471 people have been injured and 9 seriously hurt in Assembly District 66. One did not survive. The names fade. The pain does not.

      The System Favors Steel, Not Flesh

      Cars and trucks did the most damage. In three years, they killed 8 and seriously injured 20. Bikes and mopeds hurt people too, but the numbers are smaller. The streets are not safe for anyone without a steel shell. The dead are old and young, walking or riding, crossing with the signal or just trying to get home.

      A cyclist, left broken in the street, said, “All my body was blood and the sweater… All my memory is gone. When I woke up I was in the ambulance” said Myung Jin Chung. The driver ran a red, hit him, reversed, and fled. Police have video. No arrest.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Assembly Member Deborah Glick has backed some safety bills. She sponsored the law that made speed cameras run 24/7 near schools in 2022. She co-sponsored bills to raise fines for blocking bike lanes and to require safer street designs. She also pushed for more speed cameras to catch drivers hiding their plates A 7997. But some bills shift the focus to ticketing technicalities, not stopping the killing.

      Meanwhile, police crack down on cyclists and delivery workers. One protester put it plain: “It seems unfair to me that cyclists should receive a higher penalty for doing the same thing that a person in a car would do” said Cecil Scheib. The drivers keep driving. The bodies keep piling up.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      The blood on these streets is not an accident. It is a choice. Lower the speed limit. Build real protection for people walking and biking. End the double standard in enforcement. Call Deborah Glick. Call the Mayor. Demand action. Every day of delay is another family broken.

      Take action now.

      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws and oversees state policy, including traffic safety. NYC Open Data

      It belongs to borough Manhattan and city council district District 2. NYC Open Data

      It includes the Tribeca-Civic Center, Soho-Little Italy-Hudson Square, Greenwich Village, West Village, and Manhattan CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 1, District 2, and District 3, and State Senate Districts SD 27 and SD 47. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 66?
      Cars and trucks caused the most deaths and serious injuries. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes also hurt people, but less often. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speed, failure to yield, and unsafe streets. These deaths and injuries are preventable. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass laws to lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, expand speed cameras, and demand fair enforcement that protects people walking and biking. Streetsblog NYC

The demonstration, which began at Union Square, challenged new policies issuing criminal summonses for minor cycling offenses. Riders followed traffic laws, highlighting the crackdown’s contradictions. One cyclist, Erin Poland, said the policy 'is not actually protecting cyclists [but] putting them in more danger.'       E-Bike Riders Protest NYPD Crackdown
      ▸

      Hundreds rode through Manhattan. They called out harsh summonses for e-bike riders. Police target cyclists with criminal charges for minor traffic moves. Drivers get tickets. Riders face court. The city’s rules hit the vulnerable. The streets stay dangerous.

      Gothamist (2025-05-31) reports on a protest in Lower Manhattan against the NYPD’s policy of issuing criminal summonses to e-bike riders for traffic violations like running red lights or riding against traffic. Cyclists and advocates argue the penalties are harsher than those faced by drivers for similar actions. As one protester said, “It seems unfair to me that cyclists should receive a higher penalty for doing the same thing that a person in a car would do.” Police Commissioner Jessica Tisch defended the crackdown, citing the lack of licensing for e-bikes as a challenge for enforcement, but acknowledged the need for legislative reform. The article highlights inconsistent enforcement and the risks faced by vulnerable road users, especially delivery workers. Policy gaps and unequal penalties expose systemic danger on city streets.

      A 7997
      Glick sponsors bill diverting speed camera focus, potentially reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Assembly Bill 7997 pushes more speed cameras onto city streets. It aims at drivers who hide or alter plates. The bill extends camera use in school zones. Deborah Glick leads. The move cracks down on scofflaws. Vulnerable road users watch. Law waits for action.

      Assembly Bill A 7997, sponsored by Deborah Glick of District 66, sits at the sponsorship stage in the Assembly as of April 17, 2025. The bill, titled 'Relates to the use of certain photo speed violation monitoring systems for the purposes of enforcement of license plate obstruction; repealer,' permits the use of photo speed violation monitoring systems in New York City to enforce against license plate obstruction, concealment, and distortion. It also extends the use of speed cameras in certain school zones. Glick’s sponsorship signals a push to hold drivers accountable for hiding plates and dodging enforcement. The bill’s focus is clear: more cameras, less cover for reckless drivers. No official safety analyst note has been provided.
