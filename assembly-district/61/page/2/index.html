      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 61?

      Staten Island Streets Bleed—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      AD 61: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      Five dead. Twenty seriously hurt. In Assembly District 61, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, the streets have claimed five lives and left twenty more with wounds that do not heal. In the last twelve months alone, two people died and seven were seriously injured. The youngest was a child. The oldest, a senior crossing at dawn. The violence does not stop for age.

      Just weeks ago, a 16-year-old e-scooter rider was killed on College Avenue. Head trauma. A family left with silence where a voice should be. The driver stayed. The boy did not. Days later, a city worker was slashed by a cyclist after a near miss. The worker was fixing a sign. The cyclist pulled a blade. “This was an abhorrent assault of a NYC DOT employee who performs critical work to keep our city moving,” said a DOT spokesperson.

      Who Pays the Price

      Most victims are not behind the wheel. Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and SUVs are the main killers—two deaths and three serious injuries in three years. Trucks and buses add to the toll. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes leave their own scars. The numbers are not just numbers. They are bodies in the street, families at the hospital, shoes left at the curb.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Assembly Member Charles Fall has taken some steps. He voted yes to extend school speed zones, a move that keeps children safer near schools. But the work is not done. The city still delays life-saving redesigns. The mayor paused work on the 34th Street busway, leaving streets dangerous for those outside a car. Each delay is a risk. Each silence is a wound.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by leaders who look away. Call Assembly Member Fall. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action before another child’s name is written in the record of the dead.

      Do not wait. The street will not wait for you.

      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws and oversees state policy, including traffic safety. For more, see the official Assembly website.

      It belongs to borough Staten Island, city council district District 49 and state senate district SD 23. Source

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 61?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 2 deaths, 3 serious injuries, and over 300 incidents. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 1 serious injury and 5 incidents. Bikes were involved in 8 incidents, but no deaths or serious injuries. Source
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. Policies like lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, and safer street design can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, daylight intersections, and support laws that keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road.
