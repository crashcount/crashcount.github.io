      AD 61: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 5, 2025

      Blood on the Streets: The Toll in AD 61

      Three dead. Nine seriously hurt. In the last year alone, 653 people in Assembly District 61 have been injured in crashes. The dead include a child, a senior, and a middle-aged woman. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about the weather, the time, or the season. They only rise.

      Just last month, a 13-year-old boy on a moped collided with an MTA bus on Castleton Avenue. He was left in critical condition with a head injury. The street was quiet. The boy was not. He was taken to Richmond University Medical Center in critical condition with a head injury.

      A few weeks before, a 16-year-old on an e-scooter died after a crash with an SUV in Westerleigh. The driver stayed. The boy did not. Ellis suffered head trauma as a result of the crash.

      The old and the young, the careful and the quick—no one is spared.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Since 2022, AD 61 has seen six deaths and 22 serious injuries from 3,935 crashes. Children, seniors, and working people. Most injuries come from cars and SUVs—364 crashes. Trucks and buses follow, then motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes. The streets are not safe for anyone who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.

      What Has Charles Fall Done?

      Assembly Member Charles Fall voted to extend school speed zones, a move that keeps cameras rolling and children safer near schools. He voted yes to extend school speed zones, improving child pedestrian safety. He has also supported protected bike lanes and called out city delays on street redesigns. But the crisis does not pause for votes or press releases. Every week without action is another week of blood on the asphalt.

      The Call: No More Waiting

      Contact Assembly Member Fall. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns, not just promises. Join forces with groups like Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the state budget, and represents districts like AD 61 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does AD 61 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Staten Island, city council district District 49 and state senate district SD 23.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 61?
      It includes the Financial District-Battery Park City, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, St. George-New Brighton, Tompkinsville-Stapleton-Clifton-Fox Hills, West New Brighton-Silver Lake-Grymes Hill, Westerleigh-Castleton Corners, Port Richmond, Mariner’S Harbor-Arlington-Graniteville, Snug Harbor, and Staten Island CB1. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 1, District 49, and District 50, and State Senate Districts SD 23, SD 24, SD 26, and SD 27.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 61?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 61 come from cars and SUVs (364 incidents), followed by trucks and buses (34 incidents), motorcycles and mopeds (13 incidents), and bikes (8 incidents), according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Lower speeds, better street design, and stronger enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand protected bike lanes, and support laws that hold reckless drivers accountable.

      Fall Opposes Slow Pace of DOT Smart Curbs Pilot
      ▸

      DOT’s Smart Curbs pilot drags its feet. Free parking remains king. Promised microhubs for deliveries delayed. Only a sliver of free spaces become paid. Advocates call the effort timid. The city leaves most curb space untouched. Vulnerable users wait.

      The Department of Transportation’s Smart Curbs pilot, updated October 29, 2024, aims to convert free parking to paid meters and add delivery microhubs on the Upper West Side. The plan, first proposed in June, promised about 200 new metered spots and 27 loading zones, but only 175 free spaces—one-tenth of the area’s 1,700—will be removed. Microhubs, meant to reduce double-parking and delivery chaos, are delayed until next year. DOT spokespersons Vin Barone and Mona Bruno confirmed most changes are just reassignments, not true removals of free parking. Carl Mahaney of StreetopiaUWS called the slow pace disappointing: “We’ve been super eager to see these changes, see what their impact is and start measuring and observing, so it’s a little disappointing.” Parking expert Donald Shoup urged the city to reinvest meter revenue locally, but DOT declined. The pilot leaves most curb space for cars, not people. Vulnerable road users see little relief.
