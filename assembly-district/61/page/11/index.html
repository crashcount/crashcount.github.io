      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 61?

      Five Dead, Hundreds Broken—Staten Island Streets Still Kill

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 61: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Assembly District 61, the bodies keep coming. Since 2022, five people are dead. Seventeen more are left with injuries so serious they may never walk right again. The numbers do not flinch: 1,689 injured, 3,606 crashes in just over three years (NYC Open Data).

      The dead are not just numbers. A 68-year-old man, crossing at Forest Avenue and Raymond Place, was struck and killed by an SUV making a left turn. He was in the crosswalk. The driver failed to yield. The man never made it home (NYC Open Data).

      A year earlier, a 60-year-old man was killed crossing with the signal at Post Avenue. The car turned left. He bled out in the street (NYC Open Data).

      Streets Still Unsafe

      The violence is not random. Most deaths come from cars and SUVs. In this district, cars and SUVs killed two, left two more with serious injuries, and hurt over 300 people. Trucks and buses added dozens more injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds, a handful. Bikes, a few scrapes (NYC Open Data).

      The danger is everywhere. On March 15, two police officers crashed their cruiser into a Staten Island building while swerving to avoid a U-turning car. Both were hospitalized. The street offered no forgiveness. “Another vehicle attempted to make a U-turn, causing the police cruiser to swerve to avoid a collision,” reported ABC7.

      Leadership: Words and Action

      Assembly Member Charles Fall has spoken up for safer streets. He has criticized city delays and failures to redesign dangerous intersections, and has backed laws for daylighting and protected bike lanes. But the carnage continues. The policies move slow. The cars do not.

      The call is clear. “It was pretty, pretty fast and then he crashed into a sanitation car… then he’s doing circles and then he crashed out with a police car,” said a witness to a recent crash.

      What Next?

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every delay means another family broken. Call Assembly Member Fall. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns. Do not wait for another body in the road.
