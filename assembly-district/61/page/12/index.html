      Five Dead in District 61: Cars Kill, Leaders Stall

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed citation formatting, removed headline duplication, cleaned citations.)

      AD 61: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      Five people have died on the streets of Assembly District 61 since 2022. One was a child. One was an elder. Over 1,700 people have been hurt. Twenty suffered wounds so grave they may never walk the same again. Most victims were on foot, crossing with the light or waiting at the curb. The numbers do not flinch: cars and SUVs did the most harm, leaving blood on crosswalks and corners (NYC Open Data).

      On June 29, a 16-year-old riding an e-scooter was struck and killed by a Hyundai Tucson in Westerleigh. He died from head trauma. The driver stayed at the scene. There were no arrests. The street was quiet again by sundown, but a family was not (Teen E-Scooter Rider Killed In Crash).

      In January, a 68-year-old man was killed crossing Forest Avenue. An SUV turned left and hit him in the crosswalk. The cause: failure to yield, driver inattention. He never made it to the other side (NYC Open Data).

      The Voices: What We Hear on the Ground

      A witness to a crash said, “It was pretty, pretty fast and then he crashed into a sanitation car. He crashed over there and then he’s doing circles and then he crashed out with a police car.” The chaos is routine. The pain is not.

      After a city worker was slashed by a cyclist in Lower Manhattan, another witness said, “They weren’t even in the bike lane, they were parked on the corner, fixing the light or the sign or something.” The violence comes early, before most people wake. It does not wait for rush hour.

      Leadership: What Charles Fall Has—and Has Not—Done

      Assembly Member Charles Fall voted yes to extend school speed zones, a move that keeps children safer near schools (File S 8344). He has supported protected bike lanes and daylighting. He has criticized city delays and called for more accountability. But the blood still runs. The deaths still come. No law has yet stopped the turning wheels.

      What Comes Next: The Call to Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Charles Fall. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for every crosswalk, every child, every elder. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws and oversees state policies, including those affecting street safety.
      ▸ Where does AD 61 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Staten Island, city council district District 49 and state senate district SD 23.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 61?
      It includes the Financial District-Battery Park City, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, St. George-New Brighton, Tompkinsville-Stapleton-Clifton-Fox Hills, West New Brighton-Silver Lake-Grymes Hill, Westerleigh-Castleton Corners, Port Richmond, Mariner’S Harbor-Arlington-Graniteville, Snug Harbor, and Staten Island CB1. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 1, District 49, and District 50, and State Senate Districts SD 23, SD 24, SD 26, and SD 27.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 61?
      Cars and SUVs were responsible for the most harm to pedestrians in AD 61, causing 2 deaths and over 320 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths and 5 injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths and 8 injuries (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. Most deaths and injuries in AD 61 happened at intersections or crosswalks, often due to driver inattention or failure to yield. Better street design and lower speeds can prevent these tragedies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, expand protected bike lanes, enforce traffic laws that target dangerous driving, and fund safer street designs. They can also support bills that protect vulnerable road users.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      ▸

      On August 31, 2022, the Department of Transportation announced interim safety improvements for McGuinness Boulevard in Greenpoint. The street has seen 1,594 crashes, 44 cyclist injuries, 67 pedestrian injuries, and three deaths since 2013. Council Member Lincoln Restler praised the upgrades as 'a step in the right direction.' Bronwyn Breitner of Make McGuinness Safe Coalition called for a full-scale redesign, reallocating space from cars to people.
