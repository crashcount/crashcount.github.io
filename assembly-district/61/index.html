      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 61?

      Five Dead, Hundreds Broken—Staten Island Streets Still Kill

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 15, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 61: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Assembly District 61, the bodies keep coming. Since 2022, five people are dead. Seventeen more are left with injuries so serious they may never walk right again. The numbers do not flinch: 1,689 injured, 3,606 crashes in just over three years (NYC Open Data).

      The dead are not just numbers. A 68-year-old man, crossing at Forest Avenue and Raymond Place, was struck and killed by an SUV making a left turn. He was in the crosswalk. The driver failed to yield. The man never made it home (NYC Open Data).

      A year earlier, a 60-year-old man was killed crossing with the signal at Post Avenue. The car turned left. He bled out in the street (NYC Open Data).

      Streets Still Unsafe

      The violence is not random. Most deaths come from cars and SUVs. In this district, cars and SUVs killed two, left two more with serious injuries, and hurt over 300 people. Trucks and buses added dozens more injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds, a handful. Bikes, a few scrapes (NYC Open Data).

      The danger is everywhere. On March 15, two police officers crashed their cruiser into a Staten Island building while swerving to avoid a U-turning car. Both were hospitalized. The street offered no forgiveness. “Another vehicle attempted to make a U-turn, causing the police cruiser to swerve to avoid a collision,” reported ABC7.

      Leadership: Words and Action

      Assembly Member Charles Fall has spoken up for safer streets. He has criticized city delays and failures to redesign dangerous intersections, and has backed laws for daylighting and protected bike lanes. But the carnage continues. The policies move slow. The cars do not.

      The call is clear. “It was pretty, pretty fast and then he crashed into a sanitation car… then he’s doing circles and then he crashed out with a police car,” said a witness to a recent crash.

      What Next?

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every delay means another family broken. Call Assembly Member Fall. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns. Do not wait for another body in the road.

      Fall Criticizes Harmful Bedford Avenue Protected Bike Lane Removal
      ▸

      The city will rip out the protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue between Flushing and Willoughby. Cyclists lose their shield. Painted lines replace real barriers. Crash risk rises. The city ignores proven safety. Vulnerable road users pay the price.

      On June 13, 2025, Mayor Eric Adams and the Department of Transportation announced the removal of the protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue between Flushing and Willoughby Avenue. The city will replace it with a painted, unprotected lane. The official matter: 'Part of the parking-protected bike lane on a hazardous stretch of Bedford Avenue ... will be removed and replaced with a non-protected painted bike lane.' Council Member Lincoln Restler condemned the move, calling it 'a purely political decision to rip out a bike lane with no alternative.' Transportation Alternatives noted pedestrian injuries fell 10% and driver injuries 42% after the lane was installed. The safety analyst warns: 'Removing a parking-protected bike lane and replacing it with a painted lane reduces physical protection for cyclists, likely decreasing safety and discouraging cycling, especially on a hazardous stretch.' The city moves backward. Cyclists and pedestrians face more danger.

      ▸

      Third Avenue will lose car lanes. A protected bike lane and bus lane will take their place. Pedestrian islands and wider sidewalks will rise. Community Board 6 backed the plan. The city aims to calm deadly traffic and give space to people.

      On June 10, 2025, the Department of Transportation unveiled its plan to extend the Third Avenue Complete Street project from E. 59th to E. 24th Street. The DOT will install parking-protected bike lanes, painted sidewalk extensions, and pedestrian islands. The official matter summary states: 'The proposal will reduce the roadway from six or seven lanes for cars to three moving lanes, two parking lanes, plus a bus lane and a bike lane.' The safety analyst notes: 'The event text does not specify what design was implemented or how it affected pedestrians and cyclists, so no safety impact can be determined.' Installation is set for summer or fall 2025.

      Fall Opposes Harmful Federal Effort To Halt Congestion Pricing
      ▸

      Federal lawyers bark. The MTA stands firm. The U.S. DOT demands New York end congestion pricing by April 20. The state refuses. Threats fly. No action lands. Meanwhile, Manhattan streets see fewer crashes, faster buses, and more people on foot.
