      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 33?

      Five Dead, Hundreds Hurt—Who Will Stop the Blood on District 33’s Streets?

      Written July 13, 2025 (Revised July 15, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      AD 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Assembly District 33, the road does not forgive. Five people have died here in the last year. More than 800 have been hurt. Four were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the sound of sirens at midnight, the hush that falls when the street clears.

      Just weeks ago, a 39-year-old man on a scooter was killed at 116th Avenue and Nashville Boulevard. The van turned left. The man was thrown from his scooter. He died at the hospital. Police said, “As the van attempted to turn left onto Nashville Boulevard, it collided with the scooter, immediately propelling Lagredelle off the device.” [amny, 2025-06-30]

      On Hillside Avenue, a robbery suspect fleeing police on an e-bike was struck and killed by a Lexus. The chase ended in blood. “He was pronounced dead at the scene, cops said.” [New York Post, 2025-07-13]

      The Pattern Is Clear

      Crashes are not random. They cluster at intersections, at turns, at the places where people cross and drivers look away. In the last twelve months, children and elders have been hurt. One woman, 76, died as a passenger on the Cross Island Parkway. A 29-year-old man was killed crossing with the signal by a turning truck. The cause: driver inattention, unsafe turns, speed.

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      Assembly Member Clyde Vanel has voted for speed cameras and school zone protections. He backed the extension of school speed zones and the expansion of speed cameras. He co-sponsored a bill to require speed-limiting tech for repeat offenders. These are steps. But the deaths keep coming. Enforcement is slow. Cameras do not stop a driver from fleeing a scene or making a deadly turn.

      The Call

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every delay, every loophole, every unprotected crossing is a choice. Call Assembly Member Vanel. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand real consequences for repeat offenders. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents local districts like AD 33. [Open States, 2023-01-24]
      ▸ Where does AD 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 27 and state senate district SD 11. [Open States, 2023-01-24]
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 33?
      It includes the St. Albans, Hollis, Glen Oaks-Floral Park-New Hyde Park, Bellerose, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, and Queens CB13 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 23 and District 27, and State Senate Districts SD 11 and SD 14. [Open States, 2023-01-24]
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 33?
      In recent years, most pedestrian injuries and deaths were caused by Cars and Trucks (over 250 incidents), with a smaller number from Motorcycles and Mopeds (1 incident), and none from Bikes. [NYC Open Data, 2025-07-13]
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Patterns show they happen at intersections, during turns, and when drivers speed or fail to yield.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand speed camera programs, require speed-limiting tech for repeat offenders, and redesign streets to protect the most vulnerable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      A 2299
      Vanel co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 2299 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets in a year triggers forced speed control tech. Lawmakers move to curb repeat speeders. Streets demand fewer deadly risks.

      Assembly bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 16, 2025, the bill 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Primary sponsor Emily Gallagher leads a bloc of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, and others. The measure aims to clamp down on repeat speeders with mandatory speed-limiting tech. No safety analyst note was provided.
