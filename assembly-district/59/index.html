      No More Blood on Avenue L: Make Every Death the Last

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      AD 59: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Assembly District 59, the road takes its due. Sixteen people killed. Thirty-five left with injuries so severe they change a life. In just the last year, five more dead, twelve more seriously hurt. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie.

      A child, struck and killed crossing Avenue L in January. A 66-year-old man, dead at Rockaway Parkway and Seaview Avenue. A motorcyclist, thirty years old, collides with a fire truck at Flatbush and Avenue U. The FDNY says the “incident is under investigation”. The street is quiet again. The blood dries. The loss remains.

      On the Marine Parkway Bridge, a string stretched across the bike lane slashed two cyclists. One nearly lost his life. “It was an incredible amount of blood,” said Joey Eisler. The city found no crime. The danger is still there.

      Most deaths come from cars and SUVs. The data is blunt: nine killed by cars and SUVs, one by a truck, none by bikes. The street does not care who you are. It only takes.

      Leadership: Progress and Missed Chances

      Assembly Member Jaime Williams has voted for safer streets. She backed the extension of school speed zones, a move to protect children and the vulnerable. She co-sponsored bills to require complete street designs. She voted to expand speed cameras near schools. These are steps in the right direction.

      But Williams also voted to weaken bus rules, a change that puts pedestrians and cyclists at greater risk. She voted against expanding speed camera hours in 2022. She stood with those who called congestion pricing unfair, even as the toll of traffic violence rose. The record is mixed. The need is not.

      The Road Ahead: What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call Assembly Member Williams. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join Families for Safe Streets. Stand with those who have lost. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      The street remembers. So should we.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, including those that shape traffic safety and street design.[4]
      ▸ Where does AD 59 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 46 and state senate district SD 19.[4]
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 59?
      It includes the Flatlands, Marine Park-Mill Basin-Bergen Beach, Canarsie, Marine Park-Plumb Island, Mcguire Fields, Canarsie Park & Pier, Barren Island-Floyd Bennett Field, Jamaica Bay (West), Brooklyn CB56, and Brooklyn CB18 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 42 and District 46, and State Senate Districts SD 19, SD 21, and SD 22.[4]
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 59?
      Cars and Trucks: 9 deaths, 247 minor injuries, 59 moderate injuries, 7 serious injuries (total 322 incidents). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 2 minor injuries, 2 moderate injuries (total 4 incidents). Bikes: 0 deaths, 2 minor injuries, 1 moderate injury (total 3 incidents).[2]
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Policy choices—like speed limits, street design, and enforcement—decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws to hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Senate passes S 8344. School speed zone rules in New York City get extended. Lawmakers make technical fixes. The bill keeps pressure on drivers near schools. Streets stay a little safer for kids.

      Bill S 8344, titled 'Extends provisions and makes technical corrections to school speed zones in NYC; repealer,' moved through the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. Sponsored by Senator Andrew Gounardes, the bill passed Senate votes on June 12 and June 13, and cleared the Assembly on June 17. The measure extends and corrects school speed zone laws in New York City, repealing outdated provisions. Gounardes led the push, with strong support from most Senate Democrats and a split Assembly. The bill's technical fixes aim to keep protections in place for children and other vulnerable road users near schools. No safety analyst note was provided.
