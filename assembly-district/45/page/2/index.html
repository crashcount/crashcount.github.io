      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 45?

      Assembly District 45: Children Die, Politicians Stall

      Written July 7, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 45: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      In Assembly District 45, the road does not forgive. Since 2022, 15 people have died and 1,966 have been injured in crashes. Five children are among the dead. The numbers do not flinch. In the last year alone, six people were killed—three of them under 18. Ten more were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same.

      Last month, a drunk driver on Coney Island Avenue smashed into a Volvo, sending it into a police car. Two officers were left with broken bones and head wounds. A passenger was thrown from the car, left in critical condition. Police said, “One police officer suffered a broken pelvis and arm, as well as head trauma, while another suffered a broken hip” NY Daily News. The driver was charged with DWI and reckless endangerment.

      Just days ago, a driver fled a traffic stop near Ocean Parkway and Avenue X, dragging an officer with the car. The officer survived with minor injuries. Police said, “the 36-year-old suspect fled the attempted traffic stop, dragging the officer as he drove away” ABC7.

      Who Pays the Price

      The dead are not numbers. They are children crossing with the light. They are mothers holding hands. They are officers in marked cars, and passengers who never make it home. SUVs and sedans do most of the killing—nine deaths, 309 minor injuries, 89 moderate, six serious. Trucks and buses add more. Motorcycles and mopeds, too. Bikes, almost none. The violence is not random. It is the same story, told again and again.

      Leadership: Action or Excuse

      Assembly Member Misha Novakhov has stood in the way of proven safety tools. He voted against renewing New York City’s speed camera program Streetsblog NYC. He called for higher speed limits on Ocean Parkway Streetsblog NYC. He dismissed the need for speed-limiting devices, saying “six red-light or speed violations in one year [is] too little” Streetsblog NYC. He voted no on a bill to bring speed cameras to school zones in Schenectady Open States.

      He has co-sponsored bills for complete street design Open States, but has also backed measures that shift the burden to cyclists and e-scooter riders Open States. The record is mixed. The blood on the street is not.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call Assembly Member Novakhov. Demand action. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed cameras where children walk. Demand real change, not excuses. The road will not wait. Neither should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees state agencies, and represents local districts like AD 45.
      ▸ Where does AD 45 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 48 and state senate district SD 22.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 45?
      It includes the Gravesend (South), Brighton Beach, Gravesend (East)-Homecrest, Sheepshead Bay-Manhattan Beach-Gerritsen Beach, and Brooklyn CB15 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 44, District 47, and District 48, and State Senate Districts SD 22 and SD 23.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 45?
      Cars and Trucks: 9 deaths, 309 minor injuries, 89 moderate injuries, 6 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 8 minor injuries, 1 moderate injury. Bikes: No deaths, 3 minor injuries, 2 moderate injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The same patterns repeat—speeding, reckless driving, and lack of safe infrastructure. These deaths and injuries are preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can support lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws that hold dangerous drivers accountable.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
