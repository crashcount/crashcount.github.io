      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 23?

      Votes Kill. Streets Bleed. Hold Amato Accountable.

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      AD 23: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      Blood on the Parkway

      Two young lives ended on the Belt Parkway. On July 5, a BMW lost control near Cross Bay Boulevard, struck a divider, went airborne, and slammed into oncoming traffic. Flames followed. Jewel Perez, 22, and Noah Thompson, 24, died after being thrown from the car. Three others were injured. Police said, “No one in the doomed BMW was wearing a seat belt” (NY Daily News).

      A witness described the moment: “They went airborne and into the barrier” (NY Daily News). The parkway closed for hours. The city counted the dead. The families counted the cost.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the past twelve months, AD 23 saw 1,053 crashes, 3 deaths, and 13 serious injuries. In that time, 644 people were hurt. The dead are not numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. Sedans and SUVs did most of the killing and maiming.

      The violence is not new. Since 2022, 14 people have died and 24 have been seriously injured on these streets. Nearly 2,000 have been hurt. The disaster moves slow, but it does not stop (NYC Open Data).

      Leadership: Words, Votes, and Silence

      Assembly Member Stacey Pheffer Amato has taken both sides. She co-sponsored bills to make streets safer for all users. She voted yes on school speed cameras in Schenectady. But when it mattered most for New York City, she voted against renewing the city’s school zone speed camera program (Streetsblog NYC). She backed a bill to give NYPD officers a free pass on congestion pricing, a move watchdogs called a “handout” to the powerful few (Streetsblog NYC).

      The dead cannot vote. The living can.

      What Next? Demand Action Now

      Every crash is preventable. Call Assembly Member Pheffer Amato. Demand she fight for speed cameras, lower speed limits, and streets built for people, not cars. Do not wait for another body on the road.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents districts like AD 23.
      ▸ Where does AD 23 sit politically?
      It includes neighborhoods in Queens such as Ozone Park (North), Ozone Park, Howard Beach-Lindenwood, Spring Creek Park, Far Rockaway-Bayswater, Rockaway Beach-Arverne-Edgemere, Breezy Point-Belle Harbor-Rockaway Park-Broad Channel, Jamaica Bay (East), Jacob Riis Park-Fort Tilden-Breezy Point Tip, and Queens CB10. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 31 and District 32, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 15, and SD 19.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 23?
      Most deaths and injuries to pedestrians in AD 23 came from Sedans and SUVs (6 deaths, 264 injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (2 deaths, 15 injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 4 injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 4 injuries).
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 23?
      Most deaths and injuries came from Cars and Trucks (6 deaths, 264 injuries), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 4 injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 4 injuries).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Every crash is preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and support laws for lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, and demand safer street design that protects people outside cars.
