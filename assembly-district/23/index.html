      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 23?

      No More Dead Kids on the Parkway—Hold Amato Accountable Now

      Written July 16, 2025

      AD 23: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Blood on the Parkway

      Two young lives ended on the Belt Parkway. On July 5, a BMW hit the divider, vaulted into oncoming traffic, and caught fire. The driver, 24, and a passenger, 22, died. Three others were thrown from the car. Police said, “No one in the doomed BMW was wearing a seat belt” (NY Daily News). The crash left a mark on the road and on every family involved.

      Six people were hurt. The NYPD shut down the westbound lanes. The investigation continues. A survivor said, “They went airborne and into the barrier” (NY Daily News).

      The Numbers That Do Not Lie

      Since 2022, 14 people have died and 24 have been seriously injured in crashes in AD 23. Nearly 2,000 have been hurt. The violence does not slow. Cars and SUVs do the most harm—6 deaths, 7 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 2, left others broken. Motorcycles, mopeds, bikes: the toll is smaller, but the pain is the same.

      Leadership: Votes That Cut Both Ways

      Assembly Member Stacey Pheffer Amato has stood on both sides. She co-sponsored bills for safer streets. She voted to fund better designs. But when it mattered most, she voted no on extending school speed zones and keeping speed cameras running (Open States). She stood against the tools that protect children. She backed bills to weaken bus lane rules, putting walkers and riders at risk.

      What Now: The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. Every crash is a policy failure. Call Assembly Member Amato. Demand she back speed cameras, safer street designs, and laws that put people before cars. Join Families for Safe Streets. Stand up. Speak out. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like AD 23.
      ▸ Where does AD 23 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 42 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 23?
      It includes the Ozone Park (North), Ozone Park, Howard Beach-Lindenwood, Spring Creek Park, Far Rockaway-Bayswater, Rockaway Beach-Arverne-Edgemere, Breezy Point-Belle Harbor-Rockaway Park-Broad Channel, Jamaica Bay (East), Jacob Riis Park-Fort Tilden-Breezy Point Tip, and Queens CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 31 and District 32, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 15, and SD 19.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 23?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm: 6 deaths and 7 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 2 deaths and 1 serious injury. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 1 serious injury, while bikes were involved in 4 injury cases but no deaths (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are not random. Policies like speed cameras, lower speed limits, and safer street designs can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can vote for speed cameras, fund safer street designs, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking instead of just drivers.

      ▸

      Eleven city lawmakers voted no on speed cameras. Their votes keep streets exposed. Pedestrians and cyclists lose a shield. Reckless drivers win. The city’s most basic defense—rejected. The toll will be measured in blood, not words.

      On June 13 and June 17, 2025, the New York State Senate and Assembly voted on reauthorizing New York City's school zone speed camera program. The Assembly passed it 110-31, with nine city lawmakers—Alec Brook-Krasny, Lester Chang, Simcha Eichenstein, Michael Novakhov, Stacey Pheffer Amato, Sam Pirozzolo, Michael Reilly, David Weprin, Kalman Yeger—opposing. The Streetsblog NYC article, 'Ye Shall Know Their Names! Meet the Dirty Dozen City Pols Who Voted Against Speed Camera Program,' quotes lawmakers dismissing speed cameras as revenue grabs or burdens. The safety analyst notes: 'The event text does not describe a specific policy or legislative action, so no direct safety impact on pedestrians and cyclists can be assessed.'
