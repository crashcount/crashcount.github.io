      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 23?

      Blood on Amato’s Watch: Thirteen Dead, and Still No Real Reform

      Written June 20, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      AD 23: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Bodies on the Road

      In Assembly District 23, the numbers do not lie. Thirteen people killed. Twenty-three left with serious injuries. Over 1,800 hurt. The dead include a 73-year-old woman struck in a crosswalk by a turning bus. A 58-year-old cyclist, thrown from his bike, left bleeding in the street. A 27-year-old driver, ejected and killed, speed and alcohol listed in the report. These are not numbers. They are lives ended, families broken, shoes left behind on the asphalt. Crash data

      Cars and SUVs do most of the killing. Six deaths, 250 injuries. Trucks and buses, too: two deaths, 15 injuries. Bikes and motorcycles are not blameless, but the carnage comes on four wheels, not two.

      Leadership: Action and Inaction

      Assembly Member Stacey Pheffer Amato has signed on to bills that promise safer streets. She co-sponsored measures to require “complete streets” design and safe access for all users. She voted yes to expand school speed cameras. These are steps, but they are not leaps. No bill has yet forced the city to redesign the deadliest roads. No law has lowered the speed limit where the blood pools.

      But when the powerful ask for favors, Amato listens. She backed a bill to exempt NYPD officers from congestion pricing tolls, even off-duty. Watchdogs called it a $22 million giveaway. Every dollar lost is a dollar not spent on safer streets, better transit, or lives saved.

      When Governor Hochul paused congestion pricing, Amato called her own vote for it “political blackmail” and supported the halt. The money for subways and buses vanished. The risk for those outside a car grew.

      What Comes Next

      The disaster is not fate. It is policy. The tools exist: lower speed limits, redesigns, cameras that never sleep. But tools unused are as useless as promises unkept.

      Call your Assembly Member. Call your Council Member. Demand streets that do not kill. Demand action, not delay. The next victim is only a day away.

      Stacey Pheffer Amato Opposes Safety Boosting Speed Camera Program
      ▸

      Eleven city lawmakers voted no on speed cameras. Their votes keep streets exposed. Pedestrians and cyclists lose a shield. Reckless drivers win. The city’s most basic defense—rejected. The toll will be measured in blood, not words.

      On June 13 and June 17, 2025, the New York State Senate and Assembly voted on reauthorizing New York City's school zone speed camera program. The Senate passed the measure 38-21; three city senators—Stephen Chan, Andrew Lanza, Jessica Scarcella-Spanton—voted no. The Assembly passed it 110-31, with nine city lawmakers—Alec Brook-Krasny, Lester Chang, Simcha Eichenstein, Michael Novakhov, Stacey Pheffer Amato, Sam Pirozzolo, Michael Reilly, David Weprin, Kalman Yeger—opposing. The Streetsblog NYC article, 'Ye Shall Know Their Names! Meet the Dirty Dozen City Pols Who Voted Against Speed Camera Program,' quotes lawmakers dismissing speed cameras as revenue grabs or burdens. The safety analyst notes: 'The event text does not describe a specific policy or legislative action, so no direct safety impact on pedestrians and cyclists can be assessed.' Still, the votes signal disregard for proven tools that protect those outside cars.

      Pheffer Amato Calls Congestion Pricing Vote Political Blackmail
      ▸

      Governor Hochul halted congestion pricing. Lawmakers balked at raising payroll taxes. The MTA’s $15 billion plan now hangs by a thread. Projects stall. Federal funds at risk. Riders, walkers, and cyclists face uncertainty. Albany remains split. Streets stay dangerous.

      On June 6, 2024, Governor Hochul announced a pause on congestion pricing, sparking confusion in Albany. The state-level response included a rejected proposal to raise the payroll mobility tax (PMT) on NYC businesses to fill the $15 billion gap in the MTA’s capital plan. State Sen. Liz Krueger said, 'Payroll mobility taxes are actually taxes on workers... I do not think we have an appetite for that in the Senate.' Assembly Member Stacey Pheffer Amato, who once voted for congestion pricing, now supports the pause, calling the original vote 'political blackmail.' The MTA’s future projects, including the Second Avenue Subway, are threatened, as federal matching funds require local dollars. Lawmakers remain divided. No clear alternative has emerged. Vulnerable road users are left exposed as transit funding falters.
