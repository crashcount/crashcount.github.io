      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 55?

      Five Dead, Nine Broken—Brooklyn Streets Bleed While Leaders Stall

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 55: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      In Assembly District 55, the street is a gauntlet. Five dead in the last year. Nine left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. The names fade, but the numbers do not. In the past twelve months, 801 people have been hurt in 1,353 crashes. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. NYC Open Data

      On July 3rd, a 36-year-old man tried to cross Broadway at East New York Avenue. A car hit him and kept going. He died at Brookdale Hospital. The driver vanished. Police are still looking. As the victim’s family grieves, the city moves on. “A hit-and-run driver struck and killed a 36-year-old man crossing a Brooklyn street,” reported the NY Daily News. The killer is still at large.

      Two months earlier, Larry Maxwell, 72, was walking to a family cookout. A driver fleeing another crash mowed him down on Sutter Avenue. His son ran to the scene. “There are so many cameras. So why is he still at large?” asked Larnce Vargas. The answer is silence.

      Leadership: Votes and Gaps

      Assembly Member Latrice Walker has voted to extend school speed zones and expand speed cameras, moves that help shield children and families. She co-sponsored bills to require safer street designs for all users. These are steps, not solutions. In June, Walker voted to weaken bus lane rules, a move that puts pedestrians and cyclists at greater risk.

      The dead keep coming. The policies move slow.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Assembly Member Walker. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people outside cars. Join Families for Safe Streets. Stand with the living. Remember the dead. The street will not change itself.

      Act now. Or wait for another name to join the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents the people of New York State.
      ▸ Where does AD 55 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 41 and state senate district SD 19.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 55?
      It includes the Lincoln Terrace Park, Ocean Hill, Brownsville, and Brooklyn CB16 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 37, District 41, and District 42, and State Senate Districts SD 19, SD 20, and SD 25.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 55?
      Most injuries and deaths came from Sedans and SUVs (2 deaths, 303 minor injuries, 120 moderate, 5 serious), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 5 minor, 2 moderate, 2 serious), and Bikes (no deaths, 6 minor, 3 moderate, 2 serious). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The pattern is clear and relentless. These deaths and injuries are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and stronger enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, expand automated enforcement, and support laws that protect people outside cars.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Bill S 8344, titled 'Extends provisions and makes technical corrections to school speed zones in NYC; repealer,' moved through the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. Sponsored by Senator Andrew Gounardes, the bill passed Senate votes on June 12 and June 13, and cleared the Assembly on June 17. The measure extends and corrects school speed zone laws in New York City, repealing outdated provisions. Gounardes led the push, with strong support from most Senate Democrats and a split Assembly. The bill's technical fixes aim to keep protections in place for children and other vulnerable road users near schools. No safety analyst note was provided.
