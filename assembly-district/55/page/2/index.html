      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 55?

      Brooklyn Bleeds—And Politicians Stall

      Written August 6, 2025

      AD 55: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 6, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt

      In Assembly District 55, the street claims lives and leaves bodies broken. In the last twelve months, six people died. Nine hundred thirty-eight were hurt. Eleven suffered injuries so grave they may never walk the same. The dead include the old and the young. The numbers do not flinch: in just the past year, crashes rose by 31% and injuries by nearly 37% (NYC Open Data).

      The horror is not abstract. On July 3rd, a 36-year-old man was crossing near Broadway and East New York Avenue. A gray Ford hit him and kept going. He died at Brookdale Hospital. The driver vanished. “The driver of the Ford sped off, never stopping, cops said. He remained at large on Saturday” (NY Daily News).

      Just weeks before, on May 10th, Larry Maxwell, 72, was killed by a hit-and-run driver while heading to a family cookout. His son, Larnce Vargas, stood at the scene and asked, “There are so many cameras. So why is he still at large, if there’s so many cameras? Y’all can find any and everybody else, but y’all can’t find him. That don’t make sense” (NY Daily News).

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists bear the brunt. In the last year, 86 children were injured. The elderly are not spared. Cars, trucks, and buses do most of the damage. The street is a gauntlet. The pain is not spread evenly. The most vulnerable bleed the most.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Assembly Member Latrice Walker has voted for bills to extend school speed zones and expand speed cameras, moves that help protect children near schools (File S 8344). She co-sponsored bills to require safer street designs for all users (File A 1077). But she also voted to weaken bus rules, a step that puts pedestrians and cyclists at greater risk. The work is not finished. The carnage continues.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by those in power. Call Assembly Member Walker. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat dangerous drivers. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like AD 55 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does AD 55 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 41 and state senate district SD 19.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 55?
      It includes the Lincoln Terrace Park, Ocean Hill, Brownsville, and Brooklyn CB16 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 37, District 41, and District 42, and State Senate Districts SD 19, SD 20, and SD 25. (NYC Open Data)
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 55?
      Most injuries and deaths come from Cars and Trucks (462 incidents), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (9), and Bikes (11). (NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speed, hit-and-runs, and dangerous streets. These deaths and injuries are preventable with better policy and design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws that hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Latrice Walker Opposes Car Culture Normalization Supports Safer Streets
      ▸

      A new study exposes how people shrug off the deadly risks of driving. Most accept crashes and pollution as normal. The same harms spark outrage elsewhere. This double standard leaves pedestrians and cyclists in the crosshairs. The culture shields drivers, not victims.

      On January 19, 2023, researchers published a report in the Journal of Environment and Health, dissecting public attitudes toward driving dangers. The study, led by environmental psychologist Ian Walker, surveyed over 2,000 British adults. It found that 61 percent of respondents accepted driving risks as natural, while far fewer tolerated similar hazards at work or from smoking. The article, titled 'The Dangers of Driving Are Way More Normalized Than We Think,' highlights what Walker calls 'motonormativity'—a cultural blind spot that excuses harm from cars. Walker urges policy changes: 'Build a street where people can't speed... you won't have speeding.' The research shows that society’s deep-rooted car bias endangers vulnerable road users and blocks reforms that could save lives.
