      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 55?

      No More Names on Asphalt: Demand Streets That Don’t Kill

      Written July 9, 2025

      AD 55: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A man tried to cross Broadway at East New York Avenue. It was July 3rd, just before 10 p.m. A gray Ford hit him and kept going. He died at Brookdale Hospital. The driver vanished into the night. Police are still looking. This is not rare. This is the street. A hit-and-run driver struck and killed a 36-year-old man crossing a Brooklyn street, officials said Friday.

      Two months earlier, Larry Maxwell, 72, was walking to a family cookout in Brownsville. A driver fleeing another crash hit him. Maxwell died in the street. His son stood at the scene, looking for answers. “There are so many cameras. So why is he still at large, if there’s so many cameras? Y’all can find any and everybody else, but y’all can’t find him. That don’t make sense.”

      The Numbers Behind the Names

      In the past 12 months, four people have died and 703 have been injured in 1,219 crashes in AD 55. Five suffered serious injuries. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. Cars and SUVs did most of the harm, but trucks, bikes, and mopeds all left bodies broken. The dead are not numbers. They are fathers, daughters, neighbors.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Assembly Member Latrice Walker has co-sponsored bills to make streets safer for all users, including A 1077 and A 1280. She voted yes to expand school speed cameras (A 7652). These are steps. But the killings have not stopped. The streets are still deadly. Cameras and laws mean little if drivers keep fleeing and the city fails to redesign danger out of the roads.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call Assembly Member Walker. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesign. Demand action for every name lost.

      The street does not care who you are. It only takes. Do not wait for another name.

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws and oversees state policy, including traffic safety. Open States
      ▸ Where does AD 55 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 41 and state senate district SD 19. NYC Open Data

      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 55?
      Cars and SUVs: 1 death, 287 minor injuries, 114 moderate injuries, 5 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 0 deaths, 20 minor injuries, 10 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 5 minor injuries, 2 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 5 minor injuries, 3 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Most could be prevented with safer street design, lower speeds, and stronger enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass laws for lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand automated enforcement, and hold dangerous drivers accountable.

      Latrice Walker Opposes Car Culture Normalization Supports Safer Streets
      ▸

      A new study exposes how people shrug off the deadly risks of driving. Most accept crashes and pollution as normal. The same harms spark outrage elsewhere. This double standard leaves pedestrians and cyclists in the crosshairs. The culture shields drivers, not victims.

      On January 19, 2023, researchers published a report in the Journal of Environment and Health, dissecting public attitudes toward driving dangers. The study, led by environmental psychologist Ian Walker, surveyed over 2,000 British adults. It found that 61 percent of respondents accepted driving risks as natural, while far fewer tolerated similar hazards at work or from smoking. The article, titled 'The Dangers of Driving Are Way More Normalized Than We Think,' highlights what Walker calls 'motonormativity'—a cultural blind spot that excuses harm from cars. Walker urges policy changes: 'Build a street where people can't speed... you won't have speeding.' The research shows that society’s deep-rooted car bias endangers vulnerable road users and blocks reforms that could save lives.
