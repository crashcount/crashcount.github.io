      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Queens drivers keep killing. AD 25 keeps waiting.

      AD 25: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      On Sep 11 on the Clearview Expressway, a 93‑year‑old driver hit a stopped box truck and was seriously hurt. Police recorded distraction and unsafe speed by the driver (NYC Open Data).

      This Week

      • Sep 2: At 57th Road and 136th Street, a driver in a sedan hit a person walking; she was seriously hurt (NYC Open Data).
      • Jul 31: At Hollis Court Blvd and 50th Avenue, a driver turning left in an SUV hit a person on a bike; she was killed (NYC Open Data).

      The toll in this district

      Since 2022, this district has recorded 17 deaths and 2,940 injuries, including 46 serious injuries (NYC Open Data). People walking account for 4 of those deaths and 385 injuries; people on bikes for 1 death and 129 injuries (same source).

      Deadly hours stack up when the streets are full. Deaths cluster around 8 AM, early afternoon, and 6 PM in the local crash record (NYC Open Data). Named driver causes keep repeating: failure to yield, inattention, following too closely, and alcohol involvement appear throughout the severe cases here (same source).

      Hotspots are no secret: the Clearview Expressway, Long Island Expressway, and Grand Central Parkway sit at the top for injuries and deaths in this area (NYC Open Data). Main Street shows a long injury count.

      What keeps breaking people here

      • Left turns that hit people in crosswalks. Police recorded failure to yield by the driver at Union Turnpike and 164th Street in a bike–bus crash this spring (NYC Open Data).
      • The afternoon squeeze. Multiple deaths in the 1–2 PM hours; kids walking home are in this window too, whether the state logs it or not (NYC Open Data).
      • High‑speed corridors. The expressways dominate the worst outcomes in the district (same source).

      Proven local fixes are simple: daylight corners and ban parking at crosswalks; give pedestrians a head start (LPIs); harden left turns with slow‑turn geometry; slow the expressway service roads; route and manage trucks away from schools. Target enforcement for failure to yield and distraction at the listed hotspots.

      The people paid to act

      Assembly Member Nily Rozic represents this district. She is a co‑sponsor of A 2299, a bill to require intelligent speed assistance for repeat speeders (Open States). State lawmakers also moved to extend and clean up NYC school speed‑zone provisions in S 8344 in June 2025 (Open States).

      Citywide, the next step is not theory. Lower the default speed limit on residential streets. Then pass speed limiters for the worst repeat offenders. Both actions match the pattern of harm on these blocks and at these hours. The tools are on the table. The numbers do not wait.

      Take one step today. Tell your officials to act on lower speeds and speed limiters /take_action/.

      SUV Turns Left, Strikes Elderly Pedestrian
      ▸

      Mar 13 - A Chevy SUV turned left on Franklin Avenue. The driver failed to yield. The front end hit a 67-year-old man crossing with the signal. He suffered head and crush injuries. He lay conscious beneath the grill. The light stayed green.

      A 67-year-old man was crossing Franklin Avenue with the signal when a Chevy SUV made a left turn and struck him. According to the police report, 'the front end struck his head. He lay crushed and conscious beneath the grill, the light still green.' The pedestrian suffered head and crush injuries. The report lists 'Failure to Yield Right-of-Way' as the contributing factor. The driver, a 72-year-old man, was licensed and wore a lap belt. No injuries were reported for the vehicle occupants. The crash highlights the danger when drivers fail to yield to pedestrians who have the right of way.
