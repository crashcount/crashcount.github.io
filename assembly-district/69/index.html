      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 69?

      Lasher’s Watch: Three Dead, Sixteen Broken—Who’s Next?

      Written July 19, 2025

      AD 69: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 19, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Crashes in AD 69

      Three people killed. Sixteen left with serious injuries. In the last twelve months, 478 crashes tore through Assembly District 69. The dead include a child, a teenager, and an older woman. The numbers do not flinch. They do not grieve. They only rise.

      Just last month, a 43-year-old cyclist died after colliding with a pedestrian in Central Park. The news was blunt: A 43-year-old cyclist died after colliding with a pedestrian in Central Park, according to the NYPD. No details on the pedestrian. No comfort for the families. Only the silence that follows sirens.

      A 72-year-old woman was hit twice by cyclists on Central Park sidewalks. She suffered kidney damage. The police never came. “Ninety percent of their crashes have no police report and in 90 percent of these crashes over 90 percent the rider flees the scene,” said Janet Schroeder. The city’s own numbers are shadows. The pain is real.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. SUVs and cars did the most harm: 3 deaths, 3 serious injuries, and 112 more hurt. Trucks and buses left another dozen injured or worse. Bikes and mopeds, too, left scars—one serious injury, 23 more hurt. The street does not care who you are. It only takes.

      What Has Lasher Done?

      Assembly Member Micah Lasher voted to extend and expand school speed cameras, a move that keeps children safer near schools. He co-sponsored a bill to force repeat speeders to install speed-limiting tech. He voted yes on measures to protect kids, but also voted to weaken bus lane rules—putting pedestrians and cyclists at risk. The record is mixed. The danger is not.

      The Next Step: No More Waiting

      Every day without action is another day of blood on the street. Call Assembly Member Lasher. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for the most vulnerable. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents districts like AD 69.
      ▸ Where does AD 69 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 7 and state senate district SD 30.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 69?
      It includes the Upper West Side (Central), Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Morningside Heights, Central Park, Manhattan CB7, Manhattan CB64, and Manhattan CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 6 and District 7, and State Senate Districts SD 30 and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 69?
      SUVs and cars caused the most harm: 3 deaths, 3 serious injuries, and 112 more injured. Trucks and buses caused 1 serious injury and 11 more injuries. Bikes caused 1 serious injury and 13 more injuries. Mopeds caused 9 injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not random acts of fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, fund safer street redesigns, and pass laws to rein in repeat dangerous drivers.

      A 2299
      Lasher co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 2299 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets in a year triggers forced speed control tech. Lawmakers move to curb repeat speeders. Streets demand fewer deadly risks.

      Assembly bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 16, 2025, the bill 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Primary sponsor Emily Gallagher leads a bloc of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, and others. The measure aims to clamp down on repeat speeders with mandatory speed-limiting tech. No safety analyst note was provided.
