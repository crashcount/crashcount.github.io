      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 69?

      Blood on the Boulevard: How Many Deaths Before Albany Acts?

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 12, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      AD 69: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Assembly District 69, the numbers do not lie. Eight people killed. Sixteen left with serious injuries. Nearly 800 hurt since 2022. The dead include a 13-year-old girl, a 69-year-old woman, a baby. The living carry scars you cannot see.

      Just weeks ago, a 43-year-old cyclist died after colliding with a pedestrian in Central Park. The news was blunt: “A 43-year-old cyclist died after colliding with a pedestrian in Central Park, according to the NYPD.” reported Gothamist.

      On the streets, SUVs and taxis do most of the damage. SUVs alone killed three pedestrians and left dozens more injured. Bikes, mopeds, and trucks add to the toll. No one is spared. Not the old, not the young.

      Policy and Leadership: What Has Changed?

      Assembly Member Micah Lasher has taken a stand. He co-sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act, a bill that would force repeat dangerous drivers to install speed limiters on their cars according to Open States. The bill targets drivers who rack up ticket after ticket and keep driving. It is a start, but it is not law yet.

      Lasher has also called for regional funding for transit, pushing back on plans that would leave city residents footing the bill while the suburbs ride free as reported by Streetsblog NYC. But on the street, the blood keeps flowing. Every week brings another crash.

      The System Fails the Most Vulnerable

      The city cracks down on cyclists and e-bike riders. The summonses pile up. But the real danger comes from cars and trucks. “It is unjust to hold cyclists to a far higher standard than motor vehicle drivers,” said Manhattan’s Community Board 7. The message is clear: the system protects the strong and punishes the weak.

      What You Can Do

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Assembly Member Lasher. Demand he push the Stop Super Speeders Act to a vote. Tell him to fight for a 20 mph speed limit on every street. Join Families for Safe Streets and Transportation Alternatives. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents the people of New York in state government. Open States, A2299
      ▸ Where does AD 69 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 7 and state senate district SD 30. Open States, A2299
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 69?
      It includes the Upper West Side (Central), Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Morningside Heights, Central Park, Manhattan CB7, Manhattan CB64, and Manhattan CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 6 and District 7, and State Senate Districts SD 30 and SD 47. Open States, A2299
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 69?
      Cars and Trucks: SUVs and sedans caused the most harm, with SUVs alone responsible for 3 deaths and dozens of injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: Mopeds and motorcycles caused several injuries. Bikes: Bicycles were involved in a smaller number of incidents, with one serious injury. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and holding repeat dangerous drivers accountable can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws like the Stop Super Speeders Act, lower speed limits, and invest in safer street design that protects people walking and biking.

      A 2299
      Lasher co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 2299 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets in a year triggers forced speed control tech. Lawmakers move to curb repeat speeders. Streets demand fewer deadly risks.

      Assembly bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 16, 2025, the bill 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Primary sponsor Emily Gallagher leads a bloc of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, and others. The measure aims to clamp down on repeat speeders with mandatory speed-limiting tech. No safety analyst note was provided.
