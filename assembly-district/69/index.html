      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 69?

      Eight Dead, No Answers: Speed Kills, Leaders Stall

      Written August 6, 2025 (Revised August 6, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 69: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 6, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Assembly District 69, the numbers do not lie. Eight people killed. Twenty-one left with serious injuries. Since 2022, there have been 1,941 crashes. The dead include a 13-year-old girl struck by an SUV at Cathedral Parkway and Manhattan Avenue. A 69-year-old woman killed by a turning SUV at 96th and Amsterdam. A cyclist, age 43, died after colliding with a pedestrian in Central Park just this June, as reported by Gothamist. The list goes on. The street forgets no one.

      “Ninety percent of their crashes have no police report and in 90 percent of these crashes over 90 percent the rider flees the scene,” said Janet Schroeder after being hit twice by cyclists in Central Park. She was left with kidney damage and no follow-up from police. “It is so much worse than the DOT stats show,” she said. “They do have correct stats on those killed but completely skewed stats on those injured.”

      The Policy Response: Progress and Gaps

      Assembly Member Micah Lasher has taken steps. He voted to extend and expand school speed cameras, aiming to protect children at crossings. He co-sponsored a bill to require speed-limiting devices for repeat dangerous drivers, as shown in File S 8344. But he also voted to weaken bus lane rules, a move that puts more pedestrians and cyclists at risk, according to Streetsblog NYC. The work is not done. The carnage continues.

      The Relentless Math

      SUVs and cars are the main killers. They caused 3 deaths and 4 serious injuries to pedestrians. Bikes, mopeds, and trucks also left their mark, but the steel and speed of cars do the most harm. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, children, neighbors. They are the cost of delay.

      What Now?

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day the city delays lowering speed limits, every time enforcement misses the mark, another family pays. Call Assembly Member Lasher. Demand he fight for a 20 mph citywide speed limit and real accountability for repeat offenders. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like AD 69.
      ▸ Where does AD 69 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 7 and state senate district SD 30.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 69?
      It includes the Upper West Side (Central), Upper West Side-Manhattan Valley, Morningside Heights, Central Park, Manhattan CB7, Manhattan CB64, and Manhattan CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 6 and District 7, and State Senate Districts SD 30 and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 69?
      SUVs and Cars: 3 deaths, 4 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 1 serious injury. Mopeds and Motorcycles: 0 deaths, 0 serious injuries. Cars and SUVs are the main killers.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The patterns are clear and preventable. Policy and design choices make these deaths and injuries possible.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, and pass laws to keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road. They can demand street redesigns that protect people, not just cars.

      A 2299
      Lasher co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 2299 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets in a year triggers forced speed control tech. Lawmakers move to curb repeat speeders. Streets demand fewer deadly risks.

      Assembly bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 16, 2025, the bill 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Primary sponsor Emily Gallagher leads a bloc of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, and others. The measure aims to clamp down on repeat speeders with mandatory speed-limiting tech. No safety analyst note was provided.
