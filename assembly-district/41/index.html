      Eight Dead, Two Thousand Hurt. Yeger Blocks Safety. Who’s Next?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed link formatting, removed headline from body, deduped and cleaned citations.)

      AD 41: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Numbers Don’t Lie

      Eight dead. Twenty-three left with serious injuries. In just over three years, Assembly District 41 has seen 2,924 crashes—nearly three a day. Over 2,000 people hurt. The dead include the old, the young, the ones who never saw it coming. A 77-year-old woman crushed by a truck at Avenue P and East 19th Street. A 35-year-old cyclist thrown from his bike at Nostrand and Avenue R. A 57-year-old passenger killed in a tangle of sedans and SUVs on the Belt Parkway. The numbers are cold. The pain is not.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and SUVs killed at least two people on foot, leaving dozens more broken. Trucks and buses killed two. Bikes and mopeds are not spared. The street is a gauntlet. The sidewalk is no refuge. Every day, another ambulance. Every week, another family changed forever.

      Leadership: Action and Absence

      Assembly Member Kalman Yeger has stood in the way of proven safety measures. He opposed protected bike lanes, arguing for more enforcement before any new lanes are built. He voted against expanding 24/7 speed cameras, which have been shown to cut speeding and injuries (speed cameras). He has resisted congestion pricing, which would have reduced dangerous car traffic. When a cyclist was killed in Borough Park, Yeger and others called for more enforcement but not safer streets (called for more enforcement).

      The silence is loud. The delays are deadly. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has the tools to build real protection for people outside cars. But the will is missing.

      “Cameras will be 24/7 the whole year.” — DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez (DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez)

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Residents can demand better. Call Yeger. Call the Mayor. Demand 20 mph limits. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand that every child, every elder, every walker and rider gets home alive. Don’t wait for another name to become a number.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees state agencies, and represents local districts like AD 41.
      ▸ Where does AD 41 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn and city council district District 46.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 41?
      It includes the Madison neighborhood. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 45, District 46, and District 48, and State Senate Districts SD 21, SD 22, and SD 23.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 41?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians in AD 41, with 2 deaths and 6 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 2 deaths and 1 serious injury. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no deaths and no serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 2 moderate injuries, but no deaths or serious injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, protected lanes, and enforcement save lives—when leaders act.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can support lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, expand speed cameras, and push for street designs that protect people outside cars.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Yeger Opposes Bike Lanes Without Prior Enforcement Measures
      ▸

      DOT will add a mid-block crossing and concrete islands to Parkside Avenue’s bike lane after a truck killed Kala Santiago. The fix removes parking for safety. Advocates demand more protected lanes. Council Member Yeger opposes, citing parking and enforcement.

      On June 19, 2023, the Department of Transportation announced upgrades to the Parkside Avenue protected bike lane. The project, managed by Lauren Martin, adds a mid-block crossing and concrete pedestrian islands after cyclist Kala Santiago was killed by a truck at a dangerous crossing. The DOT had installed a two-way protected lane in 2021 but left a key crossing unsafe. The new fix will remove parking spots to improve safety for cyclists and pedestrians. DOT also proposed painted bike lanes in Flatbush, Midwood, and Kensington, but not protected lanes, despite high crash rates. Community Board 14 members and advocates urged quick action. Council Member Kalman Yeger opposed all bike lanes in his district, arguing for enforcement before installation. DOT plans to finish the Parkside Avenue upgrade this summer and the wider bike network by next year.
