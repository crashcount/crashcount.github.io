      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 77?

      No More Names: End Bronx Traffic Deaths Now

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 77: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt

      In Assembly District 77, the numbers do not lie. Twelve people have died on these streets since 2022. Eighteen hundred more have been injured. Twenty-one suffered wounds so grave they may never walk the same again. The dead do not get a second chance. The living carry scars.

      Just this spring, Kelvin Mitchell was killed by a hit-and-run driver on Webster Avenue. His sister stood on the sidewalk and said, “They killed my son. I need justice for my son.” The car did not stop. The street did not forgive. The community is left with a hole that cannot be filled.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs do most of the harm. Since 2022, they have killed four people and injured hundreds more. Trucks and buses took another life. Motorcycles and mopeds left fourteen people hurt. Bikes injured six. The numbers are cold, but the pain is not. Every crash is a family changed forever.

      Leadership: Votes and Silence

      Assembly Member Landon Dais has voted to extend and expand school speed zones and cameras, boosting pedestrian safety. He said yes to more protection for children near schools. But Dais also voted to weaken bus rules, a move that increases risk for people on foot and on bikes.

      The work is not done. Every day without stronger laws is another day of blood on the street. As one mother said after her son was killed, “I just believe that people should just be more cautious, because now a family is missing a loved one and it’s just not fair.”

      What Now?

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Assembly Member Dais. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, sets budgets, and represents local districts like AD 77.
      ▸ Where does AD 77 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 16 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 77?
      It includes the Concourse-Concourse Village, Highbridge, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Claremont Park, University Heights (South)-Morris Heights, Bronx CB5, and Bronx CB4 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8, District 14, and District 16, and State Senate Districts SD 29, SD 31, SD 32, and SD 33.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 77?
      Cars and SUVs killed 5 people and injured 259 pedestrians. Motorcycles and mopeds injured 14. Bikes injured 6. Trucks and buses were also involved in fatal and injurious crashes. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Policy, speed limits, and enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to reduce traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, and support laws that hold dangerous drivers accountable.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Bill S 8344, titled 'Extends provisions and makes technical corrections to school speed zones in NYC; repealer,' moved through the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. Sponsored by Senator Andrew Gounardes, the bill passed Senate votes on June 12 and June 13, and cleared the Assembly on June 17. The measure extends and corrects school speed zone laws in New York City, repealing outdated provisions. Gounardes led the push, with strong support from most Senate Democrats and a split Assembly. The bill's technical fixes aim to keep protections in place for children and other vulnerable road users near schools. No safety analyst note was provided.
