      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 36?

      No More Body Bags: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written July 3, 2025

      AD 36: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 3, 2025

      Blood on the Streets: The Human Cost

      In Astoria and Long Island City, the streets do not forgive. In the last twelve months, two people have died and 569 have been injured in crashes across Assembly District 36 (NYC Open Data). These are not just numbers. They are children with broken bones, cyclists thrown from bikes, and families left waiting at hospital doors.

      On October 22, 2024, a pickup truck fleeing police struck and killed Amanda Servedio, a cyclist returning from a ride. She “went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” said witness Jake Kwok. The chase ended, but the damage did not. Her father said, “We were certainly upset that they didn’t stop the chase… It was probably not the place to be doing a high-speed chase, in the residential neighborhood” (NY Daily News).

      Just months later, an unlicensed driver reversed onto the sidewalk outside a local school, hitting two girls and a man. A seven-year-old was left with a broken femur and a head injury. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment and driving without a license (New York Post).

      No one is safe. Not on the sidewalk. Not on a bike. Not even at the school gate.

      Leadership: Action and Silence

      Assembly Member Zohran Mamdani has spoken for safer streets. He has backed bills for protected bike lanes, faster and fare-free buses, and higher fees for heavy vehicles (Streetsblog NYC). He has supported the “Stop Super Speeders Act” to rein in the worst repeat offenders. He called for street redesigns and bus lane expansion (Streetsblog NYC).

      But the carnage continues. Policies move slow. Asphalt does not wait. Every delay is another family broken.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      The crisis is not fate. It is policy. The city can lower speed limits. It can redesign streets. It can end police chases through crowded neighborhoods. It can build real protection for people outside cars.

      Call Assembly Member Mamdani. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and sidewalks that do not end in blood.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the state budget, and represents the people of New York in Albany.
      ▸ Where does AD 36 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 22 and state senate district SD 12.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 36?
      It includes the Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Astoria Park, Long Island City-Hunters Point, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 22 and District 26, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 36?
      Most injuries to pedestrians came from cars and trucks (307 incidents), followed by motorcycles and mopeds (23), and bikes (8) (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not inevitable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, fund and build protected bike lanes, redesign dangerous intersections, and pass laws to hold reckless drivers accountable.
