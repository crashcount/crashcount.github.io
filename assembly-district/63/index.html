      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 63?

      AD 63: Bay Street, Gulf Avenue, and the cost of looking away

      Written August 24, 2025 (Revised August 24, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 63: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 24, 2025

      Another driver. Same ending.

      • On Bay Street at Norwood, a 34‑year‑old motorcyclist hit the driver‑side door of a Toyota making a K‑turn. He died at the hospital. Police said the SUV driver was making a K‑turn across lanes when it happened. amNY linked the scene and the man’s name to the crash. City data confirm the time, place, and injuries. The NYPD Collision Investigation Squad took the case. NYC Open Data lists the factors as “Unsafe Speed” and “Making U Turn.”

      • On Targee Street at Pierce, an e‑scooter struck a 58‑year‑old man crossing. He died. The rider was ejected. Both were taken to Richmond University Medical Center. No arrests at the time. The Highway District is investigating. That is what police told amNY. City records mark “Unsafe Speed.” NYC Open Data shows the pedestrian killed and the rider injured.

      • At 546 Gulf Ave., a box truck backed up and killed a 34‑year‑old worker. The driver stayed. No charges then. Investigators took over. That’s per amNY and the matching crash file in NYC Open Data.

      The names shift. The pattern does not.

      Where the street bites

      The worst harm clusters on corridors we all know. The Staten Island Expressway logged 412 injuries. NYC Open Data. Victory Boulevard shows 87 injuries; Victory Blvd another 78 with multiple serious injuries. Forest Avenue holds deaths and dozens hurt. These are the places where speed and size meet bone.

      Nights cut deep. From 7 p.m. to 11 p.m., AD 63 saw spikes, including deaths at 7 p.m., 9 p.m., and 10 p.m. Early hours hurt too: deaths at 4, 5, and 6 a.m. NYC Open Data. The clock tells the truth.

      Heavy machines do heavy damage. Trucks and buses killed four pedestrians here; cars and SUVs killed three. District stats show trucks and buses behind seven of the most severe pedestrian outcomes citywide for this district, with SUVs and sedans close behind. Pedestrians are not safe against steel.

      What the numbers say

      In this district since 2022: pedestrians killed, cyclists hurt, riders ejected, workers crushed. The mode split tells it straight: Pedestrians — 10 dead, 314 injured. Cyclists — 1 dead, 50 hurt. People inside vehicles — 7 dead, 2,056 hurt. NYC Open Data.

      Speed threads through it. “Unsafe Speed” appears in fatal files on Bay Street and Targee Street. Across the district, speed sits inside the catch‑all “other” that leads deaths and injuries. NYC Open Data.

      This year is worse. Year‑to‑date crashes are up 26% over last year. Deaths up from 2 to 5. Injuries up 69%. District stats compiled from NYC Open Data.

      Officials know what works — do they?

      At City Hall, leaders staged a crush of unregistered mopeds and e‑bikes on Staten Island. A bulldozer rolled. “This morning is not just about crushing mopeds. It is about crushing the criminal activity and quality of life activities that come with them,” said Police Commissioner Jessica Tisch. Gothamist. The mayor said mopeds “terrorized many of our pedestrians.” amNY.

      But when Albany moved to extend and clean up school‑zone speed rules, Assembly Member Sam Pirozzolo voted no. Twice recorded in June. S 8344 docket and Streetsblog NYC. The same article lists him among city lawmakers who opposed speed cameras — tools that catch speed before the sirens and the chalk.

      Three corners. One fix.

      • Victory Boulevard and Forest Avenue need daylight and slow turns. People die when drivers cut left across them. The files show deaths and serious injuries on both. NYC Open Data.
      • Gulf Avenue needs hard rules for trucks that back. No blind moves without a spotter. A man died under a box truck there. amNY.
      • Bay Street needs turn protection where K‑turns and U‑turns cross live lanes. A rider hit a door and never came home. amNY and NYC Open Data.

      Slow the cars. Stop the worst.

      The tools are on the table. A lower city speed limit saves lives. A law to force speed limiters on repeat offenders targets the few who do the most harm. Our own call to action lays it out and cites the bills. Read it. Then act. Take Action.

      Names become numbers. Corners become crosses. Slow it down. Stop the repeat speeders.

      Pirozzolo Criticizes Safety Boosting Speed Camera Program
      ▸

      Jun 23 - Eleven city lawmakers voted no on speed cameras. Their votes keep streets exposed. Pedestrians and cyclists lose a shield. Reckless drivers win. The city’s most basic defense—rejected. The toll will be measured in blood, not words.

      On June 13 and June 17, 2025, the New York State Senate and Assembly voted on reauthorizing New York City's school zone speed camera program. The Senate passed the measure 38-21; three city senators—Stephen Chan, Andrew Lanza, Jessica Scarcella-Spanton—voted no. The Assembly passed it 110-31, with nine city lawmakers—Alec Brook-Krasny, Lester Chang, Simcha Eichenstein, Michael Novakhov, Stacey Pheffer Amato, Sam Pirozzolo, Michael Reilly, David Weprin, Kalman Yeger—opposing. The Streetsblog NYC article, 'Ye Shall Know Their Names! Meet the Dirty Dozen City Pols Who Voted Against Speed Camera Program,' quotes lawmakers dismissing speed cameras as revenue grabs or burdens. The safety analyst notes: 'The event text does not describe a specific policy or legislative action, so no direct safety impact on pedestrians and cyclists can be assessed.' Still, the votes signal disregard for proven tools that protect those outside cars.
