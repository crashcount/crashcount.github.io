      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 63?

      Pirozzolo Blocks Safety—Staten Island Families Pay the Price

      Written July 9, 2025

      AD 63: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      Blood on Bay Street, Silence in Albany

      Three dead. Nine seriously hurt. That is the toll in Assembly District 63 in the past year alone (NYC Open Data). The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do. On July 5th, Jeremy Claudio, age 34, was killed when his motorcycle struck the side of a Toyota making a K-turn on Bay Street. He died in the hospital. The driver stayed at the scene. There were no arrests. The NYPD is still investigating (Motorcyclist Dies In Staten Island K-Turn Crash).

      Just weeks before, Jose Luis Gomez-Guallazaca, 58, was crossing Targee Street. An e-scooter hit him. He died at Richmond University Medical Center. The driver was thrown from the scooter. No arrests. The investigation drags on (E-Scooter Kills Pedestrian On Staten Island).

      Each crash is a life ended or broken. Each is a warning.

      The Numbers Behind the Names

      In the last twelve months, 681 people were injured in crashes here. Twelve were seriously hurt. Three died. Children, elders, workers. Most were hit by cars, SUVs, or trucks. The dead do not get a second chance (NYC Open Data).

      Pedestrians are not safe. They are struck by sedans, SUVs, trucks, buses, and even bikes. The numbers are cold: cars and SUVs killed three, trucks and buses killed five, bikes injured five (NYC Open Data).

      Leadership: Votes Against Safety

      Assembly Member Sam Pirozzolo has not led on safety. He voted against speed camera programs—even those meant to protect children near schools (Ye Shall Know Their Names! Meet the Dirty Dozen City Pols Who Voted Against Speed Camera Program, File A 7652). When the Assembly voted to renew New York City’s speed camera program, Pirozzolo said no. When asked about illegal mopeds and scooters, the mayor said, “New Yorkers have strong feelings about illegal mopeds and scooters, because we hear it all the time, especially when they are driving the wrong way down streets or sidewalks or in the dark without lights.” But in Albany, Pirozzolo’s record is clear: he stands in the way of proven tools.

      The Cost of Delay

      Every day without action is another day of risk. The dead cannot call their representatives. The living must. Demand lower speed limits. Demand speed cameras at every school. Demand leadership that puts people before politics.

      Call Assembly Member Sam Pirozzolo. Tell him: Enough.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like AD 63 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does AD 63 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Staten Island, city council district District 50 and state senate district SD 23.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 63?
      It includes the Tompkinsville-Stapleton-Clifton-Fox Hills, Rosebank-Shore Acres-Park Hill, West New Brighton-Silver Lake-Grymes Hill, Westerleigh-Castleton Corners, Mariner’S Harbor-Arlington-Graniteville, Todt Hill-Emerson Hill-Lighthouse Hill-Manor Heights, New Springville-Willowbrook-Bulls Head-Travis, Freshkills Park (North), Staten Island CB1, and Staten Island CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 49, District 50, and District 51, and State Senate Districts SD 23 and SD 24.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 63?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm to pedestrians in AD 63: 3 deaths and 243 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no pedestrian deaths. Bikes injured 5 pedestrians, but caused no deaths (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. The same deadly patterns repeat year after year.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can support speed cameras, lower speed limits, fund safer street redesigns, and push for laws that hold dangerous drivers accountable.
