      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 68?

      Another Body on the Asphalt: East Harlem Streets Are Still Killing Us

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 24, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 68: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Toll in East Harlem and Central Park

      The bodies keep coming. In the last twelve months, five people died on the streets of Assembly District 68. Ten more were left with serious injuries. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors. They are the man crushed by an SUV on Harlem River Drive. They are the pedestrian struck down at the RFK Bridge exit. They are the cyclist who never made it home from Central Park.

      Just last month, a 43-year-old man on an e-bike collided with a pedestrian in Central Park. He flew from his bike and struck his head on the curb. He died at the hospital. The pedestrian walked away with a cut hand. The police said, “Nico-Garcia was then flung from his bike and struck his head on the curb.” No arrests. No comfort. Only the sound of sirens and the scrape of shoes on pavement.

      In January, a 53-year-old man named Willow Hall tried to cross Harlem River Drive. An unlicensed driver in a black Nissan SUV hit him. Hall died. The driver stayed at the scene. It took police six months to make an arrest. “Donald Pinnock was charged Sunday with aggravated unlicensed operation of a motor vehicle.”

      Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In this district, they took four lives and left over 350 people hurt. Trucks and buses killed three more. Bikes and mopeds, one each. The old, the young, the ones just trying to cross the street—they pay the price. The numbers do not lie: In the last year, 747 people were injured in 1,410 crashes.

      What Has Eddie Gibbs Done?

      Assembly Member Eddie Gibbs has voted to extend school speed zones and expand speed cameras. He co-sponsored bills to require safer street designs. He voted for more driver education. He voted yes to extend school speed zones, improving child pedestrian safety. He co-sponsored a bill boosting street safety for all users.

      But Gibbs also voted to weaken bus rules, a move that puts pedestrians and cyclists at greater risk. The work is not done. The streets are not safe. The bodies keep coming.

      What Now? Demand More

      Call Assembly Member Gibbs. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns. Demand action against repeat dangerous drivers.

      Every day we wait, someone else does not come home. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Make them choose life.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws and oversees state policy, including traffic safety.
      ▸ Where does AD 68 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 8 and state senate district SD 29. (Source: NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 68?
      It includes the East Harlem (South), East Harlem (North), Randall’S Island, Central Park, Manhattan CB64, and Manhattan CB11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 6, District 8, and District 9, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 30.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 68?
      Cars and SUVs: 4 deaths, over 350 injuries. Trucks and buses: 3 deaths. Bikes and mopeds: 1 death each. (Source: NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speeds, and stronger enforcement. They are not random—they are the result of choices.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, expand speed cameras, and pass laws to hold dangerous drivers accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
