      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 58?

      Left Turning, Lives Ending: Who Will Stop the Killing in AD 58?

      Written July 19, 2025 (Revised July 24, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, and cleaned citations.)

      AD 58: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 19, 2025

      Death in the Crosswalk

      Just two months ago, Maurette Lafleur, 68, stepped into the crosswalk at Rutland Road and E. 95th Street. She had the walk signal. A Mercedes turned left and struck her. She died at Kings County Hospital. The driver stayed. No charges were filed that day. A witness stood on the curb and described the scene: “The lady spin around and sped through.” She heard bones break. The street swallowed another life.

      The Numbers Behind the Names

      In the last twelve months, four people have died in crashes in AD 58. Nine were seriously injured. There have been 1,236 crashes—almost four every day. Pedestrians and cyclists bear the brunt. Cars and SUVs killed four. Trucks and buses killed two. Motorcycles and mopeds, one. The numbers do not flinch. They do not grieve. They only rise.

      Leadership: Votes and Silence

      Assembly Member Monique Chandler-Waterman has voted to expand school speed cameras, a move that boosts safety for children. She also voted to weaken bus lane rules, a change that increases risk for people on foot and bike. When it came time to extend protections for school speed zones, she missed the vote. Every delay, every loophole, every missed vote leaves the most vulnerable exposed.

      The Cost of Waiting

      A witness to Lafleur’s death pleaded for action: “The government should do something, like when people are walking, no cars should be moving.” The plea is simple. The answer is not. The bodies keep coming. The numbers keep climbing.

      Call your Assembly Member. Demand a default 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against reckless drivers, not crackdowns on cyclists and pedestrians. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws and oversees state policy, including traffic safety.
      ▸ Where does AD 58 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 45 and state senate district SD 19.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 58?
      It includes the East Flatbush-Farragut, East Flatbush-Rugby, East Flatbush-Remsen Village, Holy Cross Cemetery, Canarsie, and Brooklyn CB17 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 41, District 42, District 45, and District 46, and State Senate Districts SD 19, SD 20, and SD 21.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 58?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm: 4 deaths and 419 injuries. Trucks and buses were involved in 2 deaths and 32 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 6 injuries, and bikes in 2 injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and real enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, and fund safer street designs that protect people walking and biking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
