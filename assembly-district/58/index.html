      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 58?

      Blood on Rutland Road—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned citations, and ensured correct link formatting.)

      AD 58: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      Four people killed. Eighty-eight seriously hurt. That is the toll in Assembly District 58 in the last year alone. The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do. On May 14, Maurette Lafleur, age 68, crossed Rutland Road in the crosswalk with the light. A Mercedes driver turned left and struck her. She died at Kings County Hospital. The driver stayed. No charges. A witness described the horror: “I heard the van run over her and her bones crunching.”

      A week earlier, a school principal rolled his car on Lenox Road after hitting parked cars. He refused a breath test. He was charged with DWI. The city called it an incident. The street called it a warning. He told police, “He only had one drink.”

      The Policies and the Gaps

      Assembly Member Monique Chandler-Waterman has voted for more speed cameras near schools and for expanding their reach. She backed funding for safer street designs. But when it mattered most, she missed the vote to extend school speed zone protections (see bill S 8344). She also voted to weaken bus lane rules, a move that puts people on foot and bike at greater risk. The record is mixed. The danger is not.

      The Pattern Is the Policy

      Most victims here are not in cars. They are walking. They are old, young, or just unlucky. Cars and SUVs do most of the killing. Trucks and vans take their share. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not used it. The dead do not get a second chance.

      What You Can Do

      Call Assembly Member Chandler-Waterman. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking. Do not wait for another name on the list. The street does not care who you are. The law should.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws and oversees state policy, including traffic safety.
      ▸ Where does AD 58 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 45 and state senate district SD 19.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 58?
      It includes the East Flatbush-Farragut, East Flatbush-Rugby, East Flatbush-Remsen Village, Holy Cross Cemetery, Canarsie, and Brooklyn CB17 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 41, District 42, District 45, and District 46, and State Senate Districts SD 19, SD 20, and SD 21.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 58?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 6 deaths and 419 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 6 injuries. Bikes were involved in 2 injuries.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, expand automated enforcement, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Bill S 8344, titled 'Extends provisions and makes technical corrections to school speed zones in NYC; repealer,' moved through the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. Sponsored by Senator Andrew Gounardes, the bill passed Senate votes on June 12 and June 13, and cleared the Assembly on June 17. The measure extends and corrects school speed zone laws in New York City, repealing outdated provisions. Gounardes led the push, with strong support from most Senate Democrats and a split Assembly. The bill's technical fixes aim to keep protections in place for children and other vulnerable road users near schools. No safety analyst note was provided.
