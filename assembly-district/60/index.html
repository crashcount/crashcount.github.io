      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 60?

      Graves in the Crosswalk: Deadly Streets, Absent Courage

      Written August 11, 2025 (Revised August 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned up duplicate citations.)

      AD 60: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 11, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Deaths in AD 60

      Just last month, a 21-year-old woman was killed in the Gateway Center parking lot. She was sitting on the curb when her boyfriend lost control of his car while doing donuts. The car struck her, and she died at the hospital. Police charged him with criminally negligent homicide, reckless endangerment, and reckless driving. Days later, the tire marks were still there. As police reported, “He lost control when he was doing donuts with the vehicle.”

      This is not rare. In the last 12 months, five people have died and over 1,100 have been injured in crashes in this district. Fourteen suffered injuries so severe they may never recover. The dead include pedestrians, drivers, and children. The numbers do not stop. They climb.

      Patterns of Harm: Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In the last three years, they were involved in eight deaths and hundreds of injuries. Trucks and buses injured dozens more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll. Children are not spared. In the past year, 120 people under 18 were hurt. One child lost a leg in June. The pain is not abstract. It is a mother’s scream, a child’s empty bed.

      Leadership: Votes, Silence, and the Road Ahead

      Assembly Member Nikki Lucas has voted to extend and expand school speed cameras, and to require safer street designs. She voted to keep protections in place for children near schools. According to the official record, Lucas votes yes to extend school speed zones, improving child pedestrian safety. But she also voted to weaken bus rules, a move that puts pedestrians and cyclists at greater risk. The work is not done. The streets are not safe.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Every death is preventable. Every injury is a failure of policy and will. Call Assembly Member Lucas. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat reckless drivers. Join with others. Do not let another family grieve alone. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents local districts across New York. Open States
      ▸ Where does AD 60 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 42 and state senate district SD 19. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 60?
      It includes the East New York (North), East New York-New Lots, Spring Creek-Starrett City, East New York-City Line, Shirley Chisholm State Park, and Brooklyn CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council District District 42, and State Senate District SD 19. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 60?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm, with 8 deaths and hundreds of injuries. Trucks and buses caused 30 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 9 injuries. Bikes were involved in 6 injuries, including one serious injury. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speed, reckless driving, and unsafe streets. These deaths and injuries are preventable with better laws and street design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, redesign streets for safety, and pass laws to keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road. They can also listen to residents and act quickly after each crash.

      Bill S 8344, titled 'Extends provisions and makes technical corrections to school speed zones in NYC; repealer,' moved through the Senate and Assembly in June 2025. Sponsored by Senator Andrew Gounardes, the bill passed Senate votes on June 12 and June 13, and cleared the Assembly on June 17. The measure extends and corrects school speed zone laws in New York City, repealing outdated provisions. Gounardes led the push, with strong support from most Senate Democrats and a split Assembly. The bill's technical fixes aim to keep protections in place for children and other vulnerable road users near schools. No safety analyst note was provided.
