      AD 53: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Toll in AD 53: Lives Cut Short, Streets Unchanged

      Eight people are dead. Thirty-four suffered serious injuries. In the last twelve months, 628 neighbors were hurt in 1,242 crashes. The dead do not get second chances. The injured carry scars—some seen, some not.

      A 26-year-old cyclist, crushed on Evergreen Avenue. A pedestrian, crossing with the signal, killed by a turning van on Wyckoff. A right rear passenger, 22, dead on Myrtle. The numbers pile up. The pain does not fade.

      The Human Cost: Names, Not Numbers

      Hayden Wallace, 29, was killed in a hit-and-run. His aunt remembered, “He lived life to the fullest. He was only 29 years old and lit up every room he entered. He was loved by so many,” as reported by the NY Daily News. Another family, broken by a repeat offender. “All accidents are useless but this one was even more useless because [Seabrook] had so many other offenses,” said Wallace’s aunt.

      A moped rider, thrown by a garbage truck on Flushing Avenue. A pedestrian, launched ten feet by a hit-and-run driver in Bushwick. “He gets absolutely launched, maybe 10 to 15 feet right into the middle of the intersection, and just left there,” said a friend.

      These are not accidents. They are the result of choices, policies, and inaction.

      Leadership: Action, Delay, and Missed Chances

      Assembly Member Maritza Davila has co-sponsored bills to require speed limiters for repeat speeders and to boost complete street designs. She voted to extend school speed cameras. But when it mattered, she missed key votes on school speed zone safety and bills to protect pedestrians and cyclists. She voted yes on measures that weakened bus rules and exempted some employees from bus lane enforcement—choices that put walkers and riders at risk.

      The streets demand more.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Call Assembly Member Davila. Demand she show up, vote, and fight for the living. Join with neighbors. Push for a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand speed limiters for repeat offenders. Insist on street designs that protect the most vulnerable. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents residents in districts like AD 53.
      ▸ Where does AD 53 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 34 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 53?
      It includes the East Williamsburg, Bushwick (West), Bushwick (East), Brooklyn CB4, and Brooklyn CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 34 and District 37, and State Senate District SD 18.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 53?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm to pedestrians in AD 53, with 2 deaths and 322 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 1 serious injury and 23 injuries. Bikes were involved in 21 injuries, but no deaths, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. They happen because of choices, policies, and street design—not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and support laws for lower speed limits, require speed limiters for repeat offenders, and demand safer street designs that protect people walking and cycling.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Davila Supports Safety Boosting State Participation in BQE Planning
      ▸

      Eighteen Brooklyn officials demand state DOT address BQE’s full deadly stretch. They reject piecemeal fixes. They call out decades of harm. The state’s refusal leaves neighborhoods exposed. The city’s hands are tied. The highway’s danger remains. Vulnerable lives hang in the balance.

      On February 10, 2023, eighteen Brooklyn lawmakers issued a joint letter demanding New York State Department of Transportation (DOT) participate in comprehensive planning for the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway (BQE). The group includes Reps. Nydia Velázquez, Dan Goldman, Borough President Antonio Reynoso, state Sens. Jabari Brisport, Julia Salazar, Kristen Gonzalez, Andrew Gounardes, Assembly Members Bobby Carroll, Maritza Davila, Phara Souffrant Forrest, Emily Gallagher, Jo Anne Simon, Marcela Mitaynes, and Council Members Alexa Avilés, Shahana Hanif, Crystal Hudson, Jennifer Gutiérrez, and Lincoln Restler. Senator Gounardes called the state’s refusal to join the city’s visioning process “unacceptable and irresponsible.” The lawmakers stress that the BQE’s harms—crashes, pollution, division—stretch from Bay Ridge to Greenpoint. Without state action, only city-owned segments see attention. The city’s efforts are limited. The state’s inaction leaves systemic danger unaddressed.
