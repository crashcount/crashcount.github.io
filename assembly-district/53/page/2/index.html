      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 53?

      Eight Dead, Hundreds Hurt—Who Will Stop the Bloodletting on Brooklyn’s Streets?

      Written August 8, 2025 (Revised August 8, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, removed duplicate headline, cleaned and deduped citations, ensured proper inline links and FAQ citation formatting.)

      AD 53: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 8, 2025

      The Toll This Year: Blood on Broadway, Silence on Morgan

      Eight dead. Seven hundred forty-seven injured. Fourteen left with wounds that will never heal. That is the cost of traffic violence in Assembly District 53 in the last twelve months (NYC Open Data).

      Just days ago, a 47-year-old man was struck and dragged more than fifty feet at Broadway and Suydam. The driver fled. Police have not named the victim. The street kept moving. Police were searching for the driver who killed him.

      On Morgan Avenue, a box truck killed a pedestrian crossing near Sharon Street. There is no crosswalk there. This is the third death on that stretch in three years. Juan Ignacio Serra said he was frustrated that nothing has changed since Daniel Vidal was killed.

      The dead do not get second chances. The living walk the same streets.

      Who Pays the Price? The Most Vulnerable

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. In the last year, two children under 18 were seriously hurt. Seniors are not spared. Trucks, vans, and SUVs are the usual suspects. Cars and trucks caused 348 injuries and one death. Motorcycles and mopeds, 24 injuries. Bikes, 22 (NYC Open Data).

      Hit-and-runs are routine. Drivers flee. The city shrugs. The intersection at Wyckoff and Himrod still has no justice for Ruari Fay-Handebeaux, who was thrown “maybe 10 to 15 feet right into the middle of the intersection, and just left there,” his friend said.

      Leadership: Votes, Missed Chances, and What Comes Next

      Assembly Member Maritza Davila has co-sponsored bills for safer streets and speed limiters. She voted to extend school speed cameras. But she missed key votes on school speed zone safety and on bills boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety. She voted yes to weaken bus rules, making streets riskier for those on foot and bike (Open States).

      The bills are there. The deaths keep coming.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Thoughts and Prayers

      Call Assembly Member Davila. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings and real enforcement.

      Do not wait for another name on a slab.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws and oversees state policy, including street safety (Open States).
      ▸ Where does AD 53 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 34 and state senate district SD 18 (Open States).
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 53?
      It includes the East Williamsburg, Bushwick (West), Bushwick (East), Brooklyn CB4, and Brooklyn CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 34 and District 37, and State Senate District SD 18 (Open States).
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 53?
      Cars and trucks caused 348 injuries and one death. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 24 injuries. Bikes caused 22 injuries (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and support bills for lower speed limits, protected crossings, and speed limiters for repeat offenders. They can also demand faster action from city agencies.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
