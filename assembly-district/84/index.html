      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 84?

      East 149th bleeds: six struck, three killed, and a clock that won’t stop

      Written August 25, 2025 (Revised August 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 84: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 4, 2025

      Six people went down on the corner of East 149th and Courtlandt. A Ford Mustang turned left, jumped the curb, and hit them under a sidewalk shed. The driver ran. The passenger ran. Police say the victims were taken to Lincoln, stable. “People were yelling, were in pain,” a neighbor said. “We thought it was a bomb,” said another. ABC7 and CBS New York reported the crash on July 3. The Daily News said police were still looking for the man who “rammed into six people after botching a turn.” NY Daily News.

      Bruckner. East 149th. Names you learn the hard way.

      Where the street takes first

      Since 2022, this district has logged 5,506 crashes, with 23 people killed and 3,359 injured. Heavy vehicles keep showing up in the body count: trucks and buses in 56 serious pedestrian cases; SUVs and cars in 452. Nights are worst. Deaths spike at 1 a.m., 2 a.m., 5 a.m., and again in the late rush, with a hard run from 7 a.m. through the evening. The hour with the most injuries is 4 p.m. to 6 p.m. The hotspots are plain: Bruckner Blvd and East 149 Street lead the list. Source: NYC Open Data rollup.

      Three people have already died here in 2025. A 57‑year‑old cyclist was killed by a turning MTA bus at East 149th and Brook Ave at 10:42 p.m. The bus was making a left. The cyclist was going straight. NYC crash data.

      At 3:16 p.m. on April 2, a 52‑year‑old man was crushed near Morris Ave and East 149th. Multiple SUVs were stopped in traffic. He died where he fell. NYC crash data.

      At 7:15 a.m. on April 17, a 61‑year‑old man was killed on Oak Point Ave by a box truck starting from a parked position. Another morning. Another street. NYC crash data.

      Left turns. Trucks. Night.

      The pattern does not hide. Left turns show up in the death files. Trucks and buses do too. So does the dark. In the last 12 months, deaths rose to five, up two from the prior period, with injuries up 11% year to date. Source: district period stats.

      The Mustang on July 3 wasn’t a one‑off. It was one more. Six pedestrians, ages 30 to 79, hit on a sidewalk because a driver failed a turn and kept going. “People were yelling… crying,” one witness said. The car was left behind; the names were not. ABC7 | CBS New York | NY Daily News.

      Fix the corners that kill

      Start with the corners that keep taking blood: East 149th Street and Bruckner Blvd. Add daylighting at crosswalks. Give leading pedestrian intervals at signals. Harden the left turns with slow‑turn wedges. Put physical protection where people stand—concrete, not paint. The truck streets need tight turn radii and clear truck routes away from foot traffic. The late‑night spikes call for targeted enforcement when the bodies fall—1 a.m. to 5 a.m., and again at the evening peak. Source: small‑area analysis.

      City laws that would stop the next one

      The Council and the Mayor can lower the speed limit on local streets. Albany already gave the city that power. A slower default would save lives in this district, where speed shows up in the tapes and the death sheets. See our push to use Sammy’s Law and set a 20 mph default: Take Action.

      The state can also stop the worst repeat speeders. The Stop Super Speeders Act (A2299/S4045C) would force drivers with bad records and camera histories to use speed‑limiting tech. Local Assembly Member Amanda Septimo is listed as a co‑sponsor on the Assembly bill. A 2299. The school‑zone camera program was extended this summer through technical fixes in S 8344. Keep the cameras. Slow the cars. End the repeats. For how to press your reps, see Take Action.
