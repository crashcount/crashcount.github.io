      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 84?

      Six Hit, None Safe—Bronx Streets Bleed While Leaders Blink

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 24, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, cleaned and deduped citations, improved FAQ citation formatting.)

      AD 84: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      A District Under Siege

      Six people hit. One morning. One intersection. On July 3, a Ford Mustang turned left at East 149th and Courtlandt and plowed onto the sidewalk. The driver hit a woman in the crosswalk, then five more under the scaffolding. The car stopped, the men inside ran. The street was left with blood and broken glass. “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting,” said Vivian Cole. All six went to Lincoln Hospital. All survived. This time.

      The Toll: Numbers That Don’t Lie

      In the last 12 months, 6 people died and 948 were injured on these streets. Twelve suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. Cars and SUVs did most of the damage: 5 deaths, 408 injuries. Trucks and buses killed 2, injured 45. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes left dozens more hurt. The violence is steady, not sudden. It comes in the morning, at dusk, on corners where children cross.

      Leadership: Steps Taken, Steps Missed

      Assembly Member Amanda Septimo has backed some bills for safer streets. She co-sponsored a measure to require speed-limiting tech for repeat speeders and another to redesign streets for all users. She voted to extend school speed cameras. But when it mattered, she missed a key vote to keep school speed zones safe (missed a key vote). The silence is loud. The absence is felt.

      The Voices in the Street

      The pain is not abstract. “We thought it was a bomb or something, because we are all panicking around here,” said Christina Sieh. The city moves on. The sidewalk is swept. But the fear stays.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Call Assembly Member Septimo. Demand she show up, vote, and fight for real change. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Demand streets where children can cross and live. The next victim is only a turn away.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents local districts like AD 84. See how the Assembly works.
      ▸ Where does AD 84 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 8, and state senate district SD 29. See district details.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 84?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, Concourse-Concourse Village, Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Bronx CB1, Bronx CB2, and Bronx CB4 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8, District 16, and District 17, and State Senate District SD 29. See district map.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 84?
      Cars and SUVs caused 5 deaths and 408 injuries. Trucks and buses caused 2 deaths and 45 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 1 death and 25 injuries. Bikes caused no deaths and 16 injuries. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not random—they follow patterns and can be stopped.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and support laws for lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. Showing up and voting for these changes saves lives.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      A 2299
      Septimo co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 2299 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets in a year triggers forced speed control tech. Lawmakers move to curb repeat speeders. Streets demand fewer deadly risks.

      Assembly bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 16, 2025, the bill 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Primary sponsor Emily Gallagher leads a bloc of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, and others. The measure aims to clamp down on repeat speeders with mandatory speed-limiting tech. No safety analyst note was provided.
