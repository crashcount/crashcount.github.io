      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 84?

      Six Hit, None Spared—How Many Bodies Until We Act?

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      AD 84: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      Blood on the Crosswalk

      Six people hit. One morning. One Mustang. On July 3, a driver in Melrose slammed through a crosswalk at East 149th and Courtlandt, then mounted the curb and crashed into scaffolding. The driver and passenger ran. The victims were left on the ground, crying out. “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting,” said Vivian Cole. Police are still searching for the suspects. All six were hospitalized. No one died this time. Not yet.

      Just days earlier, three more pedestrians were struck at Hunts Point and Bruckner. One man was left in critical condition. The driver faces charges. The street keeps its secrets, but the numbers do not.

      The Slow Grind of Loss

      In the last twelve months, six people have died on these streets. Eight hundred thirty-two have been injured. Twelve suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. Cars and trucks do most of the harm: five deaths, nearly four hundred injuries. Motorcycles, mopeds, bikes—they all draw blood, but the weight of steel does the killing.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Assembly Member Amanda Septimo has signed on to bills that could slow the carnage. She co-sponsors A2299, which would force repeat speeders to install devices that keep them from breaking the limit. She backs A1077, calling for streets built for people, not just cars. She voted yes on school speed cameras. These are steps. But the bodies keep coming.

      The street does not care about bills in committee. It cares about speed, steel, and silence. “We thought it was a bomb or something, because we are all panicking around here,” said Christina Sieh.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Call Assembly Member Septimo. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement. Demand streets for people, not cars.

      Every day of delay is another body on the ground. The time for patience is over.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents the people of each district in Albany.
      ▸ Where does AD 84 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 8 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 84?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, Concourse-Concourse Village, Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Bronx CB1, Bronx CB2, and Bronx CB4 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8, District 16, and District 17, and State Senate District SD 29.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 84?
      Cars and Trucks: 5 deaths, 398 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 24 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 16 injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Most are preventable with lower speeds, safer street design, and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, support street redesigns that protect people, and back laws that target repeat dangerous drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
