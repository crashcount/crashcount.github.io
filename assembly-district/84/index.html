      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 84?

      No More Waiting for Sirens: Streets Are Killing Us Now

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned citations, and ensured correct link formatting.)

      AD 84: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Street Does Not Forgive

      On July 3, a Ford Mustang jumped the curb at East 149th and Courtlandt. Six people walking in the crosswalk were struck. The car smashed into scaffolding. The driver and passenger ran. The sidewalk was left with blood and twisted metal. “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting,” said Vivian Cole. All six went to the hospital. The youngest was 30. The oldest was 79. No one has been arrested. The street is busy again. The fear lingers.

      This is not rare. In the last twelve months, six people have died and 951 have been injured in crashes in AD 84. Twelve were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Most were walking or riding bikes. The numbers do not stop. They only climb.

      The Record of Leadership

      Assembly Member Amanda Septimo has backed some bills for safer streets. She co-sponsored a measure to require speed-limiting tech for repeat speeders. She signed on to a bill for safer street design. She voted to extend school speed cameras. But when it mattered, she missed the vote to fix school speed zone laws. The law passed, but her absence left a mark. The streets do not wait for committee schedules.

      The Machines That Kill

      The numbers are plain. Cars and SUVs did the most harm—five deaths, 419 injuries. Trucks and buses killed two, injured 52. Motorcycles and mopeds killed one, injured 25. Bikes injured 16. The street does not care who you are. It only cares who is bigger, faster, heavier.

      The Voices in the Aftermath

      “We thought it was a bomb or something, because we are all panicking around here,” said Christina Sieh. The panic fades. The danger does not.

      Call to Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Assembly Member Septimo. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand speed limiters for repeat offenders. Demand streets where children and elders can cross without fear. Do not wait for the next siren.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents districts like AD 84.
      ▸ Where does AD 84 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 8 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 84?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, Concourse-Concourse Village, Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Bronx CB1, Bronx CB2, and Bronx CB4 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8, District 16, and District 17, and State Senate District SD 29.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 84?
      Cars and SUVs: 5 deaths, 419 injuries. Trucks and buses: 2 deaths, 52 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 death, 25 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 16 injuries. Data source
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not acts of nature.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, require speed limiters for repeat offenders, and fund safer street redesigns that protect people walking and biking.

      A 2299
      Septimo co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 2299 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets in a year triggers forced speed control tech. Lawmakers move to curb repeat speeders. Streets demand fewer deadly risks.

      Assembly bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 16, 2025, the bill 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Primary sponsor Emily Gallagher leads a bloc of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, and others. The measure aims to clamp down on repeat speeders with mandatory speed-limiting tech. No safety analyst note was provided.
