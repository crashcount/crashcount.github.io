      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 84?

      No One Is Safe Until Streets Change

      Written July 7, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, deduped and cleaned citations.)

      AD 84: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Street Does Not Forgive

      On July 3rd, a Ford Mustang turned left at East 149th and Courtlandt. The light was green for people crossing. The car hit a woman, then jumped the curb and plowed into five more under the scaffolding. The driver and passenger ran. Six people went to Lincoln Hospital. They were alive. They were lucky. “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting,” said Vivian Cole. The Mustang sat wrecked, plates gone. The street was left with blood and fear.

      This is not rare. In the last year, five people have died and 418 have been injured in crashes in AD 84. Six were seriously hurt. The dead do not get a second chance. The living carry scars.

      The Toll Grows, The Work Remains

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay the price. In three years, 23 people killed, 2,989 injured, 56 seriously. Cars and SUVs did most of the harm. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, mopeds, bikes—none innocent. The numbers do not lie. The bodies do not lie.

      What Has Been Done, What Must Be Done

      Assembly Member Amanda Septimo has signed on to bills that matter. She co-sponsored A 2299, which would force repeat speeders to install speed-limiting tech. She backed A 1077, pushing for streets built for people, not just cars. She voted yes on school speed cameras. She stood with others to call transit a justice issue.

      But the street keeps taking. The Mustang driver ran. The city waits for lower speed limits, for real enforcement, for streets that do not kill. “We thought it was a bomb or something, because we are all panicking around here,” said Christina Sieh. The panic is not new. The pain is not new.

      Act Now—Or Wait for the Next Siren

      Call Assembly Member Septimo. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand real action against repeat speeders. Demand streets where a walk to the store does not end in blood.

      The street will not wait. Neither should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like AD 84 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does AD 84 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 8 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 84?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, Melrose, Hunts Point, Concourse-Concourse Village, Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Bronx CB1, Bronx CB2, and Bronx CB4 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 8, District 16, and District 17, and State Senate District SD 29.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 84?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians in AD 84, with 5 deaths and 398 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 1 death and 24 injuries. Bikes were involved in 16 injuries but no deaths. NYC Open Data crash records
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and real enforcement. Calling them ‘accidents’ hides the truth.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, support speed cameras, redesign streets for safety, and back laws that target repeat dangerous drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
