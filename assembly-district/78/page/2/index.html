      Another Body on Bronx Asphalt—How Many More Before We Act?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, tightened prose, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      AD 78: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Slow Grind of Loss

      A man steps into the crosswalk at University Avenue and West Kingsbridge Road. A van turns right. He does not make it across. Sixty-five years old. Dead in the street. The city records another loss. In the last twelve months, one person died and five suffered serious injuries in Assembly District 78. Four hundred seventy-eight were hurt. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      A 15-year-old girl rides on the back of a moped. An SUV turns. She is ejected. She does not get up. Her name is not in the data, but her absence is felt. These are not accidents. They are collisions. They are choices made behind the wheel, in the design of the street, in the laws that govern speed and steel.

      The Record of Leadership

      Assembly Member George Alvarez has taken steps. He co-sponsored a bill to force speed limiters on drivers who rack up violations, aiming to stop repeat offenders before they kill again (A 2299). He backed another bill for “complete streets”—roads built for people, not just cars (A 1077). He voted yes to bring speed cameras to school zones, a proven way to slow drivers where children walk (A 7652).

      But the work is not done. On Fordham Road, plans for safer bus lanes stalled (plans stalled). Riders wait. Streets stay dangerous. The city and state have the tools. They must use them.

      The Voices and the Call

      The horror is not hidden. After a hit-and-run on the Major Deegan, police said they were “looking for a man who ran away after crashing his car” (police said). The victim died at St. Barnabas Hospital. The driver fled. The street stayed open. The risk stayed real.

      A deli worker watched gunfire erupt between drivers near the Bronx Zoo. “There were a whole lot of rounds. It was crazy” (a deli worker watched). The bullets stopped. The danger did not.

      This is the cost of delay. This is the price of inaction.

      What You Can Do

      Call George Alvarez. Call the Mayor. Call the DOT. Demand safer speeds. Demand speed cameras. Demand streets that put people first.

      Every day you wait, someone else does not come home. Take action now.
