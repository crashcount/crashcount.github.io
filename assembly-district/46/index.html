      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 46?

      Blood on the Ballot: Who Lets Our Streets Kill?

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised August 13, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 46: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Assembly District 46, the story is written in blood on the street. Ten people have died since 2022. Two were killed in the last twelve months. Sixteen more suffered serious injuries—the kind that do not heal. The numbers are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, elders, children. A 95-year-old woman, dead at the intersection of Cropsey and 24th. A 24-year-old cyclist, crushed on Neptune Avenue. A 72-year-old woman, struck crossing Marine Avenue. The list does not end. It only grows.

      The Vehicles That Kill

      Cars and SUVs are the main killers. They caused three deaths and over 300 injuries (NYC crash data). Trucks and buses did their share—24 people hurt, one killed. Motorcycles and mopeds, ten more injured. Bikes, too, but the toll is smaller. The street does not care who you are. It only cares who is bigger, faster, heavier.

      Leadership: Votes Against Safety

      Assembly Member Alec Brook-Krasny has not stood with the dead. He voted against extending school speed zones—against the cameras that catch the drivers who speed past children. He voted to weaken bus rules, making the street more dangerous for those outside a car. He co-sponsored a bill to repeal congestion pricing, a move that would put more cars on the road and more bodies in the morgue. When bills came up to make school zones safer, he voted no. Again and again, the answer was no. The silence is not neutral. It is a choice.

      The Path Forward

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. Call Brook-Krasny. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand cameras that work, laws that protect, streets that do not kill. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws and oversees state policy, including traffic safety.
      ▸ Where does AD 46 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 47 and state senate district SD 23.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 46?
      It includes the Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, Fort Hamilton, Dyker Beach Park, Coney Island-Sea Gate, Brighton Beach, Calvert Vaux Park, Brooklyn CB13, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 47, District 48, and District 50, and State Senate Districts SD 23 and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 46?
      Cars and SUVs caused 3 deaths and over 300 injuries (NYC crash data). Trucks and buses caused 24 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 10 injuries. Bikes caused 6 injuries. The majority of harm comes from cars and SUVs.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Policies like lower speed limits, better street design, and enforcement save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, support speed cameras, fund safer street designs, and oppose laws that weaken protections for people outside cars.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Brook-Krasny Opposes Safety-Boosting Speed Camera Program Reauthorization
      ▸

      Eleven city lawmakers voted no on speed cameras. Their votes keep streets exposed. Pedestrians and cyclists lose a shield. Reckless drivers win. The city’s most basic defense—rejected. The toll will be measured in blood, not words.

      On June 13 and June 17, 2025, the New York State Senate and Assembly voted on reauthorizing New York City's school zone speed camera program. The Senate passed the measure 38-21; three city senators—Stephen Chan, Andrew Lanza, Jessica Scarcella-Spanton—voted no. The Assembly passed it 110-31, with nine city lawmakers—Alec Brook-Krasny, Lester Chang, Simcha Eichenstein, Michael Novakhov, Stacey Pheffer Amato, Sam Pirozzolo, Michael Reilly, David Weprin, Kalman Yeger—opposing. The Streetsblog NYC article, 'Ye Shall Know Their Names! Meet the Dirty Dozen City Pols Who Voted Against Speed Camera Program,' quotes lawmakers dismissing speed cameras as revenue grabs or burdens. The safety analyst notes: 'The event text does not describe a specific policy or legislative action, so no direct safety impact on pedestrians and cyclists can be assessed.' Still, the votes signal disregard for proven tools that protect those outside cars.
