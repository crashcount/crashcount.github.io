      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 46?

      Brook-Krasny Votes, Brooklyn Bleeds

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 16, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 46: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In Assembly District 46, the story is written in blood and broken bones. Since 2022, ten people have died in crashes here. Over 2,000 have been injured. Sixteen left with wounds so deep they are called serious injuries. The old and the young are not spared. In the last twelve months, two people died—one was 75, the other just 18. Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians are all at risk.

      Every week, another crash. A 95-year-old woman struck dead at a Brooklyn intersection. A cyclist, 24, thrown from his bike and killed by an SUV. A pedestrian, 72, crushed while crossing Marine Avenue. The numbers do not flinch: over 1,100 crashes in the last year alone, according to NYC Open Data. Each one a life changed, a family left to pick up the pieces.

      The Record of Leadership

      Assembly Member Alec Brook-Krasny has voted against bills that would have made school zones safer and kept speed cameras running. He voted no on extending and fixing school speed zone laws. He voted no on reauthorizing the city’s speed camera program. He voted yes to weaken bus lane rules, making streets more dangerous for people outside cars. He co-sponsored a bill to repeal congestion pricing, a move that would put more cars—and more danger—on city streets. See the record.

      Brook-Krasny has spoken up against bus stop cuts, citing the needs of seniors and people with disabilities (amNY). But when it comes to the laws that protect children and the most vulnerable from speeding cars, his votes say no.

      The Path Forward

      This is not fate. These deaths are not accidents. Speed cameras, lower speed limits, and safer street designs save lives. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. The state can pass laws to stop repeat dangerous drivers. But these tools only work if leaders use them.

      Call Assembly Member Alec Brook-Krasny. Demand he support real safety measures: speed cameras, lower speed limits, and laws that protect people outside cars. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws and oversees state policy, including traffic safety. Learn more about the Assembly’s legislative function.
      ▸ Where does AD 46 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 47 and state senate district SD 23. See the district breakdown.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 46?
      It includes the Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, Fort Hamilton, Dyker Beach Park, Coney Island-Sea Gate, Brighton Beach, Calvert Vaux Park, Brooklyn CB13, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 47, District 48, and District 50, and State Senate Districts SD 23 and SD 26. See the full list of neighborhoods and districts.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 46?
      Cars and trucks were involved in 305 pedestrian injuries and deaths, motorcycles and mopeds in 10, and bikes in 6. See the crash data.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes can be prevented with proven tools like lower speed limits, speed cameras, and safer street designs. They are not random acts of fate. Crash data source.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can vote for speed cameras, lower speed limits, and laws that target repeat dangerous drivers. They can also push for safer street designs and more enforcement. See legislative actions.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood. CrashCount data source.
