      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 85?

      Sixteen Dead. Hundreds Hurt. No Excuses Left.

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 85: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In Assembly District 85, the numbers do not lie. Sixteen people killed. Over two thousand injured. In the last twelve months alone, two more lives lost, 635 hurt, twelve left with injuries so severe they will never be the same. The dead are not numbers. They are children, elders, workers, neighbors.

      Just this February, a motorbike carrying two teens collided with a minivan at East 172nd and Metcalf. The boy died. The girl survived, but not unchanged. Police said, “Both teens were thrown from the bike and rushed to Jacobi Hospital. The older victim succumbed to his injuries while the girl was in stable condition.” The driver stayed. No arrests. The street stayed the same.

      Last fall, a 56-year-old man was killed crossing East 163rd at Westchester Avenue. He was in the crosswalk. The SUV kept going. He did not.

      The Cost of Delay

      Most of these deaths could have been prevented. The same streets see the same crashes, year after year. Cars and SUVs do most of the killing—five deaths, over 300 injuries from cars and SUVs alone in this district since 2022. Trucks and buses add more. Motorcycles and mopeds, too. The city knows which streets are deadly. The city knows how to fix them. Still, the bodies pile up.

      What Has Been Done—And What Hasn’t

      Assembly Member Emérita Torres has voted to extend and expand school speed cameras, a move that boosts safety for children. Torres backed the law to keep speed zones in place, aiming to protect kids near schools. But the work is not done. The most dangerous drivers—those with dozens of camera tickets—still face no real barrier. The streets outside school hours, or outside the camera zones, remain open season.

      The Voices Left Behind

      When a stolen car slammed into parked cars on Seward Avenue this April, Robert Perez was thrown six feet. “It pushed me six feet. I hit my leg and my back.” He survived. Others do not. The shock lingers. The pain comes later.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Assembly Member Torres. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join with others. Do not wait for another child’s name to be added to the list. The street will not change itself.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like AD 85 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does AD 85 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 18 and state senate district SD 11.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 85?
      It includes the Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Crotona Park East, Soundview-Bruckner-Bronx River, Soundview-Clason Point, Soundview Park, Rikers Island, Bronx CB2, and Bronx CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 17, District 18, and District 22, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 29, SD 32, and SD 34.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 85?
      Cars and SUVs: 5 pedestrian deaths, 332 injuries. Trucks and buses: No pedestrian deaths, 29 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: No pedestrian deaths, 9 injuries. Bikes: No pedestrian deaths, 7 injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and action against repeat dangerous drivers can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand automated enforcement, redesign dangerous streets, and pass laws targeting repeat dangerous drivers.
