      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 85?

      Kids Are Dying. Streets Don’t Change. Who Will Answer for the Blood?

      Written July 7, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, and cleaned citations.)

      AD 85: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      Blood on the Streets: Recent Losses in AD 85

      Two people are dead. Twelve more are left with serious injuries. In the last twelve months alone, 574 neighbors have been hurt in 898 crashes across Assembly District 85. The dead include a teenager and a 55-year-old man. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie.

      On February 24, a motorbike carrying an 18-year-old boy and a 14-year-old girl collided with a minivan at Metcalf Avenue and East 172nd Street. Both teens were thrown from the bike. The boy died at Jacobi Hospital. The girl survived, but the impact will not leave her soon. Police said, “Both teens were thrown from the bike and rushed to Jacobi Hospital. The older victim succumbed to his injuries while the girl was in stable condition,” as reported by the New York Post. The driver stayed at the scene. No arrests. No answers.

      The next day, the story repeated. A 14-year-old girl and a 17-year-old boy, riding a bike, were struck by a Honda Odyssey at the same intersection. The boy did not make it. “The driver remained at the scene and no arrests were made. The investigation is ongoing,” according to ABC7. The street is unchanged. The blood dries. The danger remains.

      The Numbers Behind the Names

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm. In the last three years, they killed five people and injured over 300 more on these streets. Trucks and buses hurt dozens. Motorcycles and mopeds left bodies broken. The young and the old are not spared. One child dead. Four seniors killed or maimed. The violence is not random. It is routine. NYC Open Data

      Leadership: Words, Silence, and the Cost of Waiting

      Assembly Member Emérita Torres has spoken up for transit funding, but the record is thin when it comes to fighting for safer streets in her own district. No public push for lower speed limits. No press for more protected crossings or automated enforcement. The silence is loud. The families left behind hear it every day.

      What Comes Next: Action, Not Excuses

      Every crash is preventable. Every delay is a choice. Call Assembly Member Torres. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand more speed cameras. Demand streets that do not kill children. If you wait, the next name on the list could be someone you love.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws and oversees state policy alongside the State Senate.
      ▸ Where does AD 85 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, city council district District 18 and state senate district SD 11.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 85?
      It includes the Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Crotona Park East, Soundview-Bruckner-Bronx River, Soundview-Clason Point, Soundview Park, Rikers Island, Bronx CB2, and Bronx CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 17, District 18, and District 22, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 29, SD 32, and SD 34.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 85?
      Cars and SUVs killed 5 people and injured over 300. Trucks and buses caused dozens of injuries but no deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no deaths but several serious injuries. Bikes were involved in a handful of injuries but no deaths. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and stronger enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can push for lower speed limits, expand speed camera coverage, and demand safer street designs that protect people walking and biking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
