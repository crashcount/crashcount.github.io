      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 57?

      Blood on the Boulevard: Lives Lost, Leaders Silent

      Written August 4, 2025 (Revised August 10, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 57: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 4, 2025

      The Death Toll Grows

      Five people killed. Over 700 injured. That is the cost of traffic violence in Assembly District 57 in the past year alone (NYC Open Data). The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do. Just this May, a 55-year-old man was crossing Washington Avenue at Fulton Street when a Ford Explorer struck him and sped away. He died at the hospital. The driver did not stop. Police called it the second fatal hit-and-run in Brooklyn that week (police called it the second fatal hit-and-run).

      A few months earlier, a woman stepped out of a taxi on Flatbush Avenue. Another car hit her. She died before sunrise. Both drivers stayed. The city moved on (woman stepped out of a taxi).

      Vulnerable Lives, Unprotected

      SUVs, trucks, and cars do most of the killing. In three years, cars and SUVs caused three deaths and over 300 injuries to pedestrians and cyclists here. Trucks killed three more. Bikes and mopeds injured, but did not kill (NYC Open Data). The most vulnerable—those on foot, on bikes, children—pay the highest price.

      Leadership: Votes, Words, and Missed Chances

      Assembly Member Phara Souffrant Forrest has taken some steps. She voted to extend school speed zones, a move to protect children near schools (voted to extend school speed zones). She co-sponsored bills for speed limiters on repeat offenders (co-sponsored bills for speed limiters) and for automated bike lane enforcement (automated bike lane enforcement). She stood with others to demand a missing protected bike lane on Ashland Place (demand a missing protected bike lane).

      But when the city cracked down on cyclists instead of reckless drivers, Forrest called it “incredibly misguided” and “unacceptable”. When the mayor ripped out a protected bike lane on Bedford Avenue, Council Member Lincoln Restler said, “the Mayor is making a purely political decision to rip out a bike lane with no alternative.”

      The Road Ahead: Demand More

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by leaders who delay, deflect, or do too little. Call Assembly Member Forrest. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for every street.

      Do not wait for another body in the road.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like AD 57 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does AD 57 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 35 and state senate district SD 20.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 57?
      It includes the Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Crown Heights (South), and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33, District 35, and District 36, and State Senate Districts SD 20, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 57?
      Cars and SUVs caused three deaths and over 300 injuries to pedestrians and cyclists in the last three years. Trucks killed three more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes caused injuries but did not kill anyone (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Policies like lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, and enforcement against reckless drivers have been shown to save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, support automated enforcement, and hold reckless drivers accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Forrest co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 2299 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets in a year triggers forced speed control tech. Lawmakers move to curb repeat speeders. Streets demand fewer deadly risks.

      Assembly bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 16, 2025, the bill 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Primary sponsor Emily Gallagher leads a bloc of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, and others. The measure aims to clamp down on repeat speeders with mandatory speed-limiting tech. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Souffrant Forrest Supports Safety Boosting Equalized Tolling Plan
      ▸

      Brooklyn officials demand equal tolls on all Manhattan crossings. They warn free bridges funnel traffic into certain neighborhoods. Their letter calls for fairness. The MTA stays silent. The Traffic Mobility Review Board will decide. Streets hang in the balance.

      On July 18, 2023, Council Member Shahana Hanif and other Brooklyn officials called for 'equalized tolling on all crossings into Manhattan' as congestion pricing nears. Their letter to the Traffic Mobility Review Board states: 'No one community should disproportionately bear the burden of the cars and trucks passing through their neighborhood.' Hanif, along with Lincoln Restler, Alexa Avilés, Jennifer Gutiérrez, and Crystal Hudson, joined borough, state, and federal colleagues in this push. They propose a credit system to balance costs between DOT bridges and MTA tunnels, aiming to protect neighborhoods like the Brooklyn waterfront and the BQE corridor from traffic surges. The MTA declined comment. The Board will soon set final toll rates and rules. The federal government has approved the congestion pricing plan, with tolls for cars expected between $9 and $23. Over 100 exemption requests, including from taxi and for-hire vehicle drivers, are under review.
