      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 43?

      Blood on Eastern Parkway: No More Delays, No More Dead Kids

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised July 3, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      AD 43: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Toll on Eastern Parkway

      An eight-year-old boy died under the wheels of a black Honda Pilot at Eastern Parkway and Albany Avenue. He was crossing with his sister. The car dragged him. Blood pooled on the street. A witness described seeing the boy dragged from under the car by a woman. Neighbors tried to stop the bleeding. The driver stayed. No arrest. No charges. The street stayed open.

      This is not rare. In the last twelve months, AD 43 saw 2 deaths and 8 serious injuries. There were 528 injuries in 848 crashes. Children, elders, mothers. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie. A neighbor acknowledged the danger. The city moves on.

      The Record of Leadership

      Assembly Member Brian Cunningham has signed on to bills that could help. He co-sponsored measures for complete street design, advanced vehicle safety tech, and automated bike lane enforcement. He voted yes to expand speed cameras near schools. He backed scramble crosswalks at schools and street sweeper enforcement.

      But the blood keeps coming. The bills move slow. The streets do not change fast enough. SUVs and cars do most of the killing.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay means another family shattered. Call Assembly Member Cunningham. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns. Demand action, not condolences.

      The dead cannot wait. Neither should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents the people of New York State.
      ▸ Where does AD 43 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 40 and state senate district SD 20.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 43?
      It includes the Crown Heights (North), Crown Heights (South), Prospect Lefferts Gardens-Wingate, East Flatbush-Erasmus, Brooklyn CB8, and Brooklyn CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 35, District 36, District 40, and District 41, and State Senate District SD 20.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 43?
      SUVs and cars were responsible for 2 deaths, 272 minor injuries, 103 moderate injuries, and 13 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 0 deaths, 9 minor injuries, 7 moderate injuries, and 0 serious injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths, 8 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, and 1 serious injury.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement. The same deadly patterns repeat year after year.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and fund laws for lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, expand automated enforcement, and prioritize people over cars. They can act faster and refuse to accept excuses.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Cunningham Supports Safety Boosting Automated Street Cleaning Enforcement
      ▸

      A new state bill would let New York City street sweepers use cameras to catch cars blocking cleaning routes. Repeat offenders rack up most tickets. Lawmakers say dirty streets and blocked drains endanger everyone. The law would sunset in 2029.

      Assembly Bill (unnumbered) was introduced by Brooklyn Assembly Member Brian Cunningham on April 25, 2024. The bill is pending in the state legislature. The bill summary states it 'fulfills the Sanitation Department's longstanding ask for help getting vehicles out of the way of road-cleaning operations.' No formal safety analysis for vulnerable road users was provided.
