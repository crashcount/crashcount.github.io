      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 43?

      Blood on the Crosswalk, Silence in Albany

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list for accuracy and order.)

      AD 43: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      Seven dead. Thirty-two left with injuries that will never heal. In the last three years, Assembly District 43 has seen 3,816 crashes. The dead include children, elders, and the nameless. An eight-year-old boy, struck by an SUV at Eastern Parkway and Albany Avenue, never made it home. A 101-year-old woman, crossing with the signal, killed by a left-turning SUV. A 90-year-old man, hit by a moped, died in the hospital. These are not numbers. They are empty chairs at dinner tables.

      The violence does not stop. Just last week, two men died crossing a Brooklyn street. The car did not stop. Neighbors spoke plain: drivers speed on that stretch of roadway. The city moves on. The street stays the same.

      Leadership: Votes, Bills, and Missed Chances

      Assembly Member Brian Cunningham has backed some safety bills. He co-sponsored measures for bike lane cameras, complete streets, and advanced vehicle safety tech. He voted to fund safer street designs. But when it mattered most—when the Assembly voted to extend school speed zone protections—he missed the vote. The bill passed, but not with his help.

      Cunningham has spoken about blocked trucks and dirty streets, but not about the bodies in the crosswalk. “Sometimes we can’t account for whether or not the trucks actually got down the block, or whether or not cars are obstructing them,” he told Streetsblog. The catch basins get more attention than the children.

      The Road Ahead: Action or More Names on Stone

      Most deaths here come from cars and SUVs. They kill, they maim, and they keep rolling. Laws that sound good on paper mean nothing if leaders do not show up to vote, or if they water down rules for bus lanes and enforcement. The city has the power to lower speed limits. The Assembly can pass laws to stop repeat speeders. But every delay is another name on a slab.

      Call your leaders. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against reckless drivers. Do not wait for another child’s shoes left in the street. The blood is fresh. The clock is running.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like AD 43 in Albany. Source
      ▸ Where does AD 43 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 40 and state senate district SD 20. Source
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 43?
      It includes the Crown Heights (North), Crown Heights (South), Prospect Lefferts Gardens-Wingate, East Flatbush-Erasmus, Brooklyn CB8, and Brooklyn CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 35, District 36, District 40, and District 41, and State Senate District SD 20. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 43?
      Most deaths and injuries came from Cars and Trucks (3 deaths, 279 minor injuries, 105 moderate, 13 serious), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 9 minor injuries, 7 moderate, 0 serious), and Bikes (no deaths, 8 minor injuries, 4 moderate, 1 serious). Source
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speed limits, and enforcement against reckless drivers. Calling them ‘accidents’ hides the fact that policy and leadership can save lives. Source
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and support laws for lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, and ensure enforcement targets the most dangerous drivers—not just the most vulnerable road users. Source
