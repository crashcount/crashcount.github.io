      No More Names on Asphalt: Demand Streets for the Living, Not the Dead

      Written August 7, 2025 (Revised August 13, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, and cleaned citations.)

      AD 75: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 7, 2025

      Blood on the Streets: The Human Cost

      In Assembly District 75, the numbers do not lie. Eighteen people have died on these streets since 2022. Seventy-five have suffered serious injuries. In the last twelve months alone, four lives were lost, and over a thousand were injured. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors, elders, children. On February 11, an 83-year-old woman was killed crossing with the signal at Avenue of the Americas and West 58th Street. She died from internal injuries after an SUV making a left turn struck her. She had the light. She did not stand a chance. NYC crash data

      Just weeks ago, a box truck killed a man in Hell’s Kitchen. Days later, a van crashed in Midtown, and firefighters found it packed with 76 propane tanks. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment. “The FDNY said the responding officers noticed a strong smell of gas and propane tanks in the back of the van.” CBS New York report

      On July 24, a New Jersey Transit bus slammed into another at the Port Authority ramp. Thirty people were hurt. “A lot of them with musculoskeletal injuries, neck pain, back pain,” said FDNY Deputy Assistant Chief Joseph Pataky. The ramp is a known bottleneck. The city promises to fix it by 2032. The pain is now.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Assembly Member Tony Simone has taken some steps. He voted yes to extend school speed zones, a move that protects children near schools. He co-sponsored bills to require speed limiters for repeat offenders and to hold vehicle owners liable for dangerous driving. He stood up for congestion pricing, which can cut traffic and save lives. But he also backed bills that would weaken speed camera enforcement. The fight is not over.

      The Next Move Is Ours

      This is not fate. These deaths are not accidents. Every crash is a policy failure. Every delay is a choice. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has the tools to stop repeat offenders. It can redesign streets for people, not cars. But it will not act unless we force it.

      Call your council member. Call Assembly Member Simone. Demand safer speeds, stronger enforcement, and streets built for life, not death.

      Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents districts like AD 75 in Albany. See crash data
      ▸ Where does AD 75 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3 and state senate district SD 28. Crash data source
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 75?
      It includes the Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’s Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, Manhattan CB4, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, and District 6, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 47. Crash data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 75?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 7 deaths and 631 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 56 injuries, while bikes were involved in 150 injuries. The toll falls hardest on those outside the car. Crash data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These crashes are preventable. Every death and injury is the result of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, by city planners—not fate. Crash data
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, expand speed camera enforcement, require speed limiters for repeat offenders, and redesign streets to protect people walking and biking. Crash data

      A 7979
      Simone co-sponsors bill to boost safety with speed limiters for repeat offenders.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 7979 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets triggers a speed limiter. Lawmakers move to curb repeat danger. No more unchecked speeding. Streets demand it.

      Assembly bill A 7979, now in sponsorship, sits before the New York State Assembly. Introduced August 18, 2023, it 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during an eighteen month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Assemblymember Emily Gallagher leads as primary sponsor, joined by Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, Tony Simone, Jo Anne Simon, and others. The bill aims to force chronic speeders to slow down. No safety analyst note is available, but the intent is clear: rein in repeat offenders, protect those outside the car.
