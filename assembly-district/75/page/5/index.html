      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 75?

      Eighteen Dead, No Excuses: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed FAQ citation formatting, tightened prose, deduped and reordered citations)

      AD 75: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Blood on the Asphalt

      In Assembly District 75, the numbers do not lie. Eighteen people have died on these streets since 2022. Seventy-two more suffered serious injuries. The toll is not abstract. It is a body on the crosswalk, a family waiting for news that will break them.

      Just this spring, a box truck struck and killed a man near Central Park. A few blocks away, an 83-year-old woman was killed by an SUV while crossing with the signal. A 34-year-old man died at 7th Avenue and 36th Street, the victim of a distracted driver in an SUV. Pedestrians and cyclists pay the price for someone else’s hurry.

      The Cost of Delay

      Crashes keep coming. In the last 12 months alone, there were 1,734 crashes, 4 deaths, and 25 serious injuries in this district. The dead include the old and the young. The living carry scars.

      When three NJ Transit buses collided at Port Authority, fourteen people were hurt. The terminal shut down for hours. “One with moderate injuries that required extrication, 10 patients with minor injuries all transported to local area hospitals,” said FDNY Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. The cause is still under investigation. The pain is not.

      A car fire in the Lincoln Tunnel trapped commuters for over an hour. One was hospitalized. Five more were treated at the scene. “By 6:30 p.m., traffic was moving through the tunnel again, albeit with delays,” reported the New York Post.

      Leadership: Action and Inaction

      Assembly Member Tony Simone has taken steps. He voted to extend school speed zones, a move that keeps children safer near schools. He co-sponsored bills to force repeat speeders to install speed limiters and to hold vehicle owners liable for dangerous driving. He stood with advocates for car-free plazas and better bus lanes. But he also backed bills that would weaken speed camera enforcement, making it easier for reckless drivers to dodge accountability.

      The work is not done. Every delay, every loophole, every watered-down law means another life at risk. The numbers are not just numbers. They are names, faces, futures lost.

      What You Can Do

      Call Tony Simone. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat offenders. Join groups like Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Stand with the families who have lost everything. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Change it.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents the people of New York State.
      ▸ Where does AD 75 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3 and state senate district SD 28. Data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 75?
      It includes the Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, Manhattan CB4, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, and District 6, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 47. Data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 75?
      Most injuries and deaths were caused by Cars and Trucks (601 incidents, 7 deaths), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (53 incidents, no deaths), and Bikes (143 incidents, no deaths). Data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and real enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, strengthen enforcement against repeat offenders, and support bills that protect pedestrians and cyclists instead of drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Assembly bill A 551 was introduced on January 9, 2023, and is in the sponsorship stage. The bill, titled 'Relates to reducing the speed for owner liability for failure of operator to comply with certain posted maximum speed limits,' proposes to lower the threshold for owner liability to more than seven miles per hour over the posted speed limit. Assembly Members Robert C. Carroll (District 44, primary sponsor) and Tony Simone (District 75, co-sponsor) back the measure. No safety analyst assessment is available. The bill aims to hold more drivers accountable for speeding, but its impact on vulnerable road users remains unassessed.
