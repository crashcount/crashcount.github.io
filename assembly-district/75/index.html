      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 75?

      Another Body on the Asphalt—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 75: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A man steps off the curb. A truck keeps going. On April 4, a 39-year-old pedestrian was killed by a box truck on West 40th Street. He died in the street, crushed and alone. Two months earlier, an 83-year-old woman was struck and killed by an SUV while crossing with the signal at Avenue of the Americas and West 58th. She had the light. The driver turned left. She never made it across.

      In the last twelve months, four people have died and 25 have been seriously injured on the streets of Assembly District 75. 927 people have been hurt. The numbers do not stop. The pain does not fade.

      Buses Collide, Pedestrians Fall

      On July 2, three New Jersey Transit buses crashed inside the Port Authority Bus Terminal. Fourteen people were injured. One driver was trapped and had to be cut free. The terminal shut down for hours. “One bus made contact with the rear of the other bus,” said FDNY Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. The cause is still under investigation. The ramps are crowded. The danger is constant.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Assembly Member Tony Simone has backed bills to force repeat speeders to install speed-limiting tech, require safer street designs, and raise fines for speed camera violations. He co-sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force the worst offenders to slow down. But some bills stall. Some measures shift blame to those outside the car. The streets stay deadly. “He believes fewer cars, better transit, and more people on bikes are key to a more livable city,” reads a StreetsPAC endorsement. But belief is not enough. The bodies keep coming.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. Every death is preventable. Call your council member. Call your assembly member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action on repeat offenders. Demand streets where a child can cross and live. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents districts like AD 75.
      ▸ Where does AD 75 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 75?
      It includes the Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, Manhattan CB4, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, and District 6, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 75?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm to pedestrians in AD 75, with 7 deaths and 19 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths and 4 serious injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths and 5 serious injuries. Source: NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and holding repeat offenders accountable can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, require speed-limiting tech for repeat offenders, and redesign streets to protect people walking and biking.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Res 0723-2025
      Simone Supports PAWS Act Increasing Penalties for Striking Pets
      ▸

      Council calls for Albany to pass the PAWS Act. The bill adds pets to protected victims in traffic law. It hikes fines for drivers who hit animals and flee. Lawmakers push for real consequences when cars strike dogs, cats, and other companions.

      Resolution 0723-2025 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced January 23, 2025, it urges the state to pass A10660/S9915, the Protecting Animals Walking on the Street (PAWS) Act. The resolution states: 'add the term companion animals to the list of possible victim classes should a driver fail to exercise due care to avoid striking pedestrians and to increase the fine for striking a pet with a vehicle and leaving the scene without reporting the incident.' Council Members Farah N. Louis (primary sponsor), Lynn C. Schulman, Carlina Rivera, and Justin L. Brannan back the measure. The PAWS Act would raise fines for drivers who hit pets and leave, and would recognize companion animals as protected victims under traffic law. The move targets a gap in current law, where penalties for striking animals are minor—on par with an illegal U-turn. The Council’s action signals a push for stronger accountability when vehicles harm the city’s most vulnerable, including its animals.
