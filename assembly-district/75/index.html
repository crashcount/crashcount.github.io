      Simone’s Streets: Broken Bodies, Broken Promises

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, removed duplicate headline, and cleaned citations.)

      AD 75: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Toll in AD 75

      The streets of Assembly District 75 do not forgive. In the past year, four people have died and twenty-five suffered serious injuries in crashes here. Nine hundred twenty-five were hurt. The dead include a 34-year-old man struck by an SUV at 7th Avenue and West 36th Street. The driver was distracted. The man never made it home, according to NYC Open Data.

      This spring, an 83-year-old woman was killed crossing with the signal at Avenue of the Americas and West 58th. The SUV turned left. She had the light. She lost her life, as shown in NYC Open Data.

      The violence is relentless. Buses collide at Port Authority, leaving a dozen with bruises and broken bones. A food cart breaks loose, slamming into a parked car with a woman and child inside. “Firefighters forced entry into the van, removing 76 20-pound propane cylinders and 15 five-gallon fuel containers,” reported NY Daily News. The city is a minefield.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Assembly Member Tony Simone has taken some steps. He voted yes to extend school speed zones, a move that keeps children safer near schools. He co-sponsored bills to force repeat speeders to install speed limiters and to hold vehicle owners liable for dangerous driving. These are the right fights.

      But Simone also backed bills that would weaken speed camera enforcement. Every loophole is a gift to reckless drivers. Every delay is another name on a slab.

      The Numbers Don’t Lie

      SUVs and cars did the most harm—over 600 pedestrian injuries and 7 deaths in recent years. Trucks and buses killed 2, bikes injured 143. The old story: the bigger the vehicle, the harder the blow.

      The Call: Demand More

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every death is a choice made by someone in power. Call Assembly Member Simone. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement. Demand streets where a child can cross without fear.

      Do not wait for another name. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents districts like AD 75, as detailed by Open States.
      ▸ Where does AD 75 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 3, and state senate district SD 28, according to Open States.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 75?
      It includes the Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Midtown-Times Square, Manhattan CB4, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 3, District 4, and District 6, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 47, as shown by Open States.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 75?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians in AD 75, with 601 injuries and 7 deaths in recent years. Trucks and buses killed 2, bikes injured 143, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Policies like lower speed limits, better enforcement, and safer street designs can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, expand speed cameras, require speed limiters for repeat offenders, and redesign streets to protect people walking and biking.

      34th Street Busway Sparks Debate
      ▸

      Buses crawl. Riders wait. The city weighs a busway on 34th Street. Some cheer for faster trips and safer crossings. Others fear cars will flood side streets. The board backs the plan. The street waits for change.

      West Side Spirit reported on June 15, 2025, that the NYC Department of Transportation’s plan to convert 34th Street into a busway drew mixed reactions at a Community Board 6 meeting. The board voted 31-5 in favor. Supporters called current bus speeds 'painfully slow' and urged the city to 'prioritize pedestrians and transit users.' Critics worried about diverted car traffic clogging residential streets, with one resident calling for a 'traffic study' and 'action plan.' The DOT says the busway could boost bus speeds by 15 percent. The proposal would force cars to exit 34th Street quickly or face tickets, echoing rules on 14th Street. The debate highlights the tension between transit improvements and neighborhood traffic concerns.

      ▸

      A 7997
      Simone co-sponsors bill expanding camera enforcement, potentially reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 7997 lets speed cameras catch drivers hiding or altering plates. It extends camera use in school zones. Lawmakers push to close loopholes that shield reckless drivers from accountability.

      Assembly bill A 7997, now in sponsorship, aims to expand photo speed violation monitoring in New York City. The bill, sponsored by Deborah Glick with co-sponsors Jo Anne Simon, John Zaccaro Jr., Linda Rosenthal, and Tony Simone, was introduced on April 16, 2025. It 'permits the use of photo speed violation monitoring systems in New York City for the purposes of enforcement of license plate obstruction, concealment, and/or distortion; extends provisions permitting the use of speed cameras in certain school zones.' By targeting plate obstruction, the bill seeks to stop drivers from dodging speed camera enforcement, a move that could help protect pedestrians and cyclists from repeat offenders.
