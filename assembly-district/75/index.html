      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 75?

      Eighteen Dead—Still No Action

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 75: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In Assembly District 75, the street does not forgive. Eighteen people are dead since 2022. Seventy-one more carry wounds that will not heal—serious injuries, broken bodies, lives changed (NYC Open Data).

      The last year alone saw four more deaths. Eight hundred thirty-eight people were hurt. A woman, 83, was killed crossing with the signal at Avenue of the Americas and 58th. A 34-year-old man died at 7th and 36th, struck by an SUV. The numbers do not flinch. They do not lie.

      The Crashes Keep Coming

      Just last week, a car fire in the Lincoln Tunnel sent one commuter to the hospital and left five more shaken. Traffic froze for an hour. The tunnel filled with smoke and fear. “One commuter was taken to Mount Sinai West to be treated for injuries. The extent of the victim’s injuries was unclear,” said the FDNY.

      On July 2, two buses collided at Port Authority. Fourteen people were hurt. Rescue crews had to fight their way through jammed traffic to reach the trapped. “We initially had some difficulty getting up the ramp, because it backed traffic up behind it,” said Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. The city moves, but not always forward.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Assembly Member Tony Simone has signed his name to bills that could slow the carnage. He co-sponsored measures for speed limiters on repeat offenders, higher speed camera fines, and safer street design. He stood at the ribbon-cutting for Plaza33, a block turned over to people, not cars. But the blood on the pavement says more is needed.

      The most dangerous drivers still roam free. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so. Cameras catch speeders, but the worst repeat offenders keep driving. The laws are not enough. The enforcement is not enough. The deaths do not stop.

      Call to Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Tell them to lower the speed limit. Tell them to stop the super speeders. Tell them to build streets for people, not for cars. The next name on the list could be someone you love. Do not wait.

      City Plans 34th Street Busway Overhaul

      City targets 34th Street. Buses get priority. Cars must turn off. Goal: faster rides, fewer crashes. Officials cite 14th Street’s gains—speed up, crashes down. Change comes for Midtown. Riders wait for relief.

      amNY reported on May 20, 2025, that New York City’s Department of Transportation proposed a dedicated busway for 34th Street between 3rd and 9th Avenues.       City Plans Busway On 34th Street
      ▸

      City will ban most cars from 34th Street. Only buses and trucks allowed. Bus speeds crawl at 3 mph. Officials promise faster rides, safer crossings. Pedestrians get shorter, brighter crosswalks. Policy echoes 14th Street’s busway success.

      Gothamist reported on May 19, 2025, that New York City plans to restrict most passenger vehicles from 34th Street, Manhattan, between Third and Ninth Avenues. The Department of Transportation aims to allow only buses and trucks, forcing cars to turn off quickly. The M34 bus, used by 28,000 daily riders, currently averages just 3 mph during rush hour. Officials cite the 2019 14th Street busway, which increased bus speeds by 24% and reduced crash injuries. Transportation Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'The vast majority of commuters in Midtown are traveling by transit and they deserve world-class, fast, and reliable buses.' The plan includes pedestrian improvements like shorter intersections and brighter crosswalks. The move follows previous controversies over similar projects but reflects a push for safer, more efficient streets.

      A 6225
      Simone co-sponsors bill raising speed threshold, likely reducing street safety.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 6225 drops the speed threshold for owner liability. Now, drivers face penalties for going just seven miles over the limit. The bill aims to catch more speeders. Carroll and Simone back it. The fight for safer streets continues.

      Assembly bill A 6225 was introduced on February 28, 2025. It sits in the sponsorship stage. The bill, titled 'Relates to reducing the speed for owner liability for failure of operator to comply with certain posted maximum speed limits,' lowers the threshold for owner liability to more than seven miles per hour over posted limits. Assembly Members Robert C. Carroll (District 44, primary sponsor) and Tony Simone (District 75, co-sponsor) lead the push. No votes have been recorded yet. The bill targets drivers who speed, holding car owners accountable at lower speeds. This measure could close loopholes and increase enforcement. Vulnerable road users—pedestrians, cyclists, passengers—stand to gain from stricter speed control.
