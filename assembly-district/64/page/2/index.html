      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 64?

      Ten Dead, No Answers: Tannousis Lets Traffic Violence Roll On

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 21, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, ensured citation formatting, and cleaned citations.)

      AD 64: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Death Toll Grows

      Ten dead. Eighteen left with life-altering wounds. That is the price paid on the streets of Assembly District 64 since 2022. These are not just numbers. They are fathers, mothers, children—neighbors who never made it home. In the last twelve months alone, six people died and 600 were injured in 970 crashes. The old and the young are not spared. Two people over 75 killed. No one under 18, yet. But the dice keep rolling.

      The Latest Losses

      On March 7, 2025, an 80-year-old man tried to cross Hylan Boulevard. He did not make it. A Ford SUV hit him at the intersection with Benton Avenue. He died at the hospital. Police said they were “still investigating how fast the woman was driving and what the traffic signals at the intersection were displaying at the time,” as reported by Gothamist. The driver stayed. The man did not go home.

      On May 17, 2025, a 58-year-old man was crossing Targee Street. An e-scooter hit him “outside the crosswalk,” police said. He suffered “severe trauma throughout his body” and died at the hospital, reported the New York Post. The scooter rider survived. The pedestrian did not.

      Who Is Protected?

      SUVs and trucks do the most harm. Of the deaths and injuries to pedestrians, cars and SUVs are responsible for the vast majority—six deaths, 202 minor injuries, 60 moderate, and five serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed one, injured 18, and left four with serious wounds. Bikes and mopeds are not blameless, but their toll is smaller.

      Leadership: Action or Absence?

      Assembly Member Mike Tannousis has voted for some street safety bills. He supported funding for safer street designs and better driver education. But when it mattered most, he was absent. In June 2025, he missed the vote to extend school speed zone protections, as recorded by Open States. He also sponsored bills to repeal congestion pricing, a move that would put more cars on the road and more lives at risk. The silence is loud.

      What Now?

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home. Call Assembly Member Tannousis. Demand he show up for votes that protect children and elders. Demand he stop backing bills that put more cars and more danger on our streets. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees state policy, and represents local districts like AD 64.
      ▸ Where does AD 64 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Staten Island, city council district District 50 and state senate district SD 23.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 64?
      It includes the Bay Ridge, Grasmere-Arrochar-South Beach-Dongan Hills, New Dorp-Midland Beach, Todt Hill-Emerson Hill-Lighthouse Hill-Manor Heights, Oakwood-Richmondtown, Great Kills-Eltingville, Fort Wadsworth, Hoffman & Swinburne Islands, Miller Field, Great Kills Park, and Staten Island CB95 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 47, District 50, and District 51, and State Senate Districts SD 23, SD 24, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 64?
      Cars and SUVs: 7 deaths, 202 minor injuries, 60 moderate, 5 serious. Trucks and buses: 1 death, 18 minor injuries, 7 moderate, 4 serious. Bikes: 0 deaths, 8 minor injuries, 3 moderate, 1 serious. Motorcycles and mopeds: 0 deaths, 2 minor injuries.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. They are the result of policy, street design, and enforcement. Every death and injury can be prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, support automated enforcement, and oppose bills that put more cars and danger on the road.
