      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 50?

      Twelve Dead. Thousands Hurt. Streets Still Bleed.

      Written August 12, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 50: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 12, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      Twelve people are dead. Over 2,300 have been hurt. Thirty-one suffered wounds so grave they may never walk the same. This is not a war zone. This is Assembly District 50—Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg—since 2022. The numbers do not slow. In the last year alone, five more died.

      Last spring, a ten-year-old girl was crushed crossing Wallabout Street. She had the light. The driver did not yield. She never made it home, according to NYC Open Data.

      Just months ago, a man was killed by an e-bike rider who blew through a stop sign in Greenpoint. “He died basically on the spot,” said a witness. The rider stayed. The man did not get up.

      Broken Streets, Broken Promises

      The danger is not random. Morgan Avenue has seen four deaths since 2022. Each time, neighbors gather. Each time, they ask for the same things: protected bike lanes, crosswalks, daylight. Each time, the city waits. Assembly Member Emily Gallagher called it out: “Far too many preventable crashes have taken place on Morgan Ave in recent years. We must do more to ensure walkers, cyclists, and drivers are able to use Morgan Ave safely,” she said.

      On McGuinness Boulevard, a cyclist was struck where the city left the bike lane unprotected. The road diet works where it’s been installed, but the rest of the corridor waits. “The fact is that this wouldn’t be a problem if Mayor Adams had just done what he had promised to do, which was, make all of McGuinness safe,” said advocate Kevin LaCherra.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Assembly Member Gallagher has backed bills to force repeat speeders to slow down. She sponsored measures to require speed-limiting tech for drivers with a record of violations. She voted to extend school speed zones. She has stood with families after each new death, demanding more from the city and state. But the carnage continues. Policy is not pavement. Words are not protection.

      What Comes Next

      No more waiting. Call Assembly Member Gallagher. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand speed limiters for repeat offenders. Demand real protection for every street. The dead cannot call. The living must.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents districts like AD 50 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does AD 50 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 33 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 50?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, and Brooklyn CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 33 and District 34, and State Senate Districts SD 18 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 50?
      Cars and trucks were involved in 250 pedestrian injuries and deaths, motorcycles and mopeds in 20, and bikes in 13, according to recent data. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. The same streets see the same tragedies again and again. Proven changes—like protected bike lanes and lower speed limits—can prevent deaths.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, require speed limiters for repeat offenders, and demand real protection for vulnerable road users on every street.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Gallagher Supports Safety Boosting McGuinness Boulevard Road Diet
      ▸

      City will cut car lanes, add protected bike lanes, and shorten crosswalks on McGuinness Boulevard. The redesign follows deadly crashes and strong advocacy. Officials call it transformative. DOT claims similar changes cut deaths and injuries by 30 percent.

      On May 3, 2023, the NYC Department of Transportation announced a major redesign for McGuinness Boulevard in north Brooklyn. The plan, presented to Community Board 1's Transportation Committee, will remove a car lane in each direction and install protected bike lanes along a 1.1-mile stretch from Meeker Avenue to the Pulaski Bridge. The official summary states the overhaul will 'reconfigure the 1.1-mile stretch... following advocacy for safety upgrades after multiple deaths.' Council Member Lincoln Restler and Assembly Member Emily Gallagher praised the plan, calling it 'transformative' and pledging support. DOT statistics show similar road diets reduce deaths and serious injuries by 30 percent. The redesign includes shorter crosswalks, pedestrian islands, and new loading zones. This action responds to a history of crashes and the 2021 hit-and-run killing of teacher Matthew Jensen.
