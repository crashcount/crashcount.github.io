      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 51?

      Third Avenue Is Killing Us—Who Will Stop It?

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      AD 51: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      Blood on Third Avenue

      Just last Friday, two men tried to cross Third Avenue at 52nd Street. A BMW ran the red. The car did not slow. The men, Faqiu Lin, 59, and Kex Un Chen, 80, died in the street. The driver left them behind and drove home. He told police, “I had a six pack of Modelos and two drinks. I was driving the car by myself last night. It’s my fault. After I hit them, I came home.”

      The men may have been on their way to a food pantry. “We understand they may have been on their way to our Mobile Market simply trying to access food like so many New Yorkers in need,” said City Harvest. The street was not safe for them. It is not safe for anyone.

      In the last twelve months, four people have died and 873 have been injured on these streets. Seven suffered serious injuries. The dead are old and young. The numbers do not stop. The pain does not stop.

      The Toll Grows, The Danger Remains

      The carnage is not new. In the past three years, 15 people have died in Assembly District 51. More than 2,600 have been injured. Cars and trucks did most of the killing—257 crashes involving cars and SUVs led to 3 deaths and 5 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed at least one. Motorcycles and mopeds killed another. Bikes injured dozens, but did not kill.

      The intersection where Lin and Chen died is not an outlier. In five years, a three-block stretch around the crash saw 59 collisions and 80 injuries. The city calls these “accidents.” They are not accidents. They are the result of speed, red lights ignored, and streets built for cars, not people.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Assembly Member Marcela Mitaynes has backed bills to curb repeat speeders and hold vehicle owners liable for violations. She voted to extend school speed zones, improving child pedestrian safety. She co-sponsored the Stop Super Speeders Act, which would force the worst repeat offenders to install speed limiters. She has called for cleaner, safer deliveries and more daylight at corners. But the deaths keep coming. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so.

      Call to Action: Demand Action Now

      No more waiting. No more deaths. Call Assembly Member Mitaynes. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking. The blood on the street is not washed away by words. Only action will save the next life.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents districts like AD 51. Source
      ▸ Where does AD 51 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38 and state senate district SD 17. Source
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 51?
      It includes the Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Sunset Park (West), Sunset Park (Central), Green-Wood Cemetery, Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Brooklyn CB7, and Brooklyn CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38 and District 39, and State Senate Districts SD 17 and SD 26. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 51?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 3 deaths, 5 serious injuries, and 257 total crashes. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 1 death and 15 crashes. Bikes caused 28 crashes but no deaths. Source
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. Most crashes are preventable. They happen because of speeding, red lights ignored, and streets that put cars first.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, fund safer street designs, pass laws to stop repeat speeders, and hold dangerous drivers accountable.

      A 2299
      Mitaynes co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 2299 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets in a year triggers forced speed control tech. Lawmakers move to curb repeat speeders. Streets demand fewer deadly risks.

      Assembly bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 16, 2025, the bill 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Primary sponsor Emily Gallagher leads a bloc of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, and others. The measure aims to clamp down on repeat speeders with mandatory speed-limiting tech. No safety analyst note was provided.
