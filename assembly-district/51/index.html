      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 51?

      No More Bodies in the Crosswalk

      Written July 12, 2025

      AD 51: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      Blood on Third Avenue

      On July 11, a BMW ran a red light at Third Avenue and 52nd Street in Sunset Park. Two men, Kex Un Chen, 80, and Faqiu Lin, 59, were crossing the street. The car did not slow. Both men died where they fell, walking to a food pantry. The driver sped off, leaving bodies in the crosswalk. Police later arrested him and charged him with manslaughter and criminally negligent homicide. “I saw, you know, the bodies… The head was completely in half,” a witness said, his voice flat with shock.

      This was not the first time. It will not be the last. In the past year, four people have died and 881 have been injured in crashes in Assembly District 51. Seven suffered serious injuries. The dead include elders, workers, children. The living carry scars.

      A Corridor of Broken Promises

      Third Avenue is a known danger. The city planned a redesign. The mayor paused it after business pushback. Since then, 96 crashes have injured 80 people on this stretch alone. “It was only a matter of time before somebody else died on this corridor and here we are,” said Julio Peña, a local leader. Councilmember Alexa Avilés called for “serious investments in safety enhancements” on Third Avenue.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Assembly Member Marcela Mitaynes has backed bills to curb repeat dangerous driving. She co-sponsored a measure to force speed limiters on drivers who rack up violations, and another to hold car owners liable for red-light running. She has called for universal daylighting—removing parked cars from corners to give children and elders a fighting chance to be seen. But the deaths keep coming. The pace of change is slow. Paint and promises do not stop cars.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Every death is preventable. The bodies in the street are not an act of God. They are the cost of delay, of compromise, of leaders who wait for consensus while people die. Call Assembly Member Mitaynes. Call the mayor. Demand a default 20 mph speed limit. Demand the city finish the job on Third Avenue. Demand more than words. Do not wait for another body in the crosswalk.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, including those that affect traffic safety, and is made up of representatives from districts across the state. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Where does AD 51 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, city council district District 38 and state senate district SD 17. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 51?
      It includes the Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Sunset Park (West), Sunset Park (Central), Green-Wood Cemetery, Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Brooklyn CB7, and Brooklyn CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38 and District 39, and State Senate Districts SD 17 and SD 26. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 51?
      Cars and trucks were involved in 257 pedestrian injuries and deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 15. Bikes were involved in 28. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Every death and injury is the result of choices—by drivers, by policymakers, by those who design and enforce our streets. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, redesign dangerous roads, install speed cameras, and hold reckless drivers accountable. They can push for urgent action instead of waiting for consensus. NYC Open Data

      Mitaynes Backs Safety Boosting Clean Deliveries Act
      ▸

      Every day, 9,000 trucks thunder through Sunset Park and Red Hook. Warehouses choke streets. Black and Latino residents breathe the fumes and dodge danger. Lawmakers push the Clean Deliveries Act to curb the chaos. The burden falls hard. The fight is on.

      The Clean Deliveries Act, now under consideration in Albany, aims to regulate last-mile warehouse traffic and emissions across New York State. The bill responds to a new report showing Sunset Park and Red Hook face the city's highest truck volumes—over 9,000 daily trips—linked to sprawling Amazon, FedEx, and UPS facilities. The report states, 'a quarter of residents across the Empire State live within half a mile of a distribution center that’s at least 50,000 square feet, disproportionately harming Black and Latino communities.' State Sen. Andrew Gounardes, representing the affected Brooklyn neighborhoods, calls for urgent action 'for the sake of our climate and the safety of our streets.' Assembly Member Marcela Mitaynes, whose district sees the most daily truck traffic, urges colleagues to pass the Act. Advocates like Kevin Garcia say the bill is 'key legislation to protect frontline communities from increased vehicular traffic and tailpipe emissions.' The Act would require emissions reviews, pollution minimization, and studies of low-emission zones in hotspots.

      Mitaynes Supports Safety Boosting Universal Daylighting With Barriers
      ▸

      Seven Brooklyn officials urge DOT to clear cars from corners. They want boulders, planters, and bike corrals—not just paint. Their call follows deadly crashes. They press the city to use state law and federal funds. DOT promises review. Advocates back the push.

      On January 17, 2024, seven Brooklyn elected officials—including Council Member Shahana Hanif, Borough President Antonio Reynoso, Assembly Members Marcela Mitaynes, Jo Anne Simon, Robert Carroll, and State Senators Zellnor Myrie and Andrew Gounardes—issued a joint letter to the NYC Department of Transportation. They called for 'universal daylighting with hardened materials such as boulders, planters, and bike corrals' at intersections. The officials cited recent fatal crashes and urged the city to opt into a state law banning parking within 20 feet of corners. They want federal funds used for these changes. The group opposes DOT’s slow pace and reliance on paint, demanding physical barriers. Community board leaders and advocates support the move. DOT says it will review the letter and remains committed to evidence-based daylighting.
