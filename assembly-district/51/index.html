      Fourteen Dead, Still Waiting: Who Will Stop the Killing in AD51?

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, and cleaned citations.)

      AD 51: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Slow Grind of Loss

      In Assembly District 51, the numbers do not tell the whole story. But they do not lie. Fourteen people killed. Twenty-three left with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. In three and a half years, there have been 4,416 crashes. The dead include the old and the young. A 30-year-old man crushed by a truck on 60th Street. An 82-year-old struck by a motorcycle while crossing 5th Avenue. A 66-year-old woman thrown from her moped, dead on 39th Street. The details are spare, the pain is not. See NYC Open Data.

      Most of the blood is on the hands of cars and trucks. SUVs and sedans killed three pedestrians. Trucks and motorcycles each killed one. The rest are numbers, but each number is a life. Each crash is a family changed forever.

      Leadership: Action and Silence

      Assembly Member Marcela Mitaynes has not been silent. She has backed bills to hold reckless drivers to account. She co-sponsored a law to make vehicle owners liable when their cars run red lights, closing loopholes that let dangerous drivers walk away (A 5440). She pushed for speed limiters on repeat offenders (A 2299). She voted yes to bring speed cameras to school zones, protecting children where they walk (A 7652).

      Mitaynes has also called for universal daylighting—removing parked cars from corners and using hard barriers to keep sightlines clear. She joined other Brooklyn officials demanding the city stop relying on paint and start using boulders, planters, and bike corrals. “We want federal funds used for these changes,” the group wrote. They called out the city’s slow pace and demanded real protection for people on foot and bike.

      But the deaths keep coming. The trucks keep rolling. The corners stay blind.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by someone in power. The law can change. The streets can change. But only if you demand it.

      Call your Assembly Member. Call your Council Member. Demand daylighting. Demand speed cameras. Demand that every street put people before cars.

      Do not wait for the next siren. Do not wait for the next name to become a number. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents districts like AD 51 in Albany.
      ▸ Where does AD 51 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn and city council District 38.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 51?
      It includes the Sunset Park (West), Green-Wood Cemetery, and Brooklyn CB7 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 38 and District 39, and State Senate Districts SD 17 and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 51?
      Cars and trucks: 3 deaths, 75 moderate injuries, 5 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds: 2 deaths, 2 moderate injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 7 moderate injuries. Trucks: 1 death, 4 moderate injuries.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Policy, street design, and enforcement can stop deaths before they happen.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for slower speeds, daylight corners, expand speed cameras, and hold dangerous drivers accountable.

      Mitaynes Supports Safety Boosting Universal Daylighting With Barriers
      ▸

      Seven Brooklyn officials urge DOT to clear cars from corners. They want boulders, planters, and bike corrals—not just paint. Their call follows deadly crashes. They press the city to use state law and federal funds. DOT promises review. Advocates back the push.

      On January 17, 2024, seven Brooklyn elected officials—including Council Member Shahana Hanif, Borough President Antonio Reynoso, Assembly Members Marcela Mitaynes, Jo Anne Simon, Robert Carroll, and State Senators Zellnor Myrie and Andrew Gounardes—issued a joint letter to the NYC Department of Transportation. They called for 'universal daylighting with hardened materials such as boulders, planters, and bike corrals' at intersections. The officials cited recent fatal crashes and urged the city to opt into a state law banning parking within 20 feet of corners. They want federal funds used for these changes. The group opposes DOT’s slow pace and reliance on paint, demanding physical barriers. Community board leaders and advocates support the move. DOT says it will review the letter and remains committed to evidence-based daylighting.
