      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 72?

      Seven Dead, Thirty-Five Broken—Assembly 72’s Streets Still Bleed

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, ensured proper citation links, and cleaned citations.)

      AD 72: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      Blood on the Corners

      Seven dead. Thirty-five left with serious wounds. In Assembly District 72, the numbers do not lie. From January 2022 to July 2025, the streets have claimed lives and broken bodies. A child struck by a motorscooter on Edgecombe. An elderly woman hit by an SUV on Harlem River Drive. Cyclists gashed open by car doors, riders thrown from bikes, pedestrians left bleeding on Broadway. The violence is steady, unyielding. Every crash is a family changed forever.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm. Out of all pedestrian injuries and deaths, sedans and SUVs were responsible for the largest share. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes also left their mark, but the weight of the carnage falls on four wheels. The numbers are plain: 297 injuries and deaths from cars and SUVs, 17 from motorcycles and mopeds, 9 from bikes. Trucks and buses added to the toll. The street does not care who you are. It only cares what hits you.

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      Assembly Member Manny De Los Santos has taken steps. He voted to extend school speed zones, a move that keeps children safer near schools. He co-sponsored bills to require speed limiters for repeat speeders and to push for safer street designs. He backed more bike lane enforcement and better vehicle technology. But he also voted to weaken bus lane rules, a step that puts pedestrians and cyclists at greater risk. The record is mixed. The danger remains.

      The Next Fight

      The disaster is not fate. It is policy. Every delay, every watered-down law, every loophole keeps the blood flowing. The time for half-measures is over. Call Assembly Member De Los Santos. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against repeat speeders. Demand streets built for people, not just cars. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the budget, and represents districts like AD 72.
      ▸ Where does AD 72 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, city council district District 10 and state senate district SD 31.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 72?
      It includes the Kingsbridge-Marble Hill, Washington Heights (South), Washington Heights (North), Inwood, Highbridge Park, and Manhattan CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council District District 10, and State Senate District SD 31.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 72?
      Cars and SUVs: 297 injuries and deaths. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 17. Bikes: 9. Most harm comes from cars and SUVs.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Policy choices—speed limits, enforcement, street design—can prevent them.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, support automated enforcement, fund safer street designs, and pass laws targeting repeat dangerous drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up-to-date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      A 2299
      De Los Santos co-sponsors bill to boost street safety with speed limiters.
      ▸

      Assembly bill A 2299 targets reckless drivers. Eleven points or six camera tickets in a year triggers forced speed control tech. Lawmakers move to curb repeat speeders. Streets demand fewer deadly risks.

      Assembly bill A 2299, now in sponsorship, sits with the New York State Assembly. Introduced January 16, 2025, the bill 'requires the installation of intelligent speed assistance devices if a driver accumulates eleven or more points on their driving record during a 24 month period, or receives 6 speed camera or red light camera tickets during a twelve month period.' Primary sponsor Emily Gallagher leads a bloc of co-sponsors, including Rebecca Seawright, Andrew Hevesi, and others. The measure aims to clamp down on repeat speeders with mandatory speed-limiting tech. No safety analyst note was provided.
