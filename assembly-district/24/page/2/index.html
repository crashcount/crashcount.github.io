      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 24?

      Weprin Votes, Neighbors Bleed: Demand Streets That Don’t Kill

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Updated period stats)

      AD 24: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      Blood on the Streets

      In Assembly District 24, the numbers do not lie. Ten people killed. Seventeen left with serious injuries. In three and a half years, there have been 3,681 crashes. The dead include a 31-year-old woman struck on 120th Street, a 20-year-old cyclist on 164th, a 47-year-old man crossing with the signal on 101st Avenue. Each one a life ended. Each one a family changed forever.

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay the highest price. Cars, SUVs, and trucks are the main weapons. In the last year alone, 695 people were hurt—children, elders, workers, neighbors. Not one death in the past twelve months, but the injuries keep coming. The disaster does not stop. It only slows.

      Leadership: Action and Inaction

      Assembly Member David Weprin has stood on both sides of the line. He co-sponsored bills to make streets safer for all users—bills that demand sidewalks, bike lanes, and crossings that protect the vulnerable. But when it came time to vote for a bill that would put speed cameras near schools in Schenectady, he voted no. He opposed a tool proven to slow drivers and save lives.

      Weprin also joined the chorus against congestion pricing, calling it unfair, even as most New Yorkers do not drive into Manhattan and the plan would fund safer streets and better transit. He stood with those who want to ban e-scooter share, despite city data showing no deaths or serious injuries from the program and tens of thousands of safe rides. The voices of fear are loud. The facts are quiet.

      The Cost of Delay

      Every day without change is another day of risk. The city has the power to lower speed limits. Albany can renew and expand speed cameras. Lawmakers can build streets for people, not cars. But too often, the bills stall, the votes go the wrong way, and the bodies keep coming.

      As one police report put it after a car jumped the curb and hurt nine people in South Richmond Hill, “Police stated that none of the victims suffered life-threatening injuries.” ABC7. This time. Next time, who knows?

      What You Can Do

      Call David Weprin. Call your council member. Demand safer streets. Tell them to back speed cameras, lower speed limits, and fund real street redesigns. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

The police report lists 'Unsafe Speed' as the contributing factor. The data shows all drivers were licensed and traveling straight ahead. The violence of speed left lasting harm.

      An 83-year-old woman was killed when a box truck struck her as she crossed Union Turnpike at 193rd Street in Queens. The report lists no contributing factors or driver errors, and notes the truck sustained no damage. The driver was licensed and remained at the scene. No other injuries were reported. The police report does not mention helmet use, signals, or pedestrian error. The crash left a vulnerable road user dead on a city street.

      Tractor-Trailer Crushes Parked Sedan in Queens
      ▸

      A tractor-trailer struck a parked sedan on 143rd Street. The truck kept moving. Inside the car, a 19-year-old boy died. The street stayed silent. Metal twisted. No one stopped. The crash left a family shattered.

      A tractor-trailer hit a parked Nissan sedan near 86-40 143rd Street in Queens. According to the police report, the truck rolled on after the impact, leaving the sedan crushed. Inside the car, a 19-year-old male occupant was found dead, belted in his seat. The report lists 'Driver Inattention/Distraction' as a contributing factor. The truck showed no damage and did not stop. The sedan was struck on the left side doors. No helmet or signal use was listed as a factor. The crash highlights the lethal risk heavy vehicles pose to people inside parked cars when drivers are inattentive.
