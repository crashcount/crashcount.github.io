      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Evening on Union Turnpike. A man doesn’t get home.

      AD 24: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 19, 2025

      Just after evening fell on Aug 12, 2025, a driver going straight on Union Turnpike hit a 61-year-old man near 189 Street. He died there. The police log shows the sedan’s front end took the impact; the man was crossing outside a signal or crosswalk. Source.

      The math of loss on these streets

      Since 2022 in Assembly District 24, 16 people have been killed in crashes: 8 people walking, 2 people on bikes, and 6 vehicle occupants. Injuries total 3,104, with 29 listed as serious. Source.

      Danger piles up in familiar places. The Grand Central Parkway shows 345 injuries and 6 serious injuries tied to crashes in this district’s log. Union Turnpike records 39 injuries and a death. 101 Avenue shows a death and 40 injuries. Source.

      Time cuts deep, too. Deaths appear in the hourly roll at 2 AM, 5 PM, 8 PM, 10 PM, and late at night. The pattern does not spare daylight. Source.

      What the log says about causes

      Police reports in this district list driver inattention/distraction in 28 injury cases and failure to yield in 26. Disregarding traffic control appears in 11. These are choices, written in the injury counts. Source.

      Year to date, crashes are up slightly from last year (1,067 vs. 1,021). Injuries are down (671 vs. 709). Serious injuries more than doubled (12 vs. 5). The ledger cuts both ways. Source.

      Power sits with people who can act

      On Oct 19, 2023, a Queens driver beat Jasmer Singh after a crash. He died. “We will not tolerate hate in Queens,” Queens DA Melinda Katz said when the sentence came down. Source.

      Policy is quieter, but it decides who lives. In June, the Assembly voted on a bill to extend and clean up New York City’s school speed zone laws. Assembly Member David Weprin voted no. Source. He also appeared on a list of city lawmakers who opposed renewing the city’s speed camera program. Source.

      Fix the corners. Slow the turns. Cut the speed.

      Start where people get hit. Daylight corners on Union Turnpike and 101 Avenue to clear sightlines. Add leading pedestrian intervals and hardened turn treatments where drivers turn across people in crosswalks. On freight routes and near bus stops, use concrete to keep cars out of pedestrian space; set clear truck paths off neighborhood streets. These are standard tools. They are overdue.

      Then use the citywide levers. Lower speed saves lives. Holding repeat speeders to the limit saves lives. You can push for both now. Act here.

      This report covers New York State Assembly District 24 in Queens, including Jamaica Estates–Holliswood, Jamaica Hills–Briarwood, Cunningham Park, Richmond Hill, South Richmond Hill, and Oakland Gardens–Hollis Hills.

      Year to date, crashes in Assembly District 24 are 1,067 vs. 1,021 last year. Injuries are 671 vs. 709. Serious injuries are 12 vs. 5. Source: NYC Open Data crash, person, and vehicle datasets.
      ▸ Who are the local officials?
      Assembly Member David Weprin represents this district. It overlaps City Council District 24 (James F. Gennaro) and State Senate District 11 (Toby Stavisky).
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes, Persons, Vehicles) for 2022‑01‑01 to 2025‑09‑19. We filtered records mapped to Assembly District 24 and counted injuries, serious injuries, and deaths by mode, hour, and location. Data were accessed on 2025‑09‑19. You can view the datasets here.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Jul 11 - A Nissan sedan, moving too fast, struck a man crossing Jamaica Avenue. The car hit head-on. The man’s leg split open. Blood pooled on the street. The driver had no license. The night held its breath.

      A 29-year-old man was crossing near 117-13 Jamaica Avenue in Queens when a Nissan sedan struck him head-on. According to the police report, the driver was unlicensed and traveling at unsafe speed. The impact caused severe lacerations to the pedestrian’s lower leg, leaving him conscious but bleeding on the pavement. The report lists 'Unsafe Speed' and 'Passing or Lane Usage Improper' as contributing factors. The sedan’s center front end took the brunt of the crash. No injuries were reported for the occupants inside the vehicle. The system failed the man on the street. The driver’s lack of license and reckless speed turned a crossing into a scene of violence.
