      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      84th and 35th: a right turn, a man down

      AD 39: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      Just after 6 PM on Sep 13, a driver in a 2011 Toyota turned right at 84th St and 35th Ave and hit a 57-year-old man in the crosswalk. Police recorded improper turning and distraction by the driver; the man bled from the head and survived with severe injuries (NYC Open Data).

      This Week

      • Sep 13: 84th St at 35th Ave — driver turned right and hit a person walking in the crosswalk; severe injury recorded (NYC Open Data).
      • Jul 12: overnight, a driver going straight in an SUV hit a person walking outside an intersection; severe injury recorded (NYC Open Data).

      The toll on these blocks

      Since 2022, 14 people have been killed and 1,768 injured in Assembly District 39. That is 3,658 crashes in less than four years (NYC Open Data). This year, 3 people have been killed here, compared with 1 by this point last year; reported crashes fell from 726 to 536, but the deaths rose (NYC Open Data).

      Deaths cluster in the dark hours. The district saw the highest counts around 3–4 AM (NYC Open Data). Police repeatedly log inattention and failure to yield by drivers in pedestrian crashes here, including at signalized crossings (NYC Open Data).

      Where the street bleeds

      The Long Island Expressway slices through this district. It accounts for 2 deaths and 184 injuries tied to local crashes in the period (NYC Open Data). Woodhaven Boulevard shows 1 death and 56 injuries; 80th Street shows 1 death and 20 injuries. 82nd Street appears again and again in injury files (NYC Open Data).

      In crashes that injured or killed people walking here, trucks and buses are tied to 3 deaths. The heaviest vehicles do the deepest harm (NYC Open Data).

      Hit, run, repeat

      On the LIE near 97th St, police said a second driver ran over a fallen motorcyclist and kept going. “The second driver … has not been located,” they said (NY Daily News).

      On 37th Ave at 73rd St, police said an SUV driver went around a car, ran a red, hit two girls in the crosswalk, and fled. The children lived; the driver did not stay (NY Daily News).

      The fixes are known

      Corners like 84th St at 35th Ave need basics that slow turns and make walkers visible: daylighting, hardened turns, leading pedestrian intervals, and raised crossings. Repeat hotspots on Woodhaven Boulevard and the LIE frontage need design that forces down speed and tightens lanes. Night hours need focused enforcement where deaths spike. These are standard tools; the map tells you where to put them (NYC Open Data).

      Citywide, the path is plain: lower speeds and stop the worst repeat offenders. Albany already extended and corrected NYC’s school speed zone law this June; local Assembly Member Catalina Cruz voted yes (Open States). The Council and DOT can now use that foundation and move on speed. Our city can go slower, block by block, and save lives.

      Call it what it is: a choice. The next right turn at a corner like 84th and 35th does not have to end with a man on the pavement. Act now. Take action.
