      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 27?

      AD 27: Night streets, hard deaths

      Written August 27, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      AD 27: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 27, 2025

      Data window: 2022‑01‑01 to 2025‑08‑27.

      —

      Expressways take lives

      Four deaths on the Long Island, Van Wyck, and Grand Central corridors in this district since 2024. A 33‑year‑old woman was killed on the Grand Central Parkway just after midnight on May 12, 2024. A 41‑year‑old man died on the Van Wyck late morning on Dec 3, 2024. A passenger was killed on the Long Island Expressway at about 1:40 AM on Nov 23, 2024.

      The district’s worst hotspots sit on the highways: Whitestone Expressway (2 deaths, 250 injuries), Van Wyck Expressway (2 deaths, 427 injuries), and the Long Island Expressway (4 deaths, 163 injuries).

      On Feb 10, 2024, police say an unlicensed driver rear‑ended a car on the Whitestone and fled on foot; a 43‑year‑old man died at the scene. A year later, cops arrested a 63‑year‑old man. “A grand jury indicted [Vennitti] on charges of leaving the scene of an accident resulting in death and driving without a license,” the Daily News reported. His sister said she felt “relief at the arrest after 12 months.”

      —

      Late hours, heavy toll

      Night eats people here. The district logs deaths at 1 AM, 2 AM, 5 AM, 8 PM, 10 PM, and 11 PM in the hourly rollup, with sharp injury spikes after dark and before dawn, including 142 injuries at midnight and 152 at 8 PM, plus multiple serious injuries in the late evening hours, according to city data.

      Since 2022, this small map holds 16 deaths and 2,549 injuries across 4,201 crashes, with pedestrians suffering 6 deaths and 296 injuries, and cyclists 1 death and 84 injuries, per the district summary in the same dataset. Cars and SUVs cause most pedestrian harm here: sedans are tied to 115 pedestrian casualties, SUVs to 110, far more than trucks or buses.

      —

      People on foot die in the margins

      Most pedestrian deaths in these recent highway cases happened away from intersections, the records show. The forms list “Driver Inattention/Distraction” in two fatal pedestrian crashes on the expressways in Nov–Dec 2024 and Aug 2025.

      On Aug 13, 2025, two people ordering food were hit and killed in Queens. Witnesses “say a speeding car slammed into the men,” and the driver also died, CBS reported. Speed again. Bodies again.

      —

      Buses on the curb

      On July 11, 2025, an MTA bus “slammed into a light pole and ticket machine” at 57th Road and Main Street. Seven to eight people were hurt; all injuries were described as minor. “Fortunately, authorities said the injuries were minor,” and “The accident remains under investigation,” CBS reported. A companion segment noted the same crash and injuries count as “minor,” with a brief clip of the bus mounting the sidewalk, per CBS.

      —

      What the numbers say

      • Since 2022: 16 deaths, 2,549 injuries, 4,201 crashes in AD 27, per NYC Open Data.
      • Pedestrians: 6 dead, 296 injured; cyclists: 1 dead, 84 injured. Cars/SUVs dominate pedestrian harm, per the same rollup.
      • Nighttime injuries peak around midnight and 8–10 PM; deaths appear across the late hours, per the hourly distribution.

      Top contributing factors cluster under “other,” then failure to yield and distraction, per the district analysis in the dataset.

      —

      Fix the streets that kill

      Target the known places. Slow the expressway fronts and ramps near homes and shops. Daylight the corners. Harden the turns. Give walkers the head start at signals. Focus enforcement when the harm hits: late night and early morning at the known hotspots. The district analysis flags “nighttime conditions” and “repeat hotspots.” Treat them like it.

      —

      Citywide tools that work

      Albany extended New York City’s 24‑hour school‑zone speed‑camera program this June, through bill S 8344. With cameras secured, the next lever is speed itself. A citywide 20 MPH default on neighborhood streets and intelligent speed assistance for the worst repeat offenders would cut the risk. In New York, a tiny share of drivers do outsized harm: just 1.5% of motorists cause 21% of pedestrian deaths; vehicles with 16 camera tickets in 12 months are twice as likely to kill or seriously injure, and 30+ tickets multiplies the risk fifty‑fold, per Streetsblog’s analysis. Our action guide lays out both moves and who to call. See how to press your officials.

      “Two pedestrians were hit and killed while ordering from a food truck in Queens,” CBS reported. That is the headline. The fix is speed and repeat offenders. The time is now.
