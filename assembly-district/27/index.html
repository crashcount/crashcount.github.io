      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 27?

      Absent Leadership, Rising Body Count on Our Streets

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, ensured proper citation formatting, and cleaned citations.)

      AD 27: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Toll in AD 27

      The numbers do not flinch. Fourteen people dead. Thirty-two left with serious injuries. In just the last twelve months, three more lives lost, ten more bodies broken. Nearly 800 injured. The violence does not stop. It comes for the young and the old. It comes on foot, on bikes, in cars. It comes on the way to school, to work, to the store.

      Last week, an MTA bus in Flushing jumped the curb. Seven people hurt. A mother with a baby watched the bus mount the sidewalk. “That would be scary. I’ll be more cautious of my surroundings,” she said, clutching her child CBS New York. The pole came down. The bus’s front end crumpled. The street was not safe for anyone.

      A few blocks away, another bus slammed a light pole. Eight more injured. “It must be very devastating for the people that were on the bus,” a witness said. The city calls these minor injuries. The pain is not minor for those who carry it.

      Leadership: Present or Absent?

      Assembly Member Sam Berger missed vote after vote on bills to protect children and pedestrians. In June, as the Assembly weighed laws to extend and improve school speed zones, Berger was marked absent or excused again and again Open States. The bills passed. The work was done by others. Berger’s silence was louder than any speech.

      When the city tried to expand safe mobility—bike lanes, e-scooter share—Berger joined the chorus against it. He stood with those who called proven safety programs “chaos” and demanded their end, even as the data showed no deaths, no serious injuries, and tens of thousands of safe rides.

      The Road Ahead

      The violence is not fate. It is policy. Cars and SUVs killed and maimed the most. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, and bikes all left their mark. The streets are not safe until leaders make them safe. Every day of delay is another day of blood on the asphalt.

      Call Assembly Member Sam Berger. Demand votes for school speed zones, for protected bike lanes, for real accountability. Do not wait for another bus to jump the curb. Do not wait for another name to become a number. The road does not forgive. We should not either.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, including those that affect traffic safety in your district. See legislative details.
      ▸ Where does AD 27 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 20 and state senate district SD 11. See district information.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 27?
      It includes the College Point, Whitestone-Beechhurst, Kew Gardens Hills, Pomonok-Electchester-Hillcrest, Mount Hebron & Cedar Grove Cemeteries, Flushing Meadows-Corona Park, Queens CB81, Queens CB8, and Queens CB7 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council Districts District 19, District 21, and District 24, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 14, and SD 16. See district boundaries.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 27?
      Most injuries and deaths came from cars and trucks (252 incidents), with motorcycles and mopeds (4), and bikes (5) also involved. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Policies like lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can vote for lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, expand automated enforcement, and support laws that hold dangerous drivers accountable.
