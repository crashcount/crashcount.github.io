      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in AD 31?

      Twelve Dead, No Excuses: Who Pays for New York’s Bloody Streets?

      Written August 5, 2025 (Revised August 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      AD 31: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 5, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Recent Toll

      In Assembly District 31, the road does not forgive. Twelve dead. Seventeen left with serious wounds. In the last twelve months alone, crashes tore through families and neighborhoods—1,329 people injured. The numbers do not blink. They do not care if you are young or old. Three children under 18 were killed or seriously hurt. Three elders over 75 died. The Belt Parkway, Rockaway Boulevard, South Ozone Park—names that echo with sirens and loss.

      Just weeks ago, a cyclist was left in critical condition after a hit-and-run on 115th Avenue. Police found the victim unconscious. The driver did not stop. “They are now looking for evidence to help them track down the driver” reported ABC7. Days before, a moped rider, Antonio Smith-Ortiz, was killed by a wrong-way driver who sped off into the night. “The driver, who was going against traffic in the eastbound lane, then struck an unoccupied parked van before speeding off” wrote NY Daily News.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Assembly Member Khaleel Anderson has voted to extend school speed zones and boost funding for safer street designs. He co-sponsored bills to make streets safer for all users. He voted for driver education improvements. But Anderson also voted to weaken bus rules, a move that increases risk for people on foot and on bikes. He has raised concerns about speed cameras hitting Black and brown communities hardest, but the danger on the street is not spread evenly. The dead are mostly the vulnerable.

      The System Is Failing the Vulnerable

      Cars and SUVs are the main killers. In this district, they caused six pedestrian deaths and over 340 injuries. Trucks and buses hurt dozens more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll. The system still puts the burden on those outside the car. The street does not care if you have a helmet or a green light. It only cares if you survive.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every delay, every watered-down bill, every excuse costs lives. Call Assembly Member Anderson. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns. Demand that repeat speeders lose their privilege to drive. Do not wait for another siren.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, oversees the state budget, and represents local districts like AD 31.
      ▸ Where does AD 31 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, city council district District 31 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 31?
      It includes the South Ozone Park, Springfield Gardens (South)-Brookville, Rosedale, Far Rockaway-Bayswater, Rockaway Beach-Arverne-Edgemere, Rockaway Community Park, John F. Kennedy International Airport, Jamaica Bay (East), Queens CB10, and Queens CB83 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council District District 28, and State Senate District SD 10.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 31?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians in AD 31: 6 deaths and over 340 injuries. Trucks and buses caused dozens more injuries. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes were involved in additional injury cases but no pedestrian deaths.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not just accidents—they are the result of policy choices, street design, and enforcement. The high toll in AD 31 shows these deaths and injuries can be prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, support speed cameras, and pass laws to keep repeat dangerous drivers off the road.
