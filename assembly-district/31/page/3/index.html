      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Belt Parkway before dawn. A man tries to cross. Two drivers hit him.

      AD 31: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      Just before 6 AM on Sep 12, 2025, a man crossed the Belt Parkway near 146th Street. Two drivers hit him. He died there. Police said he was trying to cross and was “struck and killed by two vehicles.” The Daily News reported he was “dragged” down the highway. “Dragged,” their headline read.

      He is one of 32 people killed on the roads of Assembly District 31 since Jan 1, 2022, with 4,954 injured. City data show the toll.

      This year alone, 10 people are dead, compared with 1 by this point last year. The crashes are up too, 1,374 this year versus 1,327 last year. Those counts are from the same dataset.

      This Week

      • Sep 12: A person walking was killed on the Belt Parkway. Police recorded multiple drivers going straight westbound. NYC Open Data and AMNY.
      • Sep 6: On South Conduit Avenue at 131 Street, a truck and a moped driver collided; the moped rider was seriously hurt. NYC Open Data.
      • Sep 6: At 122 Street and 109 Avenue, a motorcycle, two SUVs, and a bike were in a crash; a 28-year-old driver was left unconscious. NYC Open Data.
      • Aug 25: On the Belt Parkway, a 29-year-old motorcyclist was ejected and badly injured. Police listed driver distraction. NYC Open Data.

      The Belt takes the most

      The Belt Parkway is this district’s deadliest corridor: 9 deaths and 973 injuries since 2022. South Conduit Avenue follows with 2 deaths and 265 injuries. City records show these totals.

      The clock tells a blunt story. Police logs show five deaths around 3 AM and five more around 6 AM. Night to dawn is when bodies pile up. Those hour counts come from the same data.

      People on foot carry the harm. At least 9 pedestrians have been killed here since 2022, with 485 injured. Dataset breakdown shows it. Police also recorded driver “failure to yield” in deadly cases. Same source.

      What leaders did — and didn’t

      Assembly Member Khaleel Anderson voted yes in June to extend and fix school speed zones in New York City, recorded under S 8344. Vote record.

      The city has the tools to slow the killing. A citywide 20 MPH default and real penalties for repeat speeders are on the table. The Stop Super Speeders Act (S4045C/A2299C) would force the worst offenders to use speed limiters; the advocacy push is laid out here. The same page urges City Hall to use Sammy’s Law to lower speeds.

      Fix the known danger

      Belt Parkway. South Conduit Avenue. North Conduit Avenue. These are the hot zones. Use simple fixes where people cross and wait: daylighting, hardened turns, longer walk time. Target the overnight hours the records flag. And enforce driver yielding where police already cite it.

      One man died before dawn on the Belt. Others did too, at 3 AM and at 6 AM. Slow the cars. Hold the repeat offenders. Start now. Take action.

      New York State Assembly District 31, covering parts of South Ozone Park, Springfield Gardens (South)–Brookville, the Rockaways, and areas around JFK Airport.
      ▸ What stands out in the crash data here?
      Since 2022, 32 people have been killed and 4,954 injured. The Belt Parkway is the deadliest corridor with 9 deaths.       ▸ What are officials doing?
      Assembly Member Khaleel Anderson voted yes on S 8344 to extend and fix NYC school speed zones. Broader speed fixes and the Stop Super Speeders Act are laid out on our action page.
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4). We filtered crashes from 2022‑01‑01 to 2025‑09‑18 within Assembly District 31 boundaries and tallied deaths, injuries, locations, hours, and modes. Data were extracted as of Sep 17, 2025. You can explore the base crash dataset here.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
