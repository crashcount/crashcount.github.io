      No More Names on Asphalt: Demand Action Now

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      AD 31: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      Blood on the Parkway

      A man dies on the Belt Parkway. His SUV leaves the road and hits a tree. The police find him behind the wheel, dead. No arrests. No answers. Just another night near JFK, another family left with silence. The Brooklyn Paper reports.

      In the last twelve months, ten people died in Assembly District 31. Sixteen were left with serious injuries. Over a thousand more were hurt. The old and the young, the quick and the slow. No one is spared. NYC Open Data confirms.

      The Numbers That Don’t Lie

      Crashes are not random. In AD 31, the carnage is steady. In the past year: 1,474 crashes. 10 dead. 1,068 injured. The dead include a 27-year-old woman, a 23-year-old man, a 78-year-old passenger. The living carry scars. Some never walk again. Some never speak again.

      The killers are not just cars. SUVs, sedans, vans, taxis, trucks, even buses—all leave bodies behind. The numbers are cold. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Assembly Member Khaleel Anderson has signed on to bills for safer streets. He co-sponsored A 1077 and A 1280, both calling for roads that protect everyone. He voted yes on A 7652, bringing speed cameras to school zones. These are steps. But Anderson has also warned against 24/7 speed cameras, saying they could hit Black and brown communities hardest. The cameras save lives. The debate drags on. The bodies pile up.

      “We have some of the highest fatalities and accidents, yet we don’t see the highest amount of resources.” Council Member Charles Barron said, as Anderson echoed concerns about camera equity.

      What Now? No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another day of blood on the street. Call Anderson. Call the council. Demand 24/7 speed cameras. Demand streets built for people, not just cars. Demand action before another name becomes a number. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York State Assembly and how does it work?
      The New York State Assembly is the lower house of the state legislature. It passes laws, approves budgets, and represents local districts like AD 31.
      ▸ Where does AD 31 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens and city council district District 31.
      ▸ Which areas are in AD 31?
      It includes the Springfield Gardens (South)-Brookville, Rockaway Community Park, John F. Kennedy International Airport, and Queens CB83 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Council District District 28, and State Senate District SD 10.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 31?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in AD 31 were caused by SUVs and sedans (4 deaths, 53 moderate/serious injuries), followed by taxis (1 death, 1 serious injury), buses and trucks (no deaths, 2 serious injuries), motorcycles/mopeds (no deaths, 1 serious injury), and bikes (no deaths, 1 moderate injury). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. The data shows patterns—speed, unsafe driving, and street design all play a role. These deaths are preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and support bills for safer street design, expand speed camera programs, and push for lower speed limits and better enforcement.
