      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Blood on Northern Boulevard—No More Ghosts in the Crosswalk

      AD 40: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Toll in Flushing: Lives Lost, Families Shattered

      A 78-year-old woman tried to cross Northern Boulevard. She never made it. A dark minivan hit her and kept going. Police said, “A 78-year-old woman was fatally struck by a hit-and-run driver as she crossed a Queens street”. No one has been arrested. Her name is not in the news. Her absence is.

      Children are not spared. In late 2023, a driver pulled out of a parking lot on College Point Boulevard and killed 3-year-old Quintas Chen. The driver fled. Assembly Member Ron Kim, who walks that block every day, said, “We’ve always recognized there was a problem on this block”. The problem remains.

      In the last 12 months, 2 people died and 448 were injured in crashes in AD 40. Fourteen were seriously hurt. The dead are mostly the old and the young. The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do.

      Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. Since 2022, cars and trucks have caused 7 deaths and 13 serious injuries to pedestrians here. Motorcycles and mopeds have hurt more. Bikes, too, leave scars. No one is safe—not the man pinned under a car with a child beside him, not the cyclist crushed by a turning truck, not the grandmother who never left the crosswalk.

      What Has Ron Kim Done?

      Assembly Member Kim has voted to extend school speed cameras and backed bills for safer street designs. He co-sponsored measures to make streets safer for all users. He voted yes to expand speed cameras near schools (S 7678), and yes to require complete street designs (A 8936).

      But Kim also voted to weaken bus lane rules (S 7785), a move that puts pedestrians and cyclists at risk. The work is not done. The danger is not gone.

      Call to Action: Demand More

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. Call Assembly Member Ron Kim. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for the old, the young, and everyone in between. Do not wait for another name to be lost to the street. Take action now.
