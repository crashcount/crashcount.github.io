NYC’s Deadly Streets: 38 Lives Lost, and Still No Action

By The Count of Crash

March 10, 2025

The week of February 24

Another Week, Another Disaster

Seven more lives gone. A 26-year-old woman killed while riding in the front seat. A 17-year-old boy crushed behind the wheel. A 57-year-old cyclist thrown from his bike. A baby, too young to walk, struck while crossing with his family. The streets took them all.

This year, 38 people have died on New York’s roads. The city’s crash numbers are down, but serious injuries are up. The destruction is relentless. The pace slows, then quickens, but it never stops.

The Human Cost

Imani Vance was 26. She was riding in a Mercedes when the driver slammed into a school bus. He was unlicensed. He fled the scene. Her mother, Lakita Vance, is left with nothing but grief. “He killed my baby,” she said. “Imani was the light of the family.” She was the party. Now, her family is planning a funeral.

Justin Diaz was 23. He had a degree in aeronautics, a future ahead of him. He was on his way to work when an off-duty firefighter ran a red light, doing 83 mph in a 25 mph zone. The firefighter was drunk. The firefighter was high. Justin Diaz died in the hospital. His brother Franklin now speaks for him: “Thanks to Pena’s actions, Justin cannot live his dreams.”

A woman stepped out of a taxi in Boerum Hill and never made it across the street. The car that hit her didn’t stop. No arrests have been made.

Solutions Ignored

The numbers should shame our leaders into action. They don’t. Albany gave New York the power to lower speed limits. The city hasn’t done it. The streets are still built for cars, not people. The blood keeps flowing.

“These statistics are outrageous,” said Assemblyman Smullen, whose son was killed in a crash. “They’re real people, real New Yorkers that are affected by this.” He has chosen to act. Will the city?

It’s simple: Lower speed limits. Redesign streets. Enforce the laws. Do it before next week’s body count.

Take Action

What you need to do now is take action. Demand the city lower speed limits. Demand safer streets. Demand an end to this carnage. Because next week, the numbers will rise again.
