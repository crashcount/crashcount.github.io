One More Dead, Hundreds More Hurt: NYC’s Streets Keep Taking Lives

By The Count of Crash

March 3, 2025

The week of February 17

The Carnage Continues

A man on a bike was killed last week. A driver hit him at 3:05 p.m. on February 18. He was 67. Another statistic. Another ghost on the streets of New York.

A three-year-old girl was hurt getting off a vehicle on February 23. Severe lacerations to the head. She will live. She might remember.

In just one week, 820 people were injured. Sixteen of them seriously, meaning crushed bones, lost limbs, or burns that may never heal right. One person died. A quiet week, if such a thing exists.

The Year So Far: A Slow-Motion Disaster

The city has seen 11,238 crashes since the start of the year. More than 6,100 injured. Thirty-one dead.

That’s fewer than last year, but what comfort is that? The goal was zero. Zero funerals. Zero parents burying children. Zero families shattered by the news that their loved one is never coming home.

Instead, we have Marcus Joseph, who lost control of his BMW and died on the Belt Parkway this week. We have the man in Brooklyn who had to cling to a car hood for 330 feet to avoid being run down in a fit of road rage. We have a girl, just three years old, bleeding in the street.

“These statistics are outrageous. They’re real people, real New Yorkers that are affected by this,” said Assemblyman Smullen, who lost his own son to reckless driving (NY1).

What Needs to Change

Slower streets save lives. London did it. Fatalities dropped. New York could do the same tomorrow. Albany gave the city the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. The city has not acted.

Congestion pricing is already reducing crashes in lower Manhattan. But the fight to keep it is still raging. Governor Hochul is defending it, even as federal officials circle, looking for reasons to kill it (#citations).

The city is finally adding double-wide bike lanes to Sixth Avenue (Streetsblog NYC). A small victory. But will they be enforced? Or will they turn into more parking for delivery trucks?

Act Now, Before the Next Victim Falls

One man last week. Thirty-one this year. Who will be next?

Speed is the killer. It is the factor that turns injuries into deaths. The city can slow the streets. It must.

Demand action. The mayor and city council need to hear from you. Take action now before the next person dies.
