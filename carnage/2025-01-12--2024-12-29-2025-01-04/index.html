Week of Dec 29

Michael Foster Died Alone in the Street. How Many More Will Follow?

Michael Foster stood at the stoplight on Caton Avenue, as he had many nights before. He would step into traffic, hand outstretched, hoping for a few dollars from passing drivers. At 7:30 p.m. on New Year’s Eve, a white Audi came too fast. It hit him, dragged him for half a mile, and left him in the street. The driver never stopped.

Noel Clauson, the superintendent of Foster’s building, saw him that night. “I saw him at the stop light. He would go out to the cars and beg for change. That night a car must have come along and hit him,” Clauson said. NY Daily News.

Foster was 64. He had no family. His roommate, John Isdore, said he “was in a drug rehab program” and mostly kept to himself. “He lived here with the two of us and we shared the apartment. He kept to himself and he didn’t have any family,” Isdore said. NY Daily News.

The City’s Most Vulnerable, Left to Die

Foster was one of three people killed on New York City streets this week. In Brooklyn alone, two pedestrians died. Across the city, 821 people were injured in crashes. Nine suffered serious injuries—crushed limbs, paralysis, wounds that will never fully heal. NYC Open Data.

Panhandlers, the unhoused, and those living on society’s edge are among the most at risk. They walk where there are no safe crossings. They stand in the road because there is nowhere else to go. And when they are hit, the city moves on.

A City That Still Fails to Protect Pedestrians

New York has promised safer streets. It has redesigned intersections, lowered speed limits, and expanded enforcement. But the deaths continue. The hit-and-run driver who killed Foster is still out there. No arrests have been made.

How many more will die before the city takes real action? Before every street is made safe, not just for those with homes and cars, but for those with nowhere else to stand?

You can demand change. Take action now. Because if the city forgets Michael Foster, it will forget the next one too.
