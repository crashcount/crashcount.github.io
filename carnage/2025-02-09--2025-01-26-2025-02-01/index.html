Week of Jan 26

The Last Survivor Falls

A Life of Survival

Mayya Gil had already beaten death. As a child, she fled the Nazis. As a young woman, she lived under Soviet rule. She survived the Chernobyl disaster. She endured the loss of her husband to COVID. At 95, she was still walking the streets of Bensonhurst, still a matriarch, still a survivor.

“She basically raised me since day one,” said her granddaughter. “She was the kindest, most generous person I’ve ever met.”

She had seven great-grandchildren. She still visited the Jewish Community Center of Bensonhurst. She still walked these streets. And then, in an instant, a cargo van ended it all.

A City That Couldn’t Protect Her

Mayya had survived war, disaster, and disease. But New York City’s streets took her life.

She was crossing Cropsey Avenue with her home health aide. A cargo van turned left. It struck them both. The aide survived. Mayya did not.

She was the second elderly pedestrian killed in Brooklyn this year. Just weeks before, 87-year-old Esther Sealy was struck and killed in Canarsie. Last year, 46 seniors died in crashes citywide.

The city knows older pedestrians are the most vulnerable. It knows drivers fail to yield. It knows intersections like Cropsey Avenue are dangerous. And yet, it lets this happen again and again.

“Everybody knows her,” said her daughter. “She was a very active lady.”

If She Couldn’t Survive, Who Can?

The driver was not arrested. No charges were filed. Mayya’s death was treated as just another accident—another life lost, another driver who will face no consequences.

“Nothing gave her more joy than just being around her family,” said her granddaughter. Now, they are left with grief and questions.

How many more elders will die before the city acts? How many more families will be shattered?

Take action now. Because if Mayya Gil couldn’t survive these streets, who can?
