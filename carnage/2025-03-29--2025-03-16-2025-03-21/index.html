Week of Mar 16

A Toddler Bleeds in Bushwick

Just before 8 p.m. on a Sunday, a mother and her toddler tried to cross Myrtle Avenue near Linden Street. There was no crosswalk. A sedan came fast. The driver didn’t slow down.

The 2-year-old girl was struck in the leg. Her 50-year-old mother was hit in the hip. Both were conscious. Both in pain. Neighbors ran out. Someone called 911.

This is a block without a signal. Without a crosswalk. Just a stretch of asphalt where drivers rarely stop. Brooklyn bore the brunt: 384 crashes, 209 injuries, 2 deaths during the week of March 16–21.

Children walk here. Elders cross to the bodega. Delivery workers ride past on e-bikes. The street does not forgive.

Intersections That Don’t Protect

In the Bronx, an 81-year-old man was struck while crossing at Westchester and Metcalf. He suffered a head injury and severe bleeding. He was unconscious when help arrived. That intersection has no pedestrian island. No leading signal. Just a light—and a prayer.

In Manhattan, a 55-year-old man was hit at Broadway and Waverly. He had the signal. It didn’t matter. He was left with crush injuries to his legs.

At Bleecker and Lafayette, a 54-year-old pedestrian was left semiconscious with head trauma. Another intersection. Another body on the ground.

The City Knows This Happens

These are not freak events. They are the cost of inaction. “It’s not a unicorn incident,” said Jack Collins, a restaurant worker in Greenpoint, after witnessing a fatal crash. “It’s happened a lot.”

He wasn’t just talking about e-bikes. He was talking about a city where people get swiped at intersections every week.

The city has the tools. Raised crosswalks. Daylighting. Lower speed limits. Sammy’s Law passed. Speed cameras work. But the streets still strike.

A City That Moves Too Slow

Mayor Adams promised to double intersection safety improvements. But the crashes keep coming.

This year, serious injuries are up 24% over last. The bodies pile up. The city moves on.

The toddler in Bushwick survived. But the next one might not.

Take action now. Because if the city won’t protect its most vulnerable, who will?
