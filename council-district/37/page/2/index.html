      Three Dead, Hundreds Hurt—District 37’s Streets Still Bleed

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, deduped and cleaned citations.)

      District 37: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      Three dead. Twenty-four left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. In the last year alone, District 37 saw 1,612 crashes, 907 injuries, and 3 deaths, according to NYC Open Data. The numbers are blunt. The pain is sharper. A 71-year-old woman, crossing Knickerbocker and Eldert, never made it to the other side. A 59-year-old man, walking with the light on Wyckoff, was struck and killed by a van. A 55-year-old man, crossing Fulton in Cypress Hills, was hit by a Ford Explorer. The driver fled. The street kept its silence.

      Police said, “the man was crossing the street in Cypress Hills when he was hit by a burgundy Ford Explorer.” The car did not stop. The man died at the hospital.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Sandy Nurse has signed her name to bills that matter. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act, pushed for protected bike lanes, and voted to end jaywalking enforcement—removing a law that too often blamed the dead instead of the driver. She called out the city’s failure to remove ghost cars: “Cars with ghost plates are a risk to public safety.” But bills alone do not save lives. Many measures sit in committee. Promises pile up. The danger does not wait.

      The Machines That Kill

      Most injuries and deaths come from cars and trucks. In three years, SUVs and sedans killed at least one person and seriously injured six more. Trucks and buses killed two. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes left more bodies broken. The street does not care what you ride or drive. It only cares who is left standing.

      What Comes Next: The Choice Is Ours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day the city delays, another family risks losing someone. Call Council Member Nurse. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action, not just words. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents local districts like District 37.
      ▸ Where does District 37 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 37?
      It includes the Bushwick (East), The Evergreens Cemetery, Cypress Hills, Highland Park-Cypress Hills Cemeteries (South), and Brooklyn CB4 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 53, AD 54, and AD 55, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 19, and SD 25.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 37?
      Cars and Trucks: 1 death, 101 moderate injuries, 6 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses: 2 deaths, 15 moderate injuries, 6 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 8 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable, or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Policy, design, and enforcement—not luck—decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, daylighting at crosswalks, protected bike lanes, and stronger penalties for dangerous driving.

      Nurse Supports Safety Boosting E-Bike Charging Infrastructure Bill
      ▸

      Council Members Nurse and Rivera push DOT to install 175 e-bike charging hubs in five years. Only three city-run stations exist now. Advocates say the plan lags behind surging e-bike use. Riders and delivery workers face mounting risks from unsafe charging.

      On October 11, 2024, Council Members Sandy Nurse and Carlina Rivera introduced a bill requiring the Department of Transportation to install 35 e-bike charging stations each year for five years, totaling 175 hubs. The bill, now before the Council, mandates at least 25 percent of stations be placed in the roadway and requires DOT to report on usage and maintenance after three years. The matter title calls for 'expanding e-bike charging and battery safety infrastructure.' Rivera said, 'We certainly want to expand the conversation around e-bike charging and battery safety as much as possible.' Industry voices, like Baruch Herzfeld of Pop Wheels, argue the plan is too small to meet demand or curb deadly indoor charging fires. Ligia Guallpa of the Worker’s Justice Project warns that infrastructure has not kept pace with e-micromobility growth, increasing risks for riders and workers. The bill follows years of slow city action, with only three public charging stations operating today.

      Int 0346-2024
      Nurse votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.
