      No More Names on Asphalt: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 41: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      Blood on the Crosswalks

      In District 41, the street is a gauntlet. In the last year, 873 people were injured and one person was killed in crashes. Six suffered injuries so grave they may never walk the same. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors. They are elders and children.

      Maurette Lafleur, 68, crossed Rutland Road with the walk signal. A Mercedes turned left and struck her down. She died at the hospital. A witness described the crash: “The lady spin around and sped through.” The driver stayed. No charges. The bones broke. The city moved on.

      Larry Maxwell, 72, was heading to a family cookout. A driver fleeing another crash hit him on Sutter Avenue. The driver vanished. Maxwell’s son asked: “There are so many cameras. So why is he still at large?” The cameras blinked. The street stayed the same.

      Who Gets Protected?

      Most injuries come from cars and SUVs. In three years, cars and SUVs killed two, trucks killed one, and motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes left dozens more hurt. Pedestrians and cyclists take the blows. Drivers walk away.

      What Has Darlene Mealy Done?

      Council Member Darlene Mealy has voted for some safety bills. She backed the law that legalized jaywalking, ending a policy that blamed the dead for their own deaths. She voted for a citywide greenway plan, and supported 24/7 speed cameras. These are steps. But she also co-sponsored bills that would target e-bike riders, shifting blame to those with the least power. The street stays deadly. The bills pile up. The bodies do too.

      What Next?

      This is not fate. Lower the speed limit. Build real protection at crossings. Demand the city use its new power to set 20 mph limits. Call your council member. Call the mayor. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 41 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 41?
      It includes the Lincoln Terrace Park, Ocean Hill, Brownsville, and Brooklyn CB16 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 55, AD 56, and AD 58, and State Senate Districts SD 19, SD 20, and SD 25.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 41?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm in District 41, with 2 deaths and 8 serious injuries to pedestrians in the last three years. Trucks caused 1 death. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 2 serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 2 moderate injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are not random. Lower speeds, better street design, and real enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected crossings, expand camera enforcement, and pass laws that protect people instead of blaming them.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Council District 41

      Res 0009-2022
      Mealy sponsors resolution pushing MTA for accessible subway stations, boosting transit safety.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to make every renovated subway station fully accessible. Only a fraction of stations have elevators. Lawmakers want no more half-measures. The resolution was filed at session’s end. Riders with disabilities remain stranded underground.

      Resolution 0009-2022 was introduced on February 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It called on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to ensure any subway station undergoing enhancement or renovation becomes fully accessible to people with disabilities. The matter title reads: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make any subway stations undergoing enhancement or renovation fully accessible to people with disabilities.' Council Member Darlene Mealy sponsored the resolution, joined by Brannan, Menin, Louis, Yeger, Hanif, Hudson, Marte, Joseph, Riley, and Brooks-Powers. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. Only 117 out of 493 subway stations are accessible. The Council’s action highlights the city’s failure to guarantee safe, equal passage for all riders. Elevators and upgrades are overdue. The bill’s filing leaves vulnerable New Yorkers waiting.

      Head-On Crash Shatters Two Drivers on Blake Avenue
      ▸

      Steel met steel on Blake Avenue. A van and a box truck collided head-on. Two men, both drivers, left injured and in shock. One’s shoulder crushed, the other’s chest battered. The street fell silent. No answers, only pain and twisted metal.

      A van and a box truck collided head-on near Blake Avenue and Strauss Street in Brooklyn. According to the police report, both vehicles were going straight when they struck each other. The 51-year-old van driver suffered a shattered shoulder and crush injuries. The 38-year-old box truck driver sustained internal chest injuries. Both men were strapped in and left in shock. The report lists the contributing factors as 'Unspecified.' No pedestrians or cyclists were involved. The crash left both vehicles with heavy front-end damage. The police report offers no further details on the cause.
