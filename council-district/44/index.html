      Felder’s Silence, Their Blood: How Many More Must Die on Ocean Parkway?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      District 44: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      In District 44, the numbers bleed. Sixteen dead. Twenty-three left with injuries so grave they will not forget. In the last year alone, seven people killed, ten more with serious wounds. Children, elders, mothers. A family wiped out on Ocean Parkway. A mother and her daughters, crossing with the light, struck down by a driver who never touched the brakes. The car was moving at 68 miles per hour. The law says 25. The street remembers their names, even if the city forgets.

      Brooklyn District Attorney Eric Gonzalez called it “one of the worst collisions I’ve ever seen on a New York City street”. The driver had a suspended license, over 20 speed camera tickets, and five for running red lights. She should not have been behind the wheel. “What is very clear is that she should not have been on the road that day,” said Gonzalez.

      The Pattern: Cars, Trucks, and the Weight of Steel

      SUVs and sedans kill most often here. Ten deaths by car or SUV, two by truck or bus, none by bike. The old and the young are not spared. In the last twelve months, two children died. Two elders. The street does not care who you are. It only cares how fast you move and how hard you hit.

      The Silence: Leadership Missing in Action

      Council Member Simcha Felder has a record of blocking safety. He voted against 24-hour speed cameras, opposed lowering speed limits, and fought street redesigns. After the Ocean Parkway killings, Felder made no public statement, skipped the funeral, and told the press, “Some have wasted no time using this tragedy as an opportunity to advance their agenda. There is a time to act but there is also a time to mourn…” told the press. The time to act is now. Every day of delay is another risk, another family left to mourn.

      What Next: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      Call Council Member Felder. Demand he support lower speed limits, more speed cameras, and street redesigns that protect people, not cars. Join Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Speak for those who cannot. The dead do not get a second chance. The living must fight for the first.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the legislative body for the city, passing laws and overseeing city agencies. Council Members represent local districts and can propose, vote on, and influence street safety policies. NYC Council – Legistar
      ▸ Where does District 44 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in District 44?
      It includes the Borough Park and Mapleton-Midwood (West) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 45, AD 47, and AD 48, and State Senate Districts SD 17 and SD 22. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 44?
      Cars and Trucks: 10 deaths, 117 moderate injuries, 7 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 3 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury. Bikes: 0 deaths, 6 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or are they just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Speeding, red-light running, and lack of street design changes are all factors that can be addressed by policy and enforcement. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can support lower speed limits, expand speed camera programs, and redesign dangerous streets to protect people walking and biking. NYC Open Data
