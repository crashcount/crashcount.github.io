      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 44?

      Blood on the Streets, Silence in City Hall

      Written July 9, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 44: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      In District 44, the street is a wound that never closes. Eighteen people have died here since 2022. Nearly 2,000 have been injured. Children, elders, workers—none are spared. In the last year alone, eight lives ended and over 600 were hurt. The numbers are bodies. The bodies are neighbors.

      Just days ago, a 90-year-old man was left bleeding on Avenue U. The moped rider who hit him wore a mask and fled. “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open,” said an eyewitness. No arrest. No peace.

      On Avenue I, a Smart Car driver “plowed into” a 70-year-old man crossing near his home. The driver stayed. Dov Broyde did not survive. No charges were filed. The city counts the dead. The street forgets nothing. NY Daily News

      Patterns of Harm

      SUVs and cars are the main killers. They caused ten deaths and over 450 injuries. Trucks and buses killed two. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no pedestrian deaths but left a trail of broken bodies. Bikes injured sixteen. The old, the young, the ones just crossing the street—none are safe.

      The violence is not random. It is mapped in blood. Three children died in the last year. A mother and her two daughters were killed crossing with the signal. The driver had 29 speed camera tickets. Still, he drove. Still, they died.

      Leadership: Action or Absence?

      Council Member Simcha Felder has not led. There are no recent bills, no public stands, no urgent calls for safer streets. The silence is loud. The dead pile up. The city has tools—lower speed limits, more cameras, safer crossings. But in District 44, the work stalls. The council member’s record is a blank page while the obituaries grow.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Thoughts and Prayers

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Council Member Felder. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people on foot and bike. Demand action before another family mourns at the curb.

      Do not wait for the next siren.

      Lawsuit Challenges Bedford Avenue Bike Lane Removal
      ▸

      City moved to erase a protected bike lane. No warning. No legal notice. Cyclists and advocates sued. They say the city broke its own law. The lane made streets safer. Now, the fight is in court. Vulnerable lives hang in the balance.

      Streetsblog NYC reported on June 17, 2025, that activists and residents sued the city to stop the removal of the Bedford Avenue protected bike lane. Plaintiffs argue the city violated Administrative Code Section 19-101.2, which requires advance notice and community input before removing bike lanes. The Department of Transportation skipped this step, announcing the removal on social media after consulting only select community members. The article quotes the lawsuit: the city acted "improperly, irrationally, without proper legal notice and in an abuse of discretion." Data cited in the suit show the upgraded lane improved safety for all road users. Councilman Lincoln Restler supported the legal action, seeking a restraining order to keep the lane. The case highlights failures in city process and the risks posed when safe infrastructure is abruptly erased.

      NYC DOT Targets Intersection Danger Zones
      ▸

      City will block cars from corners. Barriers, granite, planters will clear sightlines. DOT acts after half of deaths hit at intersections. Brooklyn gets first fixes. Cyclists and walkers may see drivers before impact. Steel and stone replace painted lines.

      The Brooklyn Paper reported on June 11, 2025, that NYC DOT will redesign intersections to protect cyclists and pedestrians. The plan uses 'hardened daylighting'—physical barriers, granite blocks, and planters—to stop cars from parking near corners. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety.' Nearly half of city traffic deaths happen at intersections, often due to blocked sightlines and turning vehicles. The redesign targets high-crash Brooklyn intersections first, including Ocean Avenue at Church Avenue. The move highlights the city's shift from painted lines to physical infrastructure, aiming to reduce systemic risk for vulnerable road users.

      ▸

      City eyes a center bus lane on Flatbush. Concrete islands promise safer crossings. Details remain thin. Cars may still block buses. The street could change. Pedestrians and riders wait. The city holds its breath.

      Streetsblog NYC (2025-06-09) reports the Department of Transportation plans a center-running bus lane on Flatbush Avenue, Brooklyn. The project aims to connect neighborhoods and calm traffic. The plan includes concrete bus boarding islands and may reduce car lanes, giving more space to pedestrians and buses. The proposal highlights the need for clear enforcement and design to keep cars out of bus lanes and protect vulnerable road users.
