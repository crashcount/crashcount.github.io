      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 40?

      No More Orphans of the Crosswalk

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 20, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      District 40: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Broken, Streets Unforgiving

      In District 40, the numbers do not lie. Five people killed. Thirty-two left with serious injuries. Over 2,700 hurt since 2022. The dead do not get a second chance. The wounded carry scars—some seen, most not. The young are not spared. Forty-eight children injured in the last year alone.

      Just last month, a teenager was crushed by a distracted driver on Foster Avenue. A three-year-old was struck on Coney Island Avenue. The old and the young, the walker and the cyclist, all at risk. Trucks and SUVs do the most harm.

      The Pattern: Same Streets, Same Blood

      The violence is not random. It is routine. Sedans, SUVs, and trucks are the main weapons. In three years, cars and SUVs caused 2 deaths and over 450 injuries to pedestrians. Trucks killed 3 more. Bikes and mopeds hurt, but they do not kill here. The crosswalk is no sanctuary. The curb is no shield.

      The Response: Council Member Joseph’s Record

      Council Member Rita Joseph has taken steps. She co-sponsored bills to speed up protected bike lanes, require speed humps near parks, and boost crash investigations. She voted to remove abandoned vehicles and to decriminalize jaywalking, a move that puts the burden back on drivers, where it belongs. She backed transparency on street upgrades and supported daylighting to clear crosswalks. But some bills—like the push for daylighting barriers at every intersection—are still stuck in committee, waiting for action.

      “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter,” said Ruiz’s sister-in-law. The grief is not abstract. It is a mother’s loss, a family’s wound.

      The Next Fight: Demand Relentless Action

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every day without safer streets is a choice. Call Council Member Joseph. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes on Empire Boulevard and every deadly stretch. Demand that stalled bills move. “Drivers speed on that stretch of roadway,” neighbors told CBS. The city knows. The council knows. Now they must act.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Call. Write. Show up. Make them feel the cost.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of residents in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 40 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 42 and state senate district SD 20.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 40?
      It includes the Prospect Lefferts Gardens-Wingate, Flatbush, Flatbush (West)-Ditmas Park-Parkville, East Flatbush-Erasmus, Brooklyn CB14, and Brooklyn CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 42, AD 43, AD 44, and AD 48, and State Senate Districts SD 20 and SD 21.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 40?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 5 deaths and 2703 injuries since 2022. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths and 14 injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths and 14 injuries.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Policy, design, and enforcement choices shape who lives and who dies on our streets.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, daylighting, and better crash investigations. They can push for action on stalled safety bills and demand city agencies act faster.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
