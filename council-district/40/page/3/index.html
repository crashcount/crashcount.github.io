      No More Excuses: Streets Are Killing Us

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, ensured proper link formatting, and cleaned citations.)

      District 40: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt

      A man dragged half a mile by a speeding Audi. A school bus jumps the curb, crushing a mother and her son against a fence. In the last twelve months, 743 people have been injured on the streets of District 40. Eleven suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Not one death this year—yet. But five dead since 2022. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      On Caton Avenue, a witness saw the last moments of a neighbor: “I saw him at the stop light. He would go out to the cars and beg for change. That night a car must have come along and hit him.” NY Daily News

      A school bus driver hit the gas instead of the brake. The bus leapt the curb. “The little boy was pretty much in shock,” a school worker said.

      The Record of Leadership

      Council Member Rita Joseph has backed bills to daylight crosswalks, add speed humps, and force the city to build protected bike lanes. She voted to end jaywalking enforcement, shifting blame away from the people most at risk. She co-sponsored the push for 100 miles of protected bike lanes each year, but the city lags far behind. Joseph supported the call for a protected bike lane on Empire Boulevard, where two cyclists and two pedestrians have died since 2011. She has spoken after tragedy: “We must continue our efforts to make our streets safer for all.” Streetsblog NYC

      But words and bills do not stop cars. The painted lanes fade. The curb cuts stay unprotected. The city delays. The dead do not come back.

      What Comes Next

      The crisis is not over. The next crash is coming. The only question is who will pay. Contact Rita Joseph. Demand real protection: daylight every crosswalk, build real barriers, lower the speed limit to 20 mph. Join Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Act now. The street will not forgive delay.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 40 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, and overlaps with Assembly Districts AD 42, AD 43, AD 44, AD 48, and State Senate Districts SD 20 and SD 21. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in District 40?
      It includes the Prospect Lefferts Gardens-Wingate and East Flatbush-Erasmus neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 42, AD 43, AD 44, AD 48, and State Senate Districts SD 20 and SD 21. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 40?
      Cars and Trucks were responsible for the most deaths and serious injuries, with 2 deaths and 10 serious injuries from SUVs and cars, and 3 deaths and 3 serious injuries from trucks and buses. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 4 moderate injuries. Bikes caused 4 moderate injuries but no deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents. They are the result of choices—speed, street design, and enforcement—that can be changed to save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, daylight crosswalks, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can also push city agencies to act faster and fund real street redesigns.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

Joseph’s district ranks low in protected bike lane coverage, despite high rates of walking, biking, and transit use. The bill sits before the Council, with DOT reviewing.

      Int 0923-2023
      Joseph co-sponsors bill to study last-mile delivery traffic impacts.
      ▸

      Council filed a bill to force a city study on truck and delivery traffic from last mile warehouses. The bill targets congestion, collisions, and harm to neighborhoods. It demands hard numbers on vehicle flow, street damage, and danger to people outside cars.

      Int 0923-2023 was introduced on February 16, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, sponsored by Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and Council Member Alexa Avilés, with over thirty co-sponsors, sought a city study on the impact of truck and delivery traffic from last mile facilities. The official summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to conducting a study of the impact that truck and delivery traffic generated by last mile facilities have on local communities and infrastructure.' The bill required the Department of Transportation to report on delivery vehicle volumes, parking, congestion, collisions, and pedestrian injuries near these hubs.
