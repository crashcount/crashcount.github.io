      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 40?

      Deadly Corners, Broken Promises: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 29, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 40: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Shattered, Streets Unforgiving

      In District 40, the numbers do not lie. Five people killed. Thirty-one left with serious injuries. Over 2,600 hurt since 2022. The dead do not speak. Their absence is felt in empty chairs, unopened doors, and the silence after sirens fade.

      Trucks and SUVs crush bodies at corners. Pedestrians are struck crossing with the light, against the light, or just trying to live. Most injuries and deaths happen at intersections. The city’s own data shows the carnage is not random. It is built into the streets.

      Leadership: Promises, Laws, and the Slow March of Change

      Council Member Rita Joseph has stood with those demanding safer streets. She co-sponsored bills to build 100 miles of protected bike lanes each year and to ban parking near crosswalks, forcing the city to clear corners and save lives. She voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed the vulnerable instead of the reckless. She backed the SAFE Streets Act and pushed for speed humps near parks and schools.

      But bills alone do not stop the bleeding. “Redesigning our streets under Vision Zero has helped us reduce traffic deaths to historic lows but we are always looking for new ways to keep people safe,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez, as the city finally began adding hard barriers at high-crash intersections in Brooklyn.

      The System Fights Back—But Not Fast Enough

      Nearly half of all traffic deaths happen at intersections. The city is rolling out hardened daylighting—granite blocks, planters, concrete—at corners where people keep dying. Advocates call it a start, but not enough. “Anything with real (not plastic) infrastructure in street corners is good news,” said Jon Orcutt, but he and others want more, and faster.

      The numbers are down, but the bodies are not gone. The work is not finished.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Call Council Member Rita Joseph. Demand faster, broader action. Tell her and the mayor: every day without real protection is another day someone does not come home. Join Transportation Alternatives or Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      The streets will not change themselves.

      Int 0080-2024
      Joseph co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by deterring hazardous vehicle obstruction.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants within half a mile of schools. Fines set at $175. Civilians can report violations. DOT must act on complaints. Council aims to clear paths for people, not cars.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after its introduction on February 8, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations," creates a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of a school. If DOT prosecutes a case using civilian evidence, the complainant gets 25% of the proceeds. Council Member Carlina Rivera leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Joseph, Menin, Nurse, Hudson, Brannan, Farías, Brewer, Salaam, Hanif, Avilés, Won, Bottcher, Krishnan, Gutiérrez, Marte, and the Brooklyn Borough President.       Joseph Withdraws Support for Harmful E-Bike Registration Bill
      ▸

      Gale Brewer rejects Holden’s e-bike registration push. She calls it ineffective and burdensome. Brewer backs bills for safer batteries and worker training. Thirty-one groups join her. Critics warn registration means more stops, fewer bikes, and more danger for riders.

      On November 13, 2023, Council Member Gale Brewer (D-Upper West Side) publicly opposed Council Member Bob Holden’s proposed e-bike registration bill. The bill, not yet passed, would require every e-bike in New York City to be registered with the Department of Transportation. Brewer stated, “I do not believe a blanket e-bike registration rule will make streets safer, nor is NYC DOT equipped to establish, manage or enforce it.” She instead sponsors Intro. 1168, mandating safe lithium-ion batteries for delivery workers, and Intro. 1163, requiring worker training and identification. Thirty-one organizations endorsed Brewer’s approach, calling Holden’s bill “ineffective, dangerous, expensive, short-sighted, and bureaucratically complicated.” Council Members Rita Joseph and Sandy Nurse also withdrew support. Opponents argue the registration bill could suppress e-bike use, increase police stops in communities of color, and worsen traffic violence. Holden dismissed these concerns, insisting his bill is vital for safety.
