      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 40?

      Empire Boulevard Bleeds—How Many Deaths Before Rita Joseph Acts?

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 40: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Five people killed. Thirty-two left with serious injuries. In District 40, from January 2022 to July 2025, the bodies keep coming. Pedestrians, cyclists, children. The street does not care who you are. It only takes.

      Cars and trucks did most of the damage. SUVs and sedans killed two. Trucks killed three. Cyclists and walkers are the ones who pay. NYC Open Data shows the pattern: the bigger the vehicle, the deeper the wound.

      Recent Crashes: Names Become Numbers

      A teenager crushed on Foster Avenue. An elderly woman struck at an intersection. A three-year-old thrown from a stroller. The stories pile up. The blood dries. The city moves on.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Council Member Rita Joseph has signed on to bills that matter. She co-sponsored laws to speed up protected bike lanes, require speed humps near parks, and boost crash investigations. She voted yes on removing abandoned vehicles that block sightlines and endanger lives. She backed a bill to decriminalize jaywalking, shifting blame away from people on foot and onto the system that fails them.

      But there is silence where there should be fire. Empire Boulevard still has no protected bike lane, even after the community board called for it. “Improvements are overdue,” advocates said. Joseph did not comment.

      The Human Cost

      A sister-in-law said, “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter.”

      A witness to another crash said, “I pass through here almost every morning… The line is really long. So, they get there early so they can get their stuff early.”

      These are not accidents. They are the result of choices. Of policy. Of delay.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Call Council Member Rita Joseph. Demand protected bike lanes on Empire Boulevard. Demand daylighting at every intersection. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action, not words.

      Every day you wait, another family waits for a call that will break them. The blood is on the street. It does not wash away.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 40 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 42 and state senate district SD 20.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 40?
      It includes the Prospect Lefferts Gardens-Wingate, Flatbush, Flatbush (West)-Ditmas Park-Parkville, East Flatbush-Erasmus, Brooklyn CB14, and Brooklyn CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 42, AD 43, AD 44, and AD 48, and State Senate Districts SD 20 and SD 21.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 40?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm to pedestrians in District 40, with 5 deaths and 32 serious injuries from January 2022 to July 2025. SUVs and sedans killed two, trucks killed three. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes caused no pedestrian deaths but did cause several injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These crashes are preventable. Policy, street design, and enforcement choices shape the risk on every block.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass laws for protected bike lanes, lower speed limits, daylight intersections, and fund enforcement that targets dangerous driving—not people walking or biking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0346-2024
      Joseph votes yes to decriminalize jaywalking, boosting pedestrian safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians now cross anywhere, any time. No summons. Law strips drivers of excuses. Streets shift. Power tilts to people on foot.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, rewrites New York’s pedestrian rules. The bill, heard by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passed on September 26, 2024, and became law on October 26, 2024. Its title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way.” Sponsored by Council Member Tiffany Cabán and co-sponsored by Narcisse, Won, Hanif, Hudson, and Restler, the law lets pedestrians cross streets anywhere, even against signals. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks. The Department of Transportation must educate all street users on new rights and responsibilities. The law removes a tool police used to target walkers. It shifts blame from people on foot to the system and those behind the wheel.

      Joseph Backs Safety Boosting Protected Bike Lane on Empire
      ▸

      Brooklyn Community Board 9 wants a protected bike lane on Empire Boulevard. The board passed a resolution after years of crashes and deaths. Cyclists and pedestrians have paid the price. Local officials back the call. DOT has not acted.

      On April 18, 2024, Brooklyn Community Board 9 passed a resolution urging the Department of Transportation to build a protected bike lane on Empire Boulevard. The board wants to upgrade the faded painted lane, which runs west to Flatbush Boulevard, to a 'vehicle-protected' lane. The matter summary states: 'bring much-needed road safety to a neighborhood.' Council Members Rita Joseph and Crystal Hudson represent the area. Hudson's spokesperson called the resolution 'a necessary step to protect cyclists and pedestrians.' Joseph and DOT did not comment. Between 2021 and 2023, Empire Boulevard saw 406 crashes, injuring 29 cyclists and 52 pedestrians. Two cyclists and two pedestrians have died since 2011. Community Board 9 has zero miles of protected bike lanes. Advocates say improvements are overdue. DOT has yet to propose changes.
