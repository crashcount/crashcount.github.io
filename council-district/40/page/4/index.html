      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 40?

      Empire Boulevard Bleeds—City Stalls, Lives Shatter

      Written July 13, 2025

      District 40: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Slow March of Harm

      In District 40, violence comes in waves. A child struck on Coney Island Avenue. An elderly woman crushed by a pickup on Albany. In the last twelve months, 768 people have been injured in crashes here. Ten were seriously hurt. One did not survive (NYC Open Data).

      The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors—children, elders, mothers, fathers. The street does not care if you cross with the light or against it. The car always wins.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and trucks do most of the damage. In this district, they have killed six people and injured hundreds more since 2022 (NYC Open Data). Trucks and SUVs crush, sedans maim, and the numbers do not lie. Bikes and mopeds hurt too, but the toll is smaller. The street is a battlefield, and the most vulnerable are always outgunned.

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      Council Member Rita Joseph has signed her name to bills that matter. She backed the push for 100 miles of protected bike lanes a year and voted to end jaywalking enforcement, a law that removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act and measures to daylight intersections, add speed humps, and expand Open Streets. These are steps, not leaps.

      But the blood on the street says more is needed. Empire Boulevard still has no protected bike lane, despite years of crashes and deaths. The city moves slow. The cars do not. As one neighbor said after another crash, “drivers speed on that stretch of roadway.”

      What Comes Next

      No one is safe until every street is safe. Call Council Member Joseph. Demand real protection for people on foot and bike. Demand a default 20 mph speed limit, protected lanes, and daylighted corners. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of local districts.
      ▸ Where does District 40 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 42 and state senate district SD 20 (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Which areas are in District 40?
      It includes the Prospect Lefferts Gardens-Wingate, Flatbush, Flatbush (West)-Ditmas Park-Parkville, East Flatbush-Erasmus, Brooklyn CB14, and Brooklyn CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 42, AD 43, AD 44, and AD 48, and State Senate Districts SD 20 and SD 21 (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 40?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 5 deaths and hundreds of injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused fewer injuries, and bikes were involved in a small number of incidents (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Better street design, lower speed limits, and real enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass laws for protected bike lanes, lower speed limits, daylight intersections, and push city agencies to act faster. They can also support enforcement focused on the most dangerous driving behaviors.

      Joseph co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by deterring hazardous vehicle obstruction.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants within half a mile of schools. Fines set at $175. Civilians can report violations. DOT must act on complaints. Council aims to clear paths for people, not cars.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after its introduction on February 8, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations," creates a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of a school.       Int 0858-2022
      Joseph co-sponsors bill boosting street safety through systemic crash investigations.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0858-2022 would force the city’s DOT to probe every serious crash. The bill demands quick action, deep analysis, and public reporting. It targets street design flaws and driver behavior. Lawmakers filed it, but the session ended before passage.

      Int 0858-2022, introduced December 21, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to overhaul how New York City investigates vehicle collisions. The bill, sponsored by Council Member Farah N. Louis (primary) and co-sponsored by Selvena N. Brooks-Powers, Shekar Krishnan, Charles Barron, Kristin Richardson Jordan, Lincoln Restler, and Rita C. Joseph, required the Department of Transportation to investigate all serious vehicular crashes. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the department of transportation to investigate vehicle collisions.' The bill would have expanded the definition of serious crashes, set strict investigation timelines, and mandated detailed public reports. It also called for reviews of street design and infrastructure at crash sites, with recommendations for safety improvements. The bill was filed at the end of the session and did not become law.
