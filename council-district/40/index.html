      Deadly Corners, Broken Promises: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 19, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed citation formatting, deduped and sorted citations.)

      District 40: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Shattered, Streets Unforgiving

      In District 40, the numbers do not lie. Five people killed. Thirty-one left with serious injuries. Over 2,600 hurt since 2022. The dead do not speak. Their absence is felt in empty chairs, unopened doors, and the silence after sirens fade.

      Trucks and SUVs crush bodies at corners. Pedestrians are struck crossing with the light, against the light, or just trying to live. Most injuries and deaths happen at intersections. The city’s own data shows the carnage is not random. It is built into the streets.

      Leadership: Promises, Laws, and the Slow March of Change

      Council Member Rita Joseph has stood with those demanding safer streets. She co-sponsored bills to build 100 miles of protected bike lanes each year and to ban parking near crosswalks, forcing the city to clear corners and save lives. She voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed the vulnerable instead of the reckless. She backed the SAFE Streets Act and pushed for speed humps near parks and schools.

      But bills alone do not stop the bleeding. “Redesigning our streets under Vision Zero has helped us reduce traffic deaths to historic lows but we are always looking for new ways to keep people safe,” said DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez, as the city finally began adding hard barriers at high-crash intersections in Brooklyn.

      The System Fights Back—But Not Fast Enough

      Nearly half of all traffic deaths happen at intersections. The city is rolling out hardened daylighting—granite blocks, planters, concrete—at corners where people keep dying. Advocates call it a start, but not enough. “Anything with real (not plastic) infrastructure in street corners is good news,” said Jon Orcutt, but he and others want more, and faster.

      The numbers are down, but the bodies are not gone. The work is not finished.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Call Council Member Rita Joseph. Demand faster, broader action. Tell her and the mayor: every day without real protection is another day someone does not come home. Join Transportation Alternatives or Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      The streets will not change themselves.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of neighborhoods across all five boroughs, as described in NYC Council passes bills on traffic crashes, senior pedestrian zones.
      ▸ Where does District 40 sit politically?
      District 40 belongs to borough Brooklyn and overlaps with Assembly Districts AD 42, AD 43, AD 44, AD 48, and State Senate Districts SD 20 and SD 21, according to NYC Council passes bills on traffic crashes, senior pedestrian zones.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 40?
      It includes the Prospect Lefferts Gardens-Wingate and East Flatbush-Erasmus neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 42, AD 43, AD 44, AD 48, and State Senate Districts SD 20 and SD 21, as detailed in NYC Council passes bills on traffic crashes, senior pedestrian zones.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 40?
      Most deaths and serious injuries to pedestrians came from Cars and Trucks (5 deaths, 123 moderate/serious injuries), with smaller numbers from Motorcycles and Mopeds (0 deaths, 4 moderate injuries), and Bikes (0 deaths, 4 moderate injuries), according to NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents. The data shows most deaths and injuries happen at intersections and are linked to street design and driver behavior—problems that can be fixed, as shown in NYC DOT Targets Intersection Danger Zones.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for protected bike lanes, ban parking near crosswalks, demand faster street redesigns, and hold city agencies accountable for delays, as advocates demand.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up-to-date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood, using NYC Open Data crash records.

      Int 1138-2024
      Joseph co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1138-2024 would ban parking and standing within 20 feet of crosswalks. It forces the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections each year. The bill sits in committee. Sponsors include Bottcher, Won, and the Public Advocate.

      Int 1138-2024, introduced December 5, 2024, is under review by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill is 'Laid Over in Committee.' Its title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.' Council Member Erik D. Bottcher is the primary sponsor, joined by Julie Won, Jumaane Williams, and others. The bill prohibits standing or parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and mandates the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections per year. The law also requires citywide outreach and reporting. This measure aims to keep sightlines clear at crossings, a known danger zone for people on foot and bike. The bill has not yet received a vote.

      Int 0346-2024
      Joseph votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.
