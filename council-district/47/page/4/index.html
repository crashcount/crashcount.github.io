      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 47?

      No More Empty Chairs: Hold Brannan Accountable for Blood on the Asphalt

      Written June 27, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In District 47, the numbers do not lie. Five people killed, 682 injured in the last year alone (NYC Open Data). The dead include a 22-year-old moped rider, a 17-year-old on a motorcycle, a 74-year-old man crossing the street, and a 95-year-old woman struck at an intersection. The living carry scars that do not heal. A passenger thrown from a moped, bones broken. A cyclist ejected onto the Belt Parkway, helmet or not, dead all the same.

      Cars and SUVs did most of the killing. They struck, they crushed, they left families with empty chairs at the table. Trucks, motorcycles, and bikes played their part, but the weight of the harm is steel and speed (NYC Open Data).

      Faces and Voices

      Joel Mota was 22. He died before sunrise, his moped smashed by a drunk, unlicensed driver in Bay Ridge. His brother remembered him simply: “He never stopped working.” The driver was arrested, then released without bail. The street stayed the same.

      A 17-year-old boy died on a moped in Midwood. The car turned left. The boy and his passenger were thrown. The police said, “There have been no arrests. The NYPD highway district collision squad is investigating.” The words are cold. The loss is not.

      Leadership: Steps and Stalls

      Council Member Justin Brannan has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, to raise penalties for blocked plates, and to legalize jaywalking—a move that stops blaming the dead for crossing the street. He co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act, pushing for lower speed limits and more rights for crash victims (SAFE Streets Act).

      But on the ground, the danger lingers. Advocates called for a redesign of Fourth Avenue in Bay Ridge. Brannan, once supportive, has gone quiet. The city passed a law to add traffic signals near schools. Brannan called it a “no-brainer,” but the signals are slow to come.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. Every crash is a policy failure, a choice to let the street stay deadly. Call Council Member Brannan. Demand action. Demand daylight at every crosswalk, protected lanes, slower speeds, and streets built for people, not for cars. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 47 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 45 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 47?
      It includes the Bay Ridge, Bath Beach, Gravesend (South), Coney Island-Sea Gate, Calvert Vaux Park, Brooklyn CB13, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 45, AD 46, and AD 64, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 23, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 47?
      Most harm came from Cars and SUVs (4 deaths, 321 injuries), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 10 injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 16 injuries). See the NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Every crash is a result of choices—by drivers, by policymakers, by those who design and enforce the rules of the road.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for safer street design, lower speed limits, ban parking near crosswalks, and demand accountability for dangerous driving. They can also push for faster implementation of safety measures and listen to advocates.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0842-2024
      Brannan sponsors bill requiring DOT to consider traffic enforcement agents, with neutral safety impact.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0842-2024 demands DOT factor traffic enforcement agents into every city safety plan. The bill sits in committee. Sponsors say it targets deadly streets. The law would force DOT to map out where agents stand guard, not just hope for safer roads.

      Bill Int 0842-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced April 18, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the department of transportation to consider placement of traffic enforcement agents in developing an interagency roadway safety plan," requires DOT to consider where traffic enforcement agents are stationed when drafting the city’s five-year safety plan. Sponsors include Council Member Justin L. Brannan (primary), Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Farah N. Louis, Lincoln Restler, and the Queens Borough President (by request). The bill responds to Local Law 12 of 2011, which already mandates a safety plan, but this measure sharpens the focus: DOT must show where agents will be placed to protect the most dangerous corridors. The bill awaits further action.
