      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 47?

      Twelve Dead. Thousands Hurt. Brannan Waits. How Many Bodies Before Action?

      Written July 16, 2025

      District 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      Twelve people have died on District 47 streets since 2022. Two were children. Four were over 65. In the same time, 2,225 neighbors were hurt. Twenty-one suffered wounds so grave they may never walk the same. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about hope or habit. They only rise.

      Just this January, a 95-year-old woman was killed by an SUV turning left at Cropsey and 24th Avenue. She died at the intersection, one more name in a long list. In September, a cyclist was struck and killed on the Belt Parkway. Last summer, a 17-year-old on a motorcycle died on the same road. The old, the young, the ones on foot or on two wheels—none are spared.

      The Human Cost

      “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter,” said Ruiz’s sister-in-law. The grief is not abstract. It sits at the kitchen table. It waits by the phone. It never leaves.

      “We are deeply saddened by the tragic deaths of two individuals in Sunset Park this morning. Our thoughts are with their families and loved ones,” said City Harvest, after two men were killed crossing to a food pantry. They were just trying to eat.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Justin Brannan has backed bills to clear abandoned cars, speed up pavement markings, and require NYPD to check plates on temp tags. He co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, but it sits in committee, not law. He voted yes on warning decals for taxis, but a sticker does not stop a speeding car. He has praised police after tragedy, but words do not build safe streets.

      The bills are slow. The deaths are fast.

      What Comes Next

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call Council Member Brannan. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand action, not condolence. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 47 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 45 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 47?
      It includes the Bay Ridge, Bath Beach, Gravesend (South), Coney Island-Sea Gate, Calvert Vaux Park, Brooklyn CB13, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 45, AD 46, and AD 64, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 23, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 47?
      Most injuries and deaths came from SUVs and Cars (4 deaths, 334 total incidents), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 10 incidents), and Bikes (no deaths, 16 incidents). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are preventable. Every death and injury is the result of choices—by drivers, by lawmakers, and by those who design our streets.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous intersections, clear sightlines, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking. They can act fast, not just talk.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1252-2025, now laid over in the Committee on Public Safety, was introduced April 24, 2025.       Int 1233-2025
      Brannan co-sponsors bill to require vegetated medians, boosting cyclist and pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council bill orders trees and plants on new medians between bike lanes and car traffic. Concrete gets green. Barriers grow roots. The city must build for life, not speed.

      Int 1233-2025 sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced April 10, 2025, it demands new medians between bicycle lanes and motor vehicles be built for trees and vegetation, pending feasibility. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code... in relation to the planting of vegetation on new medians separating bicycle lanes from motorized vehicle traffic.” Council Member Shekar Krishnan leads, joined by Bottcher, Ossé, Brannan, Hanif, and Brooks-Powers. They push the city to plant, or allow planting, in every new median. The law would take effect 120 days after passage. No safety analyst note was provided.
