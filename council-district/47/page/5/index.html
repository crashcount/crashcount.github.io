      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 47?

      Another Body Bag for Brannan’s Silence

      Written July 2, 2025

      District 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 2, 2025

      The Blood Price of Delay

      Five dead. Nearly 700 injured. That is the cost of traffic violence in District 47 in the last year alone (NYC Open Data). More than 1 in 10 of those hurt were under 18. The dead include a 95-year-old woman, a 74-year-old man, and a teenager. Each one had a name. Each one had a place to be. None of them made it home.

      Just last week, a van slammed into a tree in Bath Beach. Fourteen people were hurt, most of them adults with special needs, on their way to an adult daycare. “It’s sad because they definitely need a lot of attention more than anyone else. I hope that they’re all OK at the end of the day,” said a neighbor, watching the aftermath (ABC7).

      The Vehicles That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the damage. In the last three years, they killed four people and injured over 300 on foot. Trucks and buses killed one, bikes hurt sixteen. Motorcycles and mopeds left ten more injured. The numbers are cold, but the pain is not. A sedan turns left, a body falls. A truck makes a wide swing, a life ends. The street does not care who you are.

      What Has Been Done—And What Hasn’t

      Council Member Justin Brannan has backed bills to daylight intersections, curb repeat speeders, and boost penalties for blocked plates. He voted to legalize jaywalking, shifting blame off pedestrians and onto drivers where it belongs (NYC Council – Legistar). He co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act, pushing for lower speed limits and more rights for crash victims. But on the ground, the carnage continues. Advocates called for a redesign of Fourth Avenue in Bay Ridge. Brannan once supported it, now he is silent. The avenue remains a speedway.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Every day of delay means another family shattered. Call Council Member Brannan. Demand a Fourth Avenue redesign. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action before another child’s name is added to the list. The dead cannot speak. You can. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 47 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 45 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 47?
      It includes the Bay Ridge, Bath Beach, Gravesend (South), Coney Island-Sea Gate, Calvert Vaux Park, Brooklyn CB13, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 45, AD 46, and AD 64, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 23, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 47?
      SUVs and cars killed 4 and injured over 300 people on foot in the last three years. Trucks and buses killed 1, bikes injured 16, and motorcycles and mopeds injured 10.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement. They are not random—they follow patterns that can be changed.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can redesign dangerous streets, lower speed limits, pass and enforce safety laws, and make sure drivers—not pedestrians—are held accountable.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1151-2023
      Brannan co-sponsors solar crosswalk bill, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council pushed for 500 solar-lit crosswalks. The bill demanded action—100 new devices each year. It called for a hard look at results. But the session ended. The bill died. Streets wait. Pedestrians keep crossing in the dark.

      Int 1151-2023, introduced August 3, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to require the Department of Transportation to install at least 100 illuminated, solar-powered traffic control devices at crosswalks each year for five years—a total of 500. The bill also ordered a study comparing these devices to unlit signs, probing their power to deter traffic violations and mapping out logistical hurdles. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the installation of solar-powered crosswalks.' Council Member Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Menin, Brooks-Powers, Rivera, and over thirty others. The bill was filed at session’s end on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The city’s crosswalks remain unchanged. Vulnerable road users remain exposed.
