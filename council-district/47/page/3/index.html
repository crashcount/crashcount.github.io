      No More Excuses: Streets Are Killing Us

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened FAQ citations, deduped and sorted citations.)

      District 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Losses in District 47

      The streets do not forgive. Since January 2022, 12 people have died in traffic crashes across District 47. Over 2,200 have been injured. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. In the last year alone, four lives were lost. A 95-year-old woman was struck and killed by an SUV turning left at Cropsey Avenue and 24th Avenue. A 74-year-old man died in a crosswalk on Bay Ridge Avenue, hit by an SUV. The numbers do not flinch. They do not mourn. They only rise.

      Just days ago, two men were killed at 52nd Street and Third Avenue. A BMW ran a red light and sped away, leaving bodies in the crosswalk. Police said the driver was charged with manslaughter, criminally negligent homicide, and reckless driving. “EMS pronounced both men dead at the scene. Their identities are being withheld pending family notification,” reported amNY. The crash site has seen 59 collisions and 80 injuries in five years. The blood dries, but the danger remains.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Council Member Justin Brannan has backed bills to clear abandoned vehicles, speed up pavement markings, and require warning decals on taxis. He co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, aiming to keep sightlines clear for people on foot and bike. But he also pushed a bill to keep SUV parking costs low, a move that critics say subsidizes bigger, deadlier vehicles on city streets. “We shouldn’t be bending over backwards to further subsidize someone’s choice to drive an SUV or pickup truck in New York City,” said StreetsPAC’s Eric McClure.

      After Norman Fruchter was killed by a reversing driver in Bay Ridge, Brannan attended the vigil and said, “Confronting traffic violence also means taking personal responsibility [and] safe driving habits. It will take all of us,” as reported by Brooklyn Paper. But the laws remain weak. The most vulnerable still pay the price.

      The Next Fight: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      Every death is preventable. The city has the power to lower speed limits, redesign streets, and crack down on repeat offenders. Call Council Member Brannan. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted intersections. Demand action, not words.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district. See the NYC Council’s Legistar page for more.
      ▸ Where does District 47 sit politically?
      District 47 belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 45 and state senate district SD 17. See NYC Open Data crash records for more.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 47?
      It includes the Bay Ridge, Bath Beach, Gravesend (South), Coney Island-Sea Gate, Calvert Vaux Park, Brooklyn CB13, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 45, AD 46, and AD 64, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 23, and SD 26. See NYC Open Data crash records for more.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 47?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians came from Cars and Trucks (4 deaths, 334 total incidents), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 10 incidents), and Bikes (no deaths, 16 incidents). See NYC Open Data crash records for more.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street designs, and enforcement against reckless driving can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, install protected bike lanes, and crack down on repeat offenders.

      Int 1160-2025
      Brannan co-sponsors bill to speed up pavement markings, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly crossings for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, forces the Department of Transportation to install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. The bill, sponsored by Farah N. Louis (primary) and co-sponsored by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, Ariola, and others, passed on March 15, 2025. The law demands annual reporting on compliance and reasons for any delay. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Quick, visible lines cut confusion and protect people crossing or riding. The law took effect immediately.

      Int 1138-2024
      Brannan co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill bars cars from blocking crosswalks. No standing or parking within 20 feet. City must install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections yearly. Streets clear. Sightlines open. Danger cut.

      Int 1138-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 5, 2024. The bill states: “prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.” Council Member Erik D. Bottcher leads, joined by Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and over two dozen co-sponsors. The law orders the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections each year, up from 100. The city must also run outreach and education. The bill aims to keep crosswalks clear, improve visibility, and protect people on foot and bike. No more hiding behind parked cars. The committee laid the bill over on April 21, 2025.
