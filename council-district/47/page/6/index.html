      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 47?

      No More Dead Kids: Brannan Must Deliver Safe Streets Now

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt

      In District 47, the numbers do not lie. Twelve dead. Nineteen seriously hurt. Over two thousand injured. These are not just numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. In the last twelve months alone, five people lost their lives on these streets. One was a child. One was over seventy-five. One was under eighteen.

      The dead do not speak. But the living remember. A witness to a crash tried to stop the bleeding, wrapping a towel around a child’s head, tapping, tapping, as blood poured out. “I just saw a lot of blood gushing out of his ears, his mouth.” The driver stood in shock. The street stayed dangerous.

      The Usual Killers

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In District 47, cars and SUVs caused four deaths and over three hundred injuries to people on foot. Trucks and buses killed one. Bikes and mopeds injured, but did not kill. The old and the young are hit hardest. The numbers do not change. The story repeats.

      What Has Brannan Done?

      Council Member Justin Brannan has signed on to bills that could help. He co-sponsored laws to ban parking near crosswalks, to daylight intersections, to install solar crosswalks, to demand safer school zones, and to push for lower speed limits. He voted to legalize jaywalking, shifting blame away from people on foot. He called the school zone law a “no-brainer”. But the pace is slow. Promises stack up. Streets stay the same.

      The Next Step

      Every death is preventable. The city can lower speed limits now. It can redesign Fourth Avenue. It can clear corners and build real protection. But it will not happen unless you demand it. Call Brannan. Call the Mayor. Do not wait for another towel, another child, another name in the data.

      Act now. Demand safer streets.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 47 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 45 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 47?
      It includes the Bay Ridge, Bath Beach, Gravesend (South), Coney Island-Sea Gate, Calvert Vaux Park, Brooklyn CB13, and Brooklyn CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 45, AD 46, and AD 64, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 23, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 47?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians in District 47: 4 deaths and over 300 injuries. Trucks and buses killed one. Bikes and mopeds injured pedestrians but did not cause deaths.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and real enforcement save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, and protect people walking and biking. They can push city agencies to act faster and make safety a priority.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0501-2022
      Brannan co-sponsors bill boosting civilian reporting to improve street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants near schools. Civilians can report violators. Each offense draws a $175 fine. The city pays whistleblowers a cut. The bill stalled. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0501-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on June 2, 2022. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations,' aimed to create a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 1,320 feet of a school. The penalty: $175 per violation, enforced through the Office of Administrative Trials and Hearings. Civilians, except city employees, could file complaints and receive 25% of collected fines. The Department of Transportation would support this with a phased-in reporting program and annual public reports. Council Member Carlina Rivera led as primary sponsor, joined by over two dozen co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Brannan co-sponsors bill boosting safety by requiring quick temporary signs at broken signals.
