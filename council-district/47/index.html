      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 47?

      Four Dead, Hundreds Hurt—Brannan, Act Before the Next Funeral

      Written June 23, 2025 (Revised June 22, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned and deduped citations, fixed link formatting in FAQ.)

      District 47: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 23, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      In District 47, the numbers do not lie. Four people killed in the last year. Six hundred eighty-one injured. One was a child. One was seventy-five. One was crossing the street. One was riding home. The dead do not get a second chance.

      On June 22, Joel Mota, age 22, died at Third Avenue and 67th Street. He was riding a moped. The driver who killed him was drunk and unlicensed. His brother remembered him simply: “He never stopped working.” NY Daily News.

      A 95-year-old woman was killed by an SUV turning left at Cropsey and 24th Avenue. A 74-year-old man died crossing Bay Ridge Avenue. A 17-year-old lost his life on a motorcycle. The list is long. The pain is longer.

      Leadership: Promises, Laws, and the Waiting

      Council Member Justin Brannan has stood for some safety bills. He voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed the dead for their own deaths. He co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act, pushing for lower speed limits and more rights for crash victims. He called the school zone traffic law a “no-brainer” and said, “Some of our biggest victories have been getting a traffic signal installed near a busy intersection near a school – that shouldn’t be some huge colossal victory.” Streetsblog NYC.

      But the streets do not wait for laws. Advocates still fight for protected bike lanes on Fourth Avenue. The DOT drags its feet. Brannan, once vocal, has gone quiet as the bodies pile up. Promises do not stop cars.

      What Comes Next: Action, Not Excuses

      Speed kills. SUVs and sedans took most of the lives here. Cameras and laws help, but only if enforced. The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has the power to redesign streets. It has the power to keep children from dying on the way to school. It must use it.

      Call your council member. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand protected bike lanes. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand that every death is the last.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 47 sit politically?
      District 47 belongs to borough Brooklyn, and overlaps with Assembly Districts AD 45, AD 46, and AD 64, as well as State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 23, and SD 26. Data on crashes and injuries comes from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 47?
      It includes the Bay Ridge, Gravesend (South), Coney Island-Sea Gate, Calvert Vaux Park, and Brooklyn CB13 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 45, AD 46, and AD 64, and State Senate Districts SD 17, SD 23, and SD 26. Crash and injury data is available from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 47?
      Most injuries and deaths were caused by Cars and Trucks (4 deaths, 221 minor injuries, 93 moderate injuries, 3 serious injuries), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 9 minor injuries, 1 moderate injury, 0 serious injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 11 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury). Source: NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement can save lives and stop the next tragedy.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, daylight intersections, and demand faster action from city agencies.

      Int 0346-2024
      Brannan votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.
