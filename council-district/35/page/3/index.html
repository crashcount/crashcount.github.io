      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 35?

      No More Dead Kids: Make Crown Heights Streets Safe Now

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 35: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Shattered

      A boy steps into the crosswalk at Eastern Parkway and Albany Avenue. An SUV keeps going. The boy does not. He is eight years old. He dies at the scene. The city records another number. The family loses a son. This is Crown Heights, June 28, 2025. It is not the first time. It will not be the last.

      In the last twelve months, five people have died on District 35 streets. Nine more suffered serious injuries. Children, elders, mothers, fathers. The numbers do not flinch: 676 injured, 1269 crashes in a single year. The cars keep coming. The bodies pile up.

      The Pattern: No Safe Hour, No Safe Street

      The dead are not just numbers. A 55-year-old woman, crossing Washington Avenue at Fulton, crushed by an SUV. A 101-year-old woman, crossing with the signal on Montgomery, killed by a driver who did not yield. A 36-year-old man, hit and left to die at Broadway and East New York Avenue. The driver sped off. The city shrugs. “A hit-and-run driver struck and killed a 36-year-old man crossing a Brooklyn street,” officials said. The dead do not get to speak. The drivers do not stop.

      The Response: Promises, Laws, and the Waiting

      Council Member Crystal Hudson has signed her name to bills that promise change. She voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the walking dead. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act, calling for lower speed limits and more rights for crash victims. She backed bills for daylighting, curb extensions, and protected bike lanes. The bills are good. The streets are not yet safe. “It is imperative that we dramatically speed up street redesigns and use all of the tools at our disposal to create safer streets,” said Council Member Rivera. The clock runs out for someone every week.

      The Call: Demand More Than Words

      The blood on these streets is not an accident. It is policy, inertia, delay. Call Council Member Hudson. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real daylighting at every corner. Demand that every death is the last. Do not wait for another child’s shoes to be left in the crosswalk.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees city agencies.
      ▸ Where does District 35 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 43 and state senate district SD 20.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 35?
      It includes the Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Prospect Heights, Crown Heights (North), Crown Heights (South), Brooklyn CB8, Brooklyn CB2, and Brooklyn CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 20, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 35?
      Most injuries and deaths came from SUVs and Cars (4 deaths, 440 injuries), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 23 injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 29 injuries).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The pattern is clear and preventable: speed, failure to yield, and poor street design kill. These are not random acts—they are the result of choices and policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, and support laws that protect people walking and biking. They can refuse to delay or water down life-saving changes.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
