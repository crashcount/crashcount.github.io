      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 35?

      Eighteen Dead, Still No Action: How Many More Must Die on Hudson’s Watch?

      Written July 18, 2025

      District 35: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In District 35, the numbers do not lie. Eighteen people dead. Forty-three seriously hurt. More than 2,800 injured since 2022 (NYC Open Data). Pedestrians, cyclists, children. The youngest killed was eight. The oldest, 101. The street does not care.

      Just weeks ago, a 55-year-old woman was crushed by an SUV at Fulton and Washington. She died in the street. In June, an eight-year-old boy was killed at Eastern Parkway and Albany. The driver kept going straight. The boy never got up. The oldest victim, a woman of 101, was struck crossing with the signal. The driver failed to yield. She died at the scene. These are not accidents. They are the result of choices, policies, and the shape of our streets.

      Who Pays the Price

      SUVs and cars do the most harm. Four deaths and over 400 injuries came from them. Trucks and buses killed one, hurt dozens more. Motorcycles and mopeds, bikes—they injure, but the carnage comes on four wheels (NYC Open Data).

      The stories do not end with the dead. Families are left behind. “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter,” said Ruiz’s sister-in-law. The pain does not fade. Tire tracks linger in the lot. The city moves on.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Crystal Hudson has voted for and co-sponsored bills to clear abandoned vehicles, daylight intersections, and require curb extensions—all steps to clear sightlines and protect people on foot and bike (NYC Council – Legistar). She voted yes on warning decals for taxis to prevent dooring, and for more transparent street safety reporting. But the bills move slow. The deaths do not wait.

      No law has yet forced a citywide 20 mph speed limit. No bill has ended the reign of repeat speeders. The city still lets cars park too close to crosswalks. The most vulnerable pay the price.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call Council Member Hudson. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real daylighting at every intersection. Demand action, not delay. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 35 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 43 and state senate district SD 20.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 35?
      It includes the Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Prospect Heights, Crown Heights (North), Crown Heights (South), Brooklyn CB8, Brooklyn CB2, and Brooklyn CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 20, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 35?
      Cars and SUVs killed 5 and injured over 400 pedestrians. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 1 serious injury and 24 total injuries. Bikes caused 1 serious injury and 29 total injuries (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. They happen where policy, street design, and enforcement fail. The same streets see the same tragedies, year after year.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass laws to lower speed limits, daylight intersections, and redesign streets for safety. They can push for enforcement that targets dangerous driving, not vulnerable road users.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
