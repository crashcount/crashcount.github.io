      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 35?

      No More Names on the Asphalt: Demand Action Now

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 16, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, removed headline duplication, cleaned citations, and ensured all links and attributions are correct.)

      District 35: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A boy, age eight, was killed at Eastern Parkway and Albany Avenue. A woman, 55, died crossing Washington Avenue at Fulton. A 101-year-old woman was struck and killed in a crosswalk on Montgomery Street. In the last twelve months, five people have died and nine have suffered serious injuries in District 35. Children, elders, and working people pay the price.

      The numbers do not stop. In the past year, there were 1,384 crashes in this district. 792 people were hurt. The dead do not get a second chance. The injured carry scars that do not heal.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs and cars do most of the harm. They killed four people and injured hundreds more. Trucks and buses killed one. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes cause injuries, but they do not kill like the big machines. The street is not safe for anyone on foot or on two wheels. The risk is everywhere: at the curb, in the crosswalk, waiting for the light.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Delayed

      Council Member Crystal Hudson has voted yes on bills to remove abandoned vehicles and require taxi dooring warnings. She has co-sponsored laws to daylight intersections and build curb extensions, pushing for clear sight lines and fewer hiding places for danger. But many bills sit in committee, waiting. The city moves slow. The street does not wait.

      Hudson has spoken out after police killed a man on Eastern Parkway. She demanded more than desk duty for the officer. “These officers drove an NYPD van so fast and recklessly… they dragged my brother… and had no regard for my brother’s life,” said the victim’s sister. The city offered silence. The family sued. The pain remains.

      What Comes Next

      The crisis is not over. Every week brings new names. Contact Council Member Hudson. Demand action. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every corner. Demand that the city stop waiting for the next body. If you do not act, the numbers will keep rising.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs. See the NYC Council legislative portal.
      ▸ Where does District 35 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 43 and state senate district SD 20. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 35?
      It includes the Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Prospect Heights, Crown Heights (North), Crown Heights (South), Brooklyn CB8, Brooklyn CB2, and Brooklyn CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 20, SD 25, and SD 26. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 35?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for most deaths and injuries, with SUVs and cars killing four and injuring hundreds. Motorcycles and mopeds caused injuries but far fewer deaths. Bikes caused injuries but no deaths. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. Most deaths and injuries happen because of choices—speed, street design, enforcement. Better policies and safer streets can prevent them. See this Streetsblog NYC opinion.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, daylight intersections, redesign streets, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can push for action instead of delay. See the NYC Council legislative portal.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood. See NYC Open Data crash records.

      Letitia James Supports NYPD Procedural Reform to Prevent Reckless Driving
      ▸

      A Brooklyn family sues the city after a police van, driven recklessly by officers, killed Ronald Smith. The van sped, ran red lights, and struck Smith as he panhandled in the rain. The city faces charges of wrongful death and calls for accountability.

      On July 10, 2023, the family of Ronald Smith filed a wrongful death lawsuit against New York City in Brooklyn Supreme Court. The suit alleges that Officers Orkhan Mamedov and Evan Siegel killed Smith by 'driving recklessly at nearly double the posted speed limit, at night, running red lights, in the wrong lane, with visibility limited by rain ... without justification or cause.' Smith was struck and killed while panhandling on Eastern Parkway. The officers, transporting prisoners, had no emergency but rushed to beat a shift change. Attorney David Rankin, representing the family, criticized the city's harsh treatment of victims' families, saying, 'The City of New York, in every instance where an employee kills somebody, puts the family and loved ones though a rigorous and unnecessarily brutal process.' Attorney General Letitia James cleared the officers of criminal charges but condemned their actions and called for NYPD procedural reform. The NYPD and city officials declined to comment further, while the family seeks both accountability and compensation.
