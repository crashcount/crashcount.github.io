      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 35?

      Another Child Dead. Still Waiting for Action.

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised July 1, 2025 – Fixed crash statistics, tightened prose, cleaned citations, and ensured proper link formatting.)

      District 35: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      Children in the Crosswalk

      On June 28, an eight-year-old boy was killed crossing Eastern Parkway with his sister. The SUV did not stop in time. A witness saw the boy dragged from under the car. “I just saw a lot of blood gushing out of his ears, his mouth,” said a bystander. The driver stayed. The boy did not.

      This is not a single tragedy. In the last twelve months, District 35 saw 4 deaths and 676 injuries from crashes. Children, elders, and working people—no one is spared. SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In three years, 17 people have died on these streets. The numbers do not stop. The pain does not fade.

      The Record of Leadership

      Council Member Crystal Hudson has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and legalize crossing mid-block. She voted to end jaywalking enforcement, shifting blame away from those on foot. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act and pushed for curb extensions, daylighting, and better lighting. But on Atlantic Avenue, where four died and 473 were hurt in three years, the city delays real redesign. Hudson called for safety but stopped short of demanding a road diet. “Daylighting streets is necessary, but a bare minimum,” said Borough President Reynoso.

      On Empire Boulevard, Hudson’s office called a protected bike lane “a necessary step to protect cyclists and pedestrians” after the community board demanded it. The city has not acted. The clock runs out for someone every week.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      No law, no speech, no plan brings back a lost child. But every delay means another family waits for the call. Demand more. Call Council Member Hudson. Call the Mayor. Tell them to finish the job: redesign deadly streets, lower speed limits, and protect every crossing. Do not wait for the next siren.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 35 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 43 and state senate district SD 20.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 35?
      It includes the Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Prospect Heights, Crown Heights (North), Crown Heights (South), Brooklyn CB8, Brooklyn CB2, and Brooklyn CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 20, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 35?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians came from cars and trucks (4 deaths, 11 serious injuries), followed by motorcycles and mopeds (no deaths, 1 serious injury), and bikes (no deaths, 1 serious injury). Data source
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement can stop deaths before they happen.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and fund laws to redesign streets, lower speed limits, ban parking near crosswalks, and build protected bike lanes. They can also push city agencies to act faster and hold them accountable for delays.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Hudson Supports Safety Boosting Equalized Tolling on Manhattan Crossings
      ▸

      Brooklyn officials demand equal tolls on all Manhattan crossings. They warn free bridges funnel traffic into certain neighborhoods. Their letter calls for fairness. The MTA stays silent. The Traffic Mobility Review Board will decide. Streets hang in the balance.

      On July 18, 2023, Council Member Shahana Hanif and other Brooklyn officials called for 'equalized tolling on all crossings into Manhattan' as congestion pricing nears. Their letter to the Traffic Mobility Review Board states: 'No one community should disproportionately bear the burden of the cars and trucks passing through their neighborhood.' Over 100 exemption requests, including from taxi and for-hire vehicle drivers, are under review.

      Hudson Supports Safety Boosting State Participation in BQE Planning

      On February 10, 2023, eighteen Brooklyn lawmakers issued a joint letter demanding New York State Department of Transportation (DOT) participate in comprehensive planning for the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway (BQE). Without state action, only city-owned segments see attention. The city’s efforts are limited. The state’s inaction leaves systemic danger unaddressed.
