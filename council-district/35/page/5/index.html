      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 35?

      Brooklyn’s Dead Keep Walking—Until We Force the Streets to Change

      Written June 26, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 35: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      The streets of District 35 do not forgive. In the last year, four people died. Nine more suffered serious injuries. The toll is not numbers. It is a 101-year-old woman, struck in the crosswalk by an unlicensed driver. It is a 55-year-old man, killed crossing Washington Avenue by a driver who did not stop. It is a woman stepping from a taxi on Flatbush, hit before she could reach the curb. The city calls them accidents. The families know better.

      “She walked every morning from Crown Heights to Brookdale Hospital. She used to feed patients there for many years,” said her son. The driver who killed her did not have a license. He was charged and released. The street did not change.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Council Member Crystal Hudson has signed her name to bills that could save lives. She voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the desperate and the slow. She co-sponsored laws to ban parking near crosswalks, require curb extensions, and demand more protected bike lanes. She called for a redesign of Atlantic Avenue, one of Brooklyn’s deadliest roads. But the city delays. The redesign is years away. The paint fades. The bodies do not move.

      When the city swept away illegal placard parking, Hudson stood with the community. Cracking down on placard abuse reduces illegal parking, especially in bike lanes, crosswalks, and sidewalks, improving safety and accessibility for pedestrians and cyclists by reclaiming public space and reducing driver entitlement, reported Streetsblog.

      The Work Left Undone

      Seventeen people have died on these streets since 2022. Hundreds more are hurt each year. Most are on foot or bike. Most are hit by cars, SUVs, or trucks. The laws are written. The votes are cast. The streets remain the same.

      Call to Action:

      Do not wait for another obituary. Call Council Member Hudson. Demand real change: daylight every crosswalk, redesign every deadly corridor, and end the delays. Join with others. Make them act.

      Int 0080-2024
      Hudson co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by deterring hazardous vehicle obstruction.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants within half a mile of schools. Fines set at $175. Civilians can report violations. DOT must act on complaints. Council aims to clear paths for people, not cars.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after its introduction on February 8, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations," creates a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of a school. The penalty is $175 per violation. The Department of Transportation must launch a civilian reporting program. If DOT prosecutes a case using civilian evidence, the complainant gets 25% of the proceeds. Council Member Carlina Rivera leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Joseph, Menin, Nurse, Hudson, Brannan, Farías, Brewer, Salaam, Hanif, Avilés, Won, Bottcher, Krishnan, Gutiérrez, Marte, and the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill aims to keep streets clear for vulnerable road users, especially near schools.
