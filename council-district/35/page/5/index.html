      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 35?

      Brooklyn’s Dead Keep Walking—Until We Force the Streets to Change

      Written June 26, 2025 (Revised June 25, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      District 35: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      The streets of District 35 do not forgive. In the last year, four people died. Nine more suffered serious injuries. The toll is not numbers. It is a 101-year-old woman, struck in the crosswalk by an unlicensed driver. It is a 55-year-old man, killed crossing Washington Avenue by a driver who did not stop. It is a woman stepping from a taxi on Flatbush, hit before she could reach the curb. The city calls them accidents. The families know better.

      “She walked every morning from Crown Heights to Brookdale Hospital. She used to feed patients there for many years,” said her son. The driver who killed her did not have a license. He was charged and released. The street did not change.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Council Member Crystal Hudson has signed her name to bills that could save lives. She voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the desperate and the slow. She co-sponsored laws to ban parking near crosswalks, require curb extensions, and demand more protected bike lanes. She called for a redesign of Atlantic Avenue, one of Brooklyn’s deadliest roads. But the city delays. The redesign is years away. The paint fades. The bodies do not move.

      When the city swept away illegal placard parking, Hudson stood with the community. Cracking down on placard abuse reduces illegal parking, especially in bike lanes, crosswalks, and sidewalks, improving safety and accessibility for pedestrians and cyclists by reclaiming public space and reducing driver entitlement, reported Streetsblog.

      The Work Left Undone

      Seventeen people have died on these streets since 2022. Hundreds more are hurt each year. Most are on foot or bike. Most are hit by cars, SUVs, or trucks. The laws are written. The votes are cast. The streets remain the same.

      Call to Action:

      Do not wait for another obituary. Call Council Member Hudson. Demand real change: daylight every crosswalk, redesign every deadly corridor, and end the delays. Join with others. Make them act.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 35 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 43 and state senate district SD 20.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 35?
      It includes the Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Prospect Heights, and Crown Heights (South) neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 20, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 35?
      Most injuries and deaths were caused by Cars and Trucks (438 incidents, 4 deaths), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (23 incidents, no deaths), and Bikes (29 incidents, no deaths). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Safer street design, enforcement, and policy can stop deaths and injuries before they happen.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and fund laws for daylighting, protected bike lanes, curb extensions, and redesign deadly corridors. They can push city agencies to act faster and hold them accountable for delays.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1151-2023
      Hudson co-sponsors solar crosswalk bill, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council pushed for 500 solar-lit crosswalks. The bill demanded action—100 new devices each year. It called for a hard look at results. But the session ended. The bill died. Streets wait. Pedestrians keep crossing in the dark.

      Int 1151-2023, introduced August 3, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to require the Department of Transportation to install at least 100 illuminated, solar-powered traffic control devices at crosswalks each year for five years—a total of 500. The bill also ordered a study comparing these devices to unlit signs, probing their power to deter traffic violations and mapping out logistical hurdles. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the installation of solar-powered crosswalks.' Council Member Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Menin, Brooks-Powers, Rivera, and over thirty others. The bill was filed at session’s end on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The city’s crosswalks remain unchanged. Vulnerable road users remain exposed.

      Hudson Supports Safety Boosting Equalized Tolling on Manhattan Crossings
      ▸
