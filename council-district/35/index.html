      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 35?

      This Isn’t Traffic—It’s Manslaughter on Eastern Parkway

      Written July 9, 2025

      District 35: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      Blood on the Crosswalks

      An eight-year-old boy died at Eastern Parkway and Albany Avenue. An SUV struck him as he crossed. The record shows only the facts: head injury, death at the scene. No mention of a toy, no last words. Just a number—another child lost in Crown Heights. In the past year, five people have died on District 35 streets. Four were pedestrians. One was a child.

      A 55-year-old woman was crushed by an SUV at Washington Avenue and Fulton Street. She was not at an intersection. She died there. The data is cold, but the loss is not. In the same year, 682 people were injured in crashes here. Nine suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again (official crash data).

      The Relentless Pattern

      The numbers do not lie. SUVs and cars are the main killers—five deaths, 426 injuries. Trucks and buses killed one, injured 28. Motorcycles and mopeds left no one dead, 23 injured. Bikes injured 29, but killed no one. The streets are not safe for the old or the young. The record is clear.

      A witness saw a man struck by a moped. “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open,” the witness said. The rider wore a mask and fled. The old man was left behind.

      A son watched his father die in the street. “He didn’t stop. He just kept going,” said Henry Tziquin. The driver fled. The family waited for a miracle that never came.

      Council Member Hudson: Action and Gaps

      Council Member Crystal Hudson has co-sponsored bills to daylight intersections, expand protected bike lanes, and legalize jaywalking—measures that shift blame away from victims and demand safer design. Hudson voted yes on the law that ended jaywalking tickets, making it clear: streets belong to people, not just cars. She backed the SAFE Streets Act and curb extensions to protect those on foot. But the carnage continues. No law has yet stopped the killing.

      What Comes Next

      The disaster is slow, but it is not fate. Call Council Member Hudson. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against reckless drivers, not crackdowns on cyclists and pedestrians. Join with others. Refuse to let another child, another parent, become a number.

      Act now. The blood on the street is not yet dry.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents local districts like District 35.
      ▸ Where does District 35 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 43 and state senate district SD 20.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 35?
      It includes the Fort Greene, Clinton Hill, Prospect Heights, Crown Heights (North), Crown Heights (South), Brooklyn CB8, Brooklyn CB2, and Brooklyn CB9 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 44, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 20, SD 25, and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 35?
      Cars and SUVs: 5 deaths, 426 injuries. Trucks and Buses: 1 death, 28 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 23 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 29 injuries (official crash data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and real enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, safer crossings, and protected bike lanes. They can demand the city redesign streets to protect people, not just cars.
