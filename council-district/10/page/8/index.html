      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 10?

      Ten Dead. Hundreds Hurt. Council Stalls.

      Written August 10, 2025 (Revised August 12, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 10: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 10, 2025

      Blood on the Streets: The Human Cost

      In District 10, the numbers do not flinch. Ten people have died on these streets since 2022. Forty-five more were left with serious injuries. In the last twelve months alone, 687 people were hurt in crashes—no deaths this year, but the wounds run deep (NYC Open Data).

      The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors. A 37-year-old cyclist, helmet on, killed by a truck on West 181st (NYC Open Data CrashID 4729767). A 15-year-old boy, thrown from his moped, crushed at Saint Nicholas Avenue (NYC Open Data CrashID 4678005). A 25-year-old, head split open, left dead on Audubon Avenue (NYC Open Data CrashID 4743277). The sidewalk is not safe. The crosswalk is not safe. The bike lane is not safe.

      Leadership: Promises, Progress, and Delay

      Council Member Carmen De La Rosa has taken steps. She co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks and force the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections a year (NYC Council Legislation). The bill sits in committee. The crosswalks stay hidden. The danger waits.

      The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has not used it. The city can redesign streets. It moves slow. “Canal Street is only as safe as its most dangerous block, and even after these changes, the vast majority of the corridor will remain deadly,” said Ben Furnas. The words fit here too.

      The Next Step: Demand Action Now

      Every crash is preventable. Every delay is a choice. Call Council Member De La Rosa. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand streets where children can walk and ride without fear. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 10 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 71 and state senate district SD 31.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 10?
      It includes the Kingsbridge-Marble Hill, Washington Heights (South), Washington Heights (North), Inwood, Highbridge Park, Inwood Hill Park, and Manhattan CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 71 and AD 72, and State Senate District SD 31.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 10?
      Cars and trucks were involved in 390 pedestrian injuries and 2 deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 24 injuries. Bikes were involved in 13 injuries and no deaths.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Every crash is preventable. Safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement can stop the bloodshed.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign streets, ban parking near crosswalks, and push for daylighting and protected bike lanes. They can also pass and enforce laws that protect people walking and biking.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
