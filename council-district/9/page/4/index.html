      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 9?

      Harlem’s Kids Are Dying—Slow the Cars Now

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised August 6, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed citation formatting, deduped citations.)

      District 9: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Blood on Harlem’s Streets

      A child’s life ended on Lenox Avenue. A 3-year-old girl, crossing with the signal, was struck and killed by an SUV making a left turn. Her mother was hurt beside her. The numbers do not soften: Eight people are dead, fifty seriously injured, and over 2,300 hurt in District 9 since 2022 (NYC Open Data).

      The pain is not abstract. A 66-year-old man died on 5th Avenue, hit by a car while walking outside the crosswalk. A 59-year-old cyclist was crushed by a bus at Lenox and 138th. The city’s most vulnerable—children, elders, people on foot and bike—pay the price for every delay, every excuse, every inch given to speed and steel.

      The Dangers Multiply

      The carnage does not come from nowhere. Cars and SUVs caused the most harm: 3 deaths, 8 serious injuries, and over 400 crashes. Trucks and buses killed and maimed. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes are in the mix, but the deadliest force is always the largest vehicle (NYC Open Data).

      The news does not let up. A food cart broke loose from a van and slammed into a parked car with a woman and child inside. Firefighters found 76 propane tanks and 75 gallons of gasoline in the van. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment. Firefighters forced entry into the van, removing 76 20-pound propane cylinders and 15 five-gallon fuel containers.

      Council Member Salaam: Progress and the Gaps

      Council Member Yusef Salaam has voted for bills to clear abandoned vehicles, require taxi dooring warnings, and boost pavement markings. He sponsored a pilot for high-visibility crosswalks and backed speed humps near parks. He co-sponsored a resolution to curb repeat speeders. But the crisis is not over. The city still waits for a default 20 mph speed limit. Protected bike lanes and safe crossings lag behind the death toll.

      The Call

      Every day of delay is another risk, another family broken. Call Council Member Salaam. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and crossings where people have died. Do not wait for another child’s name to be written in blood.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 9 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 68 and state senate district SD 30.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 9?
      It includes the Manhattanville-West Harlem, Harlem (South), Harlem (North), East Harlem (North), and Manhattan CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 68, AD 70, and AD 71, and State Senate District SD 30.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 9?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for the most deaths and serious injuries, with 3 deaths and 8 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused additional harm, with 18 crashes. Bikes were involved in 24 incidents, but caused no deaths or serious injuries (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not random or inevitable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes and safe crossings, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can also push for rapid removal of abandoned vehicles and better data on crashes.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Salaam votes yes on bill requiring DOT to report micromobility data, no direct safety impact.
      ▸

The matter summary reads: 'requiring the department of transportation to provide information about bicycle and other micromobility device activity.' Council Member Selvena N.       Int 0875-2024
      Salaam co-sponsors bill boosting step street lighting, improving pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council moves to light up step streets. At least 25 stairways each year will get new lamps. Dark paths become visible. Pedestrians gain ground. Shadows shrink. Danger loses its cover.

      Int 0875-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after introduction on May 16, 2024. The bill reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the installation of pedestrian lighting on step streets.' Prime sponsor Joann Ariola, joined by sixteen co-sponsors, pushes for at least 25 step streets to be lit each year until all are covered. Step streets are open-air staircases linking streets at different heights. The bill aims to strip darkness from these paths, making them safer for people on foot. No safety analyst note was provided.
