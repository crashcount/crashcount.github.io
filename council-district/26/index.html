      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 26?

      No More Crosswalk Graves: Demand Action Now

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Bodies Keep Coming

      In District 26, the numbers are blunt. Twenty people killed. Forty-two left with serious injuries. Over 3,400 hurt since 2022. The dead include children, cyclists, elders. The living carry scars and limp through crosswalks where the paint has faded. The disaster is slow, but it does not stop.

      Just weeks ago, a 16-year-old girl was killed at a Sunnyside intersection. The cause: a left-turning sedan, sightlines blocked. She never made it across the street. On Greenpoint Avenue, a 28-year-old woman on a bike was crushed by a turning box truck. She wore a helmet. It did not matter. The truck kept going. She did not.

      The Voices in the Aftermath

      The pain is not silent. After a crash on the Belt Parkway, a survivor described how “they went airborne and into the barrier.” The car burned. Two died. Others crawled out through shattered glass. On a city bus struck by a crane, a passenger recalled “I was holding on for my life back there.” Four were hurt. The fear lingers long after the sirens fade.

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      Council Member Julie Won has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed protected bike lanes, and legalize jaywalking. She co-sponsored the universal daylighting bill, stating that “Daylighting saves lives.” She voted to end jaywalking enforcement, shifting blame away from those on foot. But the pace is slow. The city still allows cars to park up to the edge of crosswalks. Trucks still turn across bike lanes. The blood dries, but the danger remains.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Council Member Won. Demand she fight for a citywide 20 mph speed limit, for protected intersections, for real enforcement against reckless drivers—not just cyclists and walkers. Every day of delay is another family broken.

      Contact your council member and demand action now.

      Half Of NYC Taxis Now Wheelchair Accessible
      ▸

      Half the city’s taxis now take wheelchairs. Ten years of pressure forced change. The fleet stands as the most accessible in the nation. Policy, not luck, drove this. Disabled riders gain ground. Streets shift. Access grows.

      amNY reported on June 5, 2025, that New York City’s Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) reached a milestone: 50% of active taxis are now wheelchair accessible. TLC Commissioner David Do said, “We made that today. It is the most accessible fleet in the entire country.” This achievement follows a decade-long push by disability advocates and a 2013 settlement requiring half the medallion fleet to be accessible. The transition faced delays, including the COVID-19 pandemic and medallion owner financial struggles. Policy changes, such as alternating accessible vehicles and funding support, drove the shift. The article highlights systemic progress, not individual driver actions, and underscores the impact of sustained advocacy and regulatory pressure.

      Int 0193-2024
      Won votes yes on taxi dooring warning decals, with neutral safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a bill forcing taxis and for-hire cars to post warnings on rear doors. The decals tell riders to watch for cyclists before swinging open. The law aims to cut dooring. Signs come free from the city. Cyclists face less risk.

      Int 0193-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, moved through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and passed the Council on May 1, 2025. The bill reads: 'requiring taxis and for-hire vehicles to display a decal warning passengers to look for cyclists when opening the door.' Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Jennifer Gutiérrez and a long list of co-sponsors. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law orders all taxis and for-hire vehicles to post warning decals on rear passenger doors, alerting riders to check for cyclists before opening. The Taxi and Limousine Commission will provide the signs at no cost. The measure targets dooring, a common threat to cyclists. The bill takes effect 120 days after becoming law.

      Int 1105-2024
      Won votes yes to boost street safety transparency and accountability.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to post updates on every street safety project. Each month, the public will see which projects are planned, which are stalled, and which are done. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. Sunlight on every mile.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now on the Mayor's desk, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It was introduced November 13, 2024, and passed committee and full Council votes on April 10, 2025. The law, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan,' demands the Department of Transportation post annual and monthly updates on all benchmark projects—protected bike lanes, bus lanes, signals, and upgrades. Council Member Julie Won led as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law requires public reporting on project status, funding, delays, and overruns. This measure brings hard oversight and transparency to the city’s street safety promises.
