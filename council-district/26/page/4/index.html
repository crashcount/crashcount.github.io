      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 26?

      Blood on the Crosswalks, Silence at City Hall

      Written July 12, 2025

      District 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      The Slow March of Harm

      In District 26, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people have died on these streets since 2022. More than 3,500 have been injured. Forty-three were hurt so badly they may never walk the same again. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The living carry scars.

      Just last month, an SUV struck a pedestrian on Greenpoint Avenue. In April, a cyclist’s leg was crushed by a U-turning SUV on Skillman. A 16-year-old girl was killed crossing at 47th Avenue and 46th Street. The details are spare and cold. The pain is not.

      A bus passenger said, “I have a baby with me. That would be scary. I’ll be more cautious of my surroundings” after a curb-jumping crash in Queens that left seven hurt. Another survivor described, “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb… I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus”.

      The violence is relentless. Cars, trucks, buses, bikes, and mopeds all play their part. But it is the largest vehicles—SUVs, trucks, and sedans—that do the most harm. In three years, SUVs and cars alone have killed at least four and injured hundreds more.

      Council Member Julie Won: Action and Delay

      Council Member Julie Won has backed key safety bills. She co-sponsored the universal daylighting bill to ban parking near crosswalks, a move that could save lives at the city’s most dangerous corners championed daylighting. She voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable instead of fixing the street voted to legalize jaywalking.

      But the pace is slow. The daylighting bill sits in committee. The deaths do not wait. Every week without change is another week of risk.

      What Comes Next

      The crisis is not fate. It is policy. Every intersection left blind, every speed limit left high, every delay in redesign is a choice. The council can move faster. Residents can demand more. Call Council Member Won. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand streets where a child can cross and come home alive.

      Do not wait for the next siren. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, assembly district AD 30 and state senate district SD 12.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 26?
      It includes the Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Woodside, Sunnyside Yards (South), Calvary & Mount Zion Cemeteries, and Queens CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 36, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 26?
      SUVs and cars caused the most harm to pedestrians in District 26, with 4 deaths and over 300 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 21 incidents, and bikes in 18, but the largest toll comes from larger vehicles, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and fund laws for daylighting, lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, and redesign dangerous intersections. They can also hold city agencies accountable for slow progress.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
