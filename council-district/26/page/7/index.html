      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 26?

      Queens District 26: speed, steel, and the bodies left behind

      Written August 25, 2025 (Revised August 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      Queens Boulevard. Brooklyn‑Queens Expressway. Long Island Expressway. Same names. Same endings.

      Two deaths on the BQE and one on the LIE since 2022. Three more on Queens Boulevard, and one on Roosevelt Avenue. Together, these five corridors account for the heaviest toll in Council District 26. Night and late evening hit hardest: fatalities stack up at 4 a.m., then again from 10 a.m. into the rush, and after 10 p.m. The data says so.

      The drivers keep going. The bodies don’t.

      Where the street turns on you

      A 16‑year‑old girl died in daylight at 47th Avenue and 46th Street. The record lists “view obstructed” and a left turn. She was in the intersection. She never left it. NYC Open Data crash ID 4735938.

      A 28‑year‑old woman on an e‑bike was ejected and killed at Greenpoint Avenue and 43rd Street. The truck was making a right. The sheet says “driver inattention.” The outcome needs no gloss. Crash ID 4741425 in city data.

      On Roosevelt Avenue near 70th Street, at 5:10 a.m., a 38‑year‑old man crossing outside an intersection was struck and killed by a 2011 Ford SUV going straight. The line reads “Apparent Death.” We do not print his name. We note the silence that follows. Crash ID 4719380 in the dataset.

      The big roads do the most damage

      District numbers show the worst harm on five corridors: the Brooklyn‑Queens Expressway and Long Island Expressway (two deaths each), and Queens Boulevard (three deaths). Northern Boulevard shows five serious injuries, and Roosevelt Avenue adds one death. Trucks, buses, and SUVs show up again and again in the injury rollups, but it is speed and steel that cut.

      Crashes spike in the dark. Injury counts crest around midnight, then the 4–6 a.m. hours, and return in the evening rush into late night. The ledger marks deaths at 4 a.m., 10–11 a.m., 1–6 p.m., 5–6 p.m., and after 10 p.m. The pattern repeats. The injuries never stop.

      Quotes that don’t let you look away

      “Two pedestrians were hit and killed while ordering from a food truck in Queens,” a station reported. “Witnesses say a speeding car slammed into the men.” The driver also died. That is all. CBS New York.

      “A hit‑and‑run driver killed a 52‑year‑old man… The driver sped off without stopping,” police said near JFK. “No arrests have been made.” NY Daily News and Gothamist confirmed the time and place. ABC7 wrote: “The operator of the vehicle fled the scene after hitting the man.” ABC7.

      Queens prosecutors, in another case, said a wrong‑way driver “terrorized other drivers” on the Clearview and left people badly hurt. He told police he went the wrong way “because I wanted to hurt people.” A judge gave him eight years. The carnage was deliberate; the outcome, familiar. amNY.

      What the numbers say now

      From 2022 through late August 2025, District 26 logged 6,555 crashes, 3,867 injuries, and 19 deaths. Pedestrians suffered 496 injuries and five deaths. Cyclists, 460 injuries and two deaths. The hour‑by‑hour table shows the danger building after dark and before dawn. “Other” causes dominate the city forms, but neglect has a shape: turns that cut, blocked sight lines, and drivers who do not see what is right in front of them.

      Fix what is in plain sight

      Sight lines kill here. The Council member for this district backed a bill to ban parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and to build daylighting barriers at 1,000 corners a year. “Daylighting saves lives,” a supporter said when the bill was introduced. Read it in Streetsblog and the Council file for Int 1138‑2024.

      Markings matter. The Council passed a law to force fast striping after milling so crossings and lanes are visible within five business days. See Int 1160‑2025. Won voted yes. Clearing junk cars helps too; she backed a law to remove derelicts within 72 hours so corners and crosswalks are not blind. Int 0857‑2024.

      On the ground, corners like Queens Boulevard and Northern Boulevard need hardened turns and no‑parking daylighting now. Truck routes through Greenpoint Avenue and Skillman need slow entries and protected corners. Night hours need focused enforcement where the deaths stack up.

      Citywide tools that cut the body count

      Albany handed New York City the power to lower its own speed limits. The city can drop residential streets to 20 mph. That saves lives. Use it. See our Take Action explainer.

      A small share of drivers does outsized harm. “1.5% of motorists cause 21% of pedestrian deaths,” and vehicles with 16 camera tickets in a year are far more likely to kill. The proposed Stop Super Speeders Act would force those drivers to use speed‑limiting tech. Read the figures and call script in our action page, with sourcing to Streetsblog’s analysis.

      Lower the speeds. Stop the repeat offenders. Protect the crossing. The rest is names we will not get back.
