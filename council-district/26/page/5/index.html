      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 26?

      District 26: Streets Still Kill, City Still Stalls

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned citations.)

      District 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Blood on the Asphalt

      In District 26, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people have died on these streets since 2022. Forty-three more suffered serious injuries. More than 3,500 have been hurt. Each number is a body, a family, a life split open by metal and speed. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The injuries are not just bruises. They are broken bones, crushed skulls, lives changed in a blink.

      Just last year, a 16-year-old girl was killed at an intersection on 47th Avenue. She never made it across. In another crash, a cyclist was crushed by a turning truck on Greenpoint Avenue. The data shows the pattern: cars, trucks, and SUVs do most of the killing. The city calls them accidents. But the pain is not accidental. It is built into the street.

      “Daylighting Saves Lives”—But Progress Crawls

      Council Member Julie Won has taken some steps. She backed a bill to ban parking near intersections, a move known as daylighting. “Daylighting saves lives,” she said, pushing to clear sight lines so drivers can see children and elders before it is too late (Streetsblog NYC). She also voted to speed up pavement markings and to clear abandoned cars from crosswalks—small steps, but steps all the same (NYC Council – Legistar).

      But the pace is slow. The city promises a thousand intersections a year. There are tens of thousands. Every week that passes, another life is risked.

      The Human Cost

      After a bus jumped the curb in Flushing, injuring eight, a passenger said, “I have a baby with me. That would be scary. I’ll be more cautious of my surroundings” (CBS New York). But caution is not enough. The system puts the burden on the vulnerable. The driver had fallen asleep at the wheel (ABC7). The city shrugs. The street stays the same.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Residents must demand more. Call Council Member Julie Won. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand universal daylighting at every intersection. Demand that the city redesign streets for people, not cars. Every day of delay is another day of blood on the road.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of local districts.
      ▸ Where does District 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, assembly district AD 30 and state senate district SD 12.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 26?
      It includes the Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Woodside, Sunnyside Yards (South), Calvary & Mount Zion Cemeteries, and Queens CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 36, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 26?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 26 were caused by cars and trucks (4 deaths, 386 injuries), followed by motorcycles and mopeds (no deaths, 21 injuries), and bikes (no deaths, 18 injuries). See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Policies like lower speed limits, daylighting, and safer street design can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, clear sight lines at intersections, and redesign streets to protect people walking and biking.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Council District 26

      Int 0080-2024
      Won co-sponsors bill empowering civilians to report hazardous vehicle obstructions, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants near schools. Civilian complaints trigger fines. Streets clear, danger cut. Council moves to protect the vulnerable.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced February 8, 2024, the bill creates a $175 penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of schools. The Department of Transportation must set up a civilian reporting program. If a civilian complaint leads to a fine, the complainant gets 25 percent of proceeds. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code... in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints.' Council Member Carlina Rivera leads, joined by Restler, Ayala, Joseph, Menin, and others. The bill aims to keep paths clear for those most at risk.

      Int 0079-2024
      Won co-sponsors bill to boost pedestrian lighting, improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council moves to force brighter sidewalks. Bill orders 500 corridors lit each year. Shadows shrink. Pedestrians gain ground. Committee holds the bill. Streets wait.

      Int 0079-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on February 8, 2024. The bill demands the city install pedestrian lighting in at least 500 commercial corridors per year, aiming for a minimum of 1 footcandle (11 lux) on every sidewalk. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installation of pedestrian lighting fixtures.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Krishnan, Bottcher, and others. The committee has not yet voted. The bill’s text sets clear targets for coverage and contiguity, but action is stalled. Vulnerable road users remain in the dark until the city acts.
