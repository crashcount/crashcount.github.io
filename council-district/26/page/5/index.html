      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 26?

      No More Blood on Julie Won’s Watch

      Written July 14, 2025

      District 26: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 14, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Broken, Streets Unforgiving

      In District 26, the numbers do not lie. Twenty deaths. Forty-three serious injuries. Over 6,000 crashes since 2022. The dead include children, cyclists, elders. The living carry scars. In the last year alone, three people died and eleven were left with life-changing wounds (NYC Open Data).

      The pain is not abstract. On June 26, a 16-year-old girl was killed at an intersection. On July 18 last year, a 28-year-old woman riding her bike was crushed by a turning truck. The stories repeat. The sidewalk is not safe. The crosswalk is not safe. The bike lane is not safe.

      A bus jumps the curb in Flushing. Eight hurt. “I have a baby with me. That would be scary. I’ll be more cautious of my surroundings,” said Samantha Hart. The driver, new on the job, may have fallen asleep. The MTA pulled him from service. The investigation drags on (CBS New York).

      Who Bears the Weight?

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. They killed four, seriously injured four, and left hundreds with lesser wounds. Trucks and buses hurt dozens more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes also left their mark, but the carnage is driven by heavy, fast vehicles (NYC Open Data).

      The city blames distraction, inattention, bad luck. But the pattern is clear. The most vulnerable—pedestrians, cyclists, children—pay the highest price. “It must be very devastating for the people that were on the bus,” said Jacqueline Cox. The devastation is not rare. It is routine.

      Council Member Julie Won: Action and Delay

      Council Member Julie Won has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and legalize jaywalking (NYC Council – Legistar, Streetsblog NYC). She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act and pushed for more daylight at intersections (NYC Council – Legistar). These are steps in the right direction. But the pace is slow. The bills sit in committee. The deaths do not wait.

      The Next Step: Demand Relentless Change

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. Every crash is a policy failure. Call Council Member Julie Won. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings, daylighted corners, and real enforcement against reckless drivers. Do not wait for another child to die.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 26 sit politically?
      It belongs to Queens, assembly district AD 30 and state senate district SD 12 (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Which areas are in District 26?
      It includes the Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Sunnyside Yards (North), Long Island City-Hunters Point, Sunnyside, Woodside, Sunnyside Yards (South), and Calvary & Mount Zion Cemeteries neighborhoods, as well as Queens CB2. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 30, AD 36, and AD 37, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 59 (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 26?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm, with 4 deaths and 4 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds accounted for 2 serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 2 serious injuries but no deaths (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents—they follow patterns and can be prevented with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, daylighted intersections, and real accountability for dangerous drivers.

      Int 0080-2024
      Won co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by deterring hazardous vehicle obstruction.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants within half a mile of schools. Fines set at $175. Civilians can report violations. DOT must act on complaints. Council aims to clear paths for people, not cars.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after its introduction on February 8, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations," creates a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of a school. The penalty is $175 per violation. The Department of Transportation must launch a civilian reporting program. If DOT prosecutes a case using civilian evidence, the complainant gets 25% of the proceeds. Council Member Carlina Rivera leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Joseph, Menin, Nurse, Hudson, Brannan, Farías, Brewer, Salaam, Hanif, Avilés, Won, Bottcher, Krishnan, Gutiérrez, Marte, and the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill aims to keep streets clear for vulnerable road users, especially near schools.

      Int 0708-2022
      Won absent as committee advances bill boosting citywide street safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to overhaul city truck routes. The bill orders daylighting at intersections and new signage. It aims to cut crashes, boost visibility, and shrink truck miles. Sponsors demand safer streets for people on foot and bike. Change is now law.

      Int 0708-2022, now Local Law 171 of 2023, was enacted by the City Council on December 16, 2023, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The law, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to redesigning the city truck route network," requires the Department of Transportation to redesign truck routes to "improve safety, increase visibility, reduce traffic congestion, and reduce vehicle miles traveled." Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by dozens of co-sponsors. The law mandates daylighting—removing parking near intersections—to clear sight lines for drivers and protect people crossing. DOT must consult with safety groups, residents, and businesses, and replace truck route signage for better clarity. The law sets deadlines: initial report by November 2023, implementation by June 2024, and new signage by July 2024. This overhaul targets the deadly risk trucks pose to vulnerable road users at city intersections.

      Won votes yes on license plate reporting bill with no safety impact.

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to report cars with unreadable plates dodging cameras. The bill targets drivers hiding their plates from red light and speed cameras. Reports must show where, when, and how plates go unseen. Data goes public, every quarter.

      Int 0712-2022, now enacted as Local Law 155 of 2023, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and the full Council on October 19, 2023. The law, sponsored by Gale A.
