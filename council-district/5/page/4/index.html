      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 5?

      York Avenue to the FDR: Six dead, and a corridor that keeps taking

      Written August 25, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 5: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 25, 2025

      FDR, YORK, SECOND: WHERE PEOPLE DIE

      The map is simple. FDR Drive leads the body count with four deaths and 370 injuries since 2022, with seven serious injuries (NYC Open Data). 2nd Avenue holds two deaths and 71 injuries; York Avenue shows two deaths (NYC Open Data). Pedestrians are hit hardest here: eight killed, 257 injured, 15 seriously hurt. Cyclists: one killed, 214 injured (District stats). SUVs show up most often in pedestrian harm: 71 pedestrian casualties with six deaths (District stats).

      The city’s own numbers say what speed does. At 4 a.m., two dead. At 5 a.m., one. At 9 a.m., two more. The 5 p.m. hour holds four dead. Night keeps bleeding into dawn (hourly distribution).

      RECENT NAMES, HARD STREETS

      A 90‑year‑old woman died on York and East 72nd in February. The record lists “Driver Inattention/Distraction.” An SUV and a taxi were going straight. She was a pedestrian, outside an intersection. She died (CrashID 4790720).

      A 30‑year‑old woman died at York and East 87th in April. Three cars. She was in the intersection. The sheet says “Apparent Death” and “Severe Bleeding.” That is all (CrashID 4714580).

      On the FDR in February, a driver died. Unsafe speed. Ejected. The car was “Demolished” (CrashID 4790343). In July, a 55‑year‑old driver died at East 83rd and York. He lost consciousness behind the wheel, then was gone (CrashID 4832831).

      PATTERNS THE CITY ALREADY KNOWS

      “Unsafe speed” shows up in the dead. “Disregarded traffic control” does too. Failure to yield. Distraction. “Other” leads all factors in death and harm here, with 11 deaths and 402 injuries. Vulnerable road user errors are a small slice by comparison (District factors).

      Late hours are hot. So is the evening rush. The 17:00 hour holds the most death. The 14:00–16:00 hours stack injuries like cordwood (hourly distribution).

      WHAT COULD STOP THE HITS

      Daylight the turns. Keep cars back from the crosswalk. There’s a bill for that. It would ban standing or parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and force daylighting at 1,000 intersections a year. Council Member Julie Menin co‑sponsors it (Int 1138‑2024). Another bill she backs orders curb extensions each year at the worst crash corners (Int 0285‑2024).

      Small fixes matter on York and 2nd: hardened lefts, no‑parking daylighting, and curb extensions at signalized crossings. Clear sight lines save walkers and riders. The abandoned cars that block views? A new law forces the city to tow derelicts within 72 hours; Menin voted yes (Int 0857‑2024 vote).

      CITYWIDE CHOICES THAT SAVE LIVES

      Lower speeds citywide. Albany gave NYC the power. “Sammy’s Law gave NYC the power to set safer speeds,” our own call to action states. The next step is a default 20 mph, not a patchwork (Take Action).

      Stop the worst repeat speeders. The proposed Stop Super Speeders Act would require drivers with 11 DMV points in 18 months or 16 camera tickets in a year to use speed limiters that cap them within 5 mph of the posted limit (Take Action).

      AFTERMATH AND THE NEXT LIGHT

      After two people were killed at Bowery and Canal by a driver charged with going 109 mph, the city promised barriers and narrower lanes. “We are taking immediate steps to fortify this intersection,” said Transportation Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez (Gothamist). Advocates warned the rest of the corridor stays deadly. “Canal Street is only as safe as its most dangerous block,” said Ben Furnas (Gothamist). The upgrades came after two more names. That is the pattern.

      District 5 does not need another vigil. It needs slower streets and clear corners. It needs action before the sirens.

      One thing you can do today: push the city to set safer speeds and rein in repeat speeders. Start here: Take Action.

      Int 1138-2024 Menin co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Dec 5 - Council bill bars cars from blocking crosswalks. No standing or parking within 20 feet. City must install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections yearly. Streets clear. Sightlines open. Danger cut.

      Int 1138-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 5, 2024.       Int 0080-2024 Menin co-sponsors bill empowering civilians to report hazardous vehicle obstructions, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Feb 8 - Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants near schools. Civilian complaints trigger fines. Streets clear, danger cut. Council moves to protect the vulnerable.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced February 8, 2024, the bill creates a $175 penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of schools. The Department of Transportation must set up a civilian reporting program. If a civilian complaint leads to a fine, the complainant gets 25 percent of proceeds. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code... in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints.' Council Member Carlina Rivera leads, joined by Restler, Ayala, Joseph, Menin, and others. The bill aims to keep paths clear for those most at risk.
