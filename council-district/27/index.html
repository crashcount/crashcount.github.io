      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 27?

      Thirteen Dead in Two Years—District 27 Streets Are Killing Us

      Written July 12, 2025

      District 27: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      The Wounds That Do Not Heal

      In District 27, the road takes its due. Thirteen people have died since 2022. Over 3,300 have been injured. Twenty-seven were hurt so badly the city calls them serious injuries. These are not numbers. They are lives cut, bodies broken, families left with empty chairs.

      Just last week, a BMW on the Belt Parkway lost control, hit the median, and went airborne. Two people died. A survivor described the crash: “They went airborne and into the barrier.” The car caught fire. Others were thrown from the wreck. There was no time to scream. There was only the sound of metal and glass.

      On July 11, an MTA bus in Queens jumped the curb and smashed into a pole. Eight people were hurt. The driver, just 25, told investigators he “misjudged the curb.” Video later showed he had fallen asleep at the wheel. The MTA pulled him from service. One rider recalled, “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb… I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus.”

      Who Pays the Price?

      Children, elders, and the working poor are most at risk. In the last twelve months, 102 people under 18 were injured. One child was seriously hurt. Two people over 75 have died. The most common killers are cars and SUVs, responsible for over 500 injuries and seven serious injuries. Trucks and buses hurt dozens more. Bikes and mopeds are a fraction, but every crash is a wound.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Nantasha Williams has backed bills to study safer street designs, boost pedestrian lighting, and crack down on illegal parking. She voted to legalize jaywalking, a move that ends the blame game against people on foot. She co-sponsored a bill to create a task force on e-bike safety, but the bill sits in committee, its promise unkept. Williams was absent for a key vote to redesign truck routes and daylight intersections—measures proven to save lives.

      The city moves slow. The street moves fast.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Council Member Williams. Demand action. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every corner, real protection for every child, every elder, every cyclist. Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 27 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 27?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, St. Albans, Hollis, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, and Queens CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate District SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 27?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm to pedestrians in District 27, with over 500 injuries and 7 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 9 crashes, and bikes in 7. Trucks and buses also contributed to dozens of injuries. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and stronger enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, daylight intersections, safer crossings, and hold reckless drivers accountable. They can also fund street redesigns and push for rapid implementation.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Half Of NYC Taxis Now Wheelchair Accessible
      ▸

      Half the city’s taxis now take wheelchairs. Ten years of pressure forced change. The fleet stands as the most accessible in the nation. Policy, not luck, drove this. Disabled riders gain ground. Streets shift. Access grows.

      amNY reported on June 5, 2025, that New York City’s Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) reached a milestone: 50% of active taxis are now wheelchair accessible. TLC Commissioner David Do said, “We made that today. It is the most accessible fleet in the entire country.” This achievement follows a decade-long push by disability advocates and a 2013 settlement requiring half the medallion fleet to be accessible. The transition faced delays, including the COVID-19 pandemic and medallion owner financial struggles. Policy changes, such as alternating accessible vehicles and funding support, drove the shift. The article highlights systemic progress, not individual driver actions, and underscores the impact of sustained advocacy and regulatory pressure.
