      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Hillside and Parsons: a boy bleeds, and the count goes on

      District 27: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      Just after midday on Sep 2, 2025, at Parsons Boulevard and Hillside Avenue, a 16-year-old operating a motorized device was injured in a collision with a sedan. Police records show a head wound and severe bleeding in that crash NYC Open Data.

      Since Jan 1, 2022, District 27 has recorded 18 people killed and 4,851 injured on its streets. Police have flagged 40 serious injuries in that span. These are the city’s own numbers NYC Open Data.

      Where the street turns against you

      Five people have died along the Cross Island Parkway in this district. Three more on Jamaica Avenue. Those corridors keep showing up in crash logs NYC Open Data.

      At intersections, police repeatedly record drivers disregarding traffic control (1 death, 31 injuries) and failing to yield (1 serious injury, 34 injuries). These are the boxes checked on MV-104 reports, again and again NYC Open Data.

      The worst hour is around 8 PM, with four deaths logged then. Darkness comes, and the numbers rise NYC Open Data.

      People walking carry the brunt: 4 pedestrians killed, 839 injured here since 2022. People on bikes were hurt 213 times. Most pedestrian injuries involve drivers in cars and SUVs; trucks and buses trail far behind in the count NYC Open Data.

      The pace is not slowing

      Measured year to date, crashes in this district are up 8.0% from last year. Injuries are up 12.0%. Police coded serious injuries have more than tripled. The line keeps climbing NYC Open Data.

      Speed is the lever that moves the body count. As the State Senate has said, “Speed cameras have cut speeding by over 60% in locations where installed.” The tool works where we put it to work NYS Senate.

      Who holds the wrench

      Council Member Nantasha M. Williams has voted to clear abandoned cars from sightlines and crosswalks, a small but real fix (Int 0857-2024). She also backed dooring warnings in taxis (Int 0193-2024).

      But the centerline remains speed. The Council can set safer defaults. The state can rein in repeat speeders. Williams now sponsors a bill to crack down on unlicensed commuter vans (Int 1347-2025). The deadliest harm, though, still comes from drivers going too fast for human bodies to survive NYC Open Data.

      What will stop the next siren

      Start where the bodies fall: daylight corners on Jamaica Avenue and Farmers Boulevard; give pedestrians head starts; harden turns; route trucks off neighborhood streets. Then finish the job citywide: lower the default speed and fit habitual speeders with electronic limiters. These are the tools we have NYC Open Data — and the steps our own advocates lay out here.

      One boy bled at Hillside and Parsons. It does not end there. It ends when we slow the cars and keep them slow NYC Open Data.

      Take one step now. Tell City Hall and Albany to act here.

      Int 1347-2025 Williams is primary sponsor of unlicensed commuter van crackdown, worsening overall safety.
      ▸

      Aug 14 - Council moves to hit rogue vans hard. A checklist. Max fines for each breach. TLC, NYPD, DOT must act together. Crack down on illegal operators who imperil riders and people on the street. The bill sits in committee.

      Int 1347-2025 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced and referred on August 14, 2025. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to enforcing violations against unlicensed commuter vans.” It orders TLC, with NYPD and DOT, to keep a compliance checklist. When officers stop an unlicensed van, they must check every rule and issue the maximum fine for each breach. Primary sponsor Nantasha M. Williams. Co-sponsors Selvena N. Brooks-Powers, Mercedes Narcisse, and Chris Banks. Status: Committee. If enacted, it takes effect 120 days later. It targets illegal operators who put riders and bystanders at risk.

      Int 1347-2025 Williams sponsors unlicensed commuter vans crackdown bill, worsening overall street safety.
      ▸

      Aug 14 - Council introduces Int 1347-2025 to curb rogue commuter vans. TLC, NYPD, and DOT must keep a safety checklist. Officers who spot an unlicensed van stack every charge and levy max fines. A push to shield riders and people on the street.

      Int 1347-2025 is in Committee. It sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after introduction and referral on August 14, 2025. The bill’s title reads, “A Local Law … in relation to enforcing violations against unlicensed commuter vans.” It orders TLC, with NYPD and DOT, to maintain a checklist of laws on vehicle and driver licensure, service authorization, insurance, and inspections. When an officer stops an unlicensed van, they must review the list and issue the maximum penalty for each violation. Primary sponsor Nantasha M. Williams is joined by Selvena N. Brooks-Powers, Mercedes Narcisse, Chris Banks, and Farah N. Louis. The aim is to protect riders and bystanders from unlicensed operators who skip the rules.
