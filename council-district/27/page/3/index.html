      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 27?

      District 27 Bleeds—Will City Hall Stop the Killing?

      Written July 16, 2025

      District 27: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      Thirteen dead. Over 3,300 injured. In District 27, the slow grind of traffic violence does not pause. Since 2022, two people have died in the last twelve months alone. More than a thousand have been hurt. Children, elders, cyclists, pedestrians—no one is spared. The numbers are not just numbers. They are lives, broken and gone.

      Recent Crashes, Lasting Scars

      Last month, a 39-year-old man was killed at 116th Avenue and Nashville Boulevard. He was ejected from his motorized scooter after an SUV turned left in front of him. He died at the scene. The crash report lists the cause as driver inattention (NYC Open Data).

      On July 5th, a 76-year-old woman riding in the back seat of a sedan on Cross Island Parkway was killed in a collision. The car was hit head-on by another sedan changing lanes. She did not make it home (NYC Open Data).

      Voices from the Aftermath

      After a bus in Queens jumped the curb and smashed into a pole, seven people were hurt. “It must be very devastating for the people that were on the bus,” said Jacqueline Cox. Another rider, Ken Baur, remembered, “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus” (ABC7).

      Every crash is a warning. Every injury is a debt unpaid.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Nantasha Williams has voted for bills to clear abandoned vehicles from streets, to warn taxi passengers about dooring, and to increase street safety transparency. She co-sponsored a bill to curb repeat speeders. But the carnage continues. No law has yet slowed the cars or protected the children crossing the street. No bill has made the streets safe enough.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy.

      Call Council Member Williams. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking. Join Families for Safe Streets and Transportation Alternatives. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Act now. The blood on the street is not yet dry.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the legislative branch of city government. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 27 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 27?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, St. Albans, Hollis, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, and Queens CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate District SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 27?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 27 are caused by Cars and Trucks (519 incidents), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (9), and Bikes (7) (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. They are the result of choices—speed, design, enforcement—that can be changed. Safer streets and lower speeds save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, support automated enforcement, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking. They can also hold city agencies accountable for slow action.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Williams co-sponsors bill to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council orders swift removal of abandoned and unplated cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. Police target vehicles with missing or fake plates. Fewer hazards for those on foot and bike.

      Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on April 18, 2024. The bill states: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code... in relation to vehicles reported as abandoned to the department of sanitation.' Sponsored by Selvena N. Brooks-Powers (primary) and co-sponsored by over a dozen council members, it forces the Department of Sanitation to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours and empowers NYPD to tow cars with missing or obscured plates or stickers. The law aims to clear street hazards fast, reducing risks for pedestrians and cyclists.

      Williams co-sponsors bill for dynamic parking zones, minimal safety impact.
