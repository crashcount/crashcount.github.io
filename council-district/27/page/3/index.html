      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 27?

      Bodies in the Street, Promises in Committee

      Written July 7, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 27: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Slow Grind of Loss

      In District 27, the numbers do not lie. Twelve people killed. Over 3,200 injured. Twenty-five left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Each number is a body on the street, a family waiting in a hospital hallway. In the last year alone, the district saw one death and 984 injuries from crashes—more than two people hurt every day (NYC Open Data).

      Just days ago, a BMW tore across the Belt Parkway, vaulting the divider and slamming into oncoming cars. Six people were injured, three critically. Police closed the westbound lanes. The NYPD’s Collision Investigation Squad arrived, picking through the wreckage. “It was unclear what caused the driver to lose control but police said speed may have been an issue,” the Post reported.

      The Most Vulnerable Pay the Price

      Children, cyclists, and pedestrians bear the brunt. SUVs and cars caused nearly 500 pedestrian injuries in this district. Trucks and buses added dozens more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes left their own trail of broken bodies (NYC Open Data).

      A 15-year-old boy died after falling from a subway roof in Queens. EMS rushed him out on a stretcher. “The 15-year-old was rushed to Bellevue Hospital, where he died from his injuries,” the Post wrote.

      Council Member Williams: Steps Forward, Steps Missed

      Council Member Nantasha Williams has backed bills to boost pedestrian lighting, study safer street designs, and ban commercial vehicle street parking. She voted to legalize jaywalking, a move that ends the old blame game against people on foot. But she was absent for a key vote to redesign truck routes and daylight intersections—measures proven to save lives (NYC Council – Legistar).

      Some bills she co-sponsored still sit in committee. Promises, not protections. The city studies, the streets wait.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day without action is another day someone does not come home. Call Council Member Williams. Demand she push for a citywide 20 mph speed limit, daylight every intersection, and fund street redesigns now. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents residents in each district (NYC Council – Legistar).
      ▸ Where does District 27 sit politically?
      It belongs to Queens, assembly district AD 29, and state senate district SD 14 (NYC Council – Legistar).
      ▸ Which areas are in District 27?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, St. Albans, Hollis, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, and Queens CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate District SD 14 (NYC Council – Legistar).
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 27?
      SUVs and sedans caused nearly 500 pedestrian injuries in District 27. Trucks and buses were involved in 36 pedestrian injuries, motorcycles and mopeds in 9, and bikes in 6 (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not random—they follow patterns that policy can change.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass laws to lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, fund daylighting at intersections, and ensure enforcement targets the most dangerous driving.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0708-2022
      Williams absent as committee advances bill boosting citywide street safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law to overhaul city truck routes. The bill orders daylighting at intersections and new signage. It aims to cut crashes, boost visibility, and shrink truck miles. Sponsors demand safer streets for people on foot and bike. Change is now law.

      Int 0708-2022, now Local Law 171 of 2023, was enacted by the City Council on December 16, 2023, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The law, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to redesigning the city truck route network," requires the Department of Transportation to redesign truck routes to "improve safety, increase visibility, reduce traffic congestion, and reduce vehicle miles traveled." Council Member Tiffany Cabán led as primary sponsor, joined by dozens of co-sponsors. The law mandates daylighting—removing parking near intersections—to clear sight lines for drivers and protect people crossing. DOT must consult with safety groups, residents, and businesses, and replace truck route signage for better clarity. The law sets deadlines: initial report by November 2023, implementation by June 2024, and new signage by July 2024. This overhaul targets the deadly risk trucks pose to vulnerable road users at city intersections.
