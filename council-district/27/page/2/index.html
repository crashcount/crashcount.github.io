      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 27?

      Thirteen Dead, Thousands Hurt—How Many More Before We Act?

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 27: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      In District 27, the numbers do not flinch. Thirteen dead. Twenty-seven left with serious injuries. More than 3,300 hurt since 2022. Each number is a body, a family, a future cut short. In the last year alone, two people died and over 1,000 were injured in crashes on these streets (NYC Open Data).

      Just days ago, a 76-year-old woman died in the back seat of a BMW on the Cross Island Parkway. The car changed lanes, struck another, and caught fire. She never made it home (NY Daily News). On July 8, a crane slammed into a city bus in Queens Village. Emily McKinnon, a passenger, said, “We didn’t see the crane coming… then the crane just hits us.” Four were injured. She added, “The bus driver didn’t do anything wrong… all of a sudden you’re like, oh my God, this happened to me?”

      Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and sedans do most of the damage. They account for over 500 injuries to pedestrians in this district. Trucks and buses add dozens more. Bikes and mopeds are not blameless, but the carnage comes on four wheels, not two (NYC Open Data).

      Children are not spared. In the past year, 76 people under 18 were injured. One was killed. The old are not spared. One death among those over 75. The violence is steady, unyielding, and it does not care who you are.

      Leadership: Progress and Delay

      Council Member Nantasha Williams has voted for daylighting, greenways, and traffic calming near seniors. She co-sponsored bills to boost pedestrian lighting, study safer street designs, and crack down on illegal parking. She voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable for crossing the street (NYC Council – Legistar).

      But some bills stall. The push for better lighting sits in committee. The task force on e-bike safety is still just talk. Williams was absent for the vote to overhaul truck routes—a law meant to clear sight lines and protect people at intersections (NYC Council – Legistar).

      What Next? Demand Action

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. Every delay is another family broken. Call Council Member Williams. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real street redesigns, not just studies. Join Families for Safe Streets and Transportation Alternatives. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 27 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 27?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, St. Albans, Hollis, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, and Queens CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate District SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 27?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with over 500 pedestrian injuries. SUVs caused 169 injuries, sedans 253, trucks and buses 39, motorcycles and mopeds 9, and bikes were involved in 7 pedestrian injuries (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to reduce traffic violence?
      They can pass and fund laws for safer street design, lower speed limits, and stronger enforcement against dangerous driving. They can also support bills that protect vulnerable road users and ensure city agencies act quickly.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0346-2024
      Williams votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.
