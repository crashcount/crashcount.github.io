      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 27?

      Queens Streets Run Red—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed citation formatting, and cleaned citations list.)

      District 27: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The numbers do not lie. In the last 12 months, District 27 saw 1 death, 9 serious injuries, and 1,498 crashes. 991 neighbors were hurt. Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians—no one is spared. The wounds are fresh. The pain is old.

      Just days ago, a BMW tore across the Belt Parkway, struck the divider, and vaulted into oncoming cars. Six people were left broken. Two are fighting for their lives. Police closed the road and the city moved on. The driver then struck a gray 2016 CR-V, as well as a red 2022 Hyundai, reported ABC7. The NYPD’s Collision Investigation Squad is still probing. It was unclear what caused the driver to lose control but police said speed may have been an issue, said the New York Post.

      SUVs and sedans do most of the harm. In three years, cars and trucks have injured 499 people walking or biking here. Trucks and buses add dozens more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes are in the mix, but the carnage is driven by heavy steel and speed. Children are not spared: 89 kids were hurt in the last year alone.

      Council Member Nantasha Williams has backed bills to daylight intersections, study safer street designs, and boost pedestrian lighting. She voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished walkers instead of drivers. But some bills she co-sponsored—like those for better lighting and e-bike safety—are still stalled in committee. She was absent for a key vote to redesign deadly truck routes. Progress is slow. The streets are not waiting.

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by the city. Call Council Member Williams. Demand she push for a citywide 20 mph speed limit, more protected crossings, and real accountability for repeat dangerous drivers. The blood on the street is not washed away by words. Only action will do.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents local districts.
      ▸ Where does District 27 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, assembly district AD 29 and state senate district SD 14.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 27?
      It includes the Jamaica, South Jamaica, St. Albans, Hollis, Queens Village, Cambria Heights, and Queens CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 29, AD 32, and AD 33, and State Senate District SD 14.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 27?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 499 pedestrian and cyclist injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 9 injuries, and bikes caused 6 injuries. See the NYC Open Data breakdown.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not inevitable—they are the result of choices in street design, speed limits, and enforcement. Policy can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, redesign dangerous intersections, expand daylighting, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
