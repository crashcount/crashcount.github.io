      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 13?

      Blood on Pelham Parkway: Every Death Here Is a Policy Choice

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised July 3, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, deduped and fixed citation URLs.)

      District 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Death Count Keeps Rising

      In District 13, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, nine people have died and 2,606 have been injured in crashes. Thirty-five of those injuries were serious. Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians are among the dead. The streets do not forgive. Just days ago, a 31-year-old man was killed on Pelham Parkway when a driver turned left and struck his motorcycle. Police reported that “the Equinox driver stayed at the scene” and both were taken to the hospital, but only one survived (NY Daily News).

      Pedestrians are not spared. Last year, a 40-year-old man was killed by an SUV on Westchester Avenue. The crash report lists “Driver Inattention/Distraction” and “Driver Inexperience” as causes. He died in the street (NYC Open Data).

      Leadership: Votes, Silence, and Missed Chances

      Council Member Kristy Marmorato has taken some steps. She co-sponsored bills to add lighting to step streets and commercial corridors, a move that could help walkers at night. But when it mattered most, she voted no on laws that would have made medians safer and ended jaywalking enforcement—measures proven to protect the most vulnerable. She called congestion pricing a “cash grab,” ignoring the evidence that less traffic means fewer deaths (Streetsblog NYC).

      Parking, not people, gets priority. Marmorato fought to keep parking mandates near new Metro-North stations, saying, “We live in a transit desert where cars are a necessity for daily activities” (Streetsblog NYC). The result: more cars, more danger, less room for life.

      The Path Forward: No More Waiting

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a policy failure. The city has the power to lower speed limits, redesign streets, and end the reign of reckless drivers. The time for half-measures is over. Call Council Member Marmorato. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand safer crossings, protected bike lanes, and an end to policies that put cars over kids. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Change it.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 13 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 80 and state senate district SD 33.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 13?
      It includes the Throgs Neck-Schuylerville, Pelham Bay-Country Club-City Island, Hart Island, Ferry Point Park-St. Raymond Cemetery, Pelham Parkway-Van Nest, Morris Park, Pelham Gardens, Allerton, Hutchinson Metro Center, Pelham Bay Park, and Bronx CB10 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 80 and AD 82, and State Senate Districts SD 33, SD 34, and SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 13?
      Most injuries and deaths came from SUVs and Cars (3 deaths, 301 total incidents), followed by Trucks and Buses (1 death, 19 incidents), Motorcycles and Mopeds (0 deaths, 16 incidents), and Bikes (0 deaths, 4 incidents).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Every crash is preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not accidents—they are failures of policy.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, support protected bike lanes, and end policies that prioritize parking over people. Every vote and every delay has a cost.

      Int 0079-2024
      Marmorato co-sponsors bill to boost pedestrian lighting, improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council members want 500 corridors lit for walkers each year. The bill demands bright sidewalks—no less than 1 footcandle. Most corridors must connect, forming safer, well-lit routes. The measure sits in committee, waiting for action. Darkness remains a threat.

      Int 0079-2024, introduced on February 8, 2024, sits 'Laid Over in Committee' with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installation of pedestrian lighting fixtures,' would require the transportation commissioner to install sidewalk lighting in at least 500 commercial corridors per year, each lit to a minimum of 1 footcandle (11 lux). At least 450 corridors must be contiguous to others with new or existing lighting. Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Krishnan, Bottcher, and many others. The bill aims to cut through the city’s darkness, demanding light for those on foot. It remains stalled in committee, its promise unrealized.
