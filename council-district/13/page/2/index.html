      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 13?

      Nine Dead, Hundreds Hurt—How Many More Before We Put People Before Cars?

      Written June 22, 2025 (Revised June 28, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In District 13, the numbers do not lie. Nine people killed. Thirty-five left with injuries so severe they change a life. In the last twelve months alone, two more deaths, fifteen more serious injuries, and over 800 hurt. The streets run through Throgs Neck, Pelham Bay, Morris Park, and the pain runs with them.

      On the Hutchinson River Parkway, before dawn, a Honda Pilot slams into a scooter. An 18-year-old woman is thrown onto the asphalt. She does not get up. “An 18-year-old was in critical condition after being thrown from a moped in the hit-and-run, according to NYPD” (CBS New York). The driver flees. Six others go to the hospital. The road is quiet again, but nothing is the same.

      A year before, a 64-year-old cyclist is killed at Pierce Avenue and Williamsbridge Road. A pedestrian, 66, is crushed on the parkway. A 40-year-old man dies on Westchester Avenue. The list is long. The pain is longer.

      Who Pays the Price

      Most of the dead and maimed are not behind the wheel. Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and SUVs are the main weapons—three killed, six seriously hurt, 292 more injured by these machines in just three years. Trucks and buses take another life. Motorcycles, mopeds, bikes—they hurt too, but the numbers are smaller. The risk is not shared equally.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Kristy Marmorato has signed on to bills for more street lighting and faster utility pole removal. These are steps, but small ones. She voted against safer medians (NYC Council), and against ending jaywalking enforcement (NYC Council), a law that would have protected those on foot from needless tickets and blame. She calls congestion pricing a “cash grab” (amNY), fighting a measure proven to cut traffic and save lives.

      She defends parking mandates, saying, “We live in a transit desert where cars are a necessity for daily activities” (Streetsblog NYC). But every new parking spot is another reason for a car to be on the road, another risk for a child crossing the street.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      The blood on the street is not an act of God. It is a choice. Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking. Demand an end to policies that put cars first and people last. The dead cannot speak. You must.

      Take action now.

      Marmorato Opposes Safety‑Boosting Congestion Pricing Cash Grab
      ▸

      As congestion pricing begins, unions and politicians rage. They claim tolls hurt workers and raise costs. Facts show most commuters use transit. Fewer cars mean faster emergency response. The toll funds transit upgrades. The drama masks real safety gains for all.

      On January 3, 2025, public debate erupted as New York City prepared to activate congestion pricing in Manhattan. The measure, set to fund $15 billion in subway and rail improvements, drew fierce opposition from unions and Councilwoman Kristy Marmorato, who called it a 'cash grab' that would 'increase all costs in our daily lives.' The union for FDNY EMTs argued the toll would burden low-wage workers, but data shows 90% of commuters already use public transit. City officials, including mayoral spokesperson Liz Garcia, insisted emergency response would not suffer. The measure's summary notes that reducing car traffic will speed up emergency vehicles and protect passengers. The uproar highlights the tension between entrenched driving privileges and the urgent need to make streets safer for vulnerable road users.

      Int 0346-2024
      Marmorato votes no on jaywalking bill, opposing a proven safety improvement.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.

      ▸
