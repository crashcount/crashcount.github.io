      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 13?

      Blood on Pelham Parkway: Every Death Here Is a Policy Choice

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Death Count Keeps Rising

      In District 13, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, nine people have died and 2,606 have been injured in crashes. Thirty-five of those injuries were serious. Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians are among the dead. The streets do not forgive. Just days ago, a 31-year-old man was killed on Pelham Parkway when a driver turned left and struck his motorcycle. Police reported that “the Equinox driver stayed at the scene” and both were taken to the hospital, but only one survived (NY Daily News).

      Pedestrians are not spared. Last year, a 40-year-old man was killed by an SUV on Westchester Avenue. The crash report lists “Driver Inattention/Distraction” and “Driver Inexperience” as causes. He died in the street (NYC Open Data).

      Leadership: Votes, Silence, and Missed Chances

      Council Member Kristy Marmorato has taken some steps. She co-sponsored bills to add lighting to step streets and commercial corridors, a move that could help walkers at night. But when it mattered most, she voted no on laws that would have made medians safer and ended jaywalking enforcement—measures proven to protect the most vulnerable. She called congestion pricing a “cash grab,” ignoring the evidence that less traffic means fewer deaths (Streetsblog NYC).

      Parking, not people, gets priority. Marmorato fought to keep parking mandates near new Metro-North stations, saying, “We live in a transit desert where cars are a necessity for daily activities” (Streetsblog NYC). The result: more cars, more danger, less room for life.

      The Path Forward: No More Waiting

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a policy failure. The city has the power to lower speed limits, redesign streets, and end the reign of reckless drivers. The time for half-measures is over. Call Council Member Marmorato. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand safer crossings, protected bike lanes, and an end to policies that put cars over kids. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a choice. Change it.

      Int 1160-2025
      Marmorato votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.

      Int 0346-2024
      Marmorato votes no on jaywalking bill, opposing improved pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians now cross anywhere, any time. No summons. Law strips drivers of excuses. Streets shift. Power tilts to people on foot.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, rewrites New York’s pedestrian rules. The bill, heard by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passed on September 26, 2024, and became law on October 26, 2024. Its title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way.” Sponsored by Council Member Tiffany Cabán and co-sponsored by Narcisse, Won, Hanif, Hudson, and Restler, the law lets pedestrians cross streets anywhere, even against signals. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks. The Department of Transportation must educate all street users on new rights and responsibilities. The law removes a tool police used to target walkers. It shifts blame from people on foot to the system and those behind the wheel.
