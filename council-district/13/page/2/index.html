      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 13?

      Bronx River Parkway took two young riders. The system let the driver walk.

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 13: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Two men went down on the Bronx River Parkway before dawn. Police say a 21-year-old in a Mercedes tried to pass, clipped a Volkswagen, then hit two motorcycles. Both riders died. He was charged with vehicular manslaughter and DWI. He went home that day.

      “Two people were killed. He was drunk.” said a victim’s sister.

      “Police have arrested and charged a man with vehicular manslaughter and driving while intoxicated,” reported Gothamist.

      “He looks forward to [contesting the charges] in court,” his lawyer said.

      Names were released. Manuel Amarantepenalo, 19. Enrique Martinez, 21. Both from the Bronx. The highway closed for hours near Gun Hill Road. Families will carry this for years.

      —

      Where the blood pools

      District 13’s worst wounds run along the parkways and expressways. The Hutchinson River Parkway logged two deaths and 209 injuries. The Bruckner Expressway had one death and 336 injuries. Bronxdale Ave shows 16 injuries and seven serious. These are not quirks. They are patterns.

      Night hits hard here. Fatal and severe harm spikes at 4–8 a.m., 5–7 p.m., 8–11 p.m., and again near midnight, with deaths recorded at 4, 8, 10, 16, 17, 20, 21, and 23 hours in the data window. People keep getting hit when the light is thin and the lanes are wide.

      Across the district since 2022: 4 people killed and 1,084 injured in 1,512 crashes over the last 12 months tracked. Pedestrians: 4 dead, 384 hurt. Cyclists: 1 dead, 133 hurt. The bulk of harm falls on people outside the steel shell.

      —

      What’s driving it

      The city’s data groups most deaths and injuries under “other,” with 4 deaths, 603 injuries, 16 serious injuries. Distraction adds another death. Blown signals add a death. Inexperience adds a death. Failures to yield and speed show up with dozens more injuries.

      The machines that do the most damage are common ones. Sedans and SUVs account for most pedestrian harm: 143 pedestrian injuries and 2 pedestrian deaths from SUVs; 138 injuries and 1 death from sedans. Trucks killed one pedestrian. Heavy vehicles are not the only killers. Ordinary cars do enough.

      —

      The family’s question

      On the parkway case, the charges were filed. The driver was released without bail that day. A mother asked, “How could they let him go?” The DA said the investigation continues. The roadway reopened. Two funerals followed.

      —

      Hotspots that won’t cool

      Hutchinson River Parkway. Bruckner Expressway. Bronxdale Ave. East Tremont Avenue. These are the repeat scenes. People are ejected. People are pinned. Families wait at Jacobi and Montefiore.

      The hours after dark demand light and friction. Daylighting at corners. Longer walk leads. Hardened lefts where turns maim. Concrete, not paint. Targeted enforcement in the late‑night and rush windows listed above. Focus where the bodies fall.

      —

      Use the tools that work

      Speed is the blade. Albany handed the city the power to set safer limits. New Yorkers can demand a default 20 mph on local streets now. Cameras are funded through 2030. The worst repeat speeders need speed limiters before they kill again. These are live fights.

      Int 1105-2024
      Marmorato votes yes, boosting street safety transparency and project accountability.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.' Council Member Julie Won led sponsorship, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law demands DOT post annual and monthly updates on every project tied to the master plan—listing details, timelines, funding, and setbacks. This law brings sunlight to street safety work, making the city answer for every mile and missed deadline.

      Int 1160-2025
      Marmorato votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.

      Marmorato Opposes Safety‑Boosting Congestion Pricing Cash Grab
      ▸

      On January 3, 2025, public debate erupted as New York City prepared to activate congestion pricing in Manhattan. The measure, set to fund $15 billion in subway and rail improvements, drew fierce opposition from unions and Councilwoman Kristy Marmorato, who called it a 'cash grab' that would 'increase all costs in our daily lives.' The union for FDNY EMTs argued the toll would burden low-wage workers, but data shows 90% of commuters already use public transit. City officials, including mayoral spokesperson Liz Garcia, insisted emergency response would not suffer. The measure's summary notes that reducing car traffic will speed up emergency vehicles and protect passengers. The uproar highlights the tension between entrenched driving privileges and the urgent need to make streets safer for vulnerable road users.
