      No More Blood on District 16 Streets: Demand Real Action Now

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      District 16: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Toll in District 16

      Five dead. Six hundred fifty-eight injured. That is the count in District 16 over the last year. The numbers do not bleed, but the streets do. In the last twelve months, three people died in just the 35–44 age group. Seventy-eight children were hurt. The old and the young, all at risk.

      On July 11, a 79-year-old driver crashed into two cars and a pole. His passenger, Stella Nyarko-Dei, 71, died. Seven others were hurt. “I saw one lady was out on the ground. They was giving her medical attention, checking her body. She was laid out,” said Samuel Cherry. The cause is still under investigation. No arrests. No answers. Only loss.

      Patterns That Kill

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. In three years, they killed seven and injured over 350 people walking or biking. Trucks and buses killed one, injured dozens. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes hurt many more. The street is a gauntlet. The most vulnerable—children, elders, people on foot—pay the price.

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      Council Member Althea Stevens has voted for some safety bills: clearing abandoned cars, warning decals for taxis, more pavement markings. She co-sponsored a bill to give students cheaper bike share, and another to remove derelict vehicles fast. These are steps, but not leaps. No bold push for lower speeds, protected bike lanes, or real accountability for reckless drivers.

      The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not. The council can demand more. It has not. “It was a terrible sound – it was a terrible incident that happened,” said Jennifer. The sound echoes. The silence from leadership does too.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Council Member Stevens. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings and bike lanes. Demand action for the living, not memorials for the dead. The toll will not stop until the council does more than vote. It must fight.

      Call. Demand. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents local districts like District 16.
      ▸ Where does District 16 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 77 and state senate district SD 29. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in District 16?
      It includes the Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Concourse-Concourse Village, Highbridge, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Claremont Park, and Bronx CB4 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 77, AD 79, and AD 84, and State Senate Districts SD 29, SD 31, and SD 32. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 16?
      Cars and trucks killed 7 and injured over 350. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 20 injuries. Bikes caused 14 injuries, including one serious injury. Most deaths and injuries come from cars and SUVs. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not fate. They are the result of policy, street design, and enforcement. They can be prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, install protected bike lanes and crossings, and hold reckless drivers accountable. They can push for bold changes, not just small fixes.

      Gibson Urges Safety Boosting Washington Bridge Upgrades
      ▸

      Council Member Stevens and others urge DOT to fix the Washington Bridge. They want a two-way bike lane, wider walkways, better lights, and cameras. The bridge is old, narrow, and dark. Crossing is risky. They demand action to protect people.

      On September 22, 2022, Council Member Althea Stevens joined Manhattan Borough President Mark Levine, Bronx Borough President Vanessa Gibson, and Council Member Carmen de la Rosa in a joint letter to the Department of Transportation. The letter calls for urgent upgrades to the 133-year-old Washington Bridge. The officials demand a two-way protected bike lane, wider pedestrian paths, improved lighting, and safety cameras. The letter states: 'The city has done a terrific job of making wise investments in improving mobility on both sides of the Harlem River, but left the bridge with just two very narrow, poorly lit lanes for foot and bike traffic.' Stevens and her colleagues stress that the bridge is unsafe for people on foot and bike. They urge DOT to act, citing the need to reduce traffic deaths and make the bridge safe for all.
