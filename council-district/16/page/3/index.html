      Hit-and-Run, Run Amok: Demand Justice, Not More Graves

      Written August 8, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned citations, and ensured proper inline citation formatting.)

      District 16: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 8, 2025

      Death in the Night, Silence in the Day

      Just two nights ago, a woman tried to cross West 174th Street in Morris Heights. An SUV turned into a driveway and struck her. The driver did not stop. She died at St. Barnabas Hospital. Police are still searching for the person who left her in the road. A Bronx woman is dead after a hit-and-run in Morris Heights.

      This is not rare. In the last twelve months, District 16 saw 5 deaths and 777 injuries from crashes. Nine people were seriously hurt. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. The numbers do not stop. They only grow.

      The Pattern: Speed, Flight, and No Consequence

      Last year, a cab driver was killed at 77 miles per hour. The driver ran a red light, hit the cab, and fled. The cab was pushed seventy feet. The driver bled out in the street. Police said Williams managed to get out of the mangled SUV and stumble away, leaving Godwin to die.

      Most deaths come from cars and SUVs. In three years, 7 killed, 4 seriously hurt, 399 injured—all by cars and trucks. Bikes and mopeds hurt fewer, but the real threat is heavy and fast.

      Council Member Stevens: Steps and Stalls

      Council Member Althea Stevens has voted for some safety bills. She backed laws to remove abandoned cars, warn taxi passengers about cyclists, and decriminalize jaywalking. She co-sponsored a bill for discounted bike share for students. But she also signed on to bills that raise fines for e-mobility users and require e-bike registration—measures that burden the most vulnerable and do little to stop the real killers: speeding cars and reckless drivers.

      The council can do more. The city now has the power to set a 20 mph speed limit. It has not done so. The most dangerous drivers still keep their keys.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      Every day, another family loses someone. Every day, the council waits. Call Council Member Stevens. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people on foot and bike. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of residents in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 16 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 77, and state senate district SD 29. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 16?
      It includes the Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Concourse-Concourse Village, Highbridge, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Claremont Park, and Bronx CB4 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 77, AD 79, and AD 84, and State Senate Districts SD 29, SD 31, and SD 32. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 16?
      Cars and Trucks caused 7 deaths, 4 serious injuries, and 399 other injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused no deaths, 1 serious injury, and 13 other injuries. Bikes caused no deaths, 1 serious injury, and 13 other injuries. See NYC crash data.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. Lower speeds, safer street design, and better enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, pass laws to protect pedestrians and cyclists, and hold reckless drivers accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Stevens co-sponsors bill to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council orders swift removal of abandoned and unplated cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. Police target vehicles with missing or fake plates. Fewer hazards for those on foot and bike.

      Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on April 18, 2024.       Int 0606-2024
      Stevens co-sponsors e-bike registration bill, reducing overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council wants every e-bike and scooter tagged and tracked. Plates on wheels. Riders face new rules. Lawmakers say it’s about order. The bill sits in committee. Streets wait.

      Int 0606-2024 was introduced on March 7, 2024, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...requiring that every bicycle with electric assist, electric scooter and other legal motorized vehicle be licensed and registered," would force all e-bikes, e-scooters, and similar vehicles not covered by DMV rules to register with DOT and display visible plates. Council Member Inna Vernikov is the primary sponsor, joined by 26 co-sponsors including Holden, Farías, Riley, and others. The bill was laid over in committee and has not advanced.
