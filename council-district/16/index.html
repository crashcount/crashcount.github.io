      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 16?

      No More Names on the Asphalt: Demand Action Now

      Written July 24, 2025

      District 16: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 24, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Latest Toll

      In the last year, five people died and 762 were injured in District 16. Ten of those injuries were serious—lives changed in a heartbeat. The dead do not speak. The injured carry scars. Just weeks ago, a driver in a Ford Mustang failed a left turn at East 149th and Courtlandt. He mounted the curb and plowed into six people. He ran. The victims went to Lincoln Hospital. Police said they were in stable condition. The man who did it is still out there. “Cops are looking for a man who rammed into six people after botching a turn at an intersection in the Bronx,” NY Daily News reported.

      Last fall, a pedestrian fell at a Bronx gas station. The driver rolled over him, dragged him 950 feet, then stopped in a bike lane. She looked under her car. She left him there. The Bronx DA called it “egregious and show[ing] a lack of humanity.” “The defendant allegedly drove her car over the victim… then after looking under her vehicle… she allegedly drove away without reporting what happened,” said DA Darcel Clark.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. In three years, 18 people have died, 2,503 have been injured, and 28 have suffered serious injuries. Cars and SUVs killed seven. Trucks and buses killed one. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes left dozens more hurt. The numbers do not flinch. They do not grieve. They only grow.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Council Member Althea Stevens has voted for some safety bills. She backed laws to remove abandoned vehicles, add taxi dooring warnings, and decriminalize jaywalking. She co-sponsored a bill for discounted bike share for students. But Stevens also signed onto bills that raise fines and registration burdens for e-bikes—measures that fall hardest on the people who have the least and do the least harm. The council passed laws. The bodies keep coming.

      The Work Ahead

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every death is a choice made by those in power. Call Council Member Stevens. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people on foot and bike. Demand action before another name becomes a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees city agencies.
      ▸ Where does District 16 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 77 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 16?
      It includes the Claremont Village-Claremont (East), Concourse-Concourse Village, Highbridge, Mount Eden-Claremont (West), Yankee Stadium-Macombs Dam Park, Claremont Park, and Bronx CB4 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 77, AD 79, and AD 84, and State Senate Districts SD 29, SD 31, and SD 32.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 16?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm to pedestrians, with 7 deaths and 399 injuries. Trucks and buses were involved in 1 death and 32 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds injured 19 people. Bikes were involved in 14 injuries, including one serious injury. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not random. They are the result of choices—by drivers and by those who set the rules and design the streets.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, install speed cameras, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking instead of punishing them.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0857-2024
      Stevens votes yes to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council orders swift removal of abandoned, derelict cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. No plates, no stickers, no excuses. Police and sanitation must act. Safer crossings for all who walk, ride, or wait.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council on June 30, 2025. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to vehicles reported as abandoned to the department of sanitation," requires the Department of Sanitation to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours of report. The NYPD must tow cars lacking valid plates or stickers. Prime sponsor Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led, joined by Crystal Hudson, Amanda Farías, Lincoln Restler, and others. The law targets street hazards, clearing blocked sightlines and crosswalks. It aims to cut risks for pedestrians and cyclists by removing abandoned vehicles fast.

      Int 0193-2024, now law, cleared the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and passed City Council on May 1, 2025.       Int 1105-2024
      Stevens votes yes, boosting street safety transparency and project accountability.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.' Council Member Julie Won led sponsorship, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law demands DOT post annual and monthly updates on every project tied to the master plan—listing details, timelines, funding, and setbacks. This law brings sunlight to street safety work, making the city answer for every mile and missed deadline.
