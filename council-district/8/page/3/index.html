      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 8?

      Blood on the Road, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 28, 2025

      District 8: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 28, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      Seven dead. Over a thousand injured. In the last twelve months, District 8 has seen 1,065 people hurt and 7 killed in crashes. Twenty more were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same. Children, elders, workers—no one is spared. The numbers do not lie. The pain is not abstract.

      On January 26, Willow Hall tried to cross Harlem River Drive. He never made it. An unlicensed driver in a Nissan Rogue struck him down. The driver stayed at the scene, but it took police six months to bring charges. “Donald Pinnock was charged Sunday with aggravated unlicensed operation of a motor vehicle,” reported NY Daily News. The street was quiet again by sunrise. Hall was gone.

      Just weeks ago, a crash on the Hutchinson River Parkway left an 18-year-old woman fighting for her life. The moped driver fled. “Police said the moped driver sped away from the 3:45 a.m. crash… leaving the young woman to die on the road,” wrote NY Daily News. Six others were sent to the hospital. The road keeps moving.

      Who Pays, Who Acts

      SUVs and cars do the most harm. In District 8, they killed 5 people and injured 473 more. Trucks and buses killed 2. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 1. Bikes killed 1. The deadliest machines keep rolling. The most vulnerable keep paying.

      Council Member Diana Ayala has backed bills to daylight crosswalks, light step streets, and expand greenways. She voted yes to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the desperate and the poor. But the bleeding has not stopped. The bills are slow. The streets are not.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another family broken. Call Council Member Ayala. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand curb extensions, daylighting, and protected crossings. Demand action, not studies. The dead cannot wait. The living should not have to.

      Contact your Council Member and demand change now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 8 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 68 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 8?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, East Harlem (South), East Harlem (North), Randall’S Island, Bronx CB1, and Manhattan CB11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 68 and AD 84, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 30.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 8?
      SUVs and cars killed 5 people and injured 473 in District 8; trucks and buses killed 2 and injured 48; motorcycles and mopeds killed 1 and injured 23; bikes killed 1 and injured 41. (Source: NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Most deaths and injuries happen on streets that could be made safer with better design, lower speeds, and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and fund laws for lower speed limits, daylighting, curb extensions, protected bike lanes, and enforce against reckless driving.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
