      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 8?

      Bronx Streets Bleed—Council Must Act Before More Lives Are Lost

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, tightened prose, and cleaned citations.)

      District 8: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      A woman lies on Bartow Avenue. Paramedics kneel beside her. She does not get up. On July 11, a 79-year-old driver crashed into two cars and a pole in the Bronx. His passenger, Stella Nyarko-Dei, 71, died at Jacobi Hospital. Seven others were hurt. Police said, “The cause of the crash was not immediately known” according to NY Daily News. No arrests. No answers. The street is quiet again, but the loss remains.

      This is not rare. Since 2022, District 8 has seen 16 deaths and 57 serious injuries from crashes. Three children have died. More than 3,600 people have been hurt. The numbers do not stop. They only grow.

      The Machines That Kill

      The killers are not ghosts. They have names: SUVs, cars, trucks, bikes, mopeds. SUVs and cars alone caused five deaths and over 500 injuries to pedestrians. Trucks and buses killed two. Motorcycles and mopeds, two more. Even bikes have left bodies broken. The street does not care who you are. It only takes.

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      Council Member Diana Ayala has voted for some safety bills. She supported laws that force the city to remove abandoned cars, clear crosswalks, and track street safety projects on NYC Council Legistar. She co-sponsored a bill to curb repeat speeders. But the pace is slow. The deaths are not. In 2023, as bike riders died in record numbers, Ayala stood with advocates and said the city must “fulfill legal requirements to build safe streets” reported amny.com. Still, 94% of cyclist deaths happened on streets without protected lanes. The law is not enough if it stays on paper.

      The Call

      No more waiting. Call Council Member Ayala. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted crosswalks, and a 20 mph speed limit. Every day of delay is another name lost.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 8 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 68 and state senate district SD 29. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in District 8?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, East Harlem (South), East Harlem (North), Randall’S Island, Bronx CB1, and Manhattan CB11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 68 and AD 84, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 30. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 8?
      Cars and Trucks (SUVs, sedans, trucks, buses) caused most deaths and injuries: 5 deaths and over 500 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused 2 deaths and dozens of injuries. Bikes also caused injuries and at least 1 death.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not random acts of fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for protected bike lanes, daylighted crosswalks, lower speed limits, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can also demand faster action from city agencies.

      Int 1105-2024
      Ayala votes yes, boosting street safety transparency and project accountability.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.' Council Member Julie Won led sponsorship, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law demands DOT post annual and monthly updates on every project tied to the master plan—listing details, timelines, funding, and setbacks. This law brings sunlight to street safety work, making the city answer for every mile and missed deadline.

      Int 1160-2025
      Ayala votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.
