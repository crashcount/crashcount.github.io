      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 8?

      Seven Dead. Who’s Next?

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 29, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 8: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Blood on the Asphalt

      Seven dead. Eighteen left with injuries that will never heal. In the last year alone, District 8 saw 1,949 crashes. These are not just numbers—they are bodies on the street, families waiting in hospital halls. Last month, a 22-year-old passenger died on Southern Boulevard, crushed between a moped and a truck. In June, a child was killed near the RFK Bridge exit. The city keeps moving. The dead do not. NYC Open Data

      The Pattern That Never Breaks

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In the past three years, cars and trucks took five lives and left hundreds wounded. Motorcycles and mopeds killed two. Bikes killed one. Pedestrians are hit crossing, waiting, walking home. The sidewalk is not safe. The crosswalk is not safe. The numbers do not lie. The pain does not fade.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Council Member Diana Ayala has voted for laws to clear abandoned vehicles and open sightlines, making it harder for drivers to hide behind rusted metal and block the view of a child in the crosswalk. Ayala votes yes to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety. She co-sponsored bills to force the city to show its work on street safety, to track progress, and to shine light on delays. Ayala co-sponsors bill boosting street safety transparency and project accountability. She stood with others to demand the city build the bike lanes it promised, after another year when most cyclists died on streets with no protection. Ayala condemns city inaction amid deadly bike crashes

      But the pace is slow. The deaths keep coming. The city passes laws, but the blood does not dry. “They weren’t even in the bike lane, they were parked on the corner, fixing the light or the sign or something,” a witness said after a city worker was slashed by a cyclist in Lower Manhattan. The work is never done. The danger never leaves.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day without action is another day someone does not come home. Call Council Member Ayala. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes on every street. Demand that the city clear the crosswalks, daylight the corners, and end the silence. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of residents in each district. [NYC Council – Legistar]
      ▸ Where does District 8 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 68 and state senate district SD 29. [NYC Council – Legistar]
      ▸ Which areas are in District 8?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, East Harlem (South), East Harlem (North), Randall’S Island, Bronx CB1, and Manhattan CB11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 68 and AD 84, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 30. [NYC Council – Legistar]
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 8?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 5 deaths and hundreds of injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 2 deaths, and bikes caused 1 death. [NYC Open Data]
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Policies like lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, and clear crosswalks can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, clear crosswalks, and demand transparency and urgency from city agencies.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
