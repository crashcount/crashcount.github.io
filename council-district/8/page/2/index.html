      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 8?

      Sixteen Dead. Still No 20 MPH. How Many More, NYC?

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened FAQ citations, deduped and sorted citations.)

      District 8: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Human Cost

      In District 8, the street does not forgive. Sixteen people are dead. Fifty-seven more have suffered serious injuries since 2022. These are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. A child crushed on the Major Deegan. A man killed crossing Harlem River Drive. A moped passenger, age 22, dead on Bruckner Boulevard. The road keeps its silence. The city moves on.

      Last week, a Ford Mustang tore through Courtlandt Avenue and East 149th, hitting six people before smashing into scaffolding. The driver ran. The pain stayed. “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting,” said a witness. Six more bodies in the hospital. Six more families waiting for news that will not heal them.

      Patterns in the Wreckage

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. Since 2022, they have killed five people and injured hundreds more. Trucks and buses killed two. Motorcycles and mopeds, one each. Bikes, too, have left scars. The street does not care what you drive. It only cares how fast, how close, how blind.

      The Mustang crash is not rare. It is the city’s rhythm. “Once I heard the noise I turned around and it was already on the curb. It immediately backed up and fled,” said another witness. The ambulance came. The driver did not.

      Leadership: Progress and the Long Road

      Council Member Diana Ayala has signed on to bills that matter. She co-sponsored the push to ban parking near crosswalks, to light the city’s step streets, to expand curb extensions, and to legalize crossing the street outside the lines. She voted yes to the citywide greenway plan and to ending jaywalking tickets. These are steps. But the blood on the street says the steps are not enough.

      The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not. The city can redesign every deadly crossing. It has not. The city can end the reign of repeat speeders. It has not.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      Call Council Member Ayala. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand that every repeat speeder is stopped before they kill again.

      The street will not wait. Neither should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 8 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 68 and state senate district SD 29. Data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 8?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, East Harlem (South), East Harlem (North), Randall’S Island, Bronx CB1, and Manhattan CB11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 68 and AD 84, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 30. Data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 8?
      Cars and SUVs killed 5 people and injured hundreds. Trucks and buses killed 2. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 1 each. Bikes also caused deaths and injuries, but the greatest toll comes from cars and SUVs. Data from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not random—they are the result of choices and policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, ban parking near crosswalks, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can pass and enforce laws that protect people, not just cars.

      Fifth Avenue Redesign Shrinks Car Lanes
      ▸

      Fifth Avenue will lose car lanes. Sidewalks will grow. No new bike or bus lanes. Pedestrians gain space. Cyclists and bus riders get left out. The city spends big. Construction waits. Advocates say the plan misses the mark.

      The New York Post reported on May 21, 2025, that Mayor Eric Adams announced a $400 million redesign of Fifth Avenue. The plan widens sidewalks by 46% and cuts traffic lanes from five to three, aiming to boost pedestrian access. However, the redesign omits dedicated bus and bike lanes. Advocates criticized the move: 'Two mayors have now disregarded city workers' plans for a busway, throwing riders under the bus to favor luxury boutiques,' said Danny Pearlstein of Riders Alliance. Transportation Alternatives called the plan a failure, noting Fifth Avenue's slow bus speeds and lack of cycling infrastructure. The project highlights ongoing policy choices that shape street safety and access for vulnerable road users.

      Helicopter Breaks Apart Over Hudson River
      ▸

      Six died when a sightseeing helicopter shattered midair over the Hudson. The fuselage, rotor, and tail tore loose. Loud bangs echoed. The craft plunged. No black box. No warning. Only fragments and silence left behind.

      NY Daily News reported on May 7, 2025, that federal officials released images showing a sightseeing helicopter breaking apart in midair before crashing into the Hudson River, killing six. The National Transportation Safety Board’s preliminary report details how the Bell 206L-4 split into three pieces: 'the fuselage, the main rotor system, and the tail boom.' Witnesses heard 'several loud 'bangs'' before the breakup. The helicopter had flown eight tours that day, all with the same pilot, who had less than 50 hours in this model. The aircraft had a prior maintenance issue with its transmission assembly and lacked flight data recorders. The NTSB noted the pilot wore video-capable sunglasses, but they remain missing. The crash highlights gaps in oversight and the risks of repeated tour flights without robust recording or inspection requirements.
