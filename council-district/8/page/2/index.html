      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 8?

      Sixteen Dead. Thousands Wounded. Who Will Stop the Killing on Our Streets?

      Written July 12, 2025

      District 8: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      The Blood on the Asphalt

      In District 8, the numbers do not lie. Sixteen people killed. Over 3,600 injured. Fifty-seven left with wounds so deep they are called serious. These are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. They are the people who walk, ride, and cross our streets. In the last year alone, seven died and 1,180 were hurt. The disaster does not slow.

      Just last week, a 71-year-old woman was killed in Co-op City. Six more were hurt. “One lady was out on the ground. They was giving her medical attention, checking her body. She was laid out,” said Samuel Cherry. The pain is not abstract. It is a body on the pavement, sirens in the morning, a family waiting for news that will break them.

      At East 149th and Courtlandt, a Mustang jumped the curb and hit six people. “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting,” said Vivian Cole. The driver ran. The city did not.

      Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and cars did the most harm. Five deaths, 342 minor injuries, 146 moderate, 11 serious. Trucks and buses killed two, left 24 with minor wounds, 18 moderate, four serious. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes took lives too. No one is safe. The youngest and oldest are not spared. Children under 18: one dead, 282 injured. Seniors: four killed, 205 injured.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Diana Ayala has signed her name to bills that matter. She voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed the dead for their own deaths. She co-sponsored laws to ban parking near crosswalks, light up step streets, and expand greenways. These are steps. But the blood keeps flowing. The city has the power to lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not. The most dangerous drivers are still on the road.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Council Member Ayala. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action against repeat speeders. Join Families for Safe Streets and Transportation Alternatives. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 8 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 68 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 8?
      It includes the Mott Haven-Port Morris, East Harlem (South), East Harlem (North), Randall’S Island, Bronx CB1, and Manhattan CB11 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 68 and AD 84, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 30.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 8?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm: 5 deaths, 342 minor injuries, 146 moderate, and 11 serious. Trucks and buses caused 2 deaths, 24 minor injuries, 18 moderate, and 4 serious. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 2 deaths, 17 minor injuries, 5 moderate, and 1 serious. Bikes were involved in 1 death, 22 minor injuries, 17 moderate, and 2 serious injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Policies like lower speed limits, better street design, and action against repeat offenders can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, safer street design, and stronger penalties for repeat dangerous drivers. They can also fund infrastructure that protects people walking and biking.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Queensboro Bridge Splits Paths For Safety
      ▸

      Cyclists and walkers get space at last. Years of crowding end. The bridge’s narrow lane forced conflict. Now, riders and pedestrians move apart. The city takes a lane from cars. The danger shrinks. The span breathes easier.

      amNY reported on May 13, 2025, that New York City will separate cyclists and pedestrians on the Ed Koch-Queensboro Bridge. Starting May 18, the north outer roadway becomes bike-only, while the south outer roadway, once for vehicles, opens to pedestrians. The article notes, “Advocates have fought to open a separate pedestrian path, citing dangerous overcrowding that has led to conflicts among cyclists, pedestrians and micromobility users.” The bridge was the last city-owned East River crossing without split paths. Manhattan Community Board 6 urged the city to act, pressing DOT to open the path despite construction delays. The change doubles space for non-drivers and removes a vehicle lane, addressing years of systemic risk from forced mixing of vulnerable users.

      Helicopter Breaks Apart Over Hudson River
      ▸

      Six died when a sightseeing helicopter shattered midair over the Hudson. The fuselage, rotor, and tail tore loose. Loud bangs echoed. The craft plunged. No black box. No warning. Only fragments and silence left behind.

      NY Daily News reported on May 7, 2025, that federal officials released images showing a sightseeing helicopter breaking apart in midair before crashing into the Hudson River, killing six. The National Transportation Safety Board’s preliminary report details how the Bell 206L-4 split into three pieces: 'the fuselage, the main rotor system, and the tail boom.' Witnesses heard 'several loud 'bangs'' before the breakup. The helicopter had flown eight tours that day, all with the same pilot, who had less than 50 hours in this model. The aircraft had a prior maintenance issue with its transmission assembly and lacked flight data recorders. The NTSB noted the pilot wore video-capable sunglasses, but they remain missing. The crash highlights gaps in oversight and the risks of repeated tour flights without robust recording or inspection requirements.
