      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 8?

      No More Names on Asphalt

      Written June 22, 2025 (Revised June 22, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      District 8: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 22, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      A man steps onto the RFK Bridge. A car hits him. The driver flees. The man dies on the pavement. Police say the car did not stop. No arrests. ABC7 reported the facts. No names. Only loss.

      A cyclist rides in the Bronx. An MTA bus turns. The cyclist is crushed. Dead at the scene. The driver stays. No charges. The city moves on. Police are investigating two fatal crashes in the Bronx and Queens. The words are cold. The street is colder.

      In the last twelve months, 7 people died in District 8 traffic crashes. 20 more suffered serious injuries. Over a thousand were hurt. The numbers do not bleed, but people do.

      The Machines That Kill

      SUVs killed more than any other vehicle. In three years, SUVs and cars took five lives and left hundreds wounded. Trucks and buses killed two. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes each killed one. The dead do not care what hit them. The living remember.

      What Has Been Done

      Council Member Diana Ayala has voted for laws to open streets to people. She backed the bill that ended jaywalking enforcement, letting pedestrians cross where they need to. She co-sponsored bills for curb extensions, speed humps, and better lighting on city stairs. She joined the push to ban parking near crosswalks, to clear sightlines for children and elders. But the pace is slow. The danger is fast.

      The Call That Must Be Made

      Every day, the city waits. Every day, another body. Call Council Member Ayala. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted crosswalks. Demand action before the next name is just a number. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 8?
      District 8 covers Mott Haven-Port Morris, East Harlem (South), Randall’S Island, Bronx CB1, and Manhattan CB11. It also overlaps Assembly Districts AD 68 and AD 84, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 30.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 8?
      SUVs and cars caused the most deaths and injuries to pedestrians in District 8, with 5 killed and hundreds hurt. Trucks and buses killed 2. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes each killed 1.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents. Safer street design, lower speeds, and enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass laws for lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, ban parking near crosswalks, and demand enforcement against reckless driving.
      ▸ What has Council Member Diana Ayala done about traffic violence?
      She has voted for laws to legalize safer pedestrian crossings, co-sponsored bills for curb extensions, speed humps, better lighting, and supported banning parking near crosswalks.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1138-2024
      Ayala co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1138-2024 would ban parking and standing within 20 feet of crosswalks. It forces the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections each year. The bill sits in committee. Sponsors include Bottcher, Won, and the Public Advocate.

      Int 1138-2024, introduced December 5, 2024, is under review by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill is 'Laid Over in Committee.' Its title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.' Council Member Erik D. Bottcher is the primary sponsor, joined by Julie Won, Jumaane Williams, and others. The bill prohibits standing or parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and mandates the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections per year. The law also requires citywide outreach and reporting. This measure aims to keep sightlines clear at crossings, a known danger zone for people on foot and bike. The bill has not yet received a vote.

      Int 0346-2024
      Ayala votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.
