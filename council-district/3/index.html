      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      No More Names: Demand Action Before the Next Death

      Written July 13, 2025

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt

      In District 3, the numbers do not lie. Nineteen people are dead. Fifty-nine suffered serious injuries. In the past year alone, two more lives were lost and nearly 850 were hurt. The streets do not forgive. A man, 34, struck and killed by an SUV at 7th Avenue and West 36th. A 35-year-old, crushed by a garbage truck on Cornelia Street. A pedestrian, 39, killed by a box truck on West 40th. Each death leaves a hole. Each injury, a scar that does not fade.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and trucks do most of the damage. They killed eight, injured hundreds, and left dozens with wounds that will never heal. Bikes, mopeds, and motorcycles add to the toll, but the weight of death rides on four wheels. Trucks and SUVs, always bigger, always faster, always there at the moment of impact. The city’s own data shows the pattern: the larger the vehicle, the greater the harm (NYC Open Data).

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has moved on daylighting, sponsoring a bill to ban parking near crosswalks and keep sightlines clear. The bill would force the city to install barriers at a thousand intersections a year, but it sits in committee, waiting for a vote (NYC Council – Legistar). Bottcher also voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the desperate and the slow (NYC Council – Legistar). He has backed the SAFE Streets Act, a package aimed at protecting people on foot and bike. But the pace is slow. The blood dries before the ink does.

      The Words of the Wounded

      After a bus crash at Port Authority, Deputy Chief Jason Saffon said, “One bus made contact with the rear of the other bus.” Fourteen people were hurt. Emergency crews struggled to reach the scene. The ramp was blocked, the city unprepared. In another crash, Saffon said, “It required some extrication, meaning they had to use some equipment to free the patient.” The machines do not care who they trap.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day the city waits, another life is at risk. Call Council Member Bottcher. Demand a vote on daylighting. Demand safer speeds. Demand streets where a child can cross without fear. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district. [NYC Council – Legistar]
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27. [NYC Council – Legistar]
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47. [NYC Council – Legistar]
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 8 deaths and hundreds injured. Motorcycles and mopeds, as well as bikes, also contributed to injuries and a smaller share of deaths. [NYC Open Data]
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. The pattern is clear: better street design, lower speeds, and enforcement save lives. Policy can prevent these deaths.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws that slow traffic, redesign dangerous intersections, and prioritize people over cars. They can push for daylighting, protected bike lanes, and automated enforcement.

      ▸
