      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      Bodies in the Crosswalk. How Many More Before City Hall Moves?

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Human Cost

      Nineteen people are dead. Fifty-nine are left with serious injuries. In just over three years, District 3 has seen 6,073 crashes. The numbers are blunt, but the pain is sharp. A man, 34, struck and killed at 7th Avenue and West 36th by an SUV. A 35-year-old, crushed by a garbage truck on Cornelia Street. The dead do not speak. Their absence is everywhere.

      Recent Wreckage: No End in Sight

      The carnage does not slow. In the last year alone, two more lives lost, 848 injured, 23 left with wounds that will not heal. The bodies pile up: pedestrians, cyclists, children. The city records show the same pattern—SUVs, trucks, taxis, bikes, and buses all leave bodies in their wake. The most vulnerable pay the highest price. “One bus made contact with the rear of the other bus,” said FDNY Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. Fourteen injured in a single crash. The ramp was blocked. The ambulances had to drive the wrong way to reach the wounded.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has signed his name to bills that matter. He sponsored a law to ban parking near crosswalks, to clear sightlines for people on foot. He voted to legalize jaywalking, stripping away a law that punished the vulnerable. He called for public reporting on drivers who cover their plates, saying, “drivers are illegally concealing, obscuring, and defacing their license plates to continue to speed with impunity.”

      But the blood keeps flowing. Laws are passed, but the street does not forgive delay. Every day without action is another day someone does not come home.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Call Bottcher. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for every crosswalk and bike lane. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm to pedestrians in District 3, with 4 deaths and 18 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 3 serious injuries but no deaths. Bikes were involved in 3 serious injuries and no deaths. Source: NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not just accidents—they are the result of choices and policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, safer crossings, protected bike lanes, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can also push for better data and transparency.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
