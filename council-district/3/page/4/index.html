      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      Death Is on the Ballot: Demand Safer Streets Now

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      The Death Count Rises, The Streets Stay the Same

      In District 3, the numbers do not lie. Nineteen people have died on these streets since 2022. More than 2,500 have been injured. Fifty-seven were hurt so badly they may never walk the same again. The dead include a 34-year-old man struck by an SUV at 7th Avenue and West 36th Street, killed by a driver who was not paying attention. A 35-year-old was crushed by a garbage truck on Cornelia Street. Another man, 39, died under the wheels of a box truck on West 40th. These are not accidents. These are preventable deaths.

      Broken Bodies, Broken Promises

      The carnage is relentless. Cars and trucks do the most damage: 542 crashes, 16 serious injuries, 4 deaths. Bikes and mopeds are not blameless—106 crashes, 3 serious injuries—but the weight of steel and speed kills more. The numbers are cold, but the pain is not. A pedestrian thrown to the pavement. A cyclist left bleeding at the curb. The city calls it Vision Zero. The bodies say otherwise.

      What Has Bottcher Done?

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has taken some steps. He sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, aiming to clear sightlines for people on foot. He voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the desperate and the poor. Bottcher also pushed for more protected bike lanes and transparency on drivers who hide their plates to dodge speed cameras. But the pace is slow. The danger is not.

      The Words of the Wounded

      After a bus crash at Port Authority, Deputy Chief Jason Saffon said, “One bus made contact with the rear of the other bus” and “It required some extrication, meaning they had to use some equipment to free the patient.” These are the words of a city used to blood on the street. After a car fire shut the Lincoln Tunnel, a commuter wrote: “over an hour delay stuck in the Lincoln tunnel while a car was on fire.” The city moves on. The pain lingers.

      Call to Action: Demand More, Set the Pace

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Council Member Bottcher. Tell him to fight for a 20 mph speed limit, more daylighted intersections, and real enforcement against reckless drivers. Join the groups that refuse to accept another body in the road. The dead cannot speak. You can.

      Bill Int 0541-2024 sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced March 7, 2024, it seeks to ban moving billboards in New York City.       Bottcher co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by deterring hazardous vehicle obstruction.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants within half a mile of schools. Fines set at $175. Civilians can report violations. DOT must act on complaints. Council aims to clear paths for people, not cars.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after its introduction on February 8, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations," creates a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of a school.
