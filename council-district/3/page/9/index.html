      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      Nineteen Dead, Hundreds Broken—City Hall Stalls, Blood Flows

      Written July 4, 2025

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 4, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In District 3, the numbers do not lie. Nineteen people killed. Fifty-seven left with serious injuries. Two thousand five hundred and one injured since 2022. The streets run through the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’s Kitchen, and Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square. The wounds are fresh. In May, a 34-year-old man was killed at the intersection of 7th Avenue and West 36th Street by an SUV. The cause: driver inattention. In April, a 39-year-old pedestrian was crushed by a box truck on West 40th Street. In May, a garbage truck backed over a 35-year-old man on Cornelia Street. These are not accidents. They are the cost of inaction.

      The Human Cost

      A dozen people hurt when buses collided at Port Authority just this July. Fourteen more treated for injuries. One driver trapped, pulled out by emergency crews. The terminal shut down for hours. The news reported, “Port Authority Bus Terminal is closed and 14 people are being treated for non-life-threatening injuries, authorities said.”

      On the street, the story is the same. Trucks, SUVs, taxis, bikes. The city counts the bodies. The families count the days since.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Stalled

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has backed bills to clear sightlines at crosswalks, expand protected bike lanes, and end the criminalization of jaywalking. He sponsored a bill to ban parking within 20 feet of crosswalks, aiming to daylight 1,000 intersections a year. He voted yes to legalize jaywalking, shifting blame away from pedestrians. He praised the new protected bike lane on Sixth Avenue, saying, “By completing the Sixth Avenue bike lane, we are not only improving the safety of cyclists but also enhancing the overall experience for pedestrians and drivers alike.”

      But the pace is slow. The danger is not. The city redesigns intersections, but the death toll does not fall fast enough. Every day without change is another day of risk.

      The Next Fight

      Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every intersection. Demand that every bill moves, every promise kept. The blood on the street is not washed away by words. Only action will do.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      District 3 belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm to pedestrians in District 3, with 4 deaths and 16 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 3 serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 3 serious injuries but no deaths. Source: NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. Calling them ‘accidents’ hides the fact that action can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, daylight intersections, expand protected bike lanes, and hold city agencies accountable for street redesigns.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Bottcher Demands Safety Boosting Protected Bike Lanes on West Side
      ▸

      Kwok Kwan, an e-cyclist, died after a taxi passenger doored him on 11th Avenue. No summons issued. The strip is notorious for crashes. Council Member Bottcher and Senator Hoylman rallied for protected bike lanes. The city’s deadly streets claim more lives.

      On February 16, 2022, e-bike rider Kwok Kwan died after being doored by a taxi passenger on Manhattan’s 11th Avenue near 37th Street. No summons was issued to the driver or passenger for the illegal act. The area, plagued by 913 crashes in three years, has injured dozens of cyclists and pedestrians. Council Member Erik Bottcher and State Senator Brad Hoylman responded by rallying with advocacy groups, demanding protected bike lanes on 10th and 11th avenues. Hoylman tweeted, 'The City must make the streets of the West Side safer!' The Hudson River Greenway, a nearby bike path, bans legal e-bikes, forcing riders like Kwan onto dangerous streets. This was the city’s first cyclist death of 2022, amid a rising toll of road fatalities.

      Res 0002-2022
      Bottcher co-sponsors resolution to expand MTA on-demand paratransit pilot.
      ▸

      City Council calls for Albany to extend and expand the MTA On-Demand E-Hail Paratransit Pilot. The resolution demands fare parity, no ride caps, and equal service for disabled New Yorkers. Lawmakers say current limits are unjust and restrict mobility.

      Resolution 0002-2022, filed at session's end, came before the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on February 10, 2022. The measure urges passage of S.4037/A.5896, which would 'extend and expand the scope of the MTA On-Demand E-Hail Paratransit Pilot Program.' Council Member Justin L. Brannan led sponsorship, joined by Ayala, Menin, Louis, Hanif, Hudson, Bottcher, Farías, Brooks-Powers, and Brewer. The resolution slams service caps and fare surcharges, calling them 'inequitable and unjust.' It demands that paratransit users get the same fare, hours, and ride freedom as subway and bus riders. The bill would end rationing and financial barriers for disabled New Yorkers who rely on Access-A-Ride. The Council’s action highlights the systemic barriers faced by vulnerable road users and presses for equal, unrestricted access.

      Bottcher Backs Safety Boosting Bike Lanes and Enforcement
      ▸

      Council Member Bottcher calls out deadly conditions on 10th and 11th Avenues. He urges DOT to install bike lanes, refuge islands, and crack down on illegal parking. Streets remain wide, fast, and perilous for those outside cars. Bottcher wants action now.

      On February 3, 2022, Manhattan Council Member Erik Bottcher demanded urgent safety upgrades for 10th and 11th Avenues. The matter, titled 'Manhattan Council Member Demands Safety Improvements on 10th and 11th Aves,' highlights the lack of protection for pedestrians, cyclists, and transit riders. Bottcher, speaking before DOT and backed by advocates and community boards, described the avenues as 'four-lane highways' with no safe infrastructure. He called for protected bike lanes, pedestrian refuge islands, and strict enforcement against illegal parking in loading zones. Bottcher shared his own near-miss while biking, saying, 'I was inches from the wheels of an 18-wheel truck.' DOT acknowledged his push and promised to prioritize safety. Bottcher stressed the need for resources to fix dangerous intersections and pressed the city to act before more lives are lost.
