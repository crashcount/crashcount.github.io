      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      District 3 Bleeds—Will Bottcher Stop the Killing?

      Written August 6, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, deduped and sorted citations, clarified FAQ vehicle breakdown.)

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 6, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      In District 3, the street does not forgive. Since 2022, 19 people have died and 2,729 have been injured in crashes. Sixty-three were left with serious injuries—lives changed in a blink. The dead do not speak. The injured carry the story in scars and limps.

      Just last week, a 65-year-old e-bike rider was struck on Second Avenue. The driver fled, drove onto the sidewalk, and later told police he ran because he had no license. The cyclist was left with critical head trauma. The paper said, “He now faces charges of leaving the scene of an accident that caused serious injury and driving without a license” (West Side Spirit).

      On Cornelia Street, a truck backed over a 35-year-old man. On West 36th, a distracted SUV driver killed a pedestrian at the intersection. The numbers pile up, but the pain is sharp and local.

      The Shape of Danger

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm: 4 deaths, 352 minor injuries, 210 moderate, 20 serious. Trucks and buses killed 3, with 42 minor and 40 moderate injuries. Bikes injured 116 people—no deaths, but pain all the same. Motorcycles and mopeds left dozens hurt. The street is a gauntlet, and the most vulnerable—walkers, cyclists, the old—pay the highest price.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has backed bills to clear abandoned cars, speed up pavement markings, and require daylighting at crosswalks—all steps to clear sightlines and slow the street’s violence. He voted to decriminalize jaywalking, a move to stop blaming the dead for their own deaths. He co-sponsored a bill to force the city to report on drivers who cover their plates to dodge speed cameras, saying, “drivers are illegally concealing, obscuring, and defacing their license plates to continue to speed with impunity” (Streetsblog NYC).

      But the pace is slow. Bills stall. Promises fade. The street keeps killing.

      Call to Action: Demand More, Demand Now

      Contact Council Member Bottcher. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted corners, and real enforcement against reckless drivers. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Find your Council Member and act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each council district.
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 4 deaths and hundreds of injuries. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes also injured dozens, but cars and trucks remain the deadliest threat. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents. Most are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, daylight intersections, and enforce laws against reckless driving. They can pass and fund bills that put safety first.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Helicopter Breaks Apart Over Hudson River
      ▸

      Six died when a sightseeing helicopter shattered midair over the Hudson. The fuselage, rotor, and tail tore loose. Loud bangs echoed. The craft plunged. No black box. No warning. Only fragments and silence left behind.

      NY Daily News reported on May 7, 2025, that federal officials released images showing a sightseeing helicopter breaking apart in midair before crashing into the Hudson River, killing six. The National Transportation Safety Board’s preliminary report details how the Bell 206L-4 split into three pieces: 'the fuselage, the main rotor system, and the tail boom.' The crash highlights gaps in oversight and the risks of repeated tour flights without robust recording or inspection requirements.

      Int 0193-2024
      Bottcher votes yes on taxi dooring warning decals, no major safety change.
      ▸

      Council passes law. Taxis and for-hire cars must post bold warnings on rear doors. Riders face the message: look for cyclists before swinging the door. A move to cut dooring. City acts. Cyclists stay in the crosshairs.

      Int 0193-2024, now law, cleared the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and passed City Council on May 1, 2025. The bill, titled 'A Local Law...requiring taxis and for-hire vehicles to display a decal warning passengers to look for cyclists when opening the door,' demands clear warning signs on all rear passenger doors. Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Gutiérrez, Hudson, Rivera, Won, and others. The Taxi and Limousine Commission must provide decals at no cost. The law aims to fight dooring, a threat to cyclists citywide. Enacted May 31, 2025, it takes effect in 120 days.

      Int 1233-2025
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill to require vegetated medians, boosting cyclist and pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council bill orders trees and plants on new medians between bike lanes and car traffic. Concrete gets green. Barriers grow roots. The city must build for life, not speed.

      Int 1233-2025 sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced April 10, 2025, it demands new medians between bicycle lanes and motor vehicles be built for trees and vegetation, pending feasibility. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code... in relation to the planting of vegetation on new medians separating bicycle lanes from motorized vehicle traffic.” Council Member Shekar Krishnan leads, joined by Bottcher, Ossé, Brannan, Hanif, and Brooks-Powers. They push the city to plant, or allow planting, in every new median. The law would take effect 120 days after passage. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Int 1105-2024
      Bottcher votes yes, boosting street safety transparency and project accountability.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.' Council Member Julie Won led sponsorship, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law demands DOT post annual and monthly updates on every project tied to the master plan—listing details, timelines, funding, and setbacks. This law brings sunlight to street safety work, making the city answer for every mile and missed deadline.
