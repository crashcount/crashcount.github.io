      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      Nineteen Dead, Thousands Hurt—Who’s Stalling Safety on District 3 Streets?

      Written July 12, 2025

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 12, 2025

      The Numbers Don’t Lie

      Nineteen people have died on District 3 streets since 2022. Another 2,590 have been injured. Fifty-nine suffered wounds so grave they may never walk the same. Each number is a body. Each body is a life cut or changed. The dead do not speak, but the numbers do.

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Trucks and SUVs killed or maimed more than any other vehicle. Cars, cabs, bikes, mopeds—all play their part. The street is a gauntlet. No one is safe.

      Recent Crashes: The Slow Grind of Loss

      The carnage does not stop. In April, a box truck crushed a man on West 40th. In May, an SUV struck down a pedestrian at 36th and 7th. A garbage truck backed over a man on Cornelia Street. Each crash is a headline, then a memory, then a scar.

      “One bus made contact with the rear of the other bus,” said an FDNY chief after a crash at Port Authority that left fourteen hurt. Emergency crews had to fight through blocked ramps to reach the wounded. “We worked with the Port Authority police to open up the exit ramp so we could go up the wrong way and get our ambulances up there.” ABC7.

      Leadership: Promises, Laws, and the Waiting

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has backed bills to clear sightlines at crosswalks, legalize jaywalking, and demand more protected bike lanes. He sponsored a law to ban parking near crosswalks, a move that could save lives but still sits in committee. Bottcher voted yes to end jaywalking enforcement, shifting blame off pedestrians and onto the street itself. He has called for public reporting on drivers who cover their plates, exposing those who dodge the law. But the pace is slow. The blood dries before the ink does.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another body in the road. Call Bottcher. Demand he push his own bills to a vote. Demand the city drop the speed limit to 20 mph. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand action, not words. The dead can’t wait. Neither should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body, responsible for passing local laws and overseeing city agencies. Council Members represent districts and vote on bills that shape how the city runs.
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      SUVs and cars caused the most harm to pedestrians: 4 deaths, 337 minor injuries, 204 moderate injuries, and 18 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 3 deaths, 42 minor, 36 moderate, and 4 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds accounted for 0 deaths but 24 minor, 16 moderate, and 3 serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 0 deaths, 54 minor, 51 moderate, and 3 serious injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. Calling them ‘accidents’ hides the fact that policy choices decide who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, clear crosswalks, and hold reckless drivers accountable. They can also push city agencies to act faster and report on progress.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Helicopter Breaks Apart Over Hudson River
      ▸

      Six died when a sightseeing helicopter shattered midair over the Hudson. The fuselage, rotor, and tail tore loose. Loud bangs echoed. The craft plunged. No black box. No warning. Only fragments and silence left behind.

      NY Daily News reported on May 7, 2025, that federal officials released images showing a sightseeing helicopter breaking apart in midair before crashing into the Hudson River, killing six. The National Transportation Safety Board’s preliminary report details how the Bell 206L-4 split into three pieces: 'the fuselage, the main rotor system, and the tail boom.' Witnesses heard 'several loud 'bangs'' before the breakup. The helicopter had flown eight tours that day, all with the same pilot, who had less than 50 hours in this model. The aircraft had a prior maintenance issue with its transmission assembly and lacked flight data recorders. The NTSB noted the pilot wore video-capable sunglasses, but they remain missing. The crash highlights gaps in oversight and the risks of repeated tour flights without robust recording or inspection requirements.

      Int 1233-2025
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill for vegetated bike lane medians, boosting cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council bill orders new medians between bike lanes and car traffic to hold trees and plants. Parks and Transportation must decide what’s possible, then plant. The law aims to shield cyclists, break up the street, and cool the city’s hard edges.

      Int 1233-2025, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on April 10, 2025. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the planting of vegetation on new medians separating bicycle lanes from motorized vehicle traffic.” Council Member Shekar Krishnan leads as primary sponsor, joined by Bottcher, Ossé, Brannan, Hanif, and Brooks-Powers. Their action: referral to committee. The bill requires the Department of Transportation to build new medians between bike lanes and car lanes that can support trees and other vegetation, subject to feasibility. Parks and Recreation must plant or allow planting in these medians. The move promises a harder barrier between cyclists and cars, with green infrastructure as a shield.
