      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      No More Names on the Asphalt

      Written July 3, 2025

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 3, 2025

      The Blood on the Asphalt

      In District 3, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, nineteen people have died in crashes. Fifty-seven suffered serious injuries. Two thousand five hundred and one were hurt. These are not just numbers. They are lives ended, bodies broken, families left with empty chairs.

      The past year brought no relief. In the last twelve months, two more deaths. Twenty-two more with injuries so grave they may never walk the same. Seven hundred seventy-eight hurt. The wounds keep coming, slow and steady, like the traffic that never stops.

      Recent Crashes: The Cost of Delay

      Just weeks ago, a taxi struck a pedestrian at West 51st and 8th. In April, a box truck killed a man on West 40th. In May, a sedan hit a pedestrian at Bleecker. The pattern is clear. Most victims are on foot. Most drivers walk away.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has backed bills to clear sightlines at crosswalks, expand protected bike lanes, and legalize safe crossings for pedestrians. He voted yes to end jaywalking enforcement, shifting blame off those on foot and onto the system that fails them. “By completing the Sixth Avenue bike lane, we are not only improving the safety of cyclists but also enhancing the overall experience for pedestrians and drivers alike,” Bottcher said. He called for more space for people, less for cars. But the pace is slow. Paint fades. Concrete takes years.

      The bills are on the table. Some are stuck in committee. Some are law, but not yet real on the street. The danger remains.

      The Call: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      Every day of delay is another day of risk. Call Council Member Bottcher. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand protected space for every person on foot or bike. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district. Source
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27. Source
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      Cars and trucks were involved in 541 pedestrian injuries and deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 42. Bikes were involved in 103. Source
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable, or just accidents?
      Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not just accidents—they are the result of choices and policies. Source
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, daylighted intersections, and hold city agencies accountable for slow progress. Source

      Int 0143-2024
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill offering rewards for hit-and-run tips.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 0143-2024 would pay up to $1,000 for tips that help catch hit-and-run drivers who injure or kill. The bill targets the silence that lets drivers vanish. It aims to bring justice for victims left bleeding in the street.

      Int 0143-2024, now in the Committee on Public Safety, was introduced on February 28, 2024. The bill seeks to amend city law to "establish a reward for individuals who provide information leading to the apprehension, prosecution or conviction of a person who seriously injures or kills another individual in a hit-and-run accident." Council Member Rita C. Joseph leads as primary sponsor, joined by Brooks-Powers, Narcisse, Vernikov, Louis, Bottcher, Hudson, Gennaro, and Williams. The bill authorizes the mayor, on the police commissioner's recommendation, to offer up to $1,000 for actionable tips. Law enforcement and city or state employees are excluded. The bill responds to the grim fact that many hit-and-runs go unsolved, leaving victims and families without answers. It aims to break the code of silence and help bring reckless drivers to justice.

      Int 0227-2024
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill to ban commercial vehicle street parking, boosting safety.
      ▸

      Council bill targets auto shops, rentals, and gas stations clogging city streets with business vehicles. Fines run $250 to $400 per day. Impoundment possible. Exempts car dealerships. Bill sits in committee. Streets may clear, but danger remains for those on foot and bike.

      Int 0227-2024, now before the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on February 28, 2024. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting certain commercial establishments from parking vehicles on city streets,' cracks down on auto body shops, mechanics, car rental businesses, and gas stations that use public streets for business parking. The law aims to reclaim curb space, but the threat to pedestrians and cyclists from double-parked and idling vehicles persists. The bill awaits further action.

      Int 0080-2024
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by deterring hazardous vehicle obstruction.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants within half a mile of schools. Fines set at $175. Civilians can report violations. DOT must act on complaints. Council aims to clear paths for people, not cars.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after its introduction on February 8, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations," creates a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of a school. The penalty is $175 per violation. The Department of Transportation must launch a civilian reporting program. If DOT prosecutes a case using civilian evidence, the complainant gets 25% of the proceeds. Council Member Carlina Rivera leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Joseph, Menin, Nurse, Hudson, Brannan, Farías, Brewer, Salaam, Hanif, Avilés, Won, Bottcher, Krishnan, Gutiérrez, Marte, and the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill aims to keep streets clear for vulnerable road users, especially near schools.
