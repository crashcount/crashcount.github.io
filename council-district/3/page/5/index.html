      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      Blood on the Crosswalks: How Many More Will Die Before Bottcher Acts?

      Written July 7, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations.)

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In District 3, the numbers do not lie. Nineteen people killed. Fifty-seven left with serious injuries. More than 2,500 hurt since 2022. The dead include pedestrians crushed by trucks on Cornelia Street, a man struck by an SUV at 36th and 7th, and a cyclist thrown to the pavement. The city’s streets do not forgive mistakes. They punish the vulnerable.

      Just last year, a garbage truck backed over a man on Cornelia Street. He died in the road. On 7th Avenue, a distracted driver in a new SUV hit a pedestrian at the intersection. He did not get up. These are not isolated events. They are the cost of delay, the price of inaction.

      Buses Collide, Commuters Bleed

      On July 2, three NJ Transit buses crashed at the Port Authority Terminal. Fourteen people were hurt. One was trapped and had to be pulled out by emergency crews. “One with moderate injuries that required extrication, 10 patients with minor injuries all transported to local area hospitals,” said FDNY Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. The crash shut down the terminal for hours. The city rerouted buses. The morning commute became a scene of blood and sirens. The cause is still under investigation, but the pattern is clear: crowded streets, heavy vehicles, and not enough protection for the people inside or outside them.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has taken steps. He sponsored a bill to ban parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and force the city to clear sightlines at 1,000 intersections a year. The bill is still waiting for a vote. Bottcher also voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that often punished the victim instead of the driver. “Drivers are illegally concealing, obscuring, and defacing their license plates to continue to speed with impunity,” Bottcher said, pushing for more transparency on enforcement.

      But the deaths continue. The bills move slow. The streets do not wait.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every death is a warning. Every injury is a call. Demand more. Call Council Member Bottcher. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand action before another body hits the pavement.

      Do not wait for the next siren. Make the call.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      SUVs and Sedans caused the most harm to pedestrians, with 4 deaths and 16 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses were involved in 3 deaths and 4 serious injuries. Motorcycles, Mopeds, and Bikes were involved in 3 serious injuries each but no deaths.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are not just accidents—they are the result of choices and policies. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, clear sightlines at crosswalks, and invest in safer street design. They can also push for transparency and accountability in traffic enforcement.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1151-2023
      Bottcher co-sponsors solar crosswalk bill, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council pushed for 500 solar-lit crosswalks. The bill demanded action—100 new devices each year. It called for a hard look at results. But the session ended. The bill died. Streets wait. Pedestrians keep crossing in the dark.

      Int 1151-2023, introduced August 3, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to require the Department of Transportation to install at least 100 illuminated, solar-powered traffic control devices at crosswalks each year for five years—a total of 500. The bill also ordered a study comparing these devices to unlit signs, probing their power to deter traffic violations and mapping out logistical hurdles. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the installation of solar-powered crosswalks.' Council Member Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Menin, Brooks-Powers, Rivera, and over thirty others. The bill was filed at session’s end on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The city’s crosswalks remain unchanged. Vulnerable road users remain exposed.

      Res 0549-2023
      Bottcher co-sponsors SAFE Streets Act, boosting street safety and protecting pedestrians.
      ▸

      The Council called for Albany to pass Sammy’s Law, letting New York City set lower speed limits. The resolution also urged a crash victims bill of rights and stronger street safety laws. Lawmakers want fewer deaths. The bill stalled. Danger remains.

      Resolution 0549-2023, filed at session’s end, came from the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It was introduced April 11, 2023, and pushed by Council Member Shahana K. Hanif as primary sponsor, with Jennifer Gutiérrez and over twenty others co-sponsoring. The resolution urged the State Legislature and Governor to pass S.2422 ('Sammy’s Law'), which would let New York City set lower speed limits, and A.1901, a crash victims bill of rights. The matter title reads: 'Resolution calling on the New York State Legislature to pass, and the Governor to sign, S.2422, also known as ‘Sammy’s Law,’ ... and A.1901, enacting a crash victims bill of rights, as well as the other bills of the package known as the SAFE Streets Act.' The SAFE Streets Act package also includes safe passing for cyclists and complete street design mandates. The Council’s action highlights the city’s ongoing fight against reckless driving and the urgent need for stronger protections for people on foot, on bikes, and in cars.
