      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      Nineteen Dead, Still Waiting: Bottcher’s Delay Is Killing Us

      Written July 24, 2025

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 24, 2025

      The Death Count Rises

      In District 3, the numbers do not lie. Nineteen people have died on these streets since 2022. Two were killed in the last twelve months. Sixty-two suffered serious injuries. The names fade, but the pain does not. A man crushed by a truck on Cornelia Street. A pedestrian struck at 7th Avenue and West 36th. A cyclist hit, a body left broken. These are not accidents. They are the price of inaction.

      The Human Cost

      Last year, a pedestrian was killed by a driver who was not paying attention. The crash report lists the cause as “Driver Inattention/Distraction.” The result: one man dead, his body found at the intersection, his story ended in a moment (NYC Open Data). Another man, 35, was crushed by a backing garbage truck on Cornelia Street. No warning, no chance. The violence is relentless.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Delayed

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has taken some steps. He sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, aiming to clear sightlines and protect people on foot and bike. The bill would force the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections a year, up from 100. But the bill sits in committee, laid over, waiting for action. Bottcher also pushed for public reporting on drivers who cover their plates to dodge speed cameras, saying, “drivers are illegally concealing, obscuring, and defacing their license plates to continue to speed with impunity” (Streetsblog NYC). He voted yes on bills to speed up pavement markings, remove abandoned vehicles, and require taxi warning decals. But the deaths keep coming.

      The Next Fight

      The crisis is not over. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It can redesign streets to protect the people who walk and bike. It can stop blaming the dead and start saving the living. Call Council Member Bottcher. Demand action. Do not wait for another body in the road.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for the most harm to pedestrians, with 4 deaths and 20 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 3 serious injuries but no deaths. Bikes caused 4 serious injuries and no deaths (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, redesign dangerous intersections, and hold reckless drivers accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Council moves to create a parking permit enforcement unit. The bill targets city-issued permit misuse. Streets clogged by illegal parking put walkers and riders at risk. Action now sits with the transportation committee.

      Bottcher co-sponsors bill easing jaywalking rules, boosting pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians win the right to cross anywhere, signals or not. The law strips police of power to ticket walkers. Streets shift. The city must now teach all road users the new rules.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, amends city code to let pedestrians cross streets at any point, even against signals. The Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure passed it on October 26, 2024. The bill states: 'crossing against a traffic signal or outside a crosswalk will not be a violation.'       Int 0193-2024
      Bottcher co-sponsors taxi warning decal bill with neutral safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing taxis and for-hire cars to post bold warnings on doors. The signs tell passengers: look for cyclists before you open up. A small step. The city hands out the decals. No cost to drivers.

      Int 0193-2024 became law on May 31, 2025, after action by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...requiring taxis and for-hire vehicles to display a decal warning passengers to look for cyclists when opening the door,' was sponsored by Lincoln Restler and co-sponsored by over twenty council members, including Gutiérrez, Hudson, and Rivera. The law mandates clear warning decals on all rear passenger doors of taxis and for-hire vehicles. The Taxi and Limousine Commission will provide the signs at no cost. The measure aims to cut down on 'dooring'—a threat to cyclists citywide. The mayor returned the bill unsigned, but it became law.

      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants near schools. Civilian complaints trigger fines. Streets clear, danger cut. Council moves to protect the vulnerable.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced February 8, 2024, the bill creates a $175 penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of schools. The Department of Transportation must set up a civilian reporting program. If a civilian complaint leads to a fine, the complainant gets 25 percent of proceeds. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code... in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints.' Council Member Carlina Rivera leads, joined by Restler, Ayala, Joseph, Menin, and others. The bill aims to keep paths clear for those most at risk.
