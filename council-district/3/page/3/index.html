      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      Blood on the Crosswalks: How Many More Will Die Before Bottcher Acts?

      Written July 7, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations.)

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 7, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In District 3, the numbers do not lie. Nineteen people killed. Fifty-seven left with serious injuries. More than 2,500 hurt since 2022. The dead include pedestrians crushed by trucks on Cornelia Street, a man struck by an SUV at 36th and 7th, and a cyclist thrown to the pavement. The city’s streets do not forgive mistakes. They punish the vulnerable.

      Just last year, a garbage truck backed over a man on Cornelia Street. He died in the road. On 7th Avenue, a distracted driver in a new SUV hit a pedestrian at the intersection. He did not get up. These are not isolated events. They are the cost of delay, the price of inaction.

      Buses Collide, Commuters Bleed

      On July 2, three NJ Transit buses crashed at the Port Authority Terminal. Fourteen people were hurt. One was trapped and had to be pulled out by emergency crews. “One with moderate injuries that required extrication, 10 patients with minor injuries all transported to local area hospitals,” said FDNY Deputy Chief Jason Saffon. The crash shut down the terminal for hours. The city rerouted buses. The morning commute became a scene of blood and sirens. The cause is still under investigation, but the pattern is clear: crowded streets, heavy vehicles, and not enough protection for the people inside or outside them.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has taken steps. He sponsored a bill to ban parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and force the city to clear sightlines at 1,000 intersections a year. The bill is still waiting for a vote. Bottcher also voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that often punished the victim instead of the driver. “Drivers are illegally concealing, obscuring, and defacing their license plates to continue to speed with impunity,” Bottcher said, pushing for more transparency on enforcement.

      But the deaths continue. The bills move slow. The streets do not wait.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every death is a warning. Every injury is a call. Demand more. Call Council Member Bottcher. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand action before another body hits the pavement.

      Do not wait for the next siren. Make the call.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      SUVs and Sedans caused the most harm to pedestrians, with 4 deaths and 16 serious injuries. Trucks and Buses were involved in 3 deaths and 4 serious injuries. Motorcycles, Mopeds, and Bikes were involved in 3 serious injuries each but no deaths.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are not just accidents—they are the result of choices and policies. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, clear sightlines at crosswalks, and invest in safer street design. They can also push for transparency and accountability in traffic enforcement.

      Int 1138-2024
      Bottcher sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1138-2024 would ban parking and standing within 20 feet of crosswalks. It forces the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections each year. The bill sits in committee. Sponsors include Bottcher, Won, and the Public Advocate.

      Int 1138-2024, introduced December 5, 2024, is under review by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill is 'Laid Over in Committee.' Its title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.' Council Member Erik D. Bottcher is the primary sponsor, joined by Julie Won, Jumaane Williams, and others. The bill prohibits standing or parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and mandates the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections per year. The law also requires citywide outreach and reporting. This measure aims to keep sightlines clear at crossings, a known danger zone for people on foot and bike. The bill has not yet received a vote.

      Int 0346-2024
      Bottcher votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.

      Int 0766-2024
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill boosting safety by targeting obscured license plates.
      ▸

      Council targets hidden plates. Bill slaps fines and jail time on drivers who cover or deface tags. No more hiding from cameras. Law aims to strip shields from reckless motorists. Committee weighs next steps. Streets demand accountability.

      Int 0766-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced April 11, 2024, the bill bans parking, standing, stopping, or operating a vehicle with obscured or defaced license plates. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting the parking, standing, stopping, or operation of a motor vehicle with obscured or defaced license plates.' Council Member Oswald Feliz leads as primary sponsor, joined by Holden, Bottcher, Gennaro, Marte, Restler, Ung, and Paladino. The bill hits violators with up to $1,000 fines, possible jail, and escalating penalties for repeat offenses. The measure aims to end the dodge—drivers hiding plates to evade enforcement. The committee now holds the bill for review.
