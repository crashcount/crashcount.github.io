      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      No More Waiting: Stop the Killing Streets Now

      Written July 8, 2025

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 8, 2025

      The Slow March of Harm

      Nineteen dead. Fifty-seven left with injuries that will never heal. In the last three and a half years, District 3 has seen 5,916 crashes. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about age. Children, elders, workers, tourists—no one is spared. In the last twelve months alone, two people died and twenty-two suffered serious injuries. Most were walking or riding. The street does not forgive.

      Recent Wounds, Unanswered

      Just weeks ago, a man was killed by a truck on West 40th Street. A pedestrian, crushed. No warning, no second chance. Another body, another family left with silence. The data shows SUVs and trucks are the worst: SUVs killed four, trucks killed three, bikes killed none (NYC Open Data).

      The carnage is not random. It is the result of choices. A crash on Cornelia Street, a pedestrian killed by a backing garbage truck. On 7th Avenue, a man struck down by a distracted SUV driver. The stories repeat. The pain does not fade.

      Bottcher’s Record: Progress and Delay

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has moved on daylighting, sponsoring a bill to ban parking near crosswalks and keep sightlines clear. The bill would force the city to install barriers at 1,000 intersections a year, but it sits in committee, waiting for a vote (Bottcher sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks). Bottcher voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law used to blame the dead instead of the driver (Bottcher votes yes to legalize jaywalking). He has backed bills for bike lanes, greenways, and more reporting on hit-and-runs. But the pace is slow. The deaths are not.

      The Call

      Every day of delay is another day of blood on the street. Call Bottcher. Call the Council. Demand a vote on daylighting. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand action, not words. The dead cannot wait.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      SUVs killed four pedestrians, trucks killed three, and bikes killed none. SUVs and trucks caused the most deaths and serious injuries to pedestrians in District 3. Bikes caused serious injuries but no fatalities (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. The same streets and vehicles keep killing. Better design, lower speeds, and enforcement can prevent these deaths.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for safer street design, lower speed limits, and stronger accountability for dangerous driving. They can push for daylighting, protected bike lanes, and more transparency.

      Int 1138-2024
      Bottcher sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1138-2024 would ban parking and standing within 20 feet of crosswalks. It forces the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections each year. The bill sits in committee. Sponsors include Bottcher, Won, and the Public Advocate.

      Int 1138-2024, introduced December 5, 2024, is under review by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill is 'Laid Over in Committee.' Its title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.' Council Member Erik D. Bottcher is the primary sponsor, joined by Julie Won, Jumaane Williams, and others. The bill prohibits standing or parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and mandates the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections per year. The law also requires citywide outreach and reporting. This measure aims to keep sightlines clear at crossings, a known danger zone for people on foot and bike. The bill has not yet received a vote.

      Int 0346-2024
      Bottcher votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.
