      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      Second Avenue Bleeds: City Stalls, Cyclists Fall

      Written July 31, 2025

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 31, 2025

      Blood on the Streets

      Just last Tuesday, a 65-year-old man rode his e-bike down Second Avenue. A Nissan Rogue hit him. The driver fled. The man lay in the road, head split, paramedics working in silence. Police arrested the driver two hours later. He was unlicensed. The charge: leaving the scene, causing serious injury. The street shut down. The red light on the crumpled bike kept flashing. “The biker was rushed by EMS to Bellevue Hospital in critical condition.”

      This is not rare. In the last year, District 3 saw 2 deaths, 24 serious injuries, and 931 people hurt in 1,934 crashes. Cyclists, walkers, the old, the young. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do.

      Patterns of Harm

      Cars and trucks do most of the killing. In the past three and a half years, they took 19 lives and left thousands more broken. Bikes and mopeds hurt, but they do not kill like cars. Trucks back up, crush skulls. SUVs plow through crosswalks. The city counts the bodies. The city moves on.

      Leadership: Promises and Delays

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has acted. He backed a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, to keep sightlines clear. He voted to remove abandoned cars, to clear the way for people on foot and bike. He co-sponsored a bill for medians with trees to shield cyclists. He pushed for daylight at a thousand intersections a year. But the bills sit in committee. The danger does not wait.

      Bottcher once said drivers are illegally concealing, obscuring, and defacing their license plates to continue to speed with impunity. The city still lets them.

      What Now

      The blood is fresh. The danger is old. Call Bottcher. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand action before another bike light blinks in the dark, alone.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents local districts like District 3.
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 19 deaths and thousands injured since 2022. Motorcycles and mopeds caused injuries but no deaths. Bikes caused injuries but did not kill.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes can be prevented with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not random acts of fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for safer speeds, clear sightlines, protected bike lanes, and hold dangerous drivers accountable.

      Int 1233-2025
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill to require vegetated medians, boosting cyclist and pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council bill orders trees and plants on new medians between bike lanes and car traffic. Concrete gets green. Barriers grow roots. The city must build for life, not speed.

      Int 1233-2025 sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced April 10, 2025, it demands new medians between bicycle lanes and motor vehicles be built for trees and vegetation, pending feasibility. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code... in relation to the planting of vegetation on new medians separating bicycle lanes from motorized vehicle traffic.” Council Member Shekar Krishnan leads, joined by Bottcher, Ossé, Brannan, Hanif, and Brooks-Powers. They push the city to plant, or allow planting, in every new median. The law would take effect 120 days after passage. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Int 1105-2024
      Bottcher votes yes, boosting street safety transparency and project accountability.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.' Council Member Julie Won led sponsorship, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law demands DOT post annual and monthly updates on every project tied to the master plan—listing details, timelines, funding, and setbacks. This law brings sunlight to street safety work, making the city answer for every mile and missed deadline.

      Int 1160-2025
      Bottcher votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.
