      District 3: Streets Still Bleed, City Still Waits

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations.)

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Toll in District 3: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In District 3, the numbers do not flinch. Nineteen people killed. Fifty-nine left with serious injuries. More than 2,500 hurt since 2022. Trucks, SUVs, taxis—steel against flesh. A 34-year-old man, dead at the intersection of 7th Avenue and West 36th, struck by an SUV. A 35-year-old, crushed by a garbage truck on Cornelia Street. The street does not care if you are young or old. The street only takes.

      Just this spring, a sedan hit two people in Midtown. A taxi turned left and struck four more on Fifth Avenue. The pattern is old. The pain is fresh. “We want a real solution to the bike and pedestrian safety issue,” said CB 5 Vice Chair Samir Lavingia. The city keeps talking. The bodies keep coming.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has moved on some fronts. He backed a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, to keep sightlines clear and walkers alive. He voted to clear abandoned cars from the curb, to open the way for people on foot and bike. He co-sponsored a bill to speed up pavement markings, and another to plant trees in new bike lane medians. He called for transparency on license plate defacing, saying, “drivers are illegally concealing, obscuring, and defacing their license plates to continue to speed with impunity.”

      But the council moves slow. Bills sit in committee. The Fifth Avenue redesign cut bike and bus lanes, leaving two lanes for cars and little for the rest. The board warns: without real bike lanes, cyclists will ride the sidewalk, and the danger will not end. The city delays. The street does not.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Every day, another crash. Every week, another family changed. The council can act. Residents can demand it. Call Bottcher. Call the mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, not promises. Demand the city use its new power to make every street safe for the most vulnerable. The time for patience is over. The street is not patient. It only takes.

      Int 1160-2025
      Bottcher co-sponsors bill to speed up pavement markings, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly crossings for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, forces the Department of Transportation to install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. The bill, sponsored by Farah N. Louis (primary) and co-sponsored by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, Ariola, and others, passed on March 15, 2025. The law demands annual reporting on compliance and reasons for any delay. Quick, visible lines cut confusion and protect people crossing or riding. The law took effect immediately.

      Int 1138-2024
      Bottcher sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill bars cars from blocking crosswalks. No standing or parking within 20 feet. City must install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections yearly. Streets clear. Sightlines open. Danger cut.

      Int 1138-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 5, 2024. The bill states: “prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.” Council Member Erik D. Bottcher leads, joined by Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and over two dozen co-sponsors. The law orders the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections each year, up from 100. The city must also run outreach and education. The bill aims to keep crosswalks clear, improve visibility, and protect people on foot and bike. No more hiding behind parked cars. The committee laid the bill over on April 21, 2025.

      Int 0346-2024
      Bottcher votes yes to decriminalize jaywalking, boosting pedestrian safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians now cross anywhere, any time. No summons. Law strips drivers of excuses. Streets shift. Power tilts to people on foot.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, rewrites New York’s pedestrian rules. The bill, heard by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passed on September 26, 2024, and became law on October 26, 2024. Its title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way.” Sponsored by Council Member Tiffany Cabán and co-sponsored by Narcisse, Won, Hanif, Hudson, and Restler, the law lets pedestrians cross streets anywhere, even against signals. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks. The Department of Transportation must educate all street users on new rights and responsibilities. The law removes a tool police used to target walkers. It shifts blame from people on foot to the system and those behind the wheel.

      Int 0745-2024
      Bottcher votes yes on bill requiring DOT to report micromobility data, no direct safety impact.
      ▸

      City law now forces DOT to reveal where bikes and scooters go. Streets and bridges, protected or not. Data goes public. Riders’ movement, danger, and safety projects laid bare. No more hiding the facts.

      Int 0745-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It was introduced April 11, 2024, and passed on August 15, 2024. The law orders the Department of Transportation to post current bicycle and micromobility ridership data online, updated monthly or at least yearly. The matter summary reads: 'requiring the department of transportation to provide information about bicycle and other micromobility device activity.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led as primary sponsor, joined by Farías, Restler, Hudson, Schulman, and others. The law demands transparency on where riders travel and what DOT does to keep them safe. This opens the books on bike movement and exposes gaps in protection.
