      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      District 3 Bleeds—Will Bottcher Stop the Killing?

      Written August 6, 2025 (Revised August 7, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, deduped and sorted citations, clarified FAQ vehicle breakdown.)

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 6, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      In District 3, the street does not forgive. Since 2022, 19 people have died and 2,729 have been injured in crashes. Sixty-three were left with serious injuries—lives changed in a blink. The dead do not speak. The injured carry the story in scars and limps.

      Just last week, a 65-year-old e-bike rider was struck on Second Avenue. The driver fled, drove onto the sidewalk, and later told police he ran because he had no license. The cyclist was left with critical head trauma. The paper said, “He now faces charges of leaving the scene of an accident that caused serious injury and driving without a license” (West Side Spirit).

      On Cornelia Street, a truck backed over a 35-year-old man. On West 36th, a distracted SUV driver killed a pedestrian at the intersection. The numbers pile up, but the pain is sharp and local.

      The Shape of Danger

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm: 4 deaths, 352 minor injuries, 210 moderate, 20 serious. Trucks and buses killed 3, with 42 minor and 40 moderate injuries. Bikes injured 116 people—no deaths, but pain all the same. Motorcycles and mopeds left dozens hurt. The street is a gauntlet, and the most vulnerable—walkers, cyclists, the old—pay the highest price.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has backed bills to clear abandoned cars, speed up pavement markings, and require daylighting at crosswalks—all steps to clear sightlines and slow the street’s violence. He voted to decriminalize jaywalking, a move to stop blaming the dead for their own deaths. He co-sponsored a bill to force the city to report on drivers who cover their plates to dodge speed cameras, saying, “drivers are illegally concealing, obscuring, and defacing their license plates to continue to speed with impunity” (Streetsblog NYC).

      But the pace is slow. Bills stall. Promises fade. The street keeps killing.

      Call to Action: Demand More, Demand Now

      Contact Council Member Bottcher. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted corners, and real enforcement against reckless drivers. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Find your Council Member and act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each council district.
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 4 deaths and hundreds of injuries. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes also injured dozens, but cars and trucks remain the deadliest threat. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents. Most are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, daylight intersections, and enforce laws against reckless driving. They can pass and fund bills that put safety first.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1160-2025
      Bottcher votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.

      Int 1138-2024
      Bottcher sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill bars cars from blocking crosswalks. No standing or parking within 20 feet. City must install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections yearly. Streets clear. Sightlines open. Danger cut.

      Int 1138-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 5, 2024. The bill states: “prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.” Council Member Erik D. Bottcher leads, joined by Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and over two dozen co-sponsors. The law orders the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections each year, up from 100. The city must also run outreach and education. The bill aims to keep crosswalks clear, improve visibility, and protect people on foot and bike. No more hiding behind parked cars. The committee laid the bill over on April 21, 2025.

      Int 0346-2024
      Bottcher votes yes to decriminalize jaywalking, boosting pedestrian safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians now cross anywhere, any time. No summons. Law strips drivers of excuses. Streets shift. Power tilts to people on foot.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, rewrites New York’s pedestrian rules. The bill, heard by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passed on September 26, 2024, and became law on October 26, 2024. Its title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way.” Sponsored by Council Member Tiffany Cabán and co-sponsored by Narcisse, Won, Hanif, Hudson, and Restler, the law lets pedestrians cross streets anywhere, even against signals. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks. The Department of Transportation must educate all street users on new rights and responsibilities. The law removes a tool police used to target walkers. It shifts blame from people on foot to the system and those behind the wheel.
