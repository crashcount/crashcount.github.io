      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 3?

      Nineteen Dead, Still Waiting: Bottcher’s Delay Is Killing Us

      Written July 24, 2025

      District 3: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 24, 2025

      The Death Count Rises

      In District 3, the numbers do not lie. Nineteen people have died on these streets since 2022. Two were killed in the last twelve months. Sixty-two suffered serious injuries. The names fade, but the pain does not. A man crushed by a truck on Cornelia Street. A pedestrian struck at 7th Avenue and West 36th. A cyclist hit, a body left broken. These are not accidents. They are the price of inaction.

      The Human Cost

      Last year, a pedestrian was killed by a driver who was not paying attention. The crash report lists the cause as “Driver Inattention/Distraction.” The result: one man dead, his body found at the intersection, his story ended in a moment (NYC Open Data). Another man, 35, was crushed by a backing garbage truck on Cornelia Street. No warning, no chance. The violence is relentless.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Delayed

      Council Member Erik Bottcher has taken some steps. He sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, aiming to clear sightlines and protect people on foot and bike. The bill would force the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections a year, up from 100. But the bill sits in committee, laid over, waiting for action. Bottcher also pushed for public reporting on drivers who cover their plates to dodge speed cameras, saying, “drivers are illegally concealing, obscuring, and defacing their license plates to continue to speed with impunity” (Streetsblog NYC). He voted yes on bills to speed up pavement markings, remove abandoned vehicles, and require taxi warning decals. But the deaths keep coming.

      The Next Fight

      The crisis is not over. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It can redesign streets to protect the people who walk and bike. It can stop blaming the dead and start saving the living. Call Council Member Bottcher. Demand action. Do not wait for another body in the road.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 3 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 66 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 3?
      It includes the West Village, Chelsea-Hudson Yards, Hell’S Kitchen, Midtown South-Flatiron-Union Square, Manhattan CB4, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 66, AD 67, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 3?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for the most harm to pedestrians, with 4 deaths and 20 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 3 serious injuries but no deaths. Bikes caused 4 serious injuries and no deaths (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, redesign dangerous intersections, and hold reckless drivers accountable.

      Int 1160-2025
      Bottcher votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.

      Int 1138-2024
      Bottcher sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill bars cars from blocking crosswalks. No standing or parking within 20 feet. City must install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections yearly. Streets clear. Sightlines open. Danger cut.

      Int 1138-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 5, 2024. The bill states: “prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.” Council Member Erik D. Bottcher leads, joined by Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and over two dozen co-sponsors. The law orders the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections each year, up from 100. The city must also run outreach and education. The bill aims to keep crosswalks clear, improve visibility, and protect people on foot and bike. No more hiding behind parked cars. The committee laid the bill over on April 21, 2025.
