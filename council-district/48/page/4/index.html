      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 48?

      Children Bleed While Leaders Stall: Speed Kills, Action Saves

      Written July 19, 2025

      District 48: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 19, 2025

      Children in the Crosshairs

      Just yesterday, an 11-year-old boy was hit by a car near a playground on Bedford Avenue in Sheepshead Bay. He survived. Many do not. Police said only, “There is no word yet on how the accident happened”—but the boy is in the hospital, and the street is unchanged. ABC7 reported the crash.

      The Numbers Do Not Lie

      In the last twelve months, 830 people have been injured in District 48 traffic crashes. Nine were seriously hurt. Six people died in the same period last year. The victims are not just numbers. They are children, elders, cyclists, neighbors. SUVs and cars did most of the harm—six deaths, 376 minor injuries, 122 moderate injuries, and four serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed three more. Bikes and mopeds injured, but did not kill. NYC Open Data tracks these crashes.

      Leadership: Votes, Delays, and Missed Chances

      Council Member Inna Vernikov has voted for some safety bills—removing abandoned vehicles, speeding up traffic studies, and requiring pavement markings. She co-sponsored a bill to add speed humps near parks. But when it mattered most, she stood in the way. Vernikov opposed 24/7 speed cameras in school zones and called them a scheme to “take away more of our hard-earned money” according to Streetsblog NYC. She celebrated when DOT paused a long-promised bike lane project, saying, “Happy to say as a result of our conversations and advocacy, @NYC_DOT is PAUSING implementation of bike lanes.” She boasted about the pause.

      The Price of Delay

      The streets do not wait for politics. “As of Thursday, 60 pedestrians had been killed by vehicles across the city this year—10 fewer than the 70 who died by this time last year.” NY Daily News reported the toll.

      Act Now—Or Count the Bodies

      Call Council Member Vernikov. Demand she support a 20 mph speed limit, protected bike lanes, and full speed camera coverage. Tell her: every delay is another name on a slab. The dead do not get a second chance. The living need action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 48 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 22. See NYC Open Data for details.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 48?
      It includes the Brighton Beach, Gravesend (East)-Homecrest, Madison, Sheepshead Bay-Manhattan Beach-Gerritsen Beach, and Brooklyn CB15 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 45, and AD 46, and State Senate Districts SD 22 and SD 23. Neighborhood boundaries are listed in NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 48?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 6 deaths, 376 minor injuries, 122 moderate injuries, and 4 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 15 injuries and 1 serious injury. Bikes caused 8 injuries but no deaths. See the breakdown in NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can support lower speed limits, expand protected bike lanes, back 24/7 speed cameras, and push for rapid street redesigns that protect people walking and biking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0346-2024
      Vernikov votes no on jaywalking bill, opposing improved pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians now cross anywhere, any time. No summons. Law strips drivers of excuses. Streets shift. Power tilts to people on foot.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, rewrites New York’s pedestrian rules. The bill, heard by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passed on September 26, 2024, and became law on October 26, 2024. Its title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way.” Sponsored by Council Member Tiffany Cabán and co-sponsored by Narcisse, Won, Hanif, Hudson, and Restler, the law lets pedestrians cross streets anywhere, even against signals. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks. The Department of Transportation must educate all street users on new rights and responsibilities. The law removes a tool police used to target walkers. It shifts blame from people on foot to the system and those behind the wheel.

      Vernikov votes yes on bill requiring DOT to report micromobility data, no direct safety impact.

The matter summary reads: 'requiring the department of transportation to provide information about bicycle and other micromobility device activity.' Council Member Selvena N.       Int 0262-2024
      Vernikov co-sponsors bill to require speed humps near parks, improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill orders speed humps on roads beside parks over one acre. DOT can skip spots if safety or rules demand. Law aims to slow cars where families walk, run, and play.

      Int 0262-2024 sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after introduction on February 28, 2024. The bill reads: “requiring the installation of speed humps on roadways adjacent to any park equal or greater than one acre.” Lincoln Restler leads as primary sponsor, joined by eighteen co-sponsors. The Department of Transportation must install speed humps unless the commissioner finds a risk to safety or a conflict with DOT guidelines. The law would take effect 180 days after passage. The measure targets streets where parks meet traffic, aiming to slow cars and shield people outside vehicles.
