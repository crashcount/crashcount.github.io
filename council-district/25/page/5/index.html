      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      District 25: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      • Sep 13: Right turn, pedestrian down at 84 St and 35 Ave; severe bleeding recorded. NYC Open Data

      The toll on these blocks

      Since Jan 1, 2022, District 25 has seen 13 people killed and 2,270 injured in crashes. NYC Open Data.

      Year to date, crashes stand at 704 versus 876 at this point last year; reported deaths 0 versus 5; injuries 425 versus 461; serious injuries 6 versus 3. NYC Open Data.

      Injuries climb around the evening rush; the 6 PM hour shows a heavy load of harm. NYC Open Data.

      Corners that won’t forgive

      37 Avenue keeps showing up in the logs. So do Northern Boulevard and Broadway. Police reports here cite drivers disregarding traffic controls and failing to yield, with injuries and deaths to match. NYC Open Data.

      A 78‑year‑old woman was hit crossing outside the crosswalk at 37 Ave and 82 St; police recorded driver distraction. A 14‑year‑old was hit in the crosswalk at the same corner while crossing with the signal; police recorded driver inattention. NYC Open Data.

      What leaders have done — and not done

      Council Member Shekar Krishnan backed a daylighting bill to keep crosswalks clear (Int 1138‑2024) and sponsored a plan to build vegetated medians between bike lanes and traffic (Int 1233‑2025). Legistar. He also pushed the Parks Department to move faster: “The infrastructure projects, the transportation and green space projects, need to be progressing at a much much faster rate.” Streetsblog.

      State lawmakers advanced moped and e‑bike rules aimed at sellers and registration, with support noted by Krishnan and others. Streetsblog.

      But cars and trucks still do most of the damage to people walking here. Evening still hurts. Corners still hide people.

      Stop speed before it kills again

      A small group of repeat speeders does outsized harm in this city. Data show that 1.5% of drivers are tied to a fifth of pedestrian deaths, and camera‑ticket records flag the risk of those with 16 or more violations in a year. Streetsblog.

      Two moves would save lives here:

      • Lower the default city speed limit under Sammy’s Law.
      • Require intelligent speed limiters for habitual speeders.

      Both are on the table. The Council Member for this district has backed multiple safety bills. State leaders Michael Gianaris and Steven Raga know the stakes on these blocks.

      One turn at 84th and 35th put a man on the ground. The next turn does not have to end that way.
