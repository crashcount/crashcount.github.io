      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 42?

      Ten Dead in a Year—Council Still Stalls as Streets Kill

      Written August 11, 2025 (Revised August 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 42: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 11, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      Ten people have died on District 42 streets in the last year. More than 1,500 have been hurt. Fourteen were left with injuries so severe they will never be the same. The names fade. The pain does not.

      Just last month, a 21-year-old woman was killed by a sedan on Gateway Drive. She was not in the roadway. The driver was distracted and speeding. She died from crush injuries. There is no monument for her. Only a line in a spreadsheet. NYC Open Data

      The Pattern: Speed, Distraction, and Silence

      Speed and distraction kill here. A 30-year-old man died at Church Avenue and Kings Highway. The car was going too fast. The driver kept going straight. The man was crossing at the intersection. He did not make it to the other side. NYC Open Data

      A 26-year-old woman died as a passenger on Blake Avenue. The car ignored a traffic signal. It hit a parked truck. She died from internal injuries. The driver was unlicensed. The street stayed the same.

      Leadership: Small Steps, Big Gaps

      Council Member Chris Banks has voted for bills to clear abandoned vehicles from streets, speed up pavement markings, and boost transparency on safety projects. He co-sponsored a law to require bike and scooter share companies to display safety rules. He backed discounts for seniors and students on bike share. These are steps. But the bodies keep coming.

      No bill has forced slower speeds or protected every crossing. No law has stopped the next driver from speeding, or the next family from losing someone. As one advocate said after another death, “I was very frustrated that nothing has been done in more than three years since Daniel Vidal was killed.”

      The Call: Demand More, Demand Now

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every day without action is another day someone does not come home. Call Council Member Banks. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings. Demand action before another name is lost.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes local laws, oversees agencies, and represents the interests of neighborhoods across the five boroughs. NYC Council – Legistar
      ▸ Where does District 42 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 58 and state senate district SD 19. NYC Council – Legistar
      ▸ Which areas are in District 42?
      It includes the East New York (North), East New York-New Lots, Spring Creek-Starrett City, East New York-City Line, East Flatbush-Remsen Village, Jamaica Bay (West), Shirley Chisholm State Park, Brooklyn CB56, and Brooklyn CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 58, AD 59, and AD 60, and State Senate District SD 19. NYC Council – Legistar
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 42?
      Most injuries and deaths were caused by cars and trucks (11 deaths, 623 total incidents), followed by motorcycles and mopeds (no deaths, 11 incidents), and bikes (no deaths, 9 incidents). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. The same deadly patterns—speed, distraction, unsafe streets—repeat year after year. With better policies and street design, these deaths and injuries can be prevented.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous crossings, fund protected bike lanes, and pass laws that hold reckless drivers accountable. Every delay means more lives lost.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
