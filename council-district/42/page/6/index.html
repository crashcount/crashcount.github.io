      Eight Dead, Still Waiting: When Will Chris Banks Make Streets Safe?

      District 42: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Losses in District 42

      The streets of District 42 do not forgive. In the last twelve months, eight people died in crashes here. Fourteen more suffered serious injuries. The toll is not numbers. It is families torn, bodies broken, futures erased.

      Just days ago, a young woman was killed in a parking lot at Gateway Center. She was sitting on a curb. Her boyfriend lost control while doing donuts. Police charged him with criminally negligent homicide. The court record states he “lost control when he was doing donuts with the vehicle”. Her family is left with silence and questions. “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter,” said her sister-in-law.

      Speed kills here, too. In the past year, unsafe speed was a factor in deadly crashes on Pennsylvania Avenue, Church Avenue, and the Belt Parkway. Pedestrians, cyclists, and children are the first to pay. The numbers are blunt: 1,341 injured, 8 dead, 14 seriously hurt in just the last year.

      What Has Council Member Chris Banks Done?

      Council Member Chris Banks has voted for and co-sponsored bills to clear abandoned vehicles, speed up pavement markings, and expand bike share discounts for seniors and students. He backed new laws to remove derelict cars within 72 hours and require faster repainting of crosswalks—measures that help, but do not stop the blood. He also co-sponsored a bill to boost plate enforcement, targeting ghost cars and untraceable drivers. These are steps, not solutions.

      But the carnage continues. No bill has yet forced drivers to slow down. No law has made Pennsylvania Avenue safe for a child to cross. The city has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not used it.

      What Comes Next: Demand More

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every delay means another family grieves. Call Council Member Banks. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for the most vulnerable. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 42 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 58 and state senate district SD 19. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Which areas are in District 42?
      It includes the East New York (North), East New York-New Lots, Spring Creek-Starrett City, East New York-City Line, East Flatbush-Remsen Village, Jamaica Bay (West), Shirley Chisholm State Park, Brooklyn CB56, and Brooklyn CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 58, AD 59, and AD 60, and State Senate District SD 19. NYC Open Data
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 42?
      Most injuries and deaths were caused by cars and SUVs (10 deaths, 579 injuries), with smaller numbers from motorcycles and mopeds (0 deaths, 11 injuries), and bikes (0 deaths, 9 injuries). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speed, failure to yield, reckless driving. These deaths are preventable with better laws and safer streets.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking. They can act now, not after another tragedy.
