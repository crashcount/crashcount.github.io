      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 42?

      Blood on Blake Avenue: How Many More Dead Before Banks Acts?

      Written July 16, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, cleaned citations.)

      District 42: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In District 42, the numbers do not lie. Nineteen people killed. Thirty-eight left with serious injuries. Over 4,300 hurt since 2022. The streets run through East New York, New Lots, Spring Creek, and more. The dead are not just numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. Cars and SUVs did most of the killing.

      On January 24, a woman crossing with the signal at Pennsylvania and Blake was struck and killed by an SUV. She had the right of way. She did not make it home. The driver kept going. The crash report lists “failure to yield right-of-way” and “driver inattention” as causes (crash report).

      On February 27, a 26-year-old woman died as a passenger in a sedan that slammed into a parked truck on Blake Avenue. She was not the driver. She never got a second chance.

      The Human Cost

      Every crash leaves a hole. A mother, a daughter, a friend. The pain does not fade. “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter,” said Ruiz’s sister-in-law. The grief is not just private. It is public. It is the cost of inaction.

      On July 13, a driver doing donuts in a Gateway Plaza parking lot lost control and killed Madisyn Ruiz, 21. Her nephews were hurt. The driver told police he “lost control when he was doing donuts with the vehicle” (driver statement).

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Chris Banks has voted for bills to clear abandoned vehicles, speed up pavement markings, and improve crash investigations. He co-sponsored bills for discounted bike share for students and seniors. He backed more speed humps near parks. These are steps. But the blood on the street says it is not enough.

      The most vulnerable—pedestrians, cyclists, children—are still dying. The law moves slow. The cars move fast. The dead do not get a second chance.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every delay, every watered-down bill, every excuse, means another family grieves. Call Council Member Chris Banks. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people, not just paint and promises.

      Do not wait for the next name to be carved into stone. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 42 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 58 and state senate district SD 19.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 42?
      It includes the East New York (North), East New York-New Lots, Spring Creek-Starrett City, East New York-City Line, East Flatbush-Remsen Village, Jamaica Bay (West), Shirley Chisholm State Park, Brooklyn CB56, and Brooklyn CB5 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 58, AD 59, and AD 60, and State Senate District SD 19.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 42?
      Most injuries and deaths came from cars and SUVs (10 deaths, 579 injuries), followed by motorcycles and mopeds (no deaths, 11 injuries), and bikes (no deaths, 9 injuries). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Policies like lower speed limits, safer street design, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, safer crossings, protected bike lanes, and hold dangerous drivers accountable.

      Int 1252-2025
      Banks co-sponsors bill boosting plate enforcement, improving street safety for all.
      ▸

      Council bill orders NYPD to check temp plates and VINs. Cops must publish parking enforcement reports. Bill sits in Public Safety. Streets stay risky while data hides in shadows.

      Int 1252-2025, now laid over in the Committee on Public Safety, was introduced April 24, 2025. The bill reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code... in relation to police department parking enforcement.' Council Members Farah N. Louis (primary), Oswald Feliz, Justin L. Brannan, Lincoln Restler, Robert F. Holden, and Chris Banks sponsor the measure. It forces NYPD to verify license plates and VINs on vehicles with temp tags or those ticketed for violations. NYPD must also publish quarterly parking enforcement reports. No safety analyst has assessed the bill's impact on vulnerable road users.

      Int 1105-2024
      Banks votes yes to boost street safety transparency and accountability.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.' Council Member Julie Won led sponsorship, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law demands DOT post annual and monthly updates on every project tied to the master plan—listing details, timelines, funding, and setbacks. This law brings sunlight to street safety work, making the city answer for every mile and missed deadline.

      Int 1218-2025, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced March 12, 2025, by Council Members Chris Banks (primary), Susan Zhuang, and Frank Morano.
