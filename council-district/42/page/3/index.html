      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 42?

      District 42: The Road Keeps Taking

      Written August 15, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Finisher pass to tighten prose, correct tone, incorporate local analysis and citywide policy fixes, and produce SEO fields.)

      District 42: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 15, 2025

      Just Sunday: Death on the Sutter L

      A man fell onto the southbound tracks at the Sutter Avenue L station and was struck by a train around 6 p.m., police said. EMS pronounced the victim dead at the scene. NY Daily News.

      Last month: A young woman on Gateway Drive

      On 2025-07-13, a 21-year-old woman was struck and killed near 395 Gateway Drive after midnight. NYC Open Data lists driver inattention/distraction and unsafe speed as contributing factors. NYC Open Data crash record.

      The pattern is the point

      These are not isolated events. Crashes concentrate on wide, fast roads and during nighttime hours. Top local hotspots include Belt Parkway and Pennsylvania Avenue, with Linden Boulevard also showing heavy injury counts. Small-area data repeatedly names inattention/distraction and unsafe speed as leading contributing factors. Cars and SUVs account for the majority of pedestrian harms in the district.

      The remedies are straightforward and local: install leading pedestrian intervals (LPIs) and daylighting at dangerous intersections on Linden Boulevard and Pennsylvania Avenue; harden turning radii and add raised crossings on approaches to Belt Parkway; improve lighting and increase targeted night enforcement where crash rates peak; and prioritize speed-camera enforcement and other automated tools in repeat-hotspot corridors.

      What Chris Banks has done

      Council Member Chris Banks voted in favor of Int 0857-2024, the local law to speed removal of abandoned vehicles, which passed the Council on 2025-06-30. That law aims to clear sightline hazards that can endanger pedestrians and cyclists. Int 0857-2024.

      He is also a co-sponsor of Int 1347-2025, a bill in committee focused on enforcing violations against unlicensed commuter vans. The measure remains under review. Int 1347-2025.

      Enforcement matters, but design and speed management prevent crashes before they happen.

      Citywide fixes that matter here

      Local data in District 42 points to two citywide policy priorities: lower NYC’s default speed limit on city streets, and require intelligent speed‑assistance devices (speed limiters) for habitual speeders. These steps reduce kinetic violence on residential and arterial streets and give DOT the policy tools to calm faster corridors across neighborhoods.

      What must happen now

      This district needs targeted street redesigns and stronger speed management now: LPIs and daylighting on Linden and Pennsylvania; hardened turns and raised crossings on Belt Parkway approaches; better night lighting and targeted enforcement; and continued investment in automated speed enforcement at repeat‑hotspot locations.

      Call Council Member Chris Banks at 718-649-9495 or email District42@council.nyc.gov. Press DOT and City Hall, attend your community board, and join local advocacy to keep these fixes on the calendar. Start here: Take Action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Which areas are in District 42?
      It includes the East New York (North), East New York-New Lots, Spring Creek-Starrett City, East New York-City Line, East Flatbush-Remsen Village, Jamaica Bay (West), Shirley Chisholm State Park, Brooklyn CB56, and Brooklyn CB5 areas. It also overlaps parts of AD 58, AD 59, AD 60, and SD 19.
      ▸ Where does District 42 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 58 and state senate district SD 19.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 42?
      Cars and Trucks: Cars and SUVs were involved most often, with 624 pedestrian harms and 11 pedestrian deaths; trucks and buses added 38 harms and 0 deaths. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 11 harms, 0 deaths. Bikes: 9 harms, 0 deaths. (Period covered: 2022‑01‑01 to 2025‑08‑15.)
      ▸ Are these “accidents,” or are they preventable?
      They are preventable. The same places and hours keep killing—Belt Parkway, Linden Boulevard, Pennsylvania Avenue, nights and early mornings—while recurring factors like unsafe speed and inattention show where to act with slower speeds, daylighting, LPIs, hardened turns, and night enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do right now?
      Lower the default city speed to 20 mph and back speed limiters for repeat speeders; press DOT to daylight and add LPIs on Linden and Pennsylvania; harden turns and build raised crossings; keep automated enforcement and lighting running at night. Contact Council Member Chris Banks at 718‑649‑9495 or District42@council.nyc.gov, and push leadership via Take Action.
      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      It is the city’s lawmaking body. Bills are introduced, sent to committees such as Transportation and Infrastructure, and then voted on by the Council before going to the Mayor.

      Int 0193-2024
      Banks votes yes on taxi dooring warning decals, safety impact neutral.
      ▸

      Council passes law. Taxis and for-hire cars must post bold warnings on rear doors. Riders face the message: look for cyclists before swinging the door. A move to cut dooring. City acts. Cyclists stay in the crosshairs.

      Int 0193-2024, now law, cleared the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and passed City Council on May 1, 2025. The bill, titled 'A Local Law...requiring taxis and for-hire vehicles to display a decal warning passengers to look for cyclists when opening the door,' demands clear warning signs on all rear passenger doors. Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Gutiérrez, Hudson, Rivera, Won, and others. The Taxi and Limousine Commission must provide decals at no cost. The law aims to fight dooring, a threat to cyclists citywide. Enacted May 31, 2025, it takes effect in 120 days.
