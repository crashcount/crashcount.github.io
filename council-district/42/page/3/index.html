      Blind Corners, Broken Lives: Demand Action on District 42’s Deadly Streets

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 19, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, cleaned and deduped citations, improved clarity and citation formatting.)

      District 42: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      Eighteen dead. Thirty-six left with serious injuries. In District 42, the numbers do not lie. From East New York to Starrett City, the streets have become a gauntlet. In the last twelve months alone, seven people died. Over 1,200 were hurt. The youngest victim was a child. The oldest, a grandmother. Most never saw it coming.

      A 26-year-old woman, Imani Vance, was killed when an unlicensed driver ran a stop sign and slammed into a school bus. The driver fled, leaving her to die in the front seat. The District Attorney called it “a shocking disregard for human life and safety, made worse by his attempt to flee the scene instead of helping the victim” said Brooklyn DA Eric Gonzalez.

      A pedestrian crossing with the light was struck and killed by an SUV at Pennsylvania and Blake. The driver failed to yield. The street was quiet after, except for the sirens.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Council Member Chris Banks has voted for some safety measures. He backed the law that ended jaywalking enforcement, shifting blame away from pedestrians and toward drivers see vote record. He co-sponsored bills for more speed humps and better lighting near parks and schools. He voted to lower speed limits on Open Streets.

      But when it mattered most, Banks stood in the way. He opposed a bill to ban parking at corners—daylighting—that would have made children and elders more visible at crossings. He cited parking concerns according to Streetsblog NYC. The debate was not about cars. It was about lives. “The tragic loss of Imani Vance was allegedly caused by the defendant’s reckless decision to ignore a stop sign and drive at excessive speeds,” said the DA. The corners remain blind.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Speed and inattention kill. Cars and SUVs are the main weapons. The city has the tools—lower speed limits, more cameras, safer crossings. But tools unused are as useless as promises unkept.

      Call Chris Banks. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand 20 mph limits on every street. Demand action, not words.

      The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 42 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 42?
      It includes the East New York-New Lots, Spring Creek-Starrett City, East New York-City Line, Jamaica Bay (West), and Shirley Chisholm State Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 58, AD 59, and AD 60, and State Senate District SD 19.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 42?
      Most injuries and deaths came from Cars and SUVs (10 deaths, 9 serious injuries), with smaller numbers from Trucks and Buses (no deaths, 2 serious injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 1 serious injury), and Bikes (no deaths, 1 serious injury). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents. They are the result of choices—speed, failure to yield, and street design—that can be changed.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass laws for lower speed limits, ban parking at corners for better visibility, fund safer crossings, and demand enforcement against reckless driving.
