      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      A right turn at 5th and 66th

      District 4: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      Just after dusk at 5th Avenue and E 66th Street, a bus driver turned right and hit a 61-year-old woman in the crosswalk. Police logged crush injuries and a serious condition. Source.

      The toll does not slow

      Since Jan 1, 2022, District 4 has recorded 18 deaths, 3,931 injuries, and 7,099 crashes. Source.

      In the past year alone, there were 1,970 crashes, 1,106 injuries, 20 serious injuries, and 8 deaths here. Year to date, deaths are 8, up from 2 at this point last year. Source.

      Where streets fail

      Police data tie the worst harm to familiar corridors. FDR Drive leads with 4 deaths; 1st Avenue has 2; Avenue of the Americas shows 1. Source.

      The danger spikes mid‑afternoon and late at night. Three deaths came around 3 PM; three more around 10 PM. Source.

      Named contributing factors recur: police record inattention/distraction, failure to yield, and disregarded traffic control. Source.

      Heavy machines do heavy damage. For people on foot in this district since 2022, crashes involving drivers in SUVs are linked to 6 deaths; bus drivers to 2. Source.

      The fixes are on paper

      Daylighting saves sight lines. A Council bill would ban parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and require barriers at 1,000 intersections a year. Council Member Keith Powers is a co‑sponsor. Source.

      Cut the cars where the crowds are. The city has committed to a car‑free 34th Street busway in a Midtown rezoning deal. “I’m glad that we were able to secure a commitment on the 34th Street busway,” Powers said. Source. He added, “It’s time to get buses moving faster, and the busway will do just that.” Source.

      Hold power to the numbers

      This is Council District 4. Your officials include Council Member Keith Powers, Assembly Member Alex Bores, and State Senator Liz Krueger. The tools are clear: daylight intersections, harden turns, give leading walk times, and keep heavy traffic off people‑dense streets. Powers has backed daylighting and the 34th Street busway. Sources here and here.

      Citywide, there is one more lever: lower speeds and rein in repeat speeders. New Yorkers can push for both now. See how, and who to call, here.

      One turn. One body on the asphalt. The rest is policy, waiting to be used.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What area does this story cover?
      New York City Council District 4 in Manhattan. It includes Midtown–Times Square, Stuyvesant Town–Peter Cooper Village, Murray Hill–Kips Bay, East Midtown–Turtle Bay, United Nations, and Upper East Side–Carnegie Hill. It overlaps Manhattan CB5, CB6, and CB8.
      ▸ How many people have been hurt or killed here since 2022?
      Since Jan 1, 2022, the district has recorded 18 deaths, 3,931 injuries, and 7,099 crashes. In the past year, there were 1,970 crashes, 1,106 injuries, 20 serious injuries, and 8 deaths. Source.
      ▸ Where are the worst trouble spots?
      FDR Drive tops the list with 4 deaths, followed by 1st Avenue with 2 and Avenue of the Americas with 1. Risk rises around mid‑afternoon and late at night. Source.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes, Persons, Vehicles). We filtered for Council District 4 and the period Jan 1, 2022 to Sep 18, 2025, then counted total crashes, injuries, serious injuries, and deaths. We also used the last‑12‑months and year‑to‑date rollups provided in our dataset snapshot. You can explore the base datasets here.
