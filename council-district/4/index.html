      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 4?

      Four Dead, Hundreds Hurt—District 4 Streets Still Run Red

      Written July 11, 2025

      District 4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Crashes in District 4

      The streets do not forgive. In the last twelve months, four people died and thirteen suffered serious injuries in District 4. The dead include a 65-year-old, a cyclist, a pedestrian, and a man crushed by a bus. Over 900 were hurt—most of them walking, biking, or just trying to cross the street (NYC crash data).

      On January 26, a 39-year-old man was killed by a bus on East 51st. On February 18, two cyclists, both 67, died at Fifth Avenue and 51st. In June, an SUV reversed and struck an elderly pedestrian on Lexington. The numbers are cold, but the pain is not. A life ends. A family waits for a call that never comes.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Cars and trucks did the most harm—one killed, twelve seriously hurt. Motorcycles and mopeds left two with grave injuries. Bikes, too, took their toll: two deaths, two serious injuries. No one is safe, but the most vulnerable bleed the most (NYC crash data).

      Council Member Keith Powers: Action and Silence

      Council Member Keith Powers has backed some measures for safety. He co-sponsored the bill to ban parking near crosswalks, forcing daylight at intersections. He voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the desperate and the poor. He pushed for a buy-back program for unsafe e-bike batteries, saying, “We will provide them the opportunity to surrender that unsafe equipment and receive a safe replacement.”

      But Powers has also supported misguided e-bike crackdowns and speed limits that target delivery workers, not the drivers who kill. He called for using congestion pricing cameras for enforcement after the program was paused, but the streets remain clogged and dangerous (congestion pricing stalled).

      What Comes Next

      The carnage will not stop until the city lowers speed limits, redesigns streets, and ends the reign of cars over people. Call Council Member Powers. Demand a 20 mph citywide speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylight at every crossing. Demand enforcement that targets the real killers—reckless drivers, not the people trying to survive.

      Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the legislative branch of city government. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 4 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 73 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 4?
      It includes the Midtown-Times Square, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, United Nations, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Manhattan CB5, Manhattan CB6, and Manhattan CB8 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 4?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 1 death, 12 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 2 serious injuries. Bikes caused 2 deaths and 2 serious injuries (NYC crash data).
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents. Lower speeds, better street design, and strong enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, build protected bike lanes, daylight intersections, and focus enforcement on reckless drivers—not on people walking or biking.
