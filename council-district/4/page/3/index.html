      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 4?

      Six Dead. A Thousand Hurt. Councilman Powers, What Will It Take?

      Written July 28, 2025

      District 4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 28, 2025

      The Death Toll Grows

      Six people are dead. Over a thousand are hurt. In the last twelve months, District 4 saw 1,046 injuries and 16 serious injuries from 1,883 crashes. The dead include the old and the young. Three people over 65 lost their lives. Two more over 75. One in the prime of life, age 35 to 44, did not make it home (NYC Open Data).

      Just last week, a firefighter died on the FDR Drive. He fell from his motorcycle. A car hit him and kept going. Police said, “he was struck by an unknown vehicle that did not remain on the scene.” His family will bury him. The driver is still out there.

      On Canal Street, a hit-and-run killed a pedestrian and a cyclist. The community gathered to mourn. The crash, a reporter said, “is raising questions about a bigger safety problem at an intersection of Canal Street.”

      Who Pays the Price?

      Pedestrians and cyclists bleed most. In the last year, cars and SUVs killed three people and injured hundreds more. Trucks and buses added to the toll. Bikes and mopeds, too, but the numbers are dwarfed by the carnage from cars (NYC Open Data).

      The stories repeat. A woman crossing with the light, struck by a box truck. A cyclist crushed by a turning sedan. Blood on the crosswalk. Names fade. The pain remains.

      What Has Keith Powers Done?

      Council Member Keith Powers has backed some safety bills. He co-sponsored a law to ban parking near crosswalks, to “keep crosswalks clear, improve visibility, and protect people on foot and bike.” He supported repealing a law that delayed bike lanes, saying, “2023 is sadly on track to be the deadliest year in decades for cyclists, underscoring the need for a cohesive network of bike lanes across New York City.”

      He pushed for a city-funded buy-back program so delivery workers could trade unsafe e-bike batteries for safe ones: “We will provide them the opportunity to surrender that unsafe equipment and receive a safe replacement.”

      But the deaths keep coming. The bills move slow. The streets stay deadly.

      What Next?

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Council Member Powers. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand protected bike lanes, not promises. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Powers Supports Safety Boosting Sidewalk Shed Reform

      On April 18, 2025, the City Council passed and Mayor Eric Adams signed a five-bill package to overhaul sidewalk shed and scaffolding rules. Bottcher added, 'It’s time we cut back the endless sidewalk sheds that block our light.' The reforms target over 400 miles of lingering scaffolding, aiming to restore light and space to city sidewalks.

      Int 1105-2024
      Powers votes yes, boosting street safety transparency and project accountability.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.'       Int 1160-2025
      Powers votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.
