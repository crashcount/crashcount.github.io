      Blood in District 4: How Many Bodies Before City Hall Acts?

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed citation formatting, deduped citations.)

      District 4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Blood on the Asphalt

      Four people killed. Nearly 900 injured. That is the toll in District 4 over the last year. These are not just numbers—they are bodies on Lexington, 1st Avenue, 47th Street. Children, elders, cyclists, workers. Twelve suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again.

      Just last week, a city worker was slashed by an e-bike rider after a near collision at Broadway and Cedar. The worker was fixing a street sign. The cyclist nearly hit his truck. Words were exchanged. The blade came out. The worker bled. The cyclist fled. The DOT called it an “abhorrent assault of a NYC DOT employee who performs critical work to keep our city moving”. No arrests have been made.

      On July 15, a van towing a food cart broke loose near 42nd and 10th. The cart slammed into a parked car with a woman and a child inside. Police found 76 propane tanks and 75 gallons of gasoline in the van. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment. The victims survived. The city did not explode—this time. “Firefighters forced entry into the van, removing 76 20-pound propane cylinders and 15 five-gallon fuel containers.”

      Who Pays the Price

      The old, the young, the ones on foot or bike pay most. In the last year, three people aged 65–74 died. Thirty children were hurt. Cyclists and pedestrians keep getting hit. Taxis, SUVs, trucks, bikes—all play their part. But the street is not level. The bigger the vehicle, the harder the blow.

      Council Member Powers: Action and Silence

      Council Member Keith Powers has backed some safety bills. He co-sponsored a law to ban parking near crosswalks and clear sightlines for people walking and biking. He supported the repeal of a law that delayed bike lane construction, saying, “2023 is sadly on track to be the deadliest year in decades for cyclists, underscoring the need for a cohesive network of bike lanes across New York City.”

      But progress is slow. The 34th Street busway, which would have made streets safer for everyone, is on hold. Powers opposed the pause, but the cars keep coming. The city keeps waiting. The dead keep piling up.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Council Member Powers. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people on foot and bike. Every day you wait is another day someone does not come home.

      Cars and trucks caused the most harm: 1 death, 366 minor injuries, 151 moderate injuries, and 12 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 0 deaths, 16 minor injuries, 15 moderate injuries, and 2 serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 0 deaths, 52 minor injuries, 28 moderate injuries, and 2 serious injuries. Source: NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not inevitable. Policies like lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, and better enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, expand protected bike lanes, ban parking near crosswalks, and support automated enforcement against reckless driving.

      ""2023 is sadly on track to be the deadliest year in decades for cyclists, underscoring the need for a cohesive network of bike lanes across New York City. Unfortunately, current rules mean that bike lanes are often stuck in unnecessary red tape that delays their construction by months or even years. Intro. 417 will significantly streamline the approval process and I am happy to support its passage."" -- Keith Powers

      On December 7, 2023, the City Council passed Bill 417, repealing a law that delayed bike lane construction. The bill, sponsored by Council Member Lincoln Restler, cleared the Council with a 32-15 vote. It reduces the waiting period after community board notification from 90 days plus 45 after a hearing, to just 14 days. The matter summary reads: 'The City Council repealed a decade-old law that imposed lengthy delays on the city before it could break ground on new bike lanes.' Restler said, 'No other transportation project in New York City...requires this type of extended dead period.' Council Member Keith Powers backed the bill, citing rising cyclist deaths. The Department of Transportation called bike lanes 'life-saving infrastructure.' The amended bill now covers all bike lanes, regardless of length. Advocates say this will help install more lanes and protect vulnerable road users.
