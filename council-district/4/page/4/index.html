      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 4?

      Streets Still Bleed While City Hall Waits

      Written July 24, 2025

      District 4: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 24, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In District 4, the numbers do not lie. Six people killed and over a thousand injured in the last year alone. The dead include a cyclist, a child, and three people over 65. The wounded are everywhere—on bikes, on foot, at the crosswalk, on the curb.

      Just last month, a sedan turned left and crushed a cyclist on East 41st. A week before, a convertible struck a child on East 14th. The blood dries, but the street remembers.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm. In three years, they killed 4 people and injured over 550. Taxis, trucks, and buses followed. Bikes and mopeds hurt dozens, but the deadliest force is always the car.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Waiting

      Council Member Keith Powers has spoken for safer streets. He backed the repeal of a law that delayed bike lanes, saying, “2023 is sadly on track to be the deadliest year in decades for cyclists, underscoring the need for a cohesive network of bike lanes across New York City” (repeal of a law that delayed bike lanes). He co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, to keep sightlines clear for people on foot and bike. But the bill sits in committee, waiting.

      Powers also led on battery safety for delivery workers, saying, “We will provide them the opportunity to surrender that unsafe equipment and receive a safe replacement” (battery safety for delivery workers).

      But the streets are not safe. The deaths keep coming. The city moves slow. The next crash is already on the calendar.

      Call to Action: Demand More, Demand Now

      Call Council Member Powers. Demand the citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every crosswalk. Demand protected bike lanes, not just promises.

      Every day of delay is another name on the list. The blood dries, but the street remembers.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 4 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 73 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 4?
      It includes the Midtown-Times Square, Stuyvesant Town-Peter Cooper Village, Murray Hill-Kips Bay, East Midtown-Turtle Bay, United Nations, Upper East Side-Carnegie Hill, Manhattan CB5, Manhattan CB6, and Manhattan CB8 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 73, AD 74, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28 and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 4?
      Cars and trucks killed 4 people and injured over 550. Motorcycles and mopeds caused dozens of injuries but no deaths. Bikes injured 56 people and caused no deaths. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Most crashes are preventable. Better street design, lower speed limits, and stronger enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass laws for lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, ban parking near crosswalks, and demand faster action from city agencies.

      Int 1138-2024
      Powers co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill bars cars from blocking crosswalks. No standing or parking within 20 feet. City must install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections yearly. Streets clear. Sightlines open. Danger cut.

      Int 1138-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 5, 2024. The bill states: “prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.” Council Member Erik D. Bottcher leads, joined by Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and over two dozen co-sponsors. The law orders the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections each year, up from 100. The city must also run outreach and education. The bill aims to keep crosswalks clear, improve visibility, and protect people on foot and bike. No more hiding behind parked cars. The committee laid the bill over on April 21, 2025.

      Int 0346-2024
      Powers votes yes to decriminalize jaywalking, boosting pedestrian safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians now cross anywhere, any time. No summons. Law strips drivers of excuses. Streets shift. Power tilts to people on foot.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, rewrites New York’s pedestrian rules. The bill, heard by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passed on September 26, 2024, and became law on October 26, 2024. Its title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way.” Sponsored by Council Member Tiffany Cabán and co-sponsored by Narcisse, Won, Hanif, Hudson, and Restler, the law lets pedestrians cross streets anywhere, even against signals. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks. The Department of Transportation must educate all street users on new rights and responsibilities. The law removes a tool police used to target walkers. It shifts blame from people on foot to the system and those behind the wheel.

      Int 0745-2024
      Powers votes yes on bill requiring DOT to report micromobility data, no direct safety impact.
      ▸

      City law now forces DOT to reveal where bikes and scooters go. Streets and bridges, protected or not. Data goes public. Riders’ movement, danger, and safety projects laid bare. No more hiding the facts.

      Int 0745-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It was introduced April 11, 2024, and passed on August 15, 2024.       Powers Supports Safety Boosting E-Bike Battery Trade-In Program
      ▸

The program, previewed at a press conference with Mayor Adams, will open for applications by year’s end after a public hearing on August 21. Fire Commissioner Laura Kavanagh called current illegal charging 'effectively death traps.' The city will also fund a $2 million battery and moped trade-in program. Delivery worker advocates want the new stations to be free or low-cost. Community board opposition has slowed previous efforts. The mayor remains neutral on state e-bike registration proposals.
