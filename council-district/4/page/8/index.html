      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 4?

      District 4: Crosswalks filled, sirens constant

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 4: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Sixth Avenue at West 47th. A taxi stands. A woman, 61, crosses with the signal. The data lists “Driver Inexperience.” One person dies in that crash record; the pedestrian survives with a hip bruise. The driver does not. It is all in the city file for April 22, 2024.

      First Avenue. West 51st Street. Two 67‑year‑old cyclists are listed as killed at 3:05 p.m. The SUV is parked. The city log shows two deaths and little else. It is bare and cold, like a tag on a toe. The record sits in CrashID 4796322.

      On the FDR, northbound, 3:48 a.m. A 31‑year‑old motorcyclist is marked “Apparent Death.” Helmet noted. Ejected. The file is short. The life was not. See CrashID 4830014.

      District 4 has logged 5,531 crashes since 2022. Eleven people are dead. 2,995 are hurt. The city’s rollup shows SUVs and cars tied to three pedestrian deaths; buses and trucks to one more. These counts come straight from the district stats.

      Corners that don’t forgive

      The map shows pain on the avenues. Avenue of the Americas leads the list with one death and 63 injuries. Park Avenue follows with 55 wounded. First Avenue adds another death and 67 injuries. The FDR Drive shows a death and 232 injuries. Lexington logs 58 more. These are the top intersections in the district data.

      The clock tells its own story. Injuries rise through the afternoon. The worst hour is 3 p.m. to 4 p.m. with three deaths and 180 injuries. Five p.m. shows one more death and 198 injuries. Late night is not spared: deaths appear at 1 a.m., 3 a.m., 10 a.m., 9 p.m., and 10 p.m. The harm does not sleep.

      The causes list is blunt. “Other” leads, with six deaths and 1,266 injuries. “Vulnerable road user error” appears in two deaths and 149 injuries. Distraction shows 90 injuries. Alcohol is present in 13 injuries. “Failure to yield” and “disregarded traffic control” each stack dozens more. The labels are dry. The outcomes are not.

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the hit

      Pedestrians: four dead, 806 injured. Cyclists: five dead, 694 injured. The city’s “causes of pedestrian injuries” ledger points to SUVs and cars first (195 cases, three deaths), then taxis (118 injuries), then trucks (56 injuries, five serious). Bikes strike 95. Buses, 19, with one death.

      At Sixth Avenue and West 58th, 10:22 p.m., an 83‑year‑old woman crossing with the signal is hit by a left‑turning SUV. “Apparent Death,” the file says. The driver is licensed. The vehicle damage: left front bumper. It is all there in CrashID 4792095.

      At East 51st off Fifth, 9:34 p.m., a pedestrian not in the roadway is listed as killed. A southbound bus shows right‑front bumper impact. A parked boom lift is in the record. The dead man is 39. See CrashID 4788709.

      Promises and pressure

      When the city moves, it is after blood. After two people were killed at Bowery and Canal by a car allegedly doing over 100 mph, the transportation chief said, “we are taking immediate steps to fortify this intersection”. Advocates answered that “the vast majority of the corridor will remain deadly.” The words are on Gothamist and NY1.

      Here in District 4, the council member backed a bill to ban parking within 20 feet of crosswalks. Daylighting keeps people alive where drivers turn blind. He also pushed to cut bike lane red tape, calling lanes “life‑saving infrastructure.”

      What would fix these blocks now

      Start with the corners. Daylight every approach at Sixth, Park, First, Lexington. Harden the turns. Add leading walk signals. The bill on daylighting sets a pace citywide; the need on these avenues is plain in the crash counts for Avenue of the Americas and Park Avenue.

      Target the hours where bodies fall. Mid‑afternoon into the rush. Put enforcement where the deaths are: left turns at crosswalks, red lights at wide avenues, speed on the FDR.

      Then go bigger. Lower the default speed limit using city authority cited in our action guide. And fit repeat speeders with controls. The state bill would require an intelligent speed device after 11 points in 18 months or 16 camera tickets in a year. This is laid out in our Take Action page, with sources for the risk posed by repeat offenders.

      The toll of waiting

      In the last 12 months, seven died here. Year‑to‑date deaths are up six‑fold over last year. The numbers sit there, heavy. The streets keep moving.

      One last fact: at 3 p.m., this district has three deaths and 180 injuries. School lets out. Offices break. The light turns green. The counting continues.

      For ways to press City Hall and Albany to act, see our action guide.

      ▸ Where are the worst spots in District 4?
      Avenue of the Americas leads with one death and 63 injuries. Park Avenue has 55 injuries. First Avenue shows one death and 67 injuries. The FDR Drive lists one death and 232 injuries. Lexington Avenue has 58 injuries, per district data.

      Injuries climb through the afternoon. 3 p.m. to 4 p.m. shows three deaths and 180 injuries. 5 p.m. records one death and 198 injuries. Deaths also appear late at night and mid‑morning.
      ▸ What is causing these crashes?
      The top label is “Other,” tied to six deaths and 1,266 injuries. Two deaths and 149 injuries fall under “vulnerable road user error.” Distraction, failure to yield, and disregarding traffic control each add dozens more.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Daylight crosswalks, harden turns, and add leading pedestrian intervals at Sixth, Park, First, and Lexington. Target enforcement at peak hours and left turns. Then push citywide speed limits lower and require speed limiters for repeat offenders; see our action guide.
