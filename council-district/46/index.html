      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 46?

      District 46: Belt Parkway's deadly toll

      Written August 15, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Finisher pass)

      District 46: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 15, 2025

      Recent crashes

      Last week, near the Knapp Street exit of the Belt Parkway, a car flipped and a 22‑year‑old driver died; police and reporters describe several early‑morning collisions that left two people dead and others critically injured (Gothamist, 2025-08-05). A few months earlier a motorcyclist died at Avenue U and Flatbush Avenue, and in January a left turn at Avenue L and E 88th Street killed a person crossing in the crosswalk. Police said a driver struck and killed a 47‑year‑old pedestrian at Broadway and Suydam Street and then left the scene (NY Daily News, 2025-08-03).

      The toll in District 46

      Crash records show 18 people killed and 33 seriously injured in Council District 46 since 2022 (NYC Open Data, accessed 2025-08-15). The burden falls heaviest on people walking: cars and SUVs account for most pedestrian deaths (9), with trucks and buses responsible for at least 1. Contributing‑factor fields in the crash data flag broad categories such as “other” and “vulnerable road user error,” while individual reports frequently list unsafe speed and driver inattention.

      Hotspots repeat. The Belt Parkway and Avenue L appear among the district’s most dangerous locations, and Flatlands Avenue also shows a high crash count. Crashes that kill are more likely in the evening and late night—data show elevated fatal counts around 6–11 p.m.—so improved lighting and targeted night enforcement are essential.

      What Council Member Narcisse has done

      Council Member Mercedes Narcisse has supported measures that affect street safety: she voted for a law to remove abandoned vehicles quickly to clear sightlines and crosswalks (Int 0857-2024, NYC Council – Legistar, 2025-06-30). She also backed decriminalizing certain pedestrian crossings to reduce biased enforcement (Streetsblog NYC, 2024-09-26). Last week she co‑sponsored a bill to crack down on unlicensed commuter vans; that measure remains in committee (Int 1347-2025, NYC Council – Legistar, introduced 2025-08-14).

      These steps matter, but the district’s fatal crashes point to pressing engineering and enforcement needs: speed and large vehicles are central to the pattern of harm.

      Local recommendations

      • On Belt Parkway ramps and the Flatlands corridor: lower posted speeds, redesign ramp geometry to force slower turning speeds (hardened turns), and add targeted evening enforcement and improved lighting.
      • At Avenue L and other repeat intersections: daylight corners (clear parking near crosswalks), install leading pedestrian intervals (LPIs), and add high‑visibility crosswalks.
      • Use data to identify repeat hotspots and deploy short‑term traffic calming (bollards, curb extensions) while DOT pursues redesigns.

      Citywide fixes tied to local findings

      District 46’s pattern—excess speed, large vehicles, repeated hotspots—underscores two citywide priorities: lower NYC’s default speed limit, and require intelligent speed‑assistance (speed limiters) for habitual speeders. Advocates and bills at the state and city level have pushed similar measures; the Stop Super Speeders proposals, for example, would require speed‑mitigating technology for drivers with repeated camera or point violations. Local engineering and enforcement plus these broader rules together would reduce the odds that a dangerous driver or a deadly high‑speed approach returns to the same corner.

      What you can do

      Demand DOT and your Councilmember act on the known hazards: lower speeds on the Belt and Flatlands corridors, daylight Avenue L corners, install LPIs, and fund lighting and targeted night enforcement. Use our Take Action guide to contact Councilmember Mercedes Narcisse and the Mayor’s office. Hold officials to specific, local fixes now—before another family grieves.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Which areas are in District 46?
      It includes the Madison, Sheepshead Bay-Manhattan Beach-Gerritsen Beach, Flatlands, Marine Park-Mill Basin-Bergen Beach, Canarsie, Marine Park-Plumb Island, Mcguire Fields, Canarsie Park & Pier, Barren Island-Floyd Bennett Field, and Brooklyn CB56.
      ▸ Where does District 46 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 41 and state senate district SD 19.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 46?
      Cars and Trucks: 10 pedestrian deaths and 457 injuries (cars/SUVs 9 deaths, 429 injuries; trucks/buses 1 death, 28 injuries). Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 7 injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 3 injuries. Source: NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are these just unavoidable “accidents”?
      No. The same roads and hours repeat. Lower speeds, tighter turns, daylighted corners, and leading pedestrian intervals prevent crashes. Start with the Belt, Avenue L, and Flatlands. Then expand.
      ▸ What can local politicians do right now?
      Lower the citywide default speed to 20 mph. Require intelligent speed limiters for repeat speeders. Fund daylighting and LPIs at dangerous corners. Press DOT to redesign the Belt ramps and Flatlands corridor. Keep abandoned cars cleared in 72 hours. Use our Take Action guide.
      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      It is the city’s lawmaking body. Council members introduce and vote on bills, hold hearings in committees, and oversee agencies like DOT.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1347-2025
      Narcisse co-sponsors crackdown on unlicensed commuter vans, worsening overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council moves to hit rogue vans hard. A checklist. Max fines for each breach. TLC, NYPD, DOT must act together. Crack down on illegal operators who imperil riders and people on the street. The bill sits in committee.

      Int 1347-2025 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced and referred on August 14, 2025. The bill’s title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to enforcing violations against unlicensed commuter vans.” It orders TLC, with NYPD and DOT, to keep a compliance checklist. Brooks-Powers, Mercedes Narcisse, and Chris Banks. Status: Committee. If enacted, it takes effect 120 days later. It targets illegal operators who put riders and bystanders at risk.

      Int 1347-2025
      Narcisse co-sponsors unlicensed commuter van crackdown, worsening overall street safety.
      ▸

      Council introduces Int 1347-2025 to curb rogue commuter vans. TLC, NYPD, and DOT must keep a safety checklist. Officers who spot an unlicensed van stack every charge and levy max fines. A push to shield riders and people on the street.

      Int 1347-2025 is in Committee. It sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after introduction and referral on August 14, 2025. The bill’s title reads, “A Local Law … in relation to enforcing violations against unlicensed commuter vans.” It orders TLC, with NYPD and DOT, to maintain a checklist of laws on vehicle and driver licensure, service authorization, insurance, and inspections. When an officer stops an unlicensed van, they must review the list and issue the maximum penalty for each violation. Primary sponsor Nantasha M. Williams is joined by Selvena N. Brooks-Powers, Mercedes Narcisse, Chris Banks, and Farah N. Louis. The aim is to protect riders and bystanders from unlicensed operators who skip the rules.

      Int 0857-2024
      Narcisse votes yes to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council orders swift removal of abandoned, derelict cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. No plates, no stickers, no excuses. Police and sanitation must act. Safer crossings for all who walk, ride, or wait.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council on June 30, 2025. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to vehicles reported as abandoned to the department of sanitation," requires the Department of Sanitation to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours of report. The NYPD must tow cars lacking valid plates or stickers. Prime sponsor Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led, joined by Crystal Hudson, Amanda Farías, Lincoln Restler, and others. The law targets street hazards, clearing blocked sightlines and crosswalks. It aims to cut risks for pedestrians and cyclists by removing abandoned vehicles fast.
