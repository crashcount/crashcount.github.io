      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 36?

      Another Child Dead. Still Waiting for Courage at City Hall.

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, and cleaned citations.)

      District 36: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      The Toll: Children in the Crosswalk

      An eight-year-old boy is dead. On June 28, Mordica Keller was crossing Eastern Parkway with his sister when a black Honda Pilot struck him. Witnesses saw him dragged from under the SUV. “I just saw a lot of blood gushing out of his ears, his mouth,” said a witness. The driver stayed. The boy did not.

      This is not rare. In the last twelve months, District 36 saw 1,116 crashes, 637 injuries, and 3 killed. Children are not spared. Forty-eight kids were hurt. No one is safe at the curb.

      The Pattern: Streets Built for Death

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. 5 deaths, 312 minor injuries, 110 moderate injuries, and 4 serious injuries came from cars and SUVs alone. Trucks and buses add more. Cyclists and moped riders are thrown and broken. The street is a gauntlet.

      Residents know the danger. “Yeah, this street is dangerous,” said Milli Cent. The city studies. The city delays. The bodies pile up.

      The Response: Promises, Delays, and a Vanishing Bike Lane

      Council Member Chi Ossé has called for safer streets. He backed the Bedford Avenue protected bike lane, calling it “a win for everyone.” The lane was built, then Mayor Adams tore it out. Ossé condemned the removal. He has co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and speed up bike lane construction. He voted to legalize jaywalking, shifting blame off pedestrians. But the pace is slow. The dead do not wait.

      What Now: Demand Action, Not Excuses

      Children are dying. The fixes are known: slower speeds, protected crossings, daylighted corners, real bike lanes. Call Council Member Ossé. Call the Mayor. Demand they finish the job. Every day of delay is another family broken. Do not wait for another child to bleed out on the street.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 36 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 43 and state senate district SD 20.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 36?
      It includes the Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Bedford-Stuyvesant (East), Crown Heights (North), Brooklyn CB8, and Brooklyn CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 56, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 20 and SD 25.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 36?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm: 5 deaths, 312 minor injuries, 110 moderate injuries, and 4 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 0 deaths but 18 minor, 5 moderate, and 2 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths but 3 minor and 4 moderate injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths but 7 minor and 8 moderate injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to reduce traffic violence?
      They can pass and fund laws for protected bike lanes, daylighted corners, lower speed limits, and enforce driver accountability. They can also push city agencies to act faster and not delay life-saving changes.

      Int 0291-2022
      Ossé votes yes, boosting citywide safety with new greenway plan.
      ▸

      The Council passed Int 0291-2022, forcing city agencies to map, plan, and report on greenways. The law demands annual updates and public engagement. It aims to carve out safe, car-free corridors for walkers and cyclists. The mayor returned it unsigned.

      Int 0291-2022, now Local Law 115 of 2022, was enacted by the City Council on November 27, 2022. The bill came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, first introduced April 28, 2022. The measure compels the city to plan and maintain safe routes for non-drivers, with regular public reporting and accountability.

      Int 0501-2022
      Ossé co-sponsors bill boosting civilian reporting to improve street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants near schools. Civilians can report violators. Each offense draws a $175 fine. The city pays whistleblowers a cut. The bill stalled. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0501-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on June 2, 2022. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations,' aimed to create a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 1,320 feet of a school. The penalty: $175 per violation, enforced through the Office of Administrative Trials and Hearings. Civilians, except city employees, could file complaints and receive 25% of collected fines. The Department of Transportation would support this with a phased-in reporting program and annual public reports. Council Member Carlina Rivera led as primary sponsor, joined by over two dozen co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.

      Ossé Supports Safety Boosting End to Parking Minimums
      ▸

The group argues that parking minimums raise construction costs, encourage car use, and block green space. Restler warned he is 'far more likely to vote no' on projects without a parking waiver. Advocates and policy experts back the move, and related state legislation is pending.
