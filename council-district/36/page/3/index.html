      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 36?

      Blood on Broadway, Silence from City Hall

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, deduped and reordered citations.)

      District 36: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      In District 36, the street is a wound that never closes. Three people killed, 621 injured, eight left with injuries so grave they may never heal—all in the last twelve months (NYC Open Data). The dead do not speak. The living limp, or grieve, or cross with eyes fixed on the next car. Just days ago, a man tried to cross near Broadway and East New York Avenue. A gray Ford hit him and kept going. He died at Brookdale Hospital. The driver vanished. Police [said, “The driver of the Ford sped off, never stopping.”] (https://www.nydailynews.com/2025/07/04/hit-and-run-driver-mows-down-man-36-in-brooklyn-crash/)

      This was the second hit-and-run in the city in 24 hours. The numbers are not just numbers. They are the sound of sirens at night, the hush after impact, the shoes left in the crosswalk.

      Patterns of Harm

      SUVs and cars do most of the killing. In this district, they caused five of the deaths and over 400 injuries to people on foot. Trucks and buses hurt dozens more. Bikes and mopeds are not blameless, but their toll is a fraction of the carnage (NYC Open Data).

      Children are not spared. In the last year, 44 people under 18 were injured in crashes here. One child was killed in a car crash on Brooklyn Avenue and Atlantic Avenue. He was four years old (NYC Open Data).

      Leadership: Words, Votes, and Waiting

      Council Member Chi Ossé has spoken up for safer streets. He called the delay of the Bedford Avenue protected bike lane “unacceptable” and demanded action: “The Bedford Avenue bike lane has been unacceptably dangerous since it was first laid, and for years, the people of my district have been promised that it would be protected.”

      Ossé has voted to legalize jaywalking, co-sponsored bills for protected bike lanes, and backed the SAFE Streets Act. But the street still runs red. Promises and plans do not stop a speeding car. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Call Council Member Ossé. Demand the protected bike lanes. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand a 20 mph speed limit, citywide. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents local districts like District 36. [NYC Council – Legistar]
      ▸ Where does District 36 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 43 and state senate district SD 20. [NYC Open Data]
      ▸ Which areas are in District 36?
      It includes the Bedford-Stuyvesant (West), Bedford-Stuyvesant (East), Crown Heights (North), Brooklyn CB8, and Brooklyn CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 43, AD 56, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 20 and SD 25. [NYC Open Data]
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 36?
      Cars and Trucks caused 5 deaths and over 400 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds caused no deaths and a handful of injuries. Bikes caused no deaths and a small number of injuries. [NYC Open Data]
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not just accidents—they are the result of choices and policies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can push for protected bike lanes, daylighting at intersections, lower speed limits, and stronger enforcement against reckless drivers. They can also hold city agencies accountable for delays.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
