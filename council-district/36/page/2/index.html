      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 36?

      Broadway takes another life. The clock keeps ticking.

      Written August 24, 2025 (Revised August 24, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 36: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 24, 2025

      Another driver. Same ending.

      • A 47-year-old man was struck and dragged more than 50 feet at Broadway and Suydam around 3:45 a.m. The driver fled. Police said they were seeking the vehicle and, early on, that it might have been a garbage truck. The victim died there in the road (Gothamist; NY Daily News; NYC Open Data).
      • A 49-year-old pedestrian died in the crosswalk at Bedford and Lafayette just after 3 a.m. The car’s front end hit him. He never got up (NYC Open Data).

      “Police were looking … for the driver of a car that struck and dragged a 47-year-old man,” the report said. “Officials said it was not immediately clear whether the man was walking in a crosswalk.” The one fact that is clear: he is dead (Gothamist).

      Night after night.

      • In this district, deaths spike after dark. From midnight to 5 a.m., the toll rises, then again at 3–4 a.m., and at 11 p.m. Three more deaths hit at 11 p.m.; two at 3 a.m.; two at 4 a.m. (NYC Open Data).

      Three corners. One fix.

      • Broadway logged one death and 125 injuries. Atlantic Avenue logged two deaths and 158 injuries. Bedford Avenue logged one death and 70 injuries. These are repeat scenes (NYC Open Data).
      • Contributing factors in these crashes include inattention and disregarding signals. “Failure to yield” shows up again and again in the harm, even when it doesn’t end in death (NYC Open Data).

      Concrete steps:

      • Daylight every crosswalk at these corridors. Clear 20 feet of sightlines with barriers and posts. A Council bill would ban parking near crosswalks and order at least 1,000 daylighted intersections a year. It’s laid over in committee (Int 1138-2024).
      • Harden turns and add leading pedestrian intervals at Broadway, Atlantic, and Bedford. Cut the angles that break bodies. Post it. Paint it. Keep it.
      • Target late-night hotspots. The data points to the hours. Send the crews to where the blood dries (NYC Open Data).

      Officials know what works — do they?

      • The Council Member for this district pressed DOT to stop delaying the protected Bedford Avenue bike lane. “Unacceptable,” he wrote. The lane “has been unacceptably dangerous since it was first laid.” The delay drags on (Streetsblog).
      • The city passed a law to put warning decals on taxi doors to curb dooring. Small fix. Limited reach. It’s done (Int 0193-2024).
      • The Council also voted to speed removal of derelict cars that block views and crosswalks. That helps clear sightlines (Int 0857-2024).

      The pattern does not break.

      • In the last 12 months here: 1,532 crashes, 5 dead, 940 injured. This year to date: 1,012 crashes, 3 dead, 628 injured, up sharply from last year at this time (Period stats).
      • Pedestrians bear it. Seven pedestrian deaths since 2022. Hundreds hurt. SUVs lead the harm to people on foot: 175 pedestrian injuries and 5 deaths tied to SUVs, more than any other vehicle type (NYC Open Data).

      Citywide fixes. Fewer funerals.

      • Lower the speed. New York now has the power to drop residential limits. A 20 mph default cuts the force that kills. The law granting that power exists. The city must choose to use it (Take Action).
      • Stop repeat speeders. Require intelligent speed assistance for drivers who rack up violations. One bill would force it on the worst offenders. Families are already grieving the proof of what repeat speeding does (Take Action).

      One last note.

      At Broadway and Suydam, police said, “A driver struck and killed a 47-year-old pedestrian… then left the scene.” The man stayed where he fell. The driver did not (NY Daily News).

      Int 0193-2024 Ossé votes yes on taxi dooring warning decals, no major safety change.
      ▸

      May 1 - Council passes law. Taxis and for-hire cars must post bold warnings on rear doors. Riders face the message: look for cyclists before swinging the door. A move to cut dooring. City acts. Cyclists stay in the crosshairs.

      Int 0193-2024, now law, cleared the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and passed City Council on May 1, 2025. The bill, titled 'A Local Law...requiring taxis and for-hire vehicles to display a decal warning passengers to look for cyclists when opening the door,' demands clear warning signs on all rear passenger doors. Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Gutiérrez, Hudson, Rivera, Won, and others. The Taxi and Limousine Commission must provide decals at no cost. The law aims to fight dooring, a threat to cyclists citywide. Enacted May 31, 2025, it takes effect in 120 days.

      Int 1105-2024 Ossé votes yes, boosting street safety transparency and project accountability.
      ▸

      Apr 10 - Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.' Council Member Julie Won led sponsorship, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law demands DOT post annual and monthly updates on every project tied to the master plan—listing details, timelines, funding, and setbacks. This law brings sunlight to street safety work, making the city answer for every mile and missed deadline.
