      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 21?

      District 21’s deadly routine: late nights, wide roads, no mercy

      Written August 25, 2025 (Revised August 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 21: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 5, 2025

      Two boys did not come home. An 8-year-old was hit in a crosswalk at 100 Street and 31 Avenue. A pickup turned left and crushed him. Police wrote “Failure to Yield” and “Driver Inattention.” He had the light. He died there. Source.

      A man ordering from a food truck in Queens never finished his meal. Witnesses said a speeding car slammed into the men. Two pedestrians and the driver died. CBS New York reported it.

      Down by JFK, a 52-year-old crossed at 155th Street and South Conduit. A driver hit him and fled. Police said, “The driver sped off without stopping.” He died at the hospital. No arrests. NY Daily News and Gothamist both confirmed it.

      We count 9 dead and 2,676 injured on these streets since 2022. The crashes number 4,786. City data track every one.

      Where the bodies pile up

      The worst is the Grand Central Parkway: 2 deaths, 434 injuries. Northern Boulevard appears twice on the list. One entry notes a death. Roosevelt Avenue racks up injuries too. These are wide, fast roads. People walk across them to get home, to work, to school. Source.

      SUVs and sedans do most of the harm to people on foot: 389 pedestrian injuries and 1 death tied to passenger vehicles; trucks add another death and 23 cases. Bikes, mopeds, and motorcycles account for far fewer pedestrian injuries. City data.

      The clock tells a story too. Injury spikes hit late afternoon and night. Deaths stack up at 3 a.m., 4 a.m., 5 a.m., 10 a.m., 4 p.m., 6 p.m., and 10 p.m., 22:00. Darkness and speed meet tired eyes. People die. Source.

      What causes the hurt

      Officials mark “Failure to Yield,” “Distraction,” “Unsafe Speed,” and “Other.” “Other” hides 3 deaths and 792 injuries. Failure to yield appears in the 8‑year‑old’s file. Speed shows up in the teen motorcyclist’s death on Astoria Boulevard at 101 Street—“Unsafe Speed” and “Traffic Control Disregarded.” He was 16. He died there. City data.

      A patrol borough to the south keeps losing people to hit‑and‑runs. Police said detectives were looking. “No arrests have been made.” The grief does not wait. Gothamist and ABC7 said the same.

      What the law can do, now

      Albany handed the city the power to lower speeds. A 20 mph default saves lives. Our city has the tool. The Department of Transportation can draw the lines. The Council can back it. We do not need another vigil to prove what speed does. See our call to act: lower the limit citywide.

      The worst repeat offenders keep killing. One small share of drivers causes a huge share of deaths. Bills in Albany would force speed limiters on cars that rack up tickets or points. It is simple: a car that cannot race cannot mow down a family at a food truck. Read how to press your lawmakers: Stop Super Speeders.

      Local fixes that cannot wait

      • Daylight the corners on Northern Boulevard and Roosevelt Avenue. Clear the sightlines. Pull back parking.
      • Harden left turns at 31 Avenue and 100 Street. Protect walkers with steel and concrete, not paint.
      • Target late‑night speeding on the Grand Central service roads and South Conduit. Nights are when bodies fall. The data say so. Source.

      Voices on the record

      “Joseph Lee terrorized other drivers as he purposefully drove the wrong way on a busy Queens highway,” Queens DA Melinda Katz said after a jury conviction in a separate Queens case. “Two motorists were badly hurt.” Lee told police he entered the expressway “because I wanted to hurt people.” He got eight years. amNY.

      “The driver sped off without stopping.” NY Daily News.

      “Witnesses say a speeding car slammed into the men.” CBS New York.

      The list grows. The numbers are not abstract. They are names, jobs, schools, kitchens gone quiet. Lower the speeds. Lock down the repeat speeders. Do it before another corner fills with flowers.

      Take one step today. Tell City Hall and Albany to act: Take Action.

      Int 1105-2024
      Moya votes yes, boosting street safety transparency and project accountability.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.' Council Member Julie Won led sponsorship, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law demands DOT post annual and monthly updates on every project tied to the master plan—listing details, timelines, funding, and setbacks. This law brings sunlight to street safety work, making the city answer for every mile and missed deadline.
