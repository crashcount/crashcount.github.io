      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 21?

      District 21: Death on Northern, blood on the Parkway

      Written August 27, 2025 (Revised August 27, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 21: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 27, 2025

      Since 2022, District 21 has logged 4,802 crashes, 10 dead, 2,688 hurt, and 25 seriously injured. SUVs and cars lead the toll, with trucks not far behind (NYC Open Data).

      • Pedestrians: 3 dead, 477 hurt.
      • Cyclists: 2 dead, 204 hurt.
      • Vehicle occupants: 5 dead, 1,866 hurt.
        Heavy vehicles—trucks and buses—account for fewer injuries but strike hard (NYC Open Data).

      Nights are when it breaks

      The hurt piles up after dark. Injuries spike from 8 PM through 11 PM, with death appearing even earlier—two killed at 4 PM, two more at 10 PM, and more in the small hours around 3–5 AM and 10 AM (NYC Open Data). Distraction is logged often. So is failure to yield. But most deaths sit in the “other” bin, a black box with five of the ten fatalities. That silence says enough (NYC Open Data).

      On Mar 26, 2025, a 43‑year‑old driver died in a crash on 42 Ave (crash record). On Feb 26, 2025, a 23‑year‑old died at Northern Blvd and 107 St after a three‑vehicle collision (crash record). On Mar 13, 2024, an 8‑year‑old boy, crossing with the signal at 31 Ave and 100 St, was killed by a left‑turning pickup; the file lists failure to yield and distraction (crash record). On Oct 10, 2023, a 16‑year‑old on a motorcycle died at Astoria Blvd and 101 St; the record cites traffic control disregarded and unsafe speed (crash record).

      Northern. Roosevelt. The Parkway.

      The bodies collect on the big roads. The Grand Central Parkway leads with 2 deaths and 434 injuries. Roosevelt Avenue counts 77 injuries. Northern Boulevard shows a death and more than a hundred injuries across entries. These are not accidents. They are patterns.

      Just outside the district line, the hit‑and‑run near JFK at 155 St and South Conduit left a 52‑year‑old man dead around 2:30 AM. “The operator of the vehicle fled the scene,” police said. No arrests (ABC7; Gothamist; Daily News). The lesson travels: high‑speed corridors, dark hours, a driver gone.

      The year isn’t kind

      Year to date, crashes hold at 882, but deaths tripled from 1 to 3. Injuries ticked up to 555. Serious injuries rose to 7. The young and the middle‑aged carry the pain: 18–24 and 35–44 each record a death this year (period stats).

      Pedestrians are hit most by sedans and SUVs. Trucks kill, too: one pedestrian death, plus serious wounds, in a small count that hides the weight of impact (vehicle rollup).

      What would actually stop the bleeding?

      Start at the hot spots. Daylight the crosswalks on Northern and Roosevelt. Harden the lefts at 31 Ave and 100 St. Give leading pedestrian intervals that hold turning drivers back. The parkway frontage needs speed control and lighting in the night hours when bodies fall. Pull trucks off local turns around schools and parks. These fixes are basic. They work where the city installs them. They are missing where people die (NYC Open Data).

      Citywide, the solutions are on the table. Albany gave NYC the power to set safer speeds. A citywide residential 20 MPH default is ready. It cuts deaths. The tools for camera‑caught repeat offenders are ready, too: intelligent speed assistance for the cars that won’t slow down. The worst few drivers do the most harm. Slowing every street and boxing in the super speeders will save lives. Details and actions are here: Take Action.

      Voices from the record

      “Joseph Lee terrorized other drivers as he purposefully drove the wrong way on a busy Queens highway and crashed into multiple cars,” Queens DA Melinda Katz said after an eight‑year sentence in a wrong‑way case; “Two motorists were badly hurt and still have not fully recovered” (amNY).

      Police on the JFK case: “The operator of the vehicle fled the scene after hitting the man” (ABC7).

      Hold the line

      This district includes East Elmhurst, Corona, North Corona, LaGuardia, and Flushing Meadows‑Corona Park. The map is long. The pain is local. Slow the cars. Fix the turns. Light the night. And stop the worst repeat speeders. Do it now: Take Action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where are the worst crash spots in District 21?
      The Grand Central Parkway leads the toll with 2 deaths and 434 injuries. Roosevelt Avenue logs 77 injuries. Northern Boulevard shows one death and many injuries across entries, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ When do crashes hit hardest?
      Injuries surge at night, especially 8–11 PM. Deaths cluster around late night and early morning hours, with additional spikes at 4 PM and 10 PM, per the hourly distribution.
      ▸ What kinds of vehicles are hurting pedestrians here?
      Sedans and SUVs cause most pedestrian injuries. Trucks and buses appear less often but account for severe outcomes, including a pedestrian death.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ What can I do right now?
      Press the city to lower the default speed limit to 20 MPH and to rein in repeat speeders with speed limiters. Then push local fixes at Northern, Roosevelt, and 31 Ave. Start here: /take_action/.

      Int 0857-2024 Moya votes yes to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Jun 30 - Council orders swift removal of abandoned, derelict cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. No plates, no stickers, no excuses. Police and sanitation must act. Safer crossings for all who walk, ride, or wait.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council on June 30, 2025.
