      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 17?

      Bronx River Parkway kills two; District 17 keeps bleeding

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 17: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Two men died on the Bronx River Parkway before dawn on August 11. Police say a 21‑year‑old in a Mercedes tried to pass a Volkswagen, hit it, then struck two motorcycles. Both riders, Manuel Amarante Penalo, 19, and Enrique Martinez, 21, were thrown and died at the hospital. The driver was arrested and charged with vehicular manslaughter and DWI, according to the Bronx DA’s complaint. He refused a chemical test. Police told reporters the southbound lanes near E. 223rd St. shut down for hours.

      “Two people were killed. He was drunk,” the sister said in court. “He’s just walking freely?” The lawyer said, “My client is prepared to contest these charges.”

      CBS said the car “struck two men on scooters,” and both died. Police arrested the driver.

      District 17’s body count

      In Council District 17 since 2022, at least 30 people have been killed and 3,686 injured in 6,319 crashes. Heavy hours stack up at night. From 9 p.m. to midnight, deaths hit hard, with spikes at 10 p.m. and 11 p.m. Serious injuries peak from 9 p.m. to 11 p.m. City data shows the pattern.

      The toll falls heaviest on people outside the car. Pedestrians: 10 dead, 653 hurt. Cyclists: 5 dead, 288 hurt. Another two people on mopeds and motorcycles died, with 116 injured. Cars and SUVs do most of the harm. Trucks and buses add weight. The dataset records the modes.

      Top danger blocks sit on Westchester Avenue and East 163rd Street. One man, 56, died at East 163rd and Westchester in 2024. A Ford SUV going straight struck him in the crosswalk, the record says. The plates were from Texas. The crash file is public.

      Night streets, hard edges

      The hours between 9 p.m. and 3 a.m. draw blood. At 11 p.m., injuries peak; serious injuries climb. After midnight, the deaths do not stop. District data flags “nighttime conditions” and “repeat hotspots.” The map does not lie. It’s in the city ledger.

      Reported contributing factors stack this way: “other” leads the count with 13 deaths; “vulnerable road user error” shows 6; “disregarded traffic control” adds another death. Speed shows up less in reports than it does on the pavement. Those categories come from the city files.

      What the Bronx has heard, and what it hasn’t

      DOT leadership in the Bronx has wobbled. At a 2023 hearing, Council Member Rafael Salamanca pressed DOT: “There is no borough commissioner in the borough of the Bronx. Please speed it up.” He has also backed street safety bills, including fast removal of abandoned cars that block sightlines and crosswalks. He voted yes. The bill passed the Council.

      At another hearing, Council leaders attacked proven redesigns, despite data showing fewer crashes and injuries. Salamanca warned about congestion from a Bronx project. DOT said the projects save lives. The exchange is on the record.

      Fix the blocks that keep killing

      The tools are basic. Daylight the corners at Westchester and East 163rd. Harden the turns on Southern Boulevard. Add leading pedestrian intervals and give walkers the head start. Mark and protect bike space where the injuries pile up. Target the late‑night hours on the repeat corridors with enforcement that sticks. District 17’s own hotspot list points the way.

      Citywide, the road is clear too. Lower the default speed limit. Albany gave the city that power. The city has not used it fully. Force repeat speeders to slow down with intelligent speed assistance for the worst plates. The bills exist. The victims do too. Here’s how to push it.

      Int 0346-2024
      Salamanca votes yes to decriminalize jaywalking, boosting pedestrian safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians now cross anywhere, any time. No summons. Law strips drivers of excuses. Streets shift. Power tilts to people on foot.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, rewrites New York’s pedestrian rules. The bill, heard by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passed on September 26, 2024, and became law on October 26, 2024. Its title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way.” Sponsored by Council Member Tiffany Cabán and co-sponsored by Narcisse, Won, Hanif, Hudson, and Restler, the law lets pedestrians cross streets anywhere, even against signals. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks. The Department of Transportation must educate all street users on new rights and responsibilities. The law removes a tool police used to target walkers. It shifts blame from people on foot to the system and those behind the wheel.

      Int 0745-2024
      Salamanca votes yes on bill requiring DOT to report micromobility data, no direct safety impact.
      ▸

      City law now forces DOT to reveal where bikes and scooters go. Streets and bridges, protected or not. Data goes public. Riders’ movement, danger, and safety projects laid bare. No more hiding the facts.

      Int 0745-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It was introduced April 11, 2024, and passed on August 15, 2024. The law orders the Department of Transportation to post current bicycle and micromobility ridership data online, updated monthly or at least yearly. The matter summary reads: 'requiring the department of transportation to provide information about bicycle and other micromobility device activity.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led as primary sponsor, joined by Farías, Restler, Hudson, Schulman, and others. The law demands transparency on where riders travel and what DOT does to keep them safe. This opens the books on bike movement and exposes gaps in protection.
