      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Leggett and Bruckner: A Mother in the Crosswalk, and the Bill Comes Due

      District 17: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 24, 2025

      She stepped into the crosswalk at Leggett Avenue and Bruckner Boulevard. A driver going north on Bruckner hit her and kept going, police said (NY Daily News).

      She is one of 40 people killed on District 17 streets since 2022, with 4,698 injured in that span (NYC Open Data).

      Nights take people

      The toll climbs after dark. Police records show six deaths around 3 AM and five at 5 AM in this district since 2022, with injuries peaking into the night (NYC Open Data). Westchester Avenue and East 163 Street keep showing up in the crash logs (NYC Open Data).

      Trucks and buses are part of the picture here. So are SUVs and cars. People walking and people on bikes pay the price (NYC Open Data).

      This month

      • Aug 30: a driver struck a 32‑year‑old in the crosswalk at Leggett and Bruckner and fled, police said (NY Daily News).

      The record says: act

      In 2025 so far, seven people have been killed in District 17, with 893 injured. By this time last year, nine were dead and 1,025 were hurt (NYC Open Data). The bodies fell slower this year. The pain is still here.

      Council Member Rafael Salamanca, Jr. pressed the city on Bronx leadership: “There is no borough commissioner in the borough of the Bronx… Please speed it up.” (Streetsblog NYC) He co‑sponsored a bill to get derelict cars off the street fast and voted yes when it came to the floor (NYC Council Legistar). He also backed cracking down on fake plates and called out warehouse traffic harms in Council negotiations (AMNY; Gothamist).

      Fix the corners that keep bleeding

      Start with the hotspots. Daylight the approaches. Add leading pedestrian intervals. Harden the turns. On truck routes, slow the right turns and protect the crosswalks. Target night enforcement where the deaths stack up. These are basic tools. Use them.

      Slow everyone down, stop the worst

      Citywide, the next steps are on the table. Lower the default speed limit and fit repeat offenders with speed limiters. Our city already has the levers and the bills in play. The only question is whether leaders will pull them. Salamanca represents this district. Assembly Member Chantel Jackson and State Senator Jose Serrano represent it in Albany. The public can ask all three to move now.

      “Asking you to use it. Lower our residential speed limit to 20 mph citywide,” says our call to action (Take Action).

      The woman in the crosswalk did not come home. Westchester and 163rd still tally crashes. Nights still take people. The fix is not a mystery. It’s a choice.

      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How bad is traffic violence in District 17?
      From Jan 1, 2022 to Sep 24, 2025, police-reported crashes in District 17 killed 40 people and injured 4,698. In 2025 so far, seven people have died and 893 have been injured. These figures come from NYC’s open crash datasets.
      ▸ Where are the danger zones?
      Westchester Avenue and East 163 Street appear repeatedly in crash records for this district. Nighttime hours, especially around 3 AM and 5 AM, show a heavy toll in deaths and injuries, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ What has Council Member Rafael Salamanca, Jr. done on street safety?
