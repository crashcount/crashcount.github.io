      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 17?

      Bruckner at Leggett: one mother down, a city still waiting

      Written August 31, 2025 (Revised August 31, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 17: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 31, 2025

      Just after midnight at Bruckner Boulevard and Leggett Avenue, a driver hit 32‑year‑old Dilmania Lopez de Rodriguez in the crosswalk. She died at Lincoln Hospital. Police say the driver kept going (ABC7; NY Post).

      She is one of 31 people killed on District 17 streets since Jan 1, 2022, alongside 3,703 injuries tracked in this period (NYC Open Data). Pedestrians make up 11 of those deaths; cyclists, 5 (NYC Open Data).

      Cars and SUVs do most of the harm here. They’re tied to 8 pedestrian deaths and hundreds of injuries. Trucks and buses add to the toll (NYC Open Data). Nights are rough. The small‑hours show repeated deaths around 1–6 AM, with more again late at night (NYC Open Data).

      Bruckner, Westchester, 163rd: danger you can map

      The hit‑and‑run that killed Lopez de Rodriguez happened at Bruckner and Leggett. Other hot spots stack up: Westchester Avenue and East 163rd Street both show heavy injury counts in this district (NYC Open Data). The patterns repeat. The bodies do not.

      Targeted fixes are not exotic: daylight the corners, give pedestrians a head start, harden turns, slow the turns, and tame truck movements on Bruckner‑adjacent streets. Nighttime enforcement where the deaths cluster would meet the problem where it lives. These are standard tools; the question is will they be used here.

      The record says urgency. The street says wait.

      Since 2024, year‑to‑date crashes in this district are rising against last year’s pace, with injuries and serious injuries also higher (NYC Open Data). That is the backdrop to a mother dying in a crosswalk on Bruckner Boulevard.

      Even at safer‑street hearings, Bronx leaders have had to beg for basic attention. “There is no borough commissioner in the borough of the Bronx… Please speed it up,” Council Member Rafael Salamanca Jr. told DOT at a 2023 oversight hearing (Streetsblog NYC).

      Salamanca has backed some steps at City Hall, like clearing abandoned cars that block sightlines and put people at risk, which the Council passed in 2025 (NYC Council Legistar). But Bruckner and Westchester still chew people up. Nights still take them.

      Slow the cars. Stop the worst.

      District 17’s harm concentrates after dark and on fast, wide roads. That points to citywide fixes as well as corner work. Lower speeds save lives. Reining in repeat speeders does too. New York has the tools on the table; New Yorkers can demand they get used. Our guide to next steps is here.

      Council Member Rafael Salamanca Jr. represents this area. In Albany, the district’s lawmakers are Assembly Member Chantel Jackson and State Senator Jose Serrano. They know these streets. They know these names. The ask is simple and public: slow the cars, and stop the drivers who will not slow down. Start where people keep dying. Start on Bruckner.

      One woman tried to cross the road. She never made it home. The rest is numbers until someone decides it isn’t.

      ▸ Where did this happen?
      District 17 in the Bronx. The fatal hit-and-run occurred at Bruckner Boulevard and Leggett Avenue. Other danger clusters in this district include Westchester Avenue and East 163rd Street, based on NYC Open Data crash records from 2022-01-01 to 2025-08-31.
      ▸ How many people have been killed here since 2022?
      31 people were killed and 3,703 were injured on District 17 streets from 2022-01-01 through 2025-08-31, according to NYC Open Data crash, persons, and vehicles tables.
      ▸ When are crashes worst?
      Deaths recur late at night and in the early morning hours in this district, with multiple fatalities recorded around 1–6 AM and again late at night during the 2022–2025 period, per the hourly distribution in the open data.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We used NYC Open Data’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4), filtered to Council District 17 and the date window 2022-01-01 to 2025-08-31. We counted total deaths and injuries across all modes and cross-checked vehicle types for pedestrian harms. Data was accessed Aug 31, 2025. You can view the base datasets here.

      Int 1160-2025 Salamanca votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Feb 13 - Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.
