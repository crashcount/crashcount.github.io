      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 17?

      Mustang Mayhem, Bronx Bleeds—Salamanca, Stop Dodging Street Safety

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 17: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Hurt That Never Stops

      Six people went to the hospital on July 3 after a Ford Mustang jumped the curb at East 149th and Courtlandt, plowing into a sidewalk shed. The driver and passenger ran. The street was left with blood and broken glass. “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting,” a witness told the New York Post. The youngest victim was 30. The oldest, 79. All survived, but the fear lingers, as reported by CBS New York.

      In the last twelve months, eight people died on District 17 streets. Twenty more suffered serious injuries. Over a thousand were hurt. These are not just numbers—they are lives cut short, families changed, futures lost. SUVs and cars did most of the damage. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, and bikes all played their part, according to NYC Open Data.

      Leadership: Steps and Stalls

      Council Member Rafael Salamanca Jr. has taken some steps. He co-sponsored bills to tow abandoned and unregistered vehicles, crack down on fake plates, and prioritize sidewalk repairs at NYCHA sites. He voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable for crossing the street, as shown in NYC Council Legistar. He demanded the city fill the vacant Bronx DOT commissioner post, stating, “There needs to be a level of accountability and our level of accountability is reaching out to that borough commissioner. Please speed it up,” he told Streetsblog NYC.

      But Salamanca has also slowed safety projects, warning against redesigns that might cause congestion—even as crash rates fall. He missed a key vote on the citywide greenway plan. He has not led on lowering speed limits or expanding protected bike lanes—measures proven to save lives.

      The Next Fight: Action, Not Excuses

      The crisis is not fate. It is policy. Every delay means another family waits for the call. Call Council Member Salamanca. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand more protected crossings and bike lanes. Demand action, not excuses.

      Do not wait for another siren.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of local districts.
      ▸ Where does District 17 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 79 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 17?
      It includes the Melrose, Hunts Point, Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Morrisania, Crotona Park East, Soundview-Bruckner-Bronx River, Bronx CB2, and Bronx CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 79, AD 84, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 32.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 17?
      Most injuries and deaths came from SUVs and cars (7 deaths, 12 serious injuries), followed by trucks and buses (1 death, 6 serious injuries), motorcycles and mopeds (1 death, 1 serious injury), and bikes (no deaths, no serious injuries). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random accidents—they are the result of policy choices. Lower speeds, safer street design, and better enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, expand protected bike lanes and crossings, fund sidewalk repairs, and hold city agencies accountable for slow action.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0346-2024
      Salamanca votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.
