      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 17?

      Six Down in Melrose: Speed Kills, Leaders Stall

      Written July 10, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 17: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 10, 2025

      On July 3, a Ford Mustang jumped the curb at East 149th and Courtlandt. Six people, ages 30 to 79, were crossing with the light. The car hit one, then plowed through scaffolding, striking five more. The driver and passenger ran. The street was left with pain and panic. “People were yelling, were in pain, so yelling, crying, it was very upsetting,” said Vivian Cole. All six went to Lincoln Hospital. None died this time. But the scars remain.

      This is not rare. In the last twelve months, District 17 saw 8 deaths and 1,003 injuries from crashes. Nineteen people were seriously hurt. Most were walking or biking. The numbers do not heal. They only grow.

      Trucks, SUVs, and the Weight of Policy

      Trucks and SUVs do the most harm. In three years, cars and SUVs killed seven, trucks and buses killed one, motorcycles and mopeds killed one, and bikes killed none. The city’s streets are not safe for the unprotected. The data is clear. The pain is real.

      Salamanca: Votes, Silence, and the Need for Action

      Council Member Rafael Salamanca Jr. has taken some steps. He voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the desperate and the poor. He co-sponsored bills to tow abandoned cars and target fake plates. He pushed for sidewalk repairs at NYCHA sites. But when the city mapped new greenways, he was absent. When DOT redesigned Bronx streets to save lives, he complained about congestion, not danger. He called for a Bronx DOT commissioner, but the streets stayed deadly. The work is not done.

      The Call: Demand More, Demand Now

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. Call Salamanca. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit, real protected bike lanes, and enforcement that targets the reckless, not the vulnerable. “We thought it was a bomb or something, because we are all panicking around here,” said Christina Sieh. The panic will not end until the killing does. Act now.

      Int 0346-2024
      Salamanca votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.
