      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 17?

      Blood on the Crosswalk: Salamanca’s Caution Costs Lives

      Written August 11, 2025 (Revised August 24, 2025 – Fixed headline duplication, tightened prose, and cleaned citations.)

      District 17: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 11, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In District 17, the streets do not forgive. In the last twelve months, six people died and 1,165 were injured in crashes. Twenty-five suffered injuries so severe they changed lives. The numbers do not rest. They climb, year after year.

      Just last week, a 44-year-old woman was killed crossing West 174th Street in Morris Heights. The driver did not stop. Police are still searching for the car. A Bronx woman is dead after a hit-and-run in Morris Heights. The story is old, but the pain is fresh. Another neighbor gone. Another family left behind.

      On the same night, another woman was struck and killed by an SUV turning into a driveway. A witness said the driver “just rolled over that woman and killed her.” The driver left through a back exit. No arrest. No answers.

      The Numbers Behind the Names

      Cars and SUVs do most of the killing. In District 17, they caused 7 deaths and 13 serious injuries to pedestrians and cyclists since 2022. Trucks and buses added another death and six serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds killed one, seriously injuring one more. Bikes hurt, but they did not kill.

      The pain is not spread evenly. Children, elders, workers—all are at risk. In the last year, 127 people under 18 were injured. Four people over 55 died. The streets are not safe for anyone who walks, rides, or waits at the curb.

      Salamanca’s Record: Some Steps, Not Enough

      Council Member Rafael Salamanca Jr. has voted for bills to clear abandoned cars and improve street markings. He pressed the city to fill the Bronx DOT commissioner vacancy, saying there needs to be a level of accountability and urging the city to speed it up. He backed new fines for illegal ATVs and supported regulating last-mile warehouses to cut truck traffic. But when the city redesigned streets to slow cars and save lives, Salamanca warned about congestion and pushed back, even as crash rates fell.

      The councilmember has taken some steps. But the blood on the street says it is not enough.

      The Call: Demand Action Now

      Every day of delay means another life at risk. Call Council Member Salamanca. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings. Demand action, not excuses. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents local districts like District 17.
      ▸ Where does District 17 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 79 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 17?
      It includes the Melrose, Hunts Point, Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Morrisania, Crotona Park East, Soundview-Bruckner-Bronx River, Bronx CB2, and Bronx CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 79, AD 84, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 32.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 17?
      Cars and SUVs caused 7 deaths and 13 serious injuries to pedestrians and cyclists in District 17 since 2022. Trucks and buses added another death and 6 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds killed 1, seriously injuring 1 more. Bikes caused injuries but no deaths.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement. They are not random or inevitable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, enforce traffic laws, and support policies that protect people walking and biking instead of blaming them.

      Int 1105-2024
      Salamanca votes yes, boosting street safety transparency and project accountability.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.' Council Member Julie Won led sponsorship, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law demands DOT post annual and monthly updates on every project tied to the master plan—listing details, timelines, funding, and setbacks. This law brings sunlight to street safety work, making the city answer for every mile and missed deadline.

      Int 1160-2025
      Salamanca votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.
