      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 17?

      District 17: night roads, hard deaths

      Written August 26, 2025 (Revised August 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 17: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 26, 2025

      Two young men went down on the Bronx River Parkway before dawn. Police say a 21‑year‑old in a Mercedes tried to pass, hit a Volkswagen, then struck two bikes. Both riders, Manuel Amarantepenalo, 19, and Enrique Martinez, 21, died. Prosecutors charged the driver with vehicular manslaughter and DWI. “He was drunk,” a sister said in court. “Two people were killed.” Gothamist. “How could they let him go?” their mother asked. NY Daily News.

      The highway closed near Gun Hill. The bodies were taken to Jacobi and Montefiore. The case is still open. amNY.

      Where the street hits back

      A man crossing at East 163rd Street and Westchester Avenue died under an SUV’s front bumper. The record shows “Crossing, No Signal, Marked Crosswalk.” The SUV was going straight. NYC crash file (CrashID 4758741).

      On E. 149th at Brook, a 57‑year‑old cyclist struck by a bus died. The bus was turning left. The bike was going straight. NYC crash file (CrashID 4795059).

      On Morris and E. 149th, a 52‑year‑old pedestrian died in a multi‑SUV crash. NYC crash file (CrashID 4803347).

      Patterns that do not stop

      In this council district, pedestrians have taken the worst of it: 10 dead, 653 hurt. Cyclists: 5 dead, 288 hurt. People on small motorized bikes: 2 dead, 116 hurt. Cars and SUVs killed 13 of their own. Trucks and buses killed and injured too. District stats from NYC data, 2022–2025.

      Crashes pile up on Westchester Avenue and East 163rd Street. The Cross Bronx Expressway and the Bruckner show steady injury counts. Nights are cruel. From 9 p.m. to 1 a.m., deaths spike; the 10 p.m. hour shows the most serious injuries. The 11 p.m., midnight, and 5 a.m. hours each carry bodies. District hourly data (2022–2025).

      Contributing factors coded by the city say “other” dominates the carnage, with deaths tied to “vulnerable road user error,” and some to ignoring traffic controls. “Unsafe speed” is present, and “alcohol” too. The labels are blunt and cold; the loss is not. District factors.

      What leaders said — and didn’t do

      At City Hall, Council Member Rafael Salamanca pressed DOT on missing Bronx leadership. “There is no borough commissioner in the borough of the Bronx… Please speed it up.” Streetsblog.

      He voted yes to clear abandoned cars in 72 hours. The law orders DSNY to remove them and NYPD to tow cars without valid plates. It promises cleaner corners and clearer sightlines. Council file (Int 0857‑2024).

      He backed tougher fines on illegal ATVs and dirt bikes. The bill sits in committee. Council file (Int 0856‑2024).

      Local fixes now

      • Daylight the worst corners: Westchester Avenue and East 163rd Street. Pull parking back. Make people visible in the crosswalk.
      • Harden turns where buses and trucks sweep across bodies: E. 149th and Brook; E. 169th and Franklin. Give leading pedestrian intervals.
      • Focus night enforcement where deaths cluster: the late‑evening hours, through midnight. Repeat the hotspots until the numbers fall.

      Citywide brakes that save lives

      • Lower the default city speed. Fewer people die when cars move slower. Albany gave the city power. Use it. Take action.
      • Stop the repeat speeders. Require speed limiters for drivers who rack up tickets. Advocates say the worst 1.5% of drivers do outsized harm. Take action.

      This is District 17. The map changes. The names change. The toll does not.

      New York City Council District 17 in the Bronx, including Melrose, Hunts Point, Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Morrisania, Crotona Park East, and Soundview‑Bruckner‑Bronx River.

      Int 0857-2024 Salamanca votes yes to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Jun 30 - Council orders swift removal of abandoned, derelict cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. No plates, no stickers, no excuses. Police and sanitation must act. Safer crossings for all who walk, ride, or wait.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council on June 30, 2025. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to vehicles reported as abandoned to the department of sanitation," requires the Department of Sanitation to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours of report. The NYPD must tow cars lacking valid plates or stickers. Prime sponsor Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led, joined by Crystal Hudson, Amanda Farías, Lincoln Restler, and others. The law targets street hazards, clearing blocked sightlines and crosswalks. It aims to cut risks for pedestrians and cyclists by removing abandoned vehicles fast.
