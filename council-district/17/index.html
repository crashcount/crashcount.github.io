      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 17?

      Hunts Point Bleeds: Streets Are Killing Us—Who Will Act?

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, and cleaned citations.)

      District 17: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      Blood on the Sidewalks

      In District 17, the street is a gauntlet. Eight dead. Nineteen left with injuries so grave they may never walk right again. In the last twelve months, nearly a thousand people have been hurt—pedestrians, cyclists, children. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old.

      Just days ago, the pavement at Hunts Point ran red. A driver ran a red light, plowed onto the sidewalk, and struck three people. One man, 33, now fights for his life. The driver ran. Police caught him. The charges: vehicular assault, reckless endangerment, fleeing police. “Jenkins slammed on the gas and sped off east on Hunts Point Ave., blowing through a red light,” police said. The street remembers. The scars stay.

      The Deadly Routine

      This is not rare. In the last year, District 17 has seen 749 crashes, five deaths, and nine serious injuries—a rise in deaths even as crashes fell. The victims are not numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. The most vulnerable pay the price. Cars and SUVs do most of the killing, but trucks, motorcycles, and even buses leave bodies behind. “A man is facing charges after a multivehicle crash in the Bronx late Friday sent five people to the hospital,” reported CBS New York.

      Salamanca: Steps Forward, Steps Missed

      Council Member Rafael Salamanca Jr. has taken some steps. He voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable for crossing the street and shifting the focus to drivers and street design. He co-sponsored bills to tow abandoned and unregistered vehicles, and to prioritize sidewalk repairs at NYCHA senior housing—moves that could clear paths for those on foot. But he missed the vote on the citywide greenway plan, a key measure for safe routes. Some bills he backs, like e-bike registration, risk burdening those who have the least.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This crisis is not fate. It is policy. Every crash is a choice made by leaders, by laws, by silence. Call Salamanca. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand street redesigns that put people first. Do not wait for another name to become a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 17 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 79 and state senate district SD 29.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 17?
      It includes the Melrose, Hunts Point, Longwood, North & South Brother Islands, Morrisania, Crotona Park East, Soundview-Bruckner-Bronx River, Bronx CB2, and Bronx CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 79, AD 84, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 29 and SD 32.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 17?
      Most injuries and deaths come from Cars and SUVs (7 deaths, 483 injuries), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (1 death, 29 injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 11 injuries).
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. They are the result of choices—by drivers, by city design, by policy. Safer streets and lower speeds save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass laws to lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, and hold reckless drivers accountable. They can also fund sidewalk repairs and prioritize vulnerable road users.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
