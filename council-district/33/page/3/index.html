      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Restler Votes. Bodies Stack Up. Streets Still Kill.

      Written July 6, 2025 (Revised July 6, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, cleaned and deduped citations.)

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 6, 2025

      The Death Toll Grows

      In District 33, the numbers do not lie. Fifteen people are dead. Forty-eight suffered serious injuries. Over 3,100 have been hurt since 2022. The bodies are not numbers. They are neighbors. A cyclist, age 46, killed on Lynch Street. A 45-year-old woman, crushed in the back seat on Flatbush Avenue. A 49-year-old man, struck down by a bike on India Street. Each loss leaves a hole that does not close.

      Last year alone, the district saw four deaths and eleven serious injuries. The violence does not slow. It does not spare the young. It does not spare the old. It does not care if you are walking, riding, or waiting for the light to change.

      The Scene: Streets Built for Speed, Not Safety

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. They killed three people and injured 378. Trucks and buses killed two more. Bikes, too, have taken lives—two deaths, one serious injury. The street is a battlefield, and the most vulnerable pay the price.

      The press tells the same story, again and again. A 36-year-old man crossing near Broadway Junction is struck by a gray Ford. The driver flees. “The driver of the Ford sped off, never stopping,” police said. The man dies at Brookdale Hospital. No one is held. The street stays the same.

      Leadership: What Has Restler Done?

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has co-sponsored and voted for bills to redesign truck routes, clear sightlines at intersections, and legalize jaywalking—measures that shift blame away from those on foot and bike. He voted yes to redesign truck routes, aiming to “improve safety, increase visibility, reduce traffic congestion, and reduce vehicle miles traveled.” He also voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable and let drivers off the hook.

      But the bodies keep coming. Every delay, every watered-down law, means another family waits for news that will break them.

      The Next Step: Demand More

      Call Council Member Restler. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand that every bill puts people first, not cars.

      The street will not change itself. The dead cannot speak. You must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents local districts.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Brooklyn CB1, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Cars and trucks killed 5 people and injured 378. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 22 injuries but no deaths. Bikes killed 2 and injured 33.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. The same streets, the same patterns, the same victims—again and again. Safer design and lower speeds can prevent deaths.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, and end policies that blame pedestrians and cyclists instead of drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
