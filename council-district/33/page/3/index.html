      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      District 33: Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written July 11, 2025 (Revised July 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 11, 2025

      The Toll in District 33

      The streets do not forgive. In District 33, the numbers pile up. Sixteen dead. Fifty-two left with serious injuries. Over 3,200 hurt. Each number is a body, a family, a life changed or ended. In the last twelve months alone, seven people died and twenty-three suffered serious wounds—the kind you do not walk away from. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers: no one is spared.

      Recent Crashes, Lasting Scars

      Just days ago, a 90-year-old man was struck by a masked moped rider and left to die in the street. A witness said, “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open. It was a blue scooter. The driver had a mask. He hit the guy and he left.” said a witness. The killer did not stop. Police are still searching for the rider. Another neighbor said, “We want to make sure what happened doesn’t happen again.” said a neighbor.

      The dead do not get second chances. The living walk with fear. Cars, trucks, bikes, and mopeds all draw blood here. The most vulnerable—pedestrians and cyclists—pay the highest price.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has backed laws to clear sightlines at intersections, redesign truck routes, and legalize crossing the street outside the lines—measures that chip away at the dangers (Local Law 171 of 2023, Local Law 98 of 2024). He has voted to speed up protected bike lanes and co-sponsored bills for safer crossings and better lighting. These are steps, not leaps. The blood on the street says more is needed.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      No law, no leader, no camera will save us unless we demand it. Call Council Member Restler. Call the Mayor. Tell them you want a citywide 20 mph speed limit, daylight at every corner, and real protection for people on foot and bike. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees city agencies.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Brooklyn CB1, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Cars and Trucks: 7 deaths, 403 injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 0 deaths, 22 injuries. Bikes: 2 deaths, 36 injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Policies like lower speed limits, daylighting, and protected bike lanes can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, and ensure real protection for pedestrians and cyclists.

      Int 1138-2024
      Restler co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1138-2024 would ban parking and standing within 20 feet of crosswalks. It forces the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections each year. The bill sits in committee. Sponsors include Bottcher, Won, and the Public Advocate.

      Int 1138-2024, introduced December 5, 2024, is under review by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill is 'Laid Over in Committee.' Its title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.' Council Member Erik D. Bottcher is the primary sponsor, joined by Julie Won, Jumaane Williams, and others. The bill prohibits standing or parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and mandates the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections per year. The law also requires citywide outreach and reporting. This measure aims to keep sightlines clear at crossings, a known danger zone for people on foot and bike. The bill has not yet received a vote.
