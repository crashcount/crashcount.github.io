      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Seven Dead. Hundreds Hurt. Will Restler Act or Look Away?

      Written July 8, 2025

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 8, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In District 33, the numbers do not lie. Seven people killed. Nineteen left with life-altering injuries. In the last twelve months, 776 have been hurt in 1,720 crashes. The dead include a 45-year-old woman crushed in the back seat of an SUV, a 49-year-old man struck by a bike, and a 74-year-old e-bike rider thrown from his seat and killed by a turning bus. The pain is not abstract. It is a son standing in the street, searching for his father. “I see him on the floor,” said Henry Tziquin. “His eyes were closed. I’m scared—I don’t know what to do” NY Daily News.

      Hit-and-run drivers do not stop. They keep going. “He didn’t stop,” Henry said. “He just kept going.”

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. In the last three years, they killed five, left nine with serious injuries, and hurt 378 more. Trucks and buses killed two. Motorcycles and mopeds, one. Bikes, two. The streets are not safe for the old, the young, or anyone in between. Children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians pay the price.

      What Has Been Done—and What Has Not

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has backed bills to redesign truck routes, daylight intersections, and build more protected bike lanes. He voted to legalize jaywalking and co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act. These are steps. But the blood on the street says it is not enough. The city can lower speed limits. It can ban parking near crosswalks. It can force the worst drivers off the road. Every day of delay is another day someone does not come home.

      Act Now—Or Count the Dead

      Call Council Member Restler. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every crossing. Demand action, not words. The dead cannot speak. The living must. Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Brooklyn CB1, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Cars and SUVs killed 5, caused 9 serious injuries, and hurt 378 more. Trucks and buses killed 2 and caused 1 serious injury. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 1 death and no serious injuries. Bikes caused 2 deaths and 1 serious injury.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. Policies like lower speed limits, daylighting, and protected bike lanes can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, daylight intersections, protected bike lanes, and hold repeat dangerous drivers accountable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1138-2024
      Restler co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1138-2024 would ban parking and standing within 20 feet of crosswalks. It forces the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections each year. The bill sits in committee. Sponsors include Bottcher, Won, and the Public Advocate.

      Int 1138-2024, introduced December 5, 2024, is under review by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill is 'Laid Over in Committee.' Its title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.' Council Member Erik D. Bottcher is the primary sponsor, joined by Julie Won, Jumaane Williams, and others. The bill prohibits standing or parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and mandates the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections per year. The law also requires citywide outreach and reporting. This measure aims to keep sightlines clear at crossings, a known danger zone for people on foot and bike. The bill has not yet received a vote.
