      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Sixteen Dead. Thousands Hurt. Still No Safe Streets.

      Written July 13, 2025

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 13, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In District 33, the street is a gauntlet. Sixteen people have died since 2022. Over 3,200 have been hurt. Fifty-two were left with wounds so deep they are called serious injuries. These are not just numbers. They are bodies in the crosswalk, bikes twisted in the gutter, families waiting for a voice at the hospital door.

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm. They killed three, injured hundreds, and left nine with life-changing wounds. Trucks and buses killed two more. Bikes and mopeds, too, have left their mark—two deaths, dozens hurt. No one is safe. Not the old, not the young. Not the man on the corner or the child on the way to school.

      The Latest Blood on the Asphalt

      The violence does not stop. In March, a man was killed by an e-bike on Franklin Street. In April, a car turned left and crushed a child’s knee. In May, a BMW driver with alcohol in his blood killed a man on Bedford Avenue. The dead do not speak, but the street remembers. “I had a six pack of Modelos and two drinks. I was driving the car by myself last night. It’s my fault. After I hit them, I came home,” the driver told police after one crash.

      Neighbors see the danger. “Drivers speed on that stretch of roadway,” one told reporters after a double fatal hit-and-run. The city counts the dead. The city moves on.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has signed on to bills for safer truck routes, daylighting, protected bike lanes, and legalizing jaywalking. He voted yes to redesign truck routes and to end the criminalization of crossing the street on the Council’s legislative record. He co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act and bills for speed humps, solar crosswalks, and more. These are steps. But the bodies keep coming.

      Every delay is another family broken. The law can change. The street can change. But only if the pressure stays on.

      Call to Action: No More Waiting

      Call Council Member Restler. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action, not words. The dead cannot wait. The living should not have to.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Brooklyn CB1, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 5 deaths and over 400 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 22 crashes, while bikes were involved in 36, including 2 deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for safer speeds, protected bike lanes, daylighted intersections, and redesign dangerous roads.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
