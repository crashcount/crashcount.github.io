      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Bodies in the Crosswalk, Blood on the Council’s Hands

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned up citations for accuracy and order.)

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      Seven dead. Twenty-two seriously hurt. That’s just the last twelve months in District 33. The numbers are not names, but each one is a life ended or broken. A mother, a child, a man on his way home. The street does not care. The car does not stop.

      Just last week, a driver doing donuts in a Brooklyn parking lot lost control. He struck his girlfriend, Madisyn Ruiz, as she sat on the curb. She died at the hospital. Police charged him with negligent homicide. “He lost control when he was doing donuts with the vehicle,” he told police NY Daily News. Days later, the tire marks were still there.

      On Third Avenue, two men were killed by a hit-and-run driver. The driver left car parts and bodies behind. Neighbors said, “drivers speed on that stretch of roadway” CBS New York. The city swept up the glass. The danger remains.

      Who Pays the Price

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. In the last three years, cars and trucks killed or injured hundreds. Bikes and mopeds add to the toll, but the deadliest force is still the car. The old, the young, the ones just crossing the street. No helmet, no crosswalk, no law can shield you from a speeding driver.

      What Has Been Done—And What Hasn’t

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has backed some safety bills. He voted yes to remove abandoned vehicles, co-sponsored a law to warn taxi passengers about dooring, and pushed for daylighting crosswalks. He called out racial bias in NYPD traffic stops. He supported clearing illegal placard parking that blocks sightlines.

      But the pace is slow. Some bills stall in committee. Some changes are small. The street stays the same. The bodies keep coming.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real enforcement against reckless drivers, not just warnings for cyclists. Every day of delay is another family broken. The street will not change itself. Only you can force their hand.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Brooklyn CB1, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for the most harm to pedestrians in District 33, causing 3 deaths and over 400 injuries. Bikes were involved in 2 deaths and 36 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused fewer incidents.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Most crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and real enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, remove abandoned vehicles, and focus enforcement on reckless drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1069-2024
      Restler co-sponsors bill to shorten commercial vehicle parking times, safety impact neutral.

      Int 0346-2024
      Restler votes yes to decriminalize jaywalking, boosting pedestrian safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians now cross anywhere, any time. No summons. Law strips drivers of excuses. Streets shift. Power tilts to people on foot.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, rewrites New York’s pedestrian rules. The bill, heard by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passed on September 26, 2024, and became law on October 26, 2024. Its title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way.” Sponsored by Council Member Tiffany Cabán and co-sponsored by Narcisse, Won, Hanif, Hudson, and Restler, the law lets pedestrians cross streets anywhere, even against signals. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks. The Department of Transportation must educate all street users on new rights and responsibilities. The law removes a tool police used to target walkers. It shifts blame from people on foot to the system and those behind the wheel.
