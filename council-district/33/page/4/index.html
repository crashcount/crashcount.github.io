      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Empty Chairs, Broken Promises: Demand Safer Streets Now

      Written July 16, 2025

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 16, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      Sixteen dead. Fifty-two left with injuries that change a life. In District 33, the numbers do not lie. Since 2022, the streets have claimed children, elders, cyclists, and walkers. The wounds are not just numbers. They are names, faces, empty chairs at dinner tables. Cars and SUVs killed three. Trucks and buses killed two. Bikes killed two more. The rest were left broken, some never to walk again.

      Just months ago, a 74-year-old man was killed at Tillary and Jay, ejected from his e-bike after a bus turned across his path. The record shows only this: “Crush Injuries. Apparent Death.” NYC Open Data. No warning. No chance.

      The Policy Response: Promises and Small Steps

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has moved on some fronts. He voted to require taxis and for-hire vehicles to display warning decals about cyclists, a law meant to fight dooring voted. He co-sponsored bills to remove abandoned vehicles, clear crosswalks, and boost plate enforcement. He pushed for daylighting—banning parking near crosswalks to keep sightlines open. But the pace is slow. The committee laid the daylighting bill over in April. The cars still block the corners. The danger waits.

      Restler has also called out the NYPD for racial bias in traffic enforcement, calling the disparity “extreme” called out. He has stood with those who want parks for people, not parking for judges stood with those. He has supported crackdowns on illegal placard parking that blocks bike lanes and sidewalks supported crackdowns.

      The Work Left Undone

      The dead do not care about decals. They need streets that do not kill. The bills are a start, but they are not enough. The corners are still blind. The lanes are still blocked. The speed is still too high. The city has the power to lower the limit to 20 mph. It has not used it. Every day of delay is another day a mother, a child, or a neighbor does not come home.

      “Parks should be for people, not for parking.” Restler said.

      Restler called the racial disparity in traffic enforcement “extreme.” Streetsblog NYC.

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylighting at every corner. Demand streets for people, not for cars. The dead cannot speak. You must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Brooklyn CB1, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Cars and SUVs caused 3 pedestrian deaths and 9 serious injuries in District 33 since 2022. Trucks and buses caused 2 deaths and 1 serious injury. Bikes caused 2 deaths and 1 serious injury. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no pedestrian deaths or serious injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, clear corners, and safer street design can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, daylight intersections, remove abandoned vehicles, and redesign streets for safety.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1138-2024
      Restler co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill bars cars from blocking crosswalks. No standing or parking within 20 feet. City must install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections yearly. Streets clear. Sightlines open. Danger cut.

      Int 1138-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 5, 2024. The bill states: “prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.” Council Member Erik D. Bottcher leads, joined by Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and over two dozen co-sponsors. The law orders the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections each year, up from 100. The city must also run outreach and education. The bill aims to keep crosswalks clear, improve visibility, and protect people on foot and bike. No more hiding behind parked cars. The committee laid the bill over on April 21, 2025.

      Int 1069-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced September 26, 2024. The bill reads: "reducing the maximum time commercial vehicles may park." Sponsored by Kevin C. Riley and 23 others, it limits parking to three hours for most commercial vehicles, and 90 minutes for tractor-trailers, unless signs say otherwise.       Int 0346-2024
      Restler votes yes to decriminalize jaywalking, boosting pedestrian safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians now cross anywhere, any time. No summons. Law strips drivers of excuses. Streets shift. Power tilts to people on foot.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, rewrites New York’s pedestrian rules. The bill, heard by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passed on September 26, 2024, and became law on October 26, 2024. Its title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way.” Sponsored by Council Member Tiffany Cabán and co-sponsored by Narcisse, Won, Hanif, Hudson, and Restler, the law lets pedestrians cross streets anywhere, even against signals. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks. The Department of Transportation must educate all street users on new rights and responsibilities. The law removes a tool police used to target walkers. It shifts blame from people on foot to the system and those behind the wheel.
