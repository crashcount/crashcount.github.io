      Another Child Dead. What Will It Take, Councilman?

      Written June 30, 2025 (Revised June 30, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 30, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Crashes in District 33

      The bodies keep coming. Fifteen people killed. Forty-six left with serious injuries. In just the last twelve months, four more deaths, nine more lives shattered. The dead include children, cyclists, elders. The living carry scars you can’t see.

      A woman dies after a car crushes her chest on Flatbush Avenue. A cyclist is killed on Lynch Street, thrown from his bike. A child’s knee is broken by a driver who failed to yield. These are not isolated. They are the drumbeat of daily life here.

      “He Was a Boy Who Loved Life”

      The pain is not abstract. It has a name, a face. “He was a boy who loved life.” said Baila Krainer, after eight-year-old Mordechai Keller was killed crossing Eastern Parkway. “I just saw a lot of blood gushing out of his ears, his mouth.” said a witness. The driver stayed. The boy did not.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has not been silent. He backed the law that legalized jaywalking, shifting blame off pedestrians. He co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, mandate speed limiters, and expand protected bike lanes. He fought to keep the Bedford Avenue bike lane after the city tried to rip it out. He pushed for 20 mph slow zones and crackdowns on illegal placard parking.

      But the blood keeps flowing. The pace is not enough. The city’s own targets lag. The streets are still ruled by cars and trucks. The most vulnerable—kids, elders, cyclists—pay the price.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Call Council Member Restler. Demand more. Demand a 20 mph default speed limit. Demand every crosswalk cleared, every bike lane protected, every reckless driver stopped. Do not wait for another child’s name to be written in blood.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Brooklyn CB1, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 3 deaths and 9 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 22 crashes, causing injuries but no deaths. Bikes were involved in 35 crashes, with 2 deaths and 1 serious injury. Source
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and stronger enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, clear crosswalks, and crack down on reckless driving. They can also push city agencies to act faster and hold them accountable for delays.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1151-2023
      Restler sponsors bill for solar-powered crosswalks, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council pushed for 500 solar-lit crosswalks. The bill demanded action—100 new devices each year. It called for a hard look at results. But the session ended. The bill died. Streets wait. Pedestrians keep crossing in the dark.

      Int 1151-2023, introduced August 3, 2023, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, aimed to require the Department of Transportation to install at least 100 illuminated, solar-powered traffic control devices at crosswalks each year for five years—a total of 500. The bill also ordered a study comparing these devices to unlit signs, probing their power to deter traffic violations and mapping out logistical hurdles. The matter summary reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to the installation of solar-powered crosswalks.' Council Member Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Menin, Brooks-Powers, Rivera, and over thirty others. The bill was filed at session’s end on December 31, 2023. No safety analyst note was provided. The city’s crosswalks remain unchanged. Vulnerable road users remain exposed.
