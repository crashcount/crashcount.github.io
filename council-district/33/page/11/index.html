      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Fifteen Dead. Still Waiting for Courage.

      Written July 2, 2025

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 2, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      Fifteen dead. Forty-seven left with injuries that will never heal. In the last three and a half years, District 33 has seen 6,637 crashes. The numbers are blunt, but the pain is sharp. A cyclist, age 46, killed on Lynch Street. A 45-year-old woman, crushed as a passenger on Flatbush Avenue. A 49-year-old man, struck dead by a bike on India Street. These are not accidents. They are the cost of inaction.

      The Human Cost

      A child steps into the street and does not come home. A woman waits for the light, a bus takes everything. “A 23-year-old woman was critically injured Sunday when she was hit by an MTA bus on a Brooklyn street,” police said. Council Member Lincoln Restler called the crash “devastating” in a public post. The street does not care about your plans. It only remembers the bodies.

      Cars and SUVs do most of the killing. Three deaths by SUV. Two by truck. Two by bike. The rest are scattered among buses, vans, and motorcycles. The pattern is clear. The danger is not.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Restler has not been silent. He co-sponsored the law that ended jaywalking tickets, shifting blame off pedestrians and onto the street itself. He backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, require protected bike lanes, and push for speed limiters. He fought illegal police parking, saying cops should not block sidewalks and bike lanes with impunity. He called for local climate action, more bike lanes, and safer streets for all.

      But the blood keeps flowing. Four deaths already this year. The bills are good. The pace is not. “We need to roll up our sleeves and take action at the local level, at the neighborhood level on our blocks,” Restler said. The sleeves are still rolled up. The work is not done.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. Call Council Member Restler. Call the Mayor. Call the DOT. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted crosswalks. Demand that the killing stops.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district. See the NYC Council’s legislation portal.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18. Crash data is available from NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Brooklyn CB1, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59. See NYC Open Data for details.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 3 deaths and 9 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 22 crashes, but no deaths. Bikes were involved in 35 crashes, causing 2 deaths and 1 serious injury. See NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—speed, street design, and enforcement all play a role. Every crash is preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass laws for lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, and daylighted crosswalks. They can enforce rules that keep sidewalks and bike lanes clear and push for safer street design.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Council District 33

      Int 1164-2023
      Restler votes yes, advancing safer, more equitable streets for vulnerable users.
      ▸

      Council passed Int 1164-2023. The law forces DOT to map out where street safety money goes. It tracks past investment, crash rates, and who gets left behind. The city must show its work, district by district. No more hiding the gaps.

      Int 1164-2023, now enacted, amends the city code to require an 'investment roadmap' in every Streets Master Plan starting December 1, 2026. The bill moved through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passing on October 19, 2023, and became law on November 19, 2023. The law states: 'The department shall prioritize and promote...the safety of all street users...and improving equity in infrastructure investment.' Sponsored by Kevin C. Riley (primary), with Brooks-Powers, Narcisse, Hanif, Brewer, Restler, and Rivera as co-sponsors, the bill demands DOT report, by neighborhood, on safety infrastructure investment, crash rates, and demographics. It shines a light on which districts get safety upgrades and which are left exposed. The roadmap aims to close the gap for underserved areas, forcing transparency and accountability for every dollar spent on street safety.

      Int 1215-2023 was introduced in the City Council on October 5, 2023, and referred to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure.
