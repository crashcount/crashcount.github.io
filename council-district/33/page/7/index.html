      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Stop Letting a Right Turn End a Life

      Written August 15, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Edited body and FAQ for clarity and inline links; cleaned and reordered citations; removed unused sources.)

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 15, 2025

      Last week, another North Brooklyn death

      On Morgan Avenue, they asked again for fixes. “I was very frustrated that nothing has been done in more than three years since Daniel Vidal was killed,” Juan Ignacio Serra said. Deputy Mayor Polly Trottenberg said: “In areas that were formerly industrial—a lot of trucks, a lot of heavy construction activity—that are becoming residential where cycling is more popular, we’re unfortunately seeing a lot of collisions with cyclists and trucks.”

      The streets stayed the same. People did not.

      Recent deaths in District 33

      In November, an e‑bike rider, 74, died at Tillary and Jay when a bus turned right. In March, a 49‑year‑old man was killed at India St and Franklin St after a collision with an e‑bike. In May, a driver, 50, died at Bedford Ave and N 11th St. In February, a rear passenger, 45, died at State St and Flatbush Ave.

      Crossings. Turns. Impact. Silence.

      The numbers won’t look away

      So far this year, District 33 has 5 deaths, up 150% from 2 at this point last year; serious injuries are up about 73%. Cars and SUVs hit more pedestrians than anything else, followed by trucks and buses. Collisions involving bikes also caused deaths.

      Seventeen people have died since 2022. Thousands hurt.

      What your council member did

      Council Member Lincoln Restler voted to require dooring‑warning decals in every taxi and for‑hire car (Int 0193‑2024). He voted to remove derelict cars within 72 hours (Int 0857‑2024). He sponsored a resolution to let cameras ticket illegal parking and blocking that endangers walkers and riders (Res 1024‑2025). After years of abuse, NYPD cleared a Downtown Brooklyn plaza of placard cars near Tillary and Navy streets.

      These moves help. A decal won’t stop a turning bus. A tow won’t build a crossing. The Morgan Avenue corridor still waits.

      What must happen now

      • Daylight every corner. Pass and fund the crosswalk‑clear bill already laid over in committee (Int 1138‑2024).
      • Build protected lanes and marked crossings where people are dying.
      • Use camera owner‑liability to keep bike lanes, crosswalks, and bus stops clear.

      Call Restler. Call DOT. Demand concrete, not press releases. Demand a street where a right turn does not end a life.

      Int 0474-2024
      Restler co-sponsors bill for dynamic parking zones, minimal safety impact.
      ▸

      Council bill pushes demand-based parking in crowded boroughs. DOT must set rates, tweak with notice. Exempt vehicles dodge new fees. Streets may shift. Pedestrians and cyclists watch the curb.

      Int 0474-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced February 28, 2024, by Julie Won and co-sponsored by Williams, Restler, Salaam, Bottcher, Riley, Brewer, Farías, and the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill orders DOT to create at least one dynamic parking zone per borough, with rates rising or falling by real-time demand. DOT must set the range before launch and give a week’s notice for changes. Vehicles with special permits stay exempt. The matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to establishing dynamic parking zones.' No safety analyst has assessed the impact on vulnerable road users.

The bill aims to reclaim public space and reduce hazards for vulnerable road users.

      Int 0193-2024

      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing taxis and for-hire cars to post bold warnings on doors. The signs tell passengers: look for cyclists before you open up. A small step. The city hands out the decals. No cost to drivers.

      Int 0193-2024 became law on May 31, 2025, after action by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...requiring taxis and for-hire vehicles to display a decal warning passengers to look for cyclists when opening the door,' was sponsored by Lincoln Restler and co-sponsored by over twenty council members, including Gutiérrez, Hudson, and Rivera.       Pickup Turns Left, Strikes Woman in Crosswalk
      ▸

      A Dodge pickup turned left on Nassau Avenue, striking a 49-year-old woman in a marked crosswalk. She died beneath the streetlights. The driver, unharmed, failed to yield. Blood pooled on the asphalt. The city’s danger is written in her absence.

      A 49-year-old woman was killed at the corner of Nassau Avenue and Sutton Street in Brooklyn when a Dodge pickup truck, making a left turn, struck her head-on as she crossed in the marked crosswalk. According to the police report, the driver failed to yield the right-of-way. The report states, 'A Dodge pickup turned left. A 49-year-old woman crossed in the marked crosswalk. The truck struck her head-on.' The woman suffered fatal head injuries and severe bleeding, dying at the scene. The driver, a licensed man, was not injured and his vehicle sustained no damage. The police report explicitly cites 'Failure to Yield Right-of-Way' as the contributing factor. The victim was crossing without a signal but within a marked crosswalk, as noted in the police report, after the driver’s error. The crash underscores the lethal consequences of driver failure to yield at intersections.
