      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Blood on the Asphalt: District 33 Demands Action Now

      Written July 9, 2025

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 9, 2025

      The Toll in District 33

      The streets of District 33 do not forgive. Since 2022, 16 people have died and 48 have been seriously injured in crashes here. Over 3,100 have been hurt. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care if you are young or old, walking or riding. In the last year alone, five more lives were lost.

      Last spring, a 49-year-old man was killed by a bike on India Street. In February, a woman was crushed in the back seat of an SUV on Flatbush Avenue. The dead do not get second chances. The living are left to count the cost.

      The Human Cost

      The horror is not abstract. It is a son searching for his father in the street. “I see him on the floor,” said Henry Tziquin. “His eyes were closed. I’m scared—I don’t know what to do.”

      A 90-year-old man was left bleeding on the pavement after a masked moped rider hit him and fled. “He was laid out on the floor. His head was wide open,” an eyewitness told the Daily News. The driver did not stop. The city keeps searching for footage, for answers, for justice.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has backed key safety bills. He voted to redesign truck routes for safer crossings, co-sponsored laws to daylight intersections, and supported the SAFE Streets Act and speed limiters for repeat offenders. He voted yes to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable instead of the reckless. But the crisis does not wait for committee meetings. Bills can sit. Streets do not.

      What Comes Next

      The blood on the street is not fate. It is policy. Every day without change is another day someone does not come home. Call Council Member Restler. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted corners, and real consequences for drivers who kill and flee. Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees city agencies.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Brooklyn CB1, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Cars and trucks were involved in 390 crashes, causing 7 deaths and 9 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 22 crashes, with no deaths and no serious injuries. Bikes were involved in 35 crashes, causing 2 deaths and 1 serious injury.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents. The data shows that street design, speed, and enforcement decisions shape who lives and who dies.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, daylighted intersections, and real penalties for dangerous driving. They can also push for redesigns that put people before cars.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 1252-2025, introduced April 24, 2025, sits with the Committee on Public Safety.
