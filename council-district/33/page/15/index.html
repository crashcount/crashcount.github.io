      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 29, 2025 (Revised June 29, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 29, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      Six dead. Eighteen left with wounds that will not heal. In the last year alone, District 33 has seen 1,725 crashes. These are not just numbers—they are bodies on the street, families waiting at hospital doors, children who do not come home. In the past twelve months, one child was killed, thirty-one more injured. The old and the young, the walker and the cyclist—none are spared.

      Recent Crashes: The Names We Bury

      A cyclist, helmet on, struck down on Lynch Street. A pedestrian, 49, killed by a bike on Franklin. A 45-year-old woman, crushed in the back seat of an SUV on Flatbush. The dead do not speak, but the living remember. “Yeah, this street is dangerous,” said Milli Cent. The city moves on. The blood dries. The danger remains.

      Who Pays, Who Acts

      Most injuries come from cars and trucks—390 cases, with five deaths. Bikes and mopeds kill too, but the steel and speed of larger vehicles do the worst. The city blames the walker, the biker, the child. But the street stays the same. The law stays the same.

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has not been silent. He backed the law that lets pedestrians cross anywhere, ending the charade of jaywalking. He co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and raise SUV fees. When the city tried to rip out the Bedford Avenue bike lane, Restler stood with the families and said, “For months, the Mayor has repeatedly ignored our recommendations for ensuring the safety of children on Bedford Avenue” said Restler.

      But the deaths keep coming. The city drags its feet. The next name is already written.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every delay, every watered-down bill, every excuse is another body on the street. Call Council Member Restler. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand protected lanes that do not vanish overnight. Do not wait for the next siren.
