      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Fifteen Dead, Thousands Broken—The Blood Is on Their Hands

      Written June 27, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 27, 2025

      The Bodies in the Road

      In District 33, the numbers do not lie. Fifteen people have died on these streets since 2022. Three thousand more have been injured. Forty-six suffered wounds so grave they may never walk the same again. The dead include a 17-year-old boy thrown from a moped, a cyclist crushed on Lynch Street, and a woman crossing Flatbush who never made it to the other side. The city calls them accidents. The families know better.

      The Faces Left Behind

      A brother remembers a man who “never stopped working” and “was always passionate about the things that he did” said Francisco Mota. A father buries his son. A child’s route to school is marked by blood on the crosswalk. Most deaths come at the hands of cars and SUVs. But bikes, mopeds, and trucks all take their share. The street does not care who you are.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has stood with the living. He backed the SAFE Streets Act, pushed for protected bike lanes, and fought to keep the Bedford Avenue lane from being ripped out by City Hall. When the city tried to erase that protection, Restler did not mince words: “For months, the Mayor has repeatedly ignored our recommendations for ensuring the safety of children on Bedford Avenue” Restler, via BKReader. He filed suit to stop the rollback. He co-sponsored bills to daylight crosswalks, slow cars, and install speed limiters. But the blood keeps coming. Every delay, every watered-down law, every shortcut leaves another family in mourning.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These deaths are not the weather. They are the result of choices. Choices made by people in power. Choices that can be changed.

      Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected lanes that cannot be erased by a tweet. Demand that every repeat reckless driver is stopped before they kill again.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. The street will not wait. Neither should you.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body, responsible for passing local laws and overseeing city agencies. Council Members represent districts and can introduce, sponsor, and vote on legislation affecting street safety and more.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Brooklyn CB1, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Most injuries and deaths came from Cars and SUVs (5 deaths, 390 injuries), followed by Bikes (2 deaths, 35 injuries), and Trucks and Buses (2 deaths, 75 injuries).
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—repeat reckless drivers, dangerous intersections, and missing protections. These deaths and injuries are preventable with better laws and safer street design.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, daylighted crosswalks, and crack down on repeat dangerous drivers. They can also fight to keep safety infrastructure in place and push for citywide change.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0080-2024
      Restler co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by deterring hazardous vehicle obstruction.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants within half a mile of schools. Fines set at $175. Civilians can report violations. DOT must act on complaints. Council aims to clear paths for people, not cars.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after its introduction on February 8, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations," creates a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of a school. The penalty is $175 per violation. The Department of Transportation must launch a civilian reporting program. If DOT prosecutes a case using civilian evidence, the complainant gets 25% of the proceeds. Council Member Carlina Rivera leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Joseph, Menin, Nurse, Hudson, Brannan, Farías, Brewer, Salaam, Hanif, Avilés, Won, Bottcher, Krishnan, Gutiérrez, Marte, and the Brooklyn Borough President.
