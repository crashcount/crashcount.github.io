      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Fifteen Dead, Hundreds Broken—Will City Hall Ever Wake Up?

      Written July 4, 2025

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 4, 2025

      A City That Bleeds, Block by Block

      In District 33, the numbers do not lie. Fifteen people are dead. Forty-eight more are left with serious injuries. In just the last twelve months, six families lost someone forever. Seven hundred eighty-two people were hurt. The streets run through Greenpoint, Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, and more. The pain is everywhere.

      Last spring, a cyclist was killed on Lynch Street. A pedestrian died at Franklin and India. A woman was crushed in the back seat on Flatbush. The dead do not get second chances. The living carry scars.

      The Human Cost

      A van slammed into a tree in Bath Beach. Fourteen people were hurt, most of them adults with special needs. One witness said, “It’s sad because they definitely need a lot of attention more than anyone else. I hope that they’re all OK at the end of the day.”

      On the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway, a 55-year-old man lost control of his motorcycle and died. The police said he was “zipping west” when he hit the median. The road was closed for hours. The city moved on. NY Daily News

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has not been silent. He backed the law that ended jaywalking tickets, letting people cross the street without fear of a fine. He co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, speed up protected bike lanes, and push for lower speed limits. He stood with advocates to defend the Bedford Avenue bike lane when the mayor tried to rip it out. He called for action: “We need to roll up our sleeves and take action at the local level, at the neighborhood level on our blocks.”

      But the crisis is not over. The city moves too slow. The dead pile up. The pain does not end.

      What You Can Do

      Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand more protected bike lanes. Demand the city clear crosswalks and corners. Do not wait for another name on the list. The time for action is now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Brooklyn CB1, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Most injuries and deaths were caused by Cars and Trucks (7 deaths, 9 serious injuries), followed by Bikes (2 deaths, 1 serious injury), and Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, no serious injuries). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents. The data shows patterns—speed, street design, and enforcement all shape the risk. Change the system, and you save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass laws for lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, ban parking near crosswalks, and hold city agencies accountable for slow action. They can also stand with advocates to defend safety improvements when they are threatened.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Three new mid-block crossings now cut through Atlantic Avenue’s deadly stretch. Fresh paint and signals stand where drivers once sped unchecked. The city acts after a fatal crash, but danger lingers on blocks left untouched. Council calls for more.

      On February 6, 2024, the Department of Transportation announced three new mid-block pedestrian crossings on Atlantic Avenue in downtown Brooklyn. The project, supported by Council Member Lincoln Restler, follows a deadly crash that killed a 31-year-old woman. DOT crews installed crossings between Bond and Nevins, Hoyt and Bond, and Smith and Hoyt streets. The official matter summary states: 'Freshly painted crosswalks and new traffic signals will help slow down drivers and encourage more foot traffic and safe crossings.' Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez called the crossings vital for safety. Restler applauded the move but demanded further action, especially near the site of the fatal crash. He said, 'Mid-block crossings are a critical step in making this dangerous corridor safer.' The new measures do not cover all danger zones. Atlantic Avenue remains a threat for pedestrians.
