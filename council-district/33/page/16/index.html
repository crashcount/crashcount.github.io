      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Counting Bodies, Not Just Crashes: Streets Still Kill in District 33

      Written June 22, 2025 (Revised June 22, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 22, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt

      Six dead. Eighteen left with wounds that will not heal. In the last twelve months, District 33 saw 1,745 crashes. Four children were seriously hurt. A cyclist was killed on Lynch Street. A pedestrian bled out on India Street. These are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. The street does not care if you are young or old. It takes what it wants.

      Cars and SUVs did most of the damage. In three years, they killed five. Bikes killed two. Trucks and buses killed two more. The rest were left broken, sometimes for life. The pain is not spread evenly. The old, the young, the ones on foot or bike—they pay the highest price.

      The Politics of Safety

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has not been silent. He voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that blamed the dead for their own deaths. He co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks and speed up protected bike lanes. He stood with families when the mayor tried to rip out the Bedford Avenue bike lane. “For months, the Mayor has repeatedly ignored our recommendations for ensuring the safety of children on Bedford Avenue,” Restler said. “Instead of identifying solutions that enhance safety for pedestrians, cyclists, and drivers, the Mayor is making a purely political decision to rip out a bike lane with no alternative.”

      When the city tried to erase the lane, a judge stopped them. Restler filed an affidavit to keep the protection in place. The fight is not over. The city will try again. The street will claim more lives if nothing changes.

      What Comes Next

      The disaster is not fate. Every crash is a choice made by someone in power. The council can pass more laws. The mayor can build more barriers. The police can enforce the rules. But none of it matters if the public stays quiet. Contact your council member. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand protected bike lanes that cannot be erased by a backroom deal. Demand that every child can cross the street and come home.

      Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The street is hungry. It will not stop on its own.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, approves budgets, and oversees city agencies.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, and Brooklyn Navy Yard neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm, with 5 deaths and over 380 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths and 22 injuries. Bikes caused 2 deaths and 35 injuries. Trucks and buses caused 2 deaths and 75 injuries.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are not random. They happen where streets are unsafe and laws are weak. Every crash can be prevented with better design and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass laws for daylighting, build protected bike lanes, lower speed limits, and hold drivers accountable. They can fight for every street to be safe for the most vulnerable.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0501-2022
      Restler co-sponsors bill boosting civilian reporting to improve street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants near schools. Civilians can report violators. Each offense draws a $175 fine. The city pays whistleblowers a cut. The bill stalled. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0501-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on June 2, 2022. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations,' aimed to create a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 1,320 feet of a school. The penalty: $175 per violation, enforced through the Office of Administrative Trials and Hearings. Civilians, except city employees, could file complaints and receive 25% of collected fines. The Department of Transportation would support this with a phased-in reporting program and annual public reports. Council Member Carlina Rivera led as primary sponsor, joined by over two dozen co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.
