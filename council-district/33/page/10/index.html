      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 33?

      Fifteen Dead, No Excuses: Demand Safer Streets Now

      Written July 3, 2025

      District 33: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 3, 2025

      The Blood Price of the Road

      Fifteen dead. Forty-eight with injuries so grave they change a life. In District 33, the numbers do not tell the whole story, but they do not lie. Since 2022, crashes have torn through Greenpoint, Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, and beyond. Children, elders, cyclists, and walkers—no one is spared. The dead do not get a second chance. The living carry scars.

      Just this spring, a sedan struck a cyclist on Kent Avenue. A van ignored a traffic control and left a cyclist with head trauma. A child’s knee was crushed by a driver who failed to yield. The list is long. The pain is longer. Open Data shows the pattern: cars, trucks, bikes, buses—each can kill, each has.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and the Weight of Action

      Council Member Lincoln Restler has not been silent. He has backed bills to ban parking near crosswalks, require protected bike lanes, and push for speed limiters on dangerous vehicles. He co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act and voted to legalize jaywalking, shifting blame off the walker and onto the street and the driver. When a woman was struck by an MTA bus, Restler called it “devastating”. He has fought illegal NYPD parking, saying sidewalks and bike lanes are not for police cars. “We need to roll up our sleeves and take action at the local level, at the neighborhood level on our blocks,” Restler said.

      But the blood keeps coming. The laws are not enough. The pace is slow. The street does not wait.

      What Comes Next: The Fight Is Not Over

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a policy failure. The city must clear sightlines at every crosswalk, build real protection for every bike lane, and slow every car. Residents must call, write, and show up. Demand more. Demand faster. Demand that the next child, the next cyclist, the next elder, makes it home.

      Call your council member. Call the mayor. Do not let up. The road is not safe until the killing stops.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 33 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 50 and state senate district SD 18.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 33?
      It includes the Greenpoint, Williamsburg, South Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, Downtown Brooklyn-Dumbo-Boerum Hill, Brooklyn Navy Yard, Brooklyn CB1, and Brooklyn CB2 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 50, AD 52, and AD 57, and State Senate Districts SD 18, SD 25, SD 26, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 33?
      Cars and trucks were involved in 390 incidents, causing 3 deaths and 9 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 22 incidents, with no deaths. Bikes were involved in 35 incidents, causing 2 deaths and 1 serious injury. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Every crash is preventable. Death and injury on the street are the result of choices—by drivers, by policymakers, by those who design and enforce the rules.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to slow cars, protect crossings, build protected bike lanes, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can act quickly, not just talk.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

The law sets deadlines: initial report by November 2023, implementation by June 2024, and new signage by July 2024. This overhaul targets the deadly risk trucks pose to vulnerable road users at city intersections.

      Int 0712-2022
      Restler votes yes on license plate reporting bill with no safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to report cars with unreadable plates dodging cameras. The bill targets drivers hiding their plates from red light and speed cameras. Reports must show where, when, and how plates go unseen. Data goes public, every quarter.

      Int 0712-2022, now enacted as Local Law 155 of 2023, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and the full Council on October 19, 2023. The law, sponsored by Gale A. Brewer with co-sponsors including Erik D. Bottcher, Lincoln Restler, and others, amends the city code to require the Department of Transportation to report on vehicles with license plates unreadable by photo violation monitoring systems. The law’s title states it is 'in relation to reporting on motor vehicles with license plates that are unreadable by photo violation monitoring systems.' DOT must post quarterly reports showing the time, location, and reasons plates evade cameras—whether by concealment, distortion, or missing tags. The law aims to expose drivers who dodge automated enforcement, shining light on a loophole that puts pedestrians and cyclists at risk. The reports must be public and sent to the Mayor and Council Speaker.
