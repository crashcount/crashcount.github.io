      No More Blood for Parking Spots

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, deduped citations, and fixed citation formatting.)

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll: Broken Bodies, Unchanged Streets

      A man kneels to pick up food at Withers and Woodpoint. A dump truck turns. He is dead before sunrise. The driver leaves. No arrest. No answers. Police said the driver, a 49-year-old man, left the scene. No arrests have been announced.

      In the last year, four people died on District 34 streets. Nine more suffered serious injuries. Nearly 700 were hurt. The numbers do not bleed, but the people do. Pedestrians, cyclists, children. Trucks and SUVs crush, sedans strike, bikes collide. The city counts the bodies. The street stays the same.

      Leadership: Bills, Votes, and the Slow March

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez has signed her name to safety. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act, pushed for protected bike lanes, and backed the bill to ban parking near crosswalks—daylighting corners so drivers can see children before it is too late. She voted to end jaywalking tickets, shifting blame off the dead and onto the street itself. She stood with neighbors for a 20 mph speed limit in Greenpoint and Williamsburg, after years of carnage. But the pace is slow. The danger is not.

      When the city closed a deadly Bushwick intersection, Gutiérrez supported the redesign. The plan bans cars from a crash hotspot, removes parking at corners, and builds concrete for feet, not wheels. DOT says it could cut deaths by up to 45% for pedestrians. But the street was only fixed after a man died. The intersection, described as a ‘crash hotspot’ with 39 incidents since 2020, will see expanded pedestrian space and improved safety.

      The Next Fight: Speed, Cameras, and Relentless Pressure

      Speed kills. The city has the power to lower limits. It has the tools—speed cameras, daylighted corners, protected lanes. What it lacks is urgency. Every day of delay is another family broken. The numbers are not fate. They are choices.

      Call your council member. Demand 20 mph limits. Demand cameras that never sleep. Demand streets for people, not trucks.

      Do not wait for the next siren.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents local districts like District 34.
      ▸ Where does District 34 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 34?
      It includes the East Williamsburg neighborhood. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 37, AD 38, AD 50, and AD 53, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 18.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 34?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with 1 pedestrian death and 74 moderate or serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths and 4 moderate or serious injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths and 5 moderate or serious injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, daylight dangerous corners, build protected bike lanes, and support automated enforcement like speed cameras.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
