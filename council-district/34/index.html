      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 34?

      Blood on McGuinness: City Stalls, Neighbors Die

      Written July 21, 2025 (Revised July 29, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 21, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      District 34 is bleeding. Since 2022, at least 13 people have died and 2,712 have been injured in crashes on these streets (NYC Open Data). These are not just numbers—they are bodies on the pavement and families left with empty chairs. In the last twelve months alone, six more people died. Twelve were left with injuries so severe they may never walk the same again.

      Just this spring, a 42-year-old man was killed crossing Withers Street with the signal. A dump truck turned left and crushed him (NYC Open Data). He was doing everything right. The truck kept going. The city kept moving.

      The Usual Suspects

      Cars and trucks do most of the killing. Out of all pedestrian injuries and deaths, sedans and SUVs are responsible for the largest share—369 incidents. Trucks and buses follow, with 40 more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll, but the real danger comes on four wheels or more (NYC Open Data).

      The pattern is old. The pain is fresh. A cyclist was struck by a turning driver on McGuinness Boulevard just days ago. The bike lane was only paint. The city had promised more. “The fact is that this wouldn’t be a problem if Mayor Adams had just done what he had promised to do, which was, make all of McGuinness safe. The road diet works where it’s been installed and it’s needed for the entire corridor before this happens again,” said Kevin LaCherra.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez has co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks—a move to daylight intersections and save lives (NYC Council – Legistar). The bill would force the city to clear at least 1,000 crosswalks a year. It sits in committee. The city waits. The dead do not.

      The work is not done. Every delay is another risk. Every watered-down promise is another family shattered. The city can move faster. The council can demand more. The mayor can act. But only if you make them.

      Call to Action

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Call Council Member Gutiérrez. Demand the passage and funding of the daylighting bill. Demand protected bike lanes, not just paint. Demand a city where crossing the street is not a gamble with your life.

      If you do nothing, nothing will change.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents local districts like District 34.
      ▸ Where does District 34 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 37 and state senate district SD 12.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 34?
      It includes the Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Bushwick (West), Ridgewood, Brooklyn CB4, and Brooklyn CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 37, AD 38, AD 50, and AD 53, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 18.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 34?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for 369 pedestrian injuries and deaths (NYC Open Data). Motorcycles and mopeds caused 17, and bikes were involved in 21. Trucks and buses accounted for 40 incidents.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. Most deaths and injuries in District 34 happen on streets that could be made safer with better design and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and fund bills like the daylighting law, demand protected bike lanes, and push for enforcement that targets dangerous driving, not vulnerable people.

      Int 0857-2024
      Gutiérrez votes yes to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council orders swift removal of abandoned, derelict cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. No plates, no stickers, no excuses. Police and sanitation must act. Safer crossings for all who walk, ride, or wait.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council on June 30, 2025. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to vehicles reported as abandoned to the department of sanitation," requires the Department of Sanitation to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours of report. The NYPD must tow cars lacking valid plates or stickers. Prime sponsor Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led, joined by Crystal Hudson, Amanda Farías, Lincoln Restler, and others. The law targets street hazards, clearing blocked sightlines and crosswalks. It aims to cut risks for pedestrians and cyclists by removing abandoned vehicles fast.

      NYC DOT Targets Intersection Danger Zones
      ▸

      City crews will block cars from corners. Barriers, granite, planters—hard lines drawn. Sightlines open. Pedestrians and cyclists step forward, visible. Intersections, once blind, now clear. The city moves to cut the toll where most blood spills.

      amNY reported on June 11, 2025, that the NYC Department of Transportation will redesign intersections to protect pedestrians and cyclists. The plan uses 'hardened daylighting'—physical barriers, granite blocks, and planters—to stop vehicles from parking near corners. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, 'Using barriers to clear space at the intersection will help ensure pedestrians, cyclists and turning vehicles can see each other and enhance safety.' Nearly half of city traffic deaths happen at intersections. The new design will first appear at high-crash sites in Brooklyn, including Ocean Avenue at Church Avenue and several others. The move highlights a systemic effort to address visibility failures and reduce deadly conflicts at crossings.
