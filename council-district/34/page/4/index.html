      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 34?

      District 34: Morgan Avenue, Broadway and the Toll of Delay

      Written August 15, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Finisher pass: copyedit, fact-check, tighten prose, add local analysis and political solutions.)

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 15, 2025

      Morgan Avenue, again

      On 2025-08-06 a box truck struck and killed a pedestrian at Morgan Avenue and Maspeth Avenue in the early morning, the latest fatal crash on a corridor residents say is unsafe for anyone on foot or bike. This stretch has seen repeated deaths: city data show 15 people killed and 35 seriously injured in District 34 since 2022 (NYC Open Data).

      Advocates and neighbors have long demanded change. “I was very frustrated that nothing has been done in more than three years since Daniel Vidal was killed,” Juan Ignacio Serra told Streetsblog NYC, calling attention to the avenue’s role as a vital north–south connection for people who bike and walk (Streetsblog NYC).

      Broadway hit-and-run

      On 2025-08-02, before dawn at Broadway and Suydam Street, a driver struck and killed a 47-year-old pedestrian and fled the scene, police said; investigators reported the victim was dragged more than 50 feet and asked the public for tips (NY Daily News; Gothamist).

      The pattern

      Crashes in District 34 concentrate on specific corridors — Morgan Avenue and Union Avenue rank among the worst intersections — and cluster during rush hours with many serious and late-night incidents in the dataset. Contributing factors reported by the city include disregarded traffic control, failure to yield, inattention/distraction and other causes.

      Heavy vehicles feature disproportionately in fatal crashes: trucks and buses account for four pedestrian deaths in the district while cars and SUVs account for two, even as motor vehicles of all types cause hundreds of injuries (NYC Open Data).

      What your council member did — and what’s missing

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez co-sponsored Int. 1353-2025, which would require DOT to install traffic-calming or control devices next to schools within 60 days of a study determination (NYC Council – Legistar). That targets schoolfront safety, but district data show danger extends beyond school blocks into industrial and residential routes used daily by workers and families.

      Albany has debated measures to force repeat reckless drivers to slow down: proposals to require speed-limiters for habitual speeders have circulated after deadly crashes (amNY). Citywide action to slow defaults and to require intelligent speed assistance for repeat offenders would reduce the risk for every neighborhood.

      Fix the hot spots now

      • Morgan Avenue: install end-to-end protected bike lanes, physical curb separation from truck loading areas, and frequent daylighting at crossings.
      • Broadway and Union avenues: daylight intersections, add leading pedestrian intervals (LPIs), upgrade nighttime lighting, and run targeted overnight enforcement.
      • Heavy-vehicle routes: adopt truck-safety routing, require side guards and speed-limiting devices on fleets serving dense residential areas.

      Political solutions for every street

      Local crash patterns underline two citywide priorities: lower New York City’s default speed limit and require speed limiters (intelligent speed assistance) for habitual speeders. Lowering neighborhood limits to 20 mph has already been adopted in targeted slow zones; expanding a 20 mph default citywide would save lives (Brooklyn Paper). Requiring speed-limiting technology for repeat reckless drivers is supported in policy proposals discussed after recent fatal crashes (amNY).

      Act

      Call Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez and DOT. Demand protected lanes on Morgan Avenue, daylighted crossings on Broadway and Union, and a citywide default 20 mph plus mandatory speed-limiting technology for habitual speeders. Start here: Take Action (/take_action/).

      Int 1160-2025
      Gutiérrez supports council passage of bill improving street safety with prompt pavement markings.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.

      Int 1138-2024
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill bars cars from blocking crosswalks. No standing or parking within 20 feet. City must install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections yearly. Streets clear. Sightlines open. Danger cut.

      Int 1138-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 5, 2024. The bill states: “prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.” Council Member Erik D. Bottcher leads, joined by Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and over two dozen co-sponsors. The law orders the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections each year, up from 100. The city must also run outreach and education. The bill aims to keep crosswalks clear, improve visibility, and protect people on foot and bike. No more hiding behind parked cars. The committee laid the bill over on April 21, 2025.

      Reynoso Warns Against Bad Implementation Undermining Safety Boosting Bill

      Private trash trucks still maim and kill. Five years after reform, chaos rules. Only one waste zone runs. No citywide plan. Streets stay dangerous. Lawmakers and survivors demand action. The city stalls. Vulnerable New Yorkers pay the price.

      ""If done right, CWZs can eliminate millions of truck miles traveled from our streets, keep workers and pedestrians safe, and ensure quality service for all customers. The city's goal right now should be to prevent bad implementation from undermining a good bill."" -- Antonio Reynoso

No timeline exists for citywide rollout. The Department of Sanitation claims caution is needed to avoid price hikes and ensure effective change, but offers no clear metrics or deadlines. Lauren Pine, a crash survivor, pleads, 'We can't wait another day to enforce truck safety requirements.'
