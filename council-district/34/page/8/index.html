      No More Dead Kids: Clear the Crosswalks, Clear the Way

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt

      In District 34, the numbers do not lie. Eleven people killed. Thirty-four seriously injured. More than 2,600 wounded since 2022. The dead do not get a second chance. The injured carry scars you cannot see.

      A child crossing with the light, crushed by a turning truck on Richardson and Kingsland. A cyclist, ejected and bleeding, after a car door swings open on Evergreen Avenue. The violence is not random. It is relentless.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and trucks did most of the damage: 356 crashes, 1 death, 2 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 2, injured dozens more. Motorcycles and mopeds left riders broken—16 crashes, 1 serious injury. Even bikes, in the crossfire, left their mark: 20 crashes, 1 serious injury. See the data.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Delayed

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks and force the city to daylight 1,000 intersections a year. The law would keep crosswalks clear and protect people on foot and bike. But the bill sits in committee, waiting. No more hiding behind parked cars.

      The city is moving, but too slow. “Daylighting streets is necessary, but a bare minimum,” said Borough President Reynoso. The dead cannot wait for studies and hearings.

      The Call: Demand Action Now

      Every day of delay is another family broken. Call Council Member Gutiérrez. Demand a vote on the daylighting bill. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand streets where a child can cross and come home alive.

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 34 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 37 and state senate district SD 12.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 34?
      It includes the Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Bushwick (West), Ridgewood, Brooklyn CB4, and Brooklyn CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 37, AD 38, AD 50, and AD 53, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 18.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 34?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for most deaths and injuries to pedestrians: 356 crashes, 1 death, 2 serious injuries. Trucks and buses killed 2 and injured dozens more. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 16 crashes and 1 serious injury. Bikes were involved in 20 crashes, with 1 serious injury. See the data.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random. Most could be prevented with safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass laws to lower speed limits, ban parking near crosswalks, fund street redesigns, and demand faster action from city agencies.
