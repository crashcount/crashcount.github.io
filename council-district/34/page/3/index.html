      District 34: Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 19, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, deduped and reordered citations, improved FAQ citation formatting.)

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In District 34, the street is a wound that never closes. Four people killed. Ten left with serious injuries. In the last year alone, 708 hurt, 1,435 crashes—each one a story that ends in pain or silence. The dead do not speak. The living limp, or grieve, or wait for justice that never comes.

      A man kneels to pick up food on Withers Street. A dump truck turns. He is gone. The driver leaves. No arrest. “Police said the driver, a 49-year-old man, left the scene.” The city moves on. The street stays hungry.

      A cyclist is crushed on Evergreen Avenue. A pedestrian is launched ten feet by a hit-and-run in Bushwick. “He gets absolutely launched, maybe 10 to 15 feet right into the middle of the intersection, and just left there,” said a friend. The driver is gone. The intersection is unchanged.

      Trucks, SUVs, sedans—steel against flesh. In District 34, trucks and SUVs killed more than any other vehicle. The numbers are cold. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises, Votes, and the Slow Grind of Change

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez has signed her name to bills that promise safer streets. She backed the SAFE Streets Act, the push for 20 mph speed limits, and the ban on parking near crosswalks. She voted to legalize crossing mid-block, ending the old blame game against walkers. She stood with neighbors for a safer McGuinness Boulevard. She called for daylight at corners where drivers once hid behind parked cars.

      But the blood keeps running. Bills sit in committee. Promises wait for a vote. The city talks of redesigns, but corners stay blind, and trucks still turn wide. The law moves slow. The street moves fast.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another risk. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It can daylight every corner. It can build real protection for people on foot and bike. It can act, or it can wait for the next siren.

      Call your council member. Demand action. Do not wait for another name on the list. The street will not wait for you.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 34 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 34?
      It includes the East Williamsburg neighborhood. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 37, AD 38, AD 50, and AD 53, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 18. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 34?
      SUVs and cars caused 1 death and 77 injuries; trucks and buses caused 2 deaths and 10 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 4 injuries. Bikes caused 1 serious injury and 6 moderate injuries. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—trucks turning, speed, blind corners. These deaths and injuries are preventable with safer streets and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, daylighting, protected bike lanes, and redesigns that put people before cars. They can push for action, not just words.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0080-2024
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by deterring hazardous vehicle obstruction.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants within half a mile of schools. Fines set at $175. Civilians can report violations. DOT must act on complaints. Council aims to clear paths for people, not cars.

      Int 0080-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after its introduction on February 8, 2024. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations," creates a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 2,640 feet of a school. The penalty is $175 per violation. The Department of Transportation must launch a civilian reporting program. If DOT prosecutes a case using civilian evidence, the complainant gets 25% of the proceeds. Council Member Carlina Rivera leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Joseph, Menin, Nurse, Hudson, Brannan, Farías, Brewer, Salaam, Hanif, Avilés, Won, Bottcher, Krishnan, Gutiérrez, Marte, and the Brooklyn Borough President. The bill aims to keep streets clear for vulnerable road users, especially near schools.

      Int 0079-2024
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors bill to boost pedestrian lighting, improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council members want 500 corridors lit for walkers each year. The bill demands bright sidewalks—no less than 1 footcandle. Most corridors must connect, forming safer, well-lit routes. The measure sits in committee, waiting for action. Darkness remains a threat.

      Int 0079-2024, introduced on February 8, 2024, sits 'Laid Over in Committee' with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installation of pedestrian lighting fixtures,' would require the transportation commissioner to install sidewalk lighting in at least 500 commercial corridors per year, each lit to a minimum of 1 footcandle (11 lux). At least 450 corridors must be contiguous to others with new or existing lighting. Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Krishnan, Bottcher, and many others. The bill aims to cut through the city’s darkness, demanding light for those on foot. It remains stalled in committee, its promise unrealized.

      Int 0095-2024
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors school street safety study with no immediate impact.
      ▸

      Council eyes danger at school gates. Bill orders a citywide study on raised crosswalks, intersections, and speed reducers near schools. Streets choke with cars. Children cross. The city stalls. The bill sits in committee. Lives hang in the balance.

      Int 0095-2024, introduced February 8, 2024, sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill demands a study on 'the feasibility of installing raised crosswalks, raised intersections, and speed reducers at intersections and roadways adjacent to schools.' Council Member Julie Menin leads, joined by Schulman, Hudson, Brooks-Powers, Riley, Lee, Louis, Restler, and Gutiérrez as sponsors. The bill would repeal and replace Section 19-189 of the city code, requiring the Department of Transportation to report crash data and feasibility findings to the Mayor and Council Speaker. After the study, the Commissioner may install traffic-calming measures where possible. The bill remains 'Laid Over in Committee' as of June 25, 2024. No safety analyst note is available, but the bill targets systemic risk where children walk and drivers speed.

      ▸

      Int 1276-2023 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on December 20, 2023. The bill required 'taxis and for-hire vehicles to display a decal warning passengers to look for cyclists when opening the door.'
