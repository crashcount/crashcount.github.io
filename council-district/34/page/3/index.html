      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 34?

      Seven Dead in a Year—District 34 Bleeds While the City Waits

      Written August 8, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 8, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In District 34, the street does not forgive. Seven people have died in crashes here in the last year. Eight hundred forty-seven were injured. Eleven suffered wounds so grave they may never walk the same. The numbers are not just numbers. They are bodies on the pavement, families left waiting for a door that will never open again.

      Just days ago, a man was struck and dragged more than 50 feet on Broadway in Bushwick. The driver fled. Police found the man dying in the street. They are still looking for the one who killed him. Police were looking for the driver of a car that struck and dragged a 47-year-old man on a Brooklyn street over the weekend, killing him. The victim’s name has not been released. The city has not said if he was in a crosswalk. It does not matter. He is dead. The driver is gone.

      On Morgan Avenue, a box truck killed a pedestrian crossing near Sharon Street. No marked crosswalk. No protection. This is the third fatal crash on Morgan in three years. Juan Ignacio Serra, a local, was very frustrated that nothing has been done in more than three years since Daniel Vidal was killed. The city has not acted. The avenue remains a gauntlet for anyone on foot or bike.

      The Numbers Behind the Blood

      District 34 saw 1,725 crashes in the last 12 months. Seven dead. Eleven with life-altering injuries. The toll is rising. Last year, there were zero deaths in the same period. Now there are seven. Injuries are up by a third. Serious injuries up by almost half. The disaster is not sudden. It is slow, grinding, and relentless.

      Cars and trucks do most of the killing. Two deaths and 375 injuries came from sedans and SUVs. Trucks and buses killed three. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll. No one is safe. Not the young, not the old. Not the ones crossing with the light. Not the ones just trying to get home.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez has co-sponsored a bill to ban parking within 20 feet of crosswalks, forcing the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections a year. The bill sits in committee. The city waits. The dead do not. See the bill.

      The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has not used it. The city can redesign streets. It has not moved fast enough. The city can protect the most vulnerable. It has not.

      Call to Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Council Member Gutiérrez. Demand the city clear every crosswalk, lower every speed limit, and build streets that do not kill. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The blood is already on the street. Make sure it stops here.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 34 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 37 and state senate district SD 12.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 34?
      It includes the Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Bushwick (West), Ridgewood, Brooklyn CB4, and Brooklyn CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 37, AD 38, AD 50, and AD 53, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 18.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 34?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for 2 deaths and 377 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths and 17 injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths and 21 injuries. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement can stop the deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and fund laws to redesign streets, lower speed limits, and clear crosswalks. They can demand the city act faster and hold agencies accountable.

      Int 1105-2024
      Gutiérrez votes yes, boosting street safety transparency and project accountability.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.' Council Member Julie Won led sponsorship, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law demands DOT post annual and monthly updates on every project tied to the master plan—listing details, timelines, funding, and setbacks. This law brings sunlight to street safety work, making the city answer for every mile and missed deadline.

      Reynoso Urges Expansion of Safety Boosting Slow Zones
      ▸

      Dumbo’s speed limit drops to 20 mph. Brooklyn’s first Regional Slow Zone targets crowded streets. Ten severe injuries and one death in five years haunt the area. Council Member Restler and DOT push for slower traffic. Lives hang in the balance.

      On March 19, 2025, Council Member Lincoln Restler announced the lowering of Dumbo’s speed limit to 20 mph, making it Brooklyn’s first Regional Slow Zone. The measure follows the passage of Sammy’s Law, which lets the city set its own speed limits. The Department of Transportation cited ten severe injuries and one death in Dumbo over five years. The new zone covers streets packed with pedestrians, from Furman to Navy and Hudson, and John to Sands and the BQE. Restler said, 'Data has shown that a one mile per hour increase in speed results in a nearly three percent increase in mortality.' DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez added, 'Lowering vehicle speed limits by even a few miles per hour could be the difference between life or death in a traffic crash.' The change will take effect after a public comment period. Brooklyn Borough President Antonio Reynoso called for more slow zones in high-crash neighborhoods. The city plans to expand 20 mph limits to over 250 locations by the end of 2025.
