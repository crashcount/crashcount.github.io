      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 34?

      District 34: Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 20, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, deduped and reordered citations, improved FAQ citation formatting.)

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In District 34, the street is a wound that never closes. Four people killed. Ten left with serious injuries. In the last year alone, 708 hurt, 1,435 crashes—each one a story that ends in pain or silence. The dead do not speak. The living limp, or grieve, or wait for justice that never comes.

      A man kneels to pick up food on Withers Street. A dump truck turns. He is gone. The driver leaves. No arrest. “Police said the driver, a 49-year-old man, left the scene.” The city moves on. The street stays hungry.

      A cyclist is crushed on Evergreen Avenue. A pedestrian is launched ten feet by a hit-and-run in Bushwick. “He gets absolutely launched, maybe 10 to 15 feet right into the middle of the intersection, and just left there,” said a friend. The driver is gone. The intersection is unchanged.

      Trucks, SUVs, sedans—steel against flesh. In District 34, trucks and SUVs killed more than any other vehicle. The numbers are cold. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises, Votes, and the Slow Grind of Change

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez has signed her name to bills that promise safer streets. She backed the SAFE Streets Act, the push for 20 mph speed limits, and the ban on parking near crosswalks. She voted to legalize crossing mid-block, ending the old blame game against walkers. She stood with neighbors for a safer McGuinness Boulevard. She called for daylight at corners where drivers once hid behind parked cars.

      But the blood keeps running. Bills sit in committee. Promises wait for a vote. The city talks of redesigns, but corners stay blind, and trucks still turn wide. The law moves slow. The street moves fast.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another risk. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It can daylight every corner. It can build real protection for people on foot and bike. It can act, or it can wait for the next siren.

      Call your council member. Demand action. Do not wait for another name on the list. The street will not wait for you.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 34 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 34?
      It includes the East Williamsburg neighborhood. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 37, AD 38, AD 50, and AD 53, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 18. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 34?
      SUVs and cars caused 1 death and 77 injuries; trucks and buses caused 2 deaths and 10 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 4 injuries. Bikes caused 1 serious injury and 6 moderate injuries. See NYC Open Data crash records.
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. The data shows patterns—trucks turning, speed, blind corners. These deaths and injuries are preventable with safer streets and real enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, daylighting, protected bike lanes, and redesigns that put people before cars. They can push for action, not just words.

      Int 0346-2024
      Gutiérrez votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.

      Int 0875-2024
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors bill to boost step street lighting, improving pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council wants lights on city step streets. The bill orders the DOT to brighten at least 25 stairways a year. Dark stairs mean danger. Light means fewer falls, fewer hidden threats. The push comes from a broad coalition. The goal is simple: safer steps.

      Int 0875-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced May 16, 2024, it commands the Department of Transportation to install pedestrian lighting on at least 25 step streets each year. The bill’s matter title reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the installation of pedestrian lighting on step streets.' Prime sponsor Joann Ariola leads, joined by Council Members Hanks, Ayala, Salaam, Brooks-Powers, Banks, Restler, Brannan, Ung, Gutiérrez, Louis, Hudson, Schulman, Ossé, Krishnan, Nurse, Moya, and Marmorato. Step streets are public staircases linking streets at different heights. The bill aims to ensure these stairs are well-lit, reducing risk for walkers. Once all step streets are lit, the mandate ends. The measure targets a clear hazard: darkness on city stairs.
