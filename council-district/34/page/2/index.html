      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 34?

      District 34: Morgan Avenue, Broadway and the Toll of Delay

      Written August 15, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Finisher pass: copyedit, fact-check, tighten prose, add local analysis and political solutions.)

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 15, 2025

      Morgan Avenue, again

      On 2025-08-06 a box truck struck and killed a pedestrian at Morgan Avenue and Maspeth Avenue in the early morning, the latest fatal crash on a corridor residents say is unsafe for anyone on foot or bike. This stretch has seen repeated deaths: city data show 15 people killed and 35 seriously injured in District 34 since 2022 (NYC Open Data).

      Advocates and neighbors have long demanded change. “I was very frustrated that nothing has been done in more than three years since Daniel Vidal was killed,” Juan Ignacio Serra told Streetsblog NYC, calling attention to the avenue’s role as a vital north–south connection for people who bike and walk (Streetsblog NYC).

      Broadway hit-and-run

      On 2025-08-02, before dawn at Broadway and Suydam Street, a driver struck and killed a 47-year-old pedestrian and fled the scene, police said; investigators reported the victim was dragged more than 50 feet and asked the public for tips (NY Daily News; Gothamist).

      The pattern

      Crashes in District 34 concentrate on specific corridors — Morgan Avenue and Union Avenue rank among the worst intersections — and cluster during rush hours with many serious and late-night incidents in the dataset. Contributing factors reported by the city include disregarded traffic control, failure to yield, inattention/distraction and other causes.

      Heavy vehicles feature disproportionately in fatal crashes: trucks and buses account for four pedestrian deaths in the district while cars and SUVs account for two, even as motor vehicles of all types cause hundreds of injuries (NYC Open Data).

      What your council member did — and what’s missing

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez co-sponsored Int. 1353-2025, which would require DOT to install traffic-calming or control devices next to schools within 60 days of a study determination (NYC Council – Legistar). That targets schoolfront safety, but district data show danger extends beyond school blocks into industrial and residential routes used daily by workers and families.

      Albany has debated measures to force repeat reckless drivers to slow down: proposals to require speed-limiters for habitual speeders have circulated after deadly crashes (amNY). Citywide action to slow defaults and to require intelligent speed assistance for repeat offenders would reduce the risk for every neighborhood.

      Fix the hot spots now

      • Morgan Avenue: install end-to-end protected bike lanes, physical curb separation from truck loading areas, and frequent daylighting at crossings.
      • Broadway and Union avenues: daylight intersections, add leading pedestrian intervals (LPIs), upgrade nighttime lighting, and run targeted overnight enforcement.
      • Heavy-vehicle routes: adopt truck-safety routing, require side guards and speed-limiting devices on fleets serving dense residential areas.

      Political solutions for every street

      Local crash patterns underline two citywide priorities: lower New York City’s default speed limit and require speed limiters (intelligent speed assistance) for habitual speeders. Lowering neighborhood limits to 20 mph has already been adopted in targeted slow zones; expanding a 20 mph default citywide would save lives (Brooklyn Paper). Requiring speed-limiting technology for repeat reckless drivers is supported in policy proposals discussed after recent fatal crashes (amNY).

      Act

      Call Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez and DOT. Demand protected lanes on Morgan Avenue, daylighted crossings on Broadway and Union, and a citywide default 20 mph plus mandatory speed-limiting technology for habitual speeders. Start here: Take Action (/take_action/).

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Which areas are in District 34?
      It includes the Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Bushwick (West), Ridgewood, Brooklyn CB4, and Brooklyn CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 37, AD 38, AD 50, and AD 53, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 18.
      ▸ Where does District 34 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 37 and state senate district SD 12.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just "accidents"?
      Families here say they are preventable. “I was very frustrated that nothing has been done in more than three years since Daniel Vidal was killed,” said Juan Ignacio Serra.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 34?
      Cars and Trucks: cars/SUVs caused 380 pedestrian injuries and 2 deaths; trucks/buses caused 42 injuries and 4 deaths. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 17 injuries, 0 deaths. Bikes: 22 injuries, 0 deaths (city data).
      ▸ What has Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez done lately on street safety?
      She co-sponsored Int. 1353-2025, which would require DOT to install needed traffic-calming or control devices next to schools within 60 days of a study determination (Legistar).
      ▸ What can local politicians do right now?
      Back a 20 mph citywide default, fund and deliver protected lanes on Morgan and safe crossings on Broadway and Union, and press Albany to pass speed limiters for repeat reckless drivers (amNY).
      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      It’s the city’s legislature. In District 34, your representative is Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez, who sponsors and votes on laws that shape street safety.

      Int 1304-2025
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors bill requiring micromobility share systems to display safety rules.
      ▸

      Council bill orders bike and scooter share firms to show road rules at docks and in apps. Riders must review rules yearly. No extra fees. Aim: clear, visible reminders. Committee review underway.

      Bill Int 1304-2025 sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after introduction on June 11, 2025. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...requiring micromobility share system operators to display rules of the road for safe operation," demands operators post rules at stations and in apps. Riders must review these rules at least once a year before unlocking a device. The law bars operators from charging for the review time. Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers leads as primary sponsor, joined by Hanif, Brewer, Restler, Louis, Narcisse, Lee, Marte, Hanks, Banks, Gutiérrez, Ariola, and Morano. The measure aims for clear, accessible safety information but does not address street design or enforcement.

      Reynoso Supports Urgent Conduit Boulevard Safety Redesign
      ▸

      Five dead. Forty badly hurt. The Conduit slices through Queens and Brooklyn, fast and wide. DOT will study a fix. Borough presidents called for urgent change. The city will listen to neighbors. The old highway’s days are numbered. Lives hang in the balance.

      On May 13, 2025, the Department of Transportation (DOT) announced a city-funded public engagement process for a major safety redesign of Conduit Boulevard, a three-mile corridor linking Atlantic Avenue to the Belt Parkway and JFK Airport. The project follows urgent requests in 2023 from Queens Borough President Donovan Richards and Brooklyn Borough President Antonio Reynoso. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, "Brooklyn and Queens deserve a Conduit Boulevard that improves daily life, not a roadway that puts pedestrians and drivers at risk and physically divides entire neighborhoods." The corridor has seen five deaths and 40 severe injuries in five years. The median’s dirt paths show heavy pedestrian use, but only 15 crosswalks span the stretch. The public process begins in June and may run into 2026. The redesign aims to end decades of danger for vulnerable road users.
