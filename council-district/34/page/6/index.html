      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 34?

      District 34: Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 26, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 26, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In District 34, the street is a wound that never closes. Four people killed. Ten left with serious injuries. In the last year alone, 708 hurt, 1,435 crashes—each one a story that ends in pain or silence. The dead do not speak. The living limp, or grieve, or wait for justice that never comes.

      A man kneels to pick up food on Withers Street. A dump truck turns. He is gone. The driver leaves. No arrest. “Police said the driver, a 49-year-old man, left the scene.” The city moves on. The street stays hungry.

      A cyclist is crushed on Evergreen Avenue. A pedestrian is launched ten feet by a hit-and-run in Bushwick. “He gets absolutely launched, maybe 10 to 15 feet right into the middle of the intersection, and just left there,” said a friend. The driver is gone. The intersection is unchanged.

      Trucks, SUVs, sedans—steel against flesh. In District 34, trucks and SUVs killed more than any other vehicle. The numbers are cold. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises, Votes, and the Slow Grind of Change

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez has signed her name to bills that promise safer streets. She backed the SAFE Streets Act, the push for 20 mph speed limits, and the ban on parking near crosswalks. She voted to legalize crossing mid-block, ending the old blame game against walkers. She stood with neighbors for a safer McGuinness Boulevard. She called for daylight at corners where drivers once hid behind parked cars.

      But the blood keeps running. Bills sit in committee. Promises wait for a vote. The city talks of redesigns, but corners stay blind, and trucks still turn wide. The law moves slow. The street moves fast.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another risk. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It can daylight every corner. It can build real protection for people on foot and bike. It can act, or it can wait for the next siren.

      Call your council member. Demand action. Do not wait for another name on the list. The street will not wait for you.

      Take action now.

      Gutiérrez co-sponsors SAFE Streets Act, boosting street safety and protecting pedestrians.

The Council’s action highlights the city’s ongoing fight against reckless driving and the urgent need for stronger protections for people on foot, on bikes, and in cars.

      Int 0291-2022
      Gutiérrez votes yes, boosting citywide safety with new greenway plan.
      ▸

      The Council passed Int 0291-2022, forcing city agencies to map, plan, and report on greenways. The law demands annual updates and public engagement. It aims to carve out safe, car-free corridors for walkers and cyclists. The mayor returned it unsigned.

      Int 0291-2022, now Local Law 115 of 2022, was enacted by the City Council on November 27, 2022. The bill came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, first introduced April 28, 2022. The law's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to a citywide greenway plan.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers sponsored the bill, joined by dozens of co-sponsors including Rivera, Hudson, and others. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor on October 27, 2022. The law orders the Department of Transportation and Parks to identify, map, and report on greenways, and to consult with community boards. Proposals for new greenway segments or repairs must be presented to affected communities within 60 days. The mayor returned the bill unsigned, but it became law. The measure compels the city to plan and maintain safe routes for non-drivers, with regular public reporting and accountability.
