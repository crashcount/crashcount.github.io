      No More Ghosts on Broadway: Pass the Daylighting Bill Now

      Written August 6, 2025 (Revised August 6, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 6, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Silence

      District 34 bleeds. In the last twelve months, seven people died on its streets. Eight hundred forty-three were hurt. Eleven suffered injuries so grave they will never be the same. The dead do not get second chances. The living carry scars.

      Just days ago, a man was struck and dragged more than fifty feet at Broadway and Suydam Street. The driver did not stop. Police said, “Officers found the man critically injured… Detectives determined that he was hit by a vehicle traveling northbound on Broadway, and dragged more than 50 feet.” Gothamist

      A few blocks away, another driver killed a pedestrian and left him in the road. Police said, “A driver struck and killed a 47-year-old pedestrian crossing a Brooklyn street, then left the scene.” NY Daily News

      The pattern is clear. The most vulnerable—those on foot, on bikes, on mopeds—pay the price. Cars and trucks do the killing. The numbers do not lie: in three years, 14 deaths, 2,802 injuries, 35 serious injuries. NYC Open Data

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez has taken steps. She co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, forcing the city to clear sightlines and protect people at corners. The law would require daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections each year. The bill sits in committee, waiting. NYC Council – Legistar

      Every week of delay is another risk. The city moves slow. The street moves fast. The dead do not care about process.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. These are not accidents. Every crash is a policy failure. Every death is a choice made by those in power. The city can lower speed limits. It can redesign streets. It can end the hiding of drivers behind parked cars.

      Call Council Member Gutiérrez. Demand the daylighting bill moves. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand action, not words.

      The blood on the street is not washed away by silence. It is washed away by change.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 34 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 37 and state senate district SD 12.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 34?
      It includes the Williamsburg, East Williamsburg, Bushwick (West), Ridgewood, Brooklyn CB4, and Brooklyn CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 37, AD 38, AD 50, and AD 53, and State Senate Districts SD 12 and SD 18.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 34?
      Cars and trucks were responsible for 2 deaths, 288 minor injuries, 84 moderate injuries, and 3 serious injuries to pedestrians. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 0 deaths, 13 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, and 0 serious injuries. Bikes caused 0 deaths, 14 minor injuries, 6 moderate injuries, and 1 serious injury. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are preventable. They happen because of policy choices, street design, and enforcement decisions—not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and fund laws to redesign streets, lower speed limits, clear crosswalks, and hold reckless drivers accountable. Delay costs lives.

      Reynoso Supports Safety Boosting McGuinness Blvd Redesign
      ▸

      On August 16, 2023, Mayor Adams announced a compromise redesign for McGuinness Boulevard, a notorious Brooklyn corridor. The Department of Transportation will install curbside protected bike lanes and reduce car lanes from two to one in each direction on most of the strip. However, north of Calyer Street, two lanes for cars will remain open during weekdays, reverting to parking lanes at night and on weekends. The plan, shaped after lobbying by business interests and mayoral adviser Ingrid Lewis-Martin, mixes two previous DOT proposals. Mayoral spokesman Charles Lutvak said, 'Traffic safety is a key priority for Mayor Adams, and we are delivering with a redesign of McGuinness Boulevard that will make this corridor safer for all road users.' Local officials, including Council Member Lincoln Restler, called it a 'critical step' but noted it lacks key safety elements. The compromise leaves gaps. Since 2021, 62 people have been injured on this stretch. The carnage continues.
