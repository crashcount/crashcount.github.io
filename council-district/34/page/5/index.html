      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 34?

      Seven Dead in a Year—District 34 Bleeds While the City Waits

      Written August 8, 2025

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 8, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In District 34, the street does not forgive. Seven people have died in crashes here in the last year. Eight hundred forty-seven were injured. Eleven suffered wounds so grave they may never walk the same. The numbers are not just numbers. They are bodies on the pavement, families left waiting for a door that will never open again.

      Just days ago, a man was struck and dragged more than 50 feet on Broadway in Bushwick. The driver fled. Police found the man dying in the street. They are still looking for the one who killed him. Police were looking for the driver of a car that struck and dragged a 47-year-old man on a Brooklyn street over the weekend, killing him. The victim’s name has not been released. The city has not said if he was in a crosswalk. It does not matter. He is dead. The driver is gone.

      On Morgan Avenue, a box truck killed a pedestrian crossing near Sharon Street. No marked crosswalk. No protection. This is the third fatal crash on Morgan in three years. Juan Ignacio Serra, a local, was very frustrated that nothing has been done in more than three years since Daniel Vidal was killed. The city has not acted. The avenue remains a gauntlet for anyone on foot or bike.

      The Numbers Behind the Blood

      District 34 saw 1,725 crashes in the last 12 months. Seven dead. Eleven with life-altering injuries. The toll is rising. Last year, there were zero deaths in the same period. Now there are seven. Injuries are up by a third. Serious injuries up by almost half. The disaster is not sudden. It is slow, grinding, and relentless.

      Cars and trucks do most of the killing. Two deaths and 375 injuries came from sedans and SUVs. Trucks and buses killed three. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes add to the toll. No one is safe. Not the young, not the old. Not the ones crossing with the light. Not the ones just trying to get home.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez has co-sponsored a bill to ban parking within 20 feet of crosswalks, forcing the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections a year. The bill sits in committee. The city waits. The dead do not. See the bill.

      The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has not used it. The city can redesign streets. It has not moved fast enough. The city can protect the most vulnerable. It has not.

      Call to Action

      This is not fate. This is policy. Call Council Member Gutiérrez. Demand the city clear every crosswalk, lower every speed limit, and build streets that do not kill. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The blood is already on the street. Make sure it stops here.

      They can pass and fund laws to redesign streets, lower speed limits, and clear crosswalks. They can demand the city act faster and hold agencies accountable.

Brooklyn Borough President Antonio Reynoso, who led the original reforms as a council member, said, 'Crash data from [commercial waste zone] awardees is good, but crash data from CWZ applicants would have been even better.' The bill aims to end the 'Wild West' of private waste hauling, which killed seven New Yorkers in a single year. The new rules demand real-time telematics, immediate injury reports, and full transparency on crashes. Advocates say the data could help redesign streets and hold dangerous drivers to account. The city must prevent weak enforcement from gutting the law’s promise.
