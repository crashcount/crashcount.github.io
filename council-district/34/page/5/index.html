      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 34?

      District 34: Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 16, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In District 34, the street is a wound that never closes. Four people killed. Ten left with serious injuries. In the last year alone, 708 hurt, 1,435 crashes—each one a story that ends in pain or silence. The dead do not speak. The living limp, or grieve, or wait for justice that never comes.

      A man kneels to pick up food on Withers Street. A dump truck turns. He is gone. The driver leaves. No arrest. “Police said the driver, a 49-year-old man, left the scene.” The city moves on. The street stays hungry.

      A cyclist is crushed on Evergreen Avenue. A pedestrian is launched ten feet by a hit-and-run in Bushwick. “He gets absolutely launched, maybe 10 to 15 feet right into the middle of the intersection, and just left there,” said a friend. The driver is gone. The intersection is unchanged.

      Trucks, SUVs, sedans—steel against flesh. In District 34, trucks and SUVs killed more than any other vehicle. The numbers are cold. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises, Votes, and the Slow Grind of Change

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez has signed her name to bills that promise safer streets. She backed the SAFE Streets Act, the push for 20 mph speed limits, and the ban on parking near crosswalks. She voted to legalize crossing mid-block, ending the old blame game against walkers. She stood with neighbors for a safer McGuinness Boulevard. She called for daylight at corners where drivers once hid behind parked cars.

      But the blood keeps running. Bills sit in committee. Promises wait for a vote. The city talks of redesigns, but corners stay blind, and trucks still turn wide. The law moves slow. The street moves fast.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another risk. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It can daylight every corner. It can build real protection for people on foot and bike. It can act, or it can wait for the next siren.

      Call your council member. Demand action. Do not wait for another name on the list. The street will not wait for you.

      Take action now.

      Gutiérrez votes yes on bill requiring DOT to report micromobility data, no direct safety impact.
      ▸

      City law now forces DOT to reveal where bikes and scooters go. Streets and bridges, protected or not. Data goes public. Riders’ movement, danger, and safety projects laid bare. No more hiding the facts.

      Int 0745-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It was introduced April 11, 2024, and passed on August 15, 2024. The law orders the Department of Transportation to post current bicycle and micromobility ridership data online, updated monthly or at least yearly. The matter summary reads: 'requiring the department of transportation to provide information about bicycle and other micromobility device activity.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led as primary sponsor, joined by Farías, Restler, Hudson, Schulman, and others. The law demands transparency on where riders travel and what DOT does to keep them safe. This opens the books on bike movement and exposes gaps in protection.

      ▸

      Council moves to light up step streets. At least 25 stairways each year will get new lamps. Dark paths become visible. Pedestrians gain ground. Shadows shrink. Danger loses its cover.

      Int 0875-2024 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after introduction on May 16, 2024. The bill reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to requiring the installation of pedestrian lighting on step streets.' Prime sponsor Joann Ariola, joined by sixteen co-sponsors, pushes for at least 25 step streets to be lit each year until all are covered.       Reynoso Supports Transparency and Safety Data for Waste Haulers
      ▸

      City wants waste haulers to report every crash, injury, and reckless move. The rule targets companies with deadly records. Data will track harm, expose danger, and force accountability. Streets and lives hang in the balance. The city must act.

      On May 7, 2024, the Department of Sanitation proposed amendments to the Commercial Waste Zones program. The rule, not yet law, would require all contracted waste haulers to submit detailed crash and driving data twice a year. The matter targets companies like Action Carting, linked to at least five traffic deaths. Brooklyn Borough President Antonio Reynoso, who led the original reforms as a council member, said, 'Crash data from [commercial waste zone] awardees is good, but crash data from CWZ applicants would have been even better.' The bill aims to end the 'Wild West' of private waste hauling, which killed seven New Yorkers in a single year. The new rules demand real-time telematics, immediate injury reports, and full transparency on crashes. Advocates say the data could help redesign streets and hold dangerous drivers to account. The city must prevent weak enforcement from gutting the law’s promise.
