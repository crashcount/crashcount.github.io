      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 34?

      District 34: Blood on the Asphalt, Silence in City Hall

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 34: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In District 34, the street is a wound that never closes. Four people killed. Ten left with serious injuries. In the last year alone, 708 hurt, 1,435 crashes—each one a story that ends in pain or silence. The dead do not speak. The living limp, or grieve, or wait for justice that never comes.

      A man kneels to pick up food on Withers Street. A dump truck turns. He is gone. The driver leaves. No arrest. “Police said the driver, a 49-year-old man, left the scene.” The city moves on. The street stays hungry.

      A cyclist is crushed on Evergreen Avenue. A pedestrian is launched ten feet by a hit-and-run in Bushwick. “He gets absolutely launched, maybe 10 to 15 feet right into the middle of the intersection, and just left there,” said a friend. The driver is gone. The intersection is unchanged.

      Trucks, SUVs, sedans—steel against flesh. In District 34, trucks and SUVs killed more than any other vehicle. The numbers are cold. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises, Votes, and the Slow Grind of Change

      Council Member Jennifer Gutiérrez has signed her name to bills that promise safer streets. She backed the SAFE Streets Act, the push for 20 mph speed limits, and the ban on parking near crosswalks. She voted to legalize crossing mid-block, ending the old blame game against walkers. She stood with neighbors for a safer McGuinness Boulevard. She called for daylight at corners where drivers once hid behind parked cars.

      But the blood keeps running. Bills sit in committee. Promises wait for a vote. The city talks of redesigns, but corners stay blind, and trucks still turn wide. The law moves slow. The street moves fast.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      Every day of delay is another risk. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It can daylight every corner. It can build real protection for people on foot and bike. It can act, or it can wait for the next siren.

      Call your council member. Demand action. Do not wait for another name on the list. The street will not wait for you.

      Take action now.

The statement, titled 'Congestion Pricing Benefits Are Inarguable; Its Minor Problems are Easily Fixable,' urged city, state, and federal leaders to advance the Central Business District Tolling Program. Wright said, 'Congestion pricing will have benefits for communities across the city and region: It will reduce traffic congestion and improve air quality. It will raise vital revenue for public transit. It will make the transportation system more equitable.' Brooklyn Borough President Antonio Reynoso and Council Member Lincoln Restler planned a rally in support. The statement acknowledged minor impacts, like increased traffic on select highways, but insisted these can be addressed by electrifying fleets and capping highways. The focus remained on the program’s broad benefits for vulnerable road users and city residents.

      Int 0501-2022
      Gutiérrez co-sponsors bill boosting civilian reporting to improve street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants near schools. Civilians can report violators. Each offense draws a $175 fine. The city pays whistleblowers a cut. The bill stalled. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0501-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on June 2, 2022. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations,' aimed to create a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 1,320 feet of a school. The penalty: $175 per violation, enforced through the Office of Administrative Trials and Hearings. Civilians, except city employees, could file complaints and receive 25% of collected fines. The Department of Transportation would support this with a phased-in reporting program and annual public reports. Council Member Carlina Rivera led as primary sponsor, joined by over two dozen co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law.
