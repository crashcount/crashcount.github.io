      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 6?

      Seven Dead. Still Waiting. No Excuses Left.

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 6: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      Seven dead. Twelve seriously hurt. In the last year alone, District 6 has seen 434 crashes, 236 injuries, and seven lives lost, according to NYC Open Data. The dead are not numbers. They are neighbors: a 74-year-old cyclist killed on West End Avenue, a 57-year-old man crushed at Broadway and 86th, an 83-year-old woman struck at 58th and Sixth. The old, the young, the ones just crossing the street. SUVs killed four. Trucks, buses, bikes, and mopeds took others.

      The Latest Wounds

      Just months ago, a cyclist was killed in Central Park, another on West End Avenue. A pedestrian died at Broadway and 86th. The pattern is the same: a person steps out, a car or truck or bike does not stop. The street stays open. The blood washes away. The city moves on.

      Leadership: Promises and Pressure

      Council Member Gale A. Brewer has backed some safety bills. She co-sponsored a law to ban parking near crosswalks, aiming to clear sightlines and protect people on foot and bike. The bill would force the city to install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections each year, not just 100, as detailed in NYC Council legislation. But the bill sits in committee, waiting. Brewer has also pushed for delivery app companies to provide safety gear and training, saying, “My bill will make sure the situation changes.” She has spoken against criminal penalties for delivery workers, stating, “I am not supportive of criminal penalties.”

      But the deaths keep coming. The city delays. The council debates. The streets do not forgive.

      What Next? The Work Is Not Done

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a failure. Call Council Member Brewer. Demand the council pass the daylighting bill. Demand real protected bike lanes, not just promises. Demand that delivery workers get the protection they need. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Contact your leaders. Demand action. Lives depend on it.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 6 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 67 and state senate district SD 28.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 6?
      It includes the Upper West Side-Lincoln Square, Upper West Side (Central), Central Park, Manhattan CB7, and Manhattan CB64 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 67, AD 68, AD 69, and AD 75, and State Senate Districts SD 28, SD 30, and SD 47.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 6?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm to pedestrians in District 6, with 5 deaths and 6 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no pedestrian deaths but 1 serious injury. Bikes caused no pedestrian deaths but 6 serious injuries.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Every crash is preventable. Safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws that protect people walking and biking, fund protected bike lanes, ban parking near crosswalks, and hold dangerous drivers accountable.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
