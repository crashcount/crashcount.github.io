      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 1?

      No More Broken Bodies: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 15, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Streets Unforgiving

      Five dead. Fourteen left with life-altering wounds. In the last year alone, District 1 saw 680 people injured in 1,458 crashes. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about age. A cyclist, 44, slammed into a van door on Broome Street and was thrown under a truck. Medics tried to bring him back. They could not. “I saw a body on the ground, and then the fire people were trying to revive him. It didn’t look good,” said a witness. The street kept moving.

      Pedestrians, cyclists, children. The most vulnerable pay the highest price. Trucks, SUVs, cars—they do not yield. In three years, 35 people suffered serious injuries. Eleven did not survive. The dead do not get a second chance.

      Marte’s Record: Action and Delay

      Council Member Christopher Marte has stood up for safer streets. He backed the Delancey Street redesign, calling the corridor “an accident waiting to happen.” He co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, expand protected bike lanes, and legalize jaywalking, shifting blame away from those on foot. He pushed for lower speed limits and regional slow zones. But the city moves slow. The first slow zone covers a handful of blocks. Most streets remain unchanged. Promises do not stop trucks.

      The System Grinds On

      The danger is not random. Cars and trucks caused most deaths and injuries. Buses, mopeds, bikes—all played their part. The city counts the bodies. The council passes bills. The curb stays deadly. “I opened the door. I didn’t even see the guy. I only saw the accident,” said the van driver. The cyclist never saw tomorrow.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. Every crash is a policy choice. Every death is a failure to act. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand protected lanes, not just promises. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Take action now.

      Int 0857-2024
      Marte votes yes to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council orders swift removal of abandoned, derelict cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. No plates, no stickers, no excuses. Police and sanitation must act. Safer crossings for all who walk, ride, or wait.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council on June 30, 2025. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to vehicles reported as abandoned to the department of sanitation," requires the Department of Sanitation to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours of report. The NYPD must tow cars lacking valid plates or stickers. Prime sponsor Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led, joined by Crystal Hudson, Amanda Farías, Lincoln Restler, and others. The law targets street hazards, clearing blocked sightlines and crosswalks. It aims to cut risks for pedestrians and cyclists by removing abandoned vehicles fast.

      City Launches Certified E-Bike Trade-In Program
      ▸

      A lithium battery fire killed Fazil Khan in Harlem. The blaze spread fast, doors failed, and 22 others were hurt. The city now swaps uncertified e-bikes for safer ones. Fires persist, but deaths have dropped. Danger remains for riders and neighbors.

      West Side Spirit (2025-06-12) reports New York City’s new e-bike trade-in program, part of the Charge Safe, Ride Safe plan, after deadly lithium-ion battery fires. In February 2024, journalist Fazil Khan died in a Harlem apartment fire started by a charging battery; 22 others were injured. The article notes, 'Fires from unregulated e-bike batteries have already caused too much harm, including tragic loss of life.' The city’s $2 million pilot lets delivery workers exchange uncertified e-bikes and batteries for certified, UL-standard models. The program also requires a safety course. While battery-related fires rose slightly from 268 in 2023 to 277 in 2024, fatalities fell from 18 to 6. The article highlights ongoing risks from uncertified batteries and the city’s push for safer equipment.

      Congestion Pricing Tolls Face Legal Showdown
      ▸

      Tolls still hang over Third Avenue. Deadlines pass. Courts hold the answer. Fewer cars enter Manhattan. Millions flow to transit. The fight is bureaucratic, not on the street. Riders wait. The city’s pulse slows, but the outcome is uncertain.

      West Side Spirit reported on May 23, 2025, that New York’s congestion pricing tolls remain in place despite three missed federal deadlines to remove them. The article details a standoff between the MTA, New York State, and U.S. DOT Secretary Sean Duffy, who demands the tolls be taken down, threatening to withhold highway funds. The MTA argues the legality of the tolls and seeks a court injunction, stating, 'congestion pricing is legal and proper.' The system, operational since January 2025, has reduced daily vehicle entries into Manhattan’s core by 76,000 in April and raised $159 million in the first quarter. The dispute highlights tensions over funding priorities and the impact on lower-income drivers, but the courts, not drivers or pedestrians, will decide the fate of the tolls.
