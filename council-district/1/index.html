      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 1?

      Twelve Dead, Thousands Hurt—How Many More Before Marte Acts?

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Blood on the Streets

      In District 1, the numbers do not lie. Twelve people killed. Thirty-seven left with serious injuries. Over 2,300 hurt since 2022. The dead include children, elders, cyclists, and pedestrians. The living carry scars you cannot see.

      Just last week, a city worker was slashed by a delivery cyclist after a near miss at Broadway and Cedar. He was fixing a street sign. He was not in the bike lane. “They weren’t even in the bike lane, they were parked on the corner, fixing the light or the sign or something,” a witness said. The cyclist cut him and fled. The worker went to the hospital. The city kept moving. Read the account.

      Days earlier, a van towing a food cart broke loose and slammed into a parked car with a woman and child inside. Police found 76 propane tanks and 75 gallons of gasoline in the van. The driver was charged with reckless endangerment. The woman and child survived. This is not luck. This is a city playing with fire. See the report.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Cars and trucks do the most harm. Since 2022, they have killed at least eight people and injured hundreds more. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes have also left bodies broken. The old, the young, the ones just crossing the street. The numbers are not just numbers. They are names, faces, families.

      What Has Marte Done?

      Council Member Christopher Marte has backed bills to clear abandoned vehicles, ban fake plates, and boost lighting and speed humps near parks. He voted to decriminalize jaywalking and to require taxi door warning decals. He co-sponsored a bill to daylight crosswalks, keeping parked cars from blocking sightlines. But some bills move slow. Some changes are small. The city studies, delays, and the street keeps taking.

      On Canal Street, Marte said the time for action is long overdue. He pledged support for urgent changes. But the street is still the same. “He acknowledged the time is long overdue to take action in response to the dire conditions of Canal Street. He pledged his support to take action.” Read the pledge.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      This did not have to happen. Every death, every injury, every child in a hospital bed. These are not accidents. They are the price of delay. Call Council Member Marte. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand streets for people, not cars. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the legislative branch of city government. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the people of each district.
      ▸ Where does District 1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 61 and state senate district SD 26.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 1?
      It includes the Financial District-Battery Park City, Tribeca-Civic Center, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, Soho-Little Italy-Hudson Square, Chinatown-Two Bridges, Lower East Side, Manhattan CB2, Manhattan CB3, and Manhattan CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 61, AD 65, and AD 66, and State Senate Districts SD 26 and SD 27.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 1?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm, with at least 9 deaths and hundreds injured since 2022. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes also caused injuries and deaths, but at lower numbers.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents?
      No. The data shows patterns and repeat causes—speed, blocked sightlines, reckless driving. These deaths and injuries are preventable.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws to lower speed limits, redesign streets, clear blocked crosswalks, and hold dangerous drivers accountable. They can act faster and refuse to delay life-saving changes.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council on June 30, 2025. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to vehicles reported as abandoned to the department of sanitation," requires the Department of Sanitation to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours of report.
