      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 1?

      Canal Street Kills—City Shrugs, Bodies Stack

      Written July 28, 2025

      District 1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 28, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      Just last week, a hit-and-run driver killed a pedestrian and a cyclist at a Canal Street intersection. The driver fled. The community gathered for a vigil. The news anchor said the crash is “raising questions about a bigger safety problem at an intersection of Canal Street” (CBS New York).

      A few days before, a firefighter died on the FDR Drive. He fell from his motorcycle. A driver ran him over and left him in the road. Police said he was “struck by an unknown vehicle that did not remain on the scene” (ABC7).

      In the past twelve months, District 1 saw 3 deaths and 13 serious injuries. There were 807 injuries in total. The dead include children and elders. The living carry scars.

      The Numbers Are Names

      The numbers do not stop. Since 2022, District 1 has seen 13 deaths, 36 serious injuries, and 2,418 total injuries. Cars and trucks did most of the killing. Trucks alone took four lives. Bikes and mopeds are not blameless, but the weight of death rolls on four wheels.

      Marte’s Record: Small Steps, Slow Change

      Council Member Christopher Marte has voted for some safety bills. He backed laws to remove abandoned cars, require taxi dooring warnings, and co-sponsored bills for better lighting and speed humps near parks. He called for “immediate action” on Canal Street, saying the time is long overdue (Streetsblog NYC).

      But the street stays the same. Canal Street is still a gauntlet. The city studies, delays, and the bodies pile up. Bills for daylighting and crosswalk safety sit in committee. The most vulnerable—pedestrians, cyclists, children—wait for the city to choose them over cars.

      Call to Action: Demand More Than Words

      The deaths are not fate. They are policy. Call Council Member Marte. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings, daylighted corners, and real enforcement. Join Families for Safe Streets and Transportation Alternatives. Do not wait for another vigil. Make them act before another name becomes a number.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 1 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 61 and state senate district SD 26.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 1?
      It includes the Financial District-Battery Park City, Tribeca-Civic Center, The Battery-Governors Island-Ellis Island-Liberty Island, Soho-Little Italy-Hudson Square, Chinatown-Two Bridges, Lower East Side, Manhattan CB2, Manhattan CB3, and Manhattan CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 61, AD 65, and AD 66, and State Senate Districts SD 26 and SD 27.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 1?
      Cars and trucks caused the most harm to pedestrians in District 1, with 9 deaths and 17 serious injuries. Trucks alone were responsible for 4 deaths. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in 2 serious injuries but no deaths. Bikes were involved in 1 death and 1 serious injury.
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. The deaths and injuries in District 1 are the result of policy choices, not fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, protected crossings, daylighted corners, and real accountability for dangerous driving.

      Int 0857-2024
      Marte votes yes to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council orders swift removal of abandoned, derelict cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. No plates, no stickers, no excuses. Police and sanitation must act. Safer crossings for all who walk, ride, or wait.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council on June 30, 2025. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to vehicles reported as abandoned to the department of sanitation," requires the Department of Sanitation to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours of report. The NYPD must tow cars lacking valid plates or stickers. Prime sponsor Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led, joined by Crystal Hudson, Amanda Farías, Lincoln Restler, and others. The law targets street hazards, clearing blocked sightlines and crosswalks. It aims to cut risks for pedestrians and cyclists by removing abandoned vehicles fast.
