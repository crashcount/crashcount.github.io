      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 1?

      No More Broken Bodies: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 1: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Streets Unforgiving

      Five dead. Fourteen left with life-altering wounds. In the last year alone, District 1 saw 680 people injured in 1,458 crashes. The numbers do not flinch. They do not care about age. A cyclist, 44, slammed into a van door on Broome Street and was thrown under a truck. Medics tried to bring him back. They could not. “I saw a body on the ground, and then the fire people were trying to revive him. It didn’t look good,” said a witness. The street kept moving.

      Pedestrians, cyclists, children. The most vulnerable pay the highest price. Trucks, SUVs, cars—they do not yield. In three years, 35 people suffered serious injuries. Eleven did not survive. The dead do not get a second chance.

      Marte’s Record: Action and Delay

      Council Member Christopher Marte has stood up for safer streets. He backed the Delancey Street redesign, calling the corridor “an accident waiting to happen.” He co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, expand protected bike lanes, and legalize jaywalking, shifting blame away from those on foot. He pushed for lower speed limits and regional slow zones. But the city moves slow. The first slow zone covers a handful of blocks. Most streets remain unchanged. Promises do not stop trucks.

      The System Grinds On

      The danger is not random. Cars and trucks caused most deaths and injuries. Buses, mopeds, bikes—all played their part. The city counts the bodies. The council passes bills. The curb stays deadly. “I opened the door. I didn’t even see the guy. I only saw the accident,” said the van driver. The cyclist never saw tomorrow.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. Every crash is a policy choice. Every death is a failure to act. Call your council member. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand protected lanes, not just promises. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Take action now.

      Int 0346-2024
      Marte votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.

      Int 1039-2024
      Marte co-sponsors bike share fee cap, boosting cycling safety and access.
      ▸

      Council bill Int 1039-2024 would stop bike share operators from charging members more than a subway fare for short rides. The cap covers e-bikes up to one hour and regular bikes up to two hours. The bill sits in committee.

      Int 1039-2024, now in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced on September 12, 2024. The bill reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to setting maximum amounts for certain member usage fees charged by a bike share operator.' Council Member Chi A. Ossé leads as primary sponsor, joined by Restler, Marte, Hudson, Cabán, Brannan, Hanif, Brewer, and Abreu. The bill would prohibit bike share companies from charging members more than the base subway fare for e-bike rides of one hour or less, or regular bike rides of two hours or less. The law would take effect 120 days after passage and apply to new or renewed contracts. This measure aims to keep bike share affordable for New Yorkers, making cycling a more accessible option.

      Int 0177-2024
      Marte co-sponsors bill boosting street safety by targeting fake license plates.
      ▸

      Council moves to outlaw driving with fake or expired plates. The bill sets fines and a short grace period for expired tags. Lawmakers say this targets scofflaws who dodge accountability. The measure now sits with the Public Safety Committee.

      Int 0177-2024, now in the Committee on Public Safety, was introduced on February 28, 2024. The bill reads: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to prohibiting the operation of a motor vehicle with fraudulent or expired license plates.' Council Member Selvena N. The bill makes it unlawful to drive with fake or expired plates, including temporary ones. Civil penalties apply, but drivers with expired plates get a 10-day window to fix the issue. The bill aims to close loopholes that let reckless drivers evade detection and accountability. It awaits further action in committee.
