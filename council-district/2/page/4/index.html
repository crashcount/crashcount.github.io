      Fifteen Dead, Still Waiting: Make District 2 Streets Safe Now

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed grammar, tightened prose, ensured proper citation links, removed duplicate headline, deduped citations.)

      District 2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In District 2, the street is a wound that never closes. Since 2022, 15 people have died in crashes here. Twenty-eight more were seriously injured. These are not just numbers—they are mothers, sons, neighbors. A 79-year-old woman struck dead in a crosswalk at Bowery and East 4th. A 31-year-old woman killed by an SUV on the FDR. A 49-year-old man crushed by a bus at 3rd Avenue and 28th. The city records the deaths. The families keep the grief, as shown in NYC Open Data.

      The Latest Blows

      The violence does not stop. In June, a bus killed a man at 3rd Avenue. Last summer, a sedan hit a 59-year-old man on the FDR. The stories repeat. Cars and trucks did most of the killing—7 deaths, 6 serious injuries. Buses and trucks took 3 more lives. Bikes left 5 people with serious injuries, but no deaths. The street does not care who you are. It only takes.

      Leadership: Small Steps, Slow Change

      Council Member Carlina Rivera has backed some bills to clear crosswalks and fix broken sidewalks. She voted to remove abandoned vehicles and speed up pavement markings. She co-sponsored a bill to fine property owners who leave sidewalks cracked and dangerous. But the pace is slow. The city still allows cars to hide pedestrians at corners. The council still debates how much to ask of drivers. “We need to do something to make sure bicyclists feel safer,” said a local leader at a recent hearing, but the lanes are still missing.

      The Call

      Fifteen dead. Twenty-eight broken. The street is not safe until the killing stops. Call Council Member Rivera. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every corner. Demand action, not delay. The next name on the list could be yours.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 2 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 65 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 2?
      It includes the Greenwich Village, Lower East Side, East Village, Gramercy, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 65, AD 66, and AD 74, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 2?
      Cars and trucks caused 7 deaths and 6 serious injuries to pedestrians in District 2. Buses and trucks caused 3 more deaths. Bikes caused no deaths but 5 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no deaths and no serious injuries, according to NYC Open Data.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. Most deaths and injuries can be prevented with safer street design, lower speeds, and better enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass laws to lower speed limits, clear crosswalks, redesign dangerous streets, and hold drivers accountable. They can also fund and enforce these changes.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
