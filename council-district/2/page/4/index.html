      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      East 28th and 3rd: a bus, a man, and a death

      District 2: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      A man walked at East 28th Street and 3rd Avenue. The driver of a bus hit him. He died there. NYC Open Data, CrashID 4820937.

      He is one of 16 people killed and 2,340 injured on District 2 streets since Jan 1, 2022, across 4,235 crashes. The city’s own data shows 38 serious injuries in that span. NYC Open Data.

      Where the street bites back

      Avenue D. FDR Drive. 1st Avenue. These are among the district’s worst corridors by deaths and injuries. NYC Open Data.

      Police records show drivers killing at all hours, but late day is cruel. At 4 PM, three people died. At 7 PM, two more. NYC Open Data.

      Drivers’ choices are on the page. Police recorded “unsafe speed” in cases here, with two serious injuries tied to it. They recorded a driver “disregarded traffic control” in a crash that killed. NYC Open Data.

      Heavy vehicles add weight to the harm. Buses and trucks are linked to three pedestrian deaths here; cars and SUVs to seven. NYC Open Data.

      The pattern doesn’t quit

      This year to date, District 2 has 760 crashes, 2 deaths, and 10 serious injuries. At this point last year, it was 759 crashes, 7 deaths, and 7 serious injuries. Fewer deaths so far. More people badly hurt. NYC Open Data.

      One name from these blocks: a 49-year-old man killed by a bus driver at E 28 St and 3 Ave on Jun 16, 2025. Police listed driver distraction and a crush injury. NYC Open Data, CrashID 4820937.

      Who moves, who stalls

      The Council can daylight crosswalks faster. Council Member Carlina Rivera co‑sponsors a bill to ban parking within 20 feet of crosswalks and require barriers at 1,000 intersections a year (Int 1138‑2024). The committee laid it over in April. NYC Council – Legistar.

      Sidewalks break ankles and lives. Rivera also backs fines for owners who ignore sidewalk hazards (Int 1320‑2025). It sits in committee. NYC Council – Legistar.

      Albany handed the city the power to lower speeds. The city can set safer defaults under Sammy’s Law. Use it. Make 20 MPH the rule on our residential streets. /take_action/.

      Repeat speeders keep showing up. The state can require speed limiters for the worst offenders. The Stop Super Speeders Act is on the table in Albany. Ask State Senator Brian Kavanagh (SD 27) and Assembly Member Grace Lee (AD 65): will you co‑sponsor and push it? /take_action/.

      Build what was promised

      The city released a greenway plan to map gaps and “strengths and weaknesses.” It arrived late and lists studies, not build dates. The East Side gap remains. Streetsblog NYC.

      Daylight the corners. Harden the turns. Slow the cars. Name the streets where people die, and fix them.

      A man died at East 28th and 3rd. The tools sit on the shelf. Pick them up. /take_action/.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ How were these numbers calculated?
      We analyzed NYC’s Motor Vehicle Collisions datasets (Crashes h9gi-nx95, Persons f55k-p6yu, Vehicles bm4k-52h4) filtered to Council District 2 for 2022-01-01 to 2025-09-18. We used fields for crash date, injury severity (including “Killed” and “Serious Injury”), person type, and vehicle type to total crashes, injuries, serious injuries, and deaths; and to identify corridors and hours with deaths. Data was extracted on Sep 17–18, 2025. You can start from the crash dataset here and filter by Council District 2 and date range to reproduce the counts.
      ▸ What are the worst spots in District 2?
      Our analysis flags Avenue D, FDR Drive, 1st Avenue, 3rd Avenue, and Avenue C as top locations by deaths and injuries since 2022, with Avenue D and FDR Drive among the deadliest.
      ▸ What causes show up in police reports here?
      Police records in this district include crashes with drivers recorded for “unsafe speed” (serious injuries) and “disregarded traffic control” (a death). Driver distraction also appears in fatal cases.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ What can fix this, now?
      Daylight crosswalks and harden turns (Int 1138‑2024), repair broken sidewalks faster (Int 1320‑2025), set a 20 MPH default under Sammy’s Law, and pass the Stop Super Speeders Act to require speed limiters for repeat offenders. Take action /here.

      Int 1138-2024 Rivera co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Dec 5 - Council bill bars cars from blocking crosswalks. No standing or parking within 20 feet. City must install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections yearly. Streets clear. Sightlines open. Danger cut.

      Int 1138-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 5, 2024. The bill states: “prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.” Council Member Erik D. Bottcher leads, joined by Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and over two dozen co-sponsors. The law orders the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections each year, up from 100. The city must also run outreach and education. The bill aims to keep crosswalks clear, improve visibility, and protect people on foot and bike. No more hiding behind parked cars. The committee laid the bill over on April 21, 2025.

      Int 0346-2024 Rivera votes yes to decriminalize jaywalking, boosting pedestrian safety citywide.
      ▸

      Sep 26 - Council ends jaywalking penalties. Pedestrians now cross anywhere, any time. No summons. Law strips drivers of excuses. Streets shift. Power tilts to people on foot.

      Int 0346-2024, now enacted, rewrites New York’s pedestrian rules. The bill, heard by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, passed on September 26, 2024, and became law on October 26, 2024. Its title: “A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way.” Sponsored by Council Member Tiffany Cabán and co-sponsored by Narcisse, Won, Hanif, Hudson, and Restler, the law lets pedestrians cross streets anywhere, even against signals. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks. The Department of Transportation must educate all street users on new rights and responsibilities. The law removes a tool police used to target walkers. It shifts blame from people on foot to the system and those behind the wheel.
