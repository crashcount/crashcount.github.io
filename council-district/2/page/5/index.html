      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 2?

      Fourteen Dead, Hundreds Hurt—Rivera’s District Bleeds While Drivers Walk Free

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 23, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 2: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt

      In District 2, the numbers do not lie. Fourteen dead. Twenty-nine left with serious wounds. In the last twelve months alone, 493 people were hurt in 865 crashes. The youngest was a child. The oldest, a grandmother. The street does not care.

      A cyclist in the West Village was thrown from his bike, bones broken, memory lost. “All my body was blood and the sweater,” he said. The driver ran. The city moved on.

      On East 4th and Avenue D, a car made a U-turn and used its weight as a weapon. A man was left on the pavement, the driver gone. No arrests. No peace. Police are still looking.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Pedestrians and cyclists take the brunt. Cars and SUVs killed seven. Trucks and buses killed two. Bikes left five with serious injuries. The city counts the bodies. The families count the days.

      A witness saw a cyclist hit. “The guy’s bike goes in one way. The guy flies in the other direction. It was pretty crazy,” he said. The horror is routine now.

      Rivera’s Record: Progress and Gaps

      Council Member Carlina Rivera has voted for bills to daylight intersections, speed up protected bike lanes, and legalize crossing mid-block. She co-sponsored the city’s greenway master plan and pushed for solar crosswalks and better lighting. She voted to end jaywalking enforcement, a law that too often blamed the dead instead of the driver. But the carnage continues. No law yet has stopped the bleeding.

      What Now?

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Rivera. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected crossings and real enforcement. Join Families for Safe Streets and Transportation Alternatives. Do not wait for another name on the list.

      Take action now.

      Int 0501-2022
      Rivera sponsors bill empowering civilian reporting of vehicle obstructions, boosting pedestrian and cyclist safety.
      ▸

      Council bill targets cars blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, and hydrants near schools. Civilians can report violators. Each offense draws a $175 fine. The city pays whistleblowers a cut. The bill stalled. Streets stay dangerous.

      Int 0501-2022 was introduced to the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure on June 2, 2022. The bill, titled 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to hazardous obstruction by vehicles and civilian complaints to the department of transportation for hazardous obstruction violations,' aimed to create a new civil penalty for vehicles blocking bike lanes, bus lanes, sidewalks, crosswalks, or hydrants within 1,320 feet of a school. The penalty: $175 per violation, enforced through the Office of Administrative Trials and Hearings. Civilians, except city employees, could file complaints and receive 25% of collected fines. The Department of Transportation would support this with a phased-in reporting program and annual public reports. Council Member Carlina Rivera led as primary sponsor, joined by over two dozen co-sponsors. The bill was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023, and did not become law. No safety analyst note was provided.
