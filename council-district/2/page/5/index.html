      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 2?

      Fifteen Dead. Hundreds Hurt. Who Still Thinks These Streets Are Safe?

      Written August 5, 2025 (Revised August 13, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, deduped and sorted citations.)

      District 2: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 5, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Recent Toll

      In District 2, the numbers do not lie. Since January 2022, 15 people have died and 30 have been seriously injured on these streets. Six hundred were hurt in the last year alone. The dead include the old and the young. The wounds are deep and do not heal.

      Just weeks ago, a 65-year-old e-bike rider was struck on Second Avenue. The driver fled, later telling police he ran because he had no license. The cyclist was left with critical head trauma. The paper put it plain: “He now faces charges of leaving the scene of an accident that caused serious injury and driving without a license.” West Side Spirit

      On East 4th Street, a 79-year-old woman was killed at the crosswalk. A taxi hit her as she crossed. The record says only this: “Apparent Death.” NYC Open Data

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      Cars and SUVs do the most harm. In this district, they killed 7, injured hundreds, and left families broken. Trucks and buses killed 3 more. Bikes and mopeds hurt dozens. The street is a gauntlet. The sidewalk is no refuge. The old, the young, the cyclist, the walker—none are safe.

      Council Member Rivera: Progress and Gaps

      Council Member Carlina Rivera has backed bills to speed up pavement markings, boost street safety transparency, and force faster sidewalk repairs. She voted to clear abandoned vehicles and co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks. These are steps, not leaps. But the carnage continues.

      The city can lower speed limits. It can redesign streets. It can end the reign of repeat offenders. But every delay costs lives. As one resident said after a recent crash, “I really want there to be speed humps because it’s just terrifying.”

      Call to Action: Demand More

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Council Member Rivera. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand action, not excuses. Every day of delay is another day of blood on the street.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 2 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Manhattan, assembly district AD 65 and state senate district SD 27.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 2?
      It includes the Greenwich Village, Lower East Side, East Village, Gramercy, Manhattan CB2, and Manhattan CB3 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 65, AD 66, and AD 74, and State Senate Districts SD 27, SD 28, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 2?
      Cars and SUVs killed 7 pedestrians and injured hundreds. Trucks and buses killed 3 more. Bikes caused 64 injuries, including 5 serious ones. Motorcycles and mopeds hurt 15.
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Most crashes are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and enforcement against reckless drivers can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, clear blocked crosswalks, and pass laws that hold reckless drivers accountable.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0080-2024
