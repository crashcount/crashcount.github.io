      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 12?

      Blood on Riley’s Streets: Demand Safe Roads Now

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed citation formatting, and deduped citations.)

      District 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Numbers Don’t Lie

      Eleven people killed. Over 2,000 injured. In District 12, these are not just numbers. They are mothers, sons, neighbors. Since 2022, there have been 3,357 crashes. Thirty-six people suffered serious injuries. Eleven never made it home. Most of the dead and wounded were walking or riding. The street did not forgive them.

      Recent Crashes: No End in Sight

      The violence does not stop. On July 11, a 79-year-old driver crashed into two cars and a pole in Co-op City. His 71-year-old passenger, Stella Nyarko-Dei, died. Six others were hurt. A witness described the scene: “I saw one lady was out on the ground. They was giving her medical attention, checking her body. She was laid out.”

      Just days before, a driver in a Ford Mustang rammed into six people after botching a turn at an intersection in the Bronx. The driver fled. The victims were left on the sidewalk, waiting for help.

      SUVs and cars do most of the harm. In the last three years, they killed six, seriously hurt seven, and injured hundreds more. Trucks, mopeds, and bikes also left their mark, but the biggest threat rolls on four wheels.

      What Has Council Member Riley Done?

      Council Member Kevin C. Riley has voted for some safety bills. He backed laws to remove abandoned vehicles, improve street safety transparency, and add pavement markings. He co-sponsored a bill to require speed humps near parks. He voted to decriminalize jaywalking, a move that boosts pedestrian safety citywide.

      But some actions miss the mark. Riley co-sponsored an e-bike registration bill that shifts the burden to riders, not drivers. He voted for taxi dooring decals—small steps, not real change. The streets remain dangerous. The dead cannot speak for themselves.

      The Next Step: Demand More

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every crash is a choice made by the city. Call Council Member Riley. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking. Do not wait for the next siren.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx, assembly district AD 80 and state senate district SD 36.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 12?
      It includes the Co-Op City, Allerton, Williamsbridge-Olinville, Eastchester-Edenwald-Baychester, and Bronx CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 80, AD 82, and AD 83, and State Senate District SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 12?
      SUVs and cars killed 6 people and injured hundreds. Trucks killed 1 and injured dozens. Mopeds and motorcycles caused 1 death and several injuries. Bikes were involved in a handful of injuries but no deaths. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and better enforcement save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund street redesigns, support automated enforcement, and pass laws that protect people walking and biking.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
