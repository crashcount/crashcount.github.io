      No More Deaths on White Plains Road—Make Safety the Law, Not a Slogan

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      A woman lies on White Plains Road, struck by an SUV. She is 78. The driver stays. The woman dies at Jacobi Medical Center. Her name is not in the data, but her absence is felt. In the last twelve months, four people have died and 596 have been injured in crashes across District 12. Eight suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again. Most were walking. Some were just children.

      A coach stands outside his home in Baychester. A BMW, a pickup, parked cars. Metal and glass. The coach is gone. His mother says, “These arrests cannot bring back my child. Still, something has to be done.” The numbers do not grieve, but families do.

      SUVs and sedans hit hardest. In three years, cars and trucks killed at least five people here. Mopeds and bikes are not spared, but the weight of death rolls on four wheels.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Kevin C. Riley has voted for some changes. He backed the law that ended jaywalking tickets, shifting blame away from those on foot and toward the street itself. He co-sponsored bills for speed humps near parks, better lighting, and more signs near schools. He called out the rising death toll, saying, “The tragic increase of fatal traffic violence in the Bronx so far this year is a testament to the immense work that still must be done to ensure all New York City streets are safe for all.”

      But the blood on the street does not care for studies or advisory boards. Most bills are still in committee. Signs do not stop cars. Speed humps are rare. The city can lower the speed limit to 20 mph. It has not.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. Lower the speed. Build real protection. Demand more than words. Call Council Member Riley. Call the Mayor. Join the fight. Every day without action is another day someone does not come home.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents each district’s residents.
      ▸ Where does District 12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 12?
      It includes the Co-Op City, Williamsbridge-Olinville, and Eastchester-Edenwald-Baychester neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 80, AD 82, and AD 83, and State Senate District SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 12?
      Most deaths and injuries to pedestrians came from SUVs and Sedans (5 deaths, 76 moderate injuries, 7 serious injuries), with fewer from Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 3 moderate injuries, 1 serious injury), and Bikes (no deaths, 1 moderate injury, no serious injuries). Data source
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random accidents. Lower speeds, safer street design, and real enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes and crosswalks, and pass laws that put safety for walkers and cyclists first.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Traffic Safety Timeline for Council District 12

      Res 0009-2022
      Riley co-sponsors resolution for accessible subways, improving safety for vulnerable riders.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to make every renovated subway station fully accessible. Only a fraction of stations have elevators. Lawmakers want no more half-measures. The resolution was filed at session’s end. Riders with disabilities remain stranded underground.

      Resolution 0009-2022 was introduced on February 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It called on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to ensure any subway station undergoing enhancement or renovation becomes fully accessible to people with disabilities. The matter title reads: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make any subway stations undergoing enhancement or renovation fully accessible to people with disabilities.' Council Member Darlene Mealy sponsored the resolution, joined by Brannan, Menin, Louis, Yeger, Hanif, Hudson, Marte, Joseph, Riley, and Brooks-Powers. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. Only 117 out of 493 subway stations are accessible. The Council’s action highlights the city’s failure to guarantee safe, equal passage for all riders. Elevators and upgrades are overdue. The bill’s filing leaves vulnerable New Yorkers waiting.
