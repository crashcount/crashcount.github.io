      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 12?

      Ten Dead, Hundreds Broken—Lower the Speed or Count More Bodies

      Written June 18, 2025 (Revised June 20, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, tightened FAQ citation, deduped and sorted citations.)

      District 12: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 18, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Broken

      In District 12, the numbers do not lie. Ten people killed. Thirty-six left with serious injuries. Nearly two thousand hurt. These are not just numbers. They are mothers, fathers, children. In the last twelve months alone, four people died and 589 were injured in 819 crashes. The dead include a 76-year-old woman struck by an SUV on White Plains Road, a 60-year-old man crushed on Givan Avenue, and a 64-year-old man killed crossing at Bronxwood Avenue. Each one gone in a moment. Each one leaves a hole that never heals.

      The Vehicles: Steel Against Flesh

      SUVs and cars did most of the killing. Five deaths, seventy-seven moderate injuries, seven serious injuries. Trucks and buses took another life. Motorcycles and mopeds left more bodies broken. The street is a gauntlet. The sidewalk is no refuge. The numbers are clear: the bigger the vehicle, the greater the harm.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Kevin C. Riley has taken steps. He voted to legalize jaywalking, ending a law that punished the vulnerable and did nothing to stop the carnage. He co-sponsored bills for more school safety signs, speed humps near parks, better lighting, and safer crosswalks. He called out the crisis: “The tragic increase of fatal traffic violence in the Bronx so far this year is a testament to the immense work that still must be done to ensure all New York City streets are safe for all” said Riley.

      But bills for signs and studies do not slow a speeding car. Speed kills. A pedestrian hit at 30 mph is five times more likely to die than at 20 mph. The city now has the power to lower speed limits. Every day of delay is another risk, another family shattered.

      The Next Step: Demand More

      Call Council Member Riley. Call the Mayor. Demand a 20 mph speed limit on every street. Demand real protection for walkers and cyclists. Join Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Do not wait for another name to join the list.

      Take action now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 12 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Bronx.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 12?
      It includes the Co-Op City, Williamsbridge-Olinville, and Eastchester-Edenwald-Baychester neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 80, AD 82, and AD 83, and State Senate District SD 36.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 12?
      Cars and SUVs caused the most harm: 5 deaths, 77 moderate injuries, and 7 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 1 death, 6 moderate injuries, and 1 serious injury. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 1 serious injury and 3 moderate injuries. Bikes caused 1 moderate injury. (Source: NYC Open Data)
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, better street design, and enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, and pass laws that protect pedestrians and cyclists instead of blaming them.

      Res 0009-2022
      Riley co-sponsors resolution for accessible subways, improving safety for vulnerable riders.
      ▸

      Council called on the MTA to make every renovated subway station fully accessible. Only a fraction of stations have elevators. Lawmakers want no more half-measures. The resolution was filed at session’s end. Riders with disabilities remain stranded underground.

      Resolution 0009-2022 was introduced on February 10, 2022, in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It called on the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to ensure any subway station undergoing enhancement or renovation becomes fully accessible to people with disabilities. The matter title reads: 'Resolution calling upon the Metropolitan Transportation Authority to make any subway stations undergoing enhancement or renovation fully accessible to people with disabilities.' Council Member Darlene Mealy sponsored the resolution, joined by Brannan, Menin, Louis, Yeger, Hanif, Hudson, Marte, Joseph, Riley, and Brooks-Powers. The resolution was filed at the end of session on December 31, 2023. Only 117 out of 493 subway stations are accessible. The Council’s action highlights the city’s failure to guarantee safe, equal passage for all riders. Elevators and upgrades are overdue. The bill’s filing leaves vulnerable New Yorkers waiting.
