      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 19?

      Fourteen Dead, Still No Safe Streets—Who Will Stop the Killing?

      Written July 18, 2025

      District 19: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      In District 19, the numbers do not lie. Fourteen dead. Forty-eight with injuries so severe they will not forget. In the past twelve months, three people died and fifteen were seriously hurt. One was a child. One was an elder. The rest were neighbors, crossing streets, riding bikes, or just walking home.

      On June 11, a 74-year-old man was killed at the intersection of 217th Street and Northern Boulevard. He was crossing. An SUV hit him. He died at the scene. The driver was unlicensed. The street was unchanged. The story is always the same.

      On July 11, a Q20 bus in Flushing jumped the curb and smashed into a pole. Eight people were hurt. A passenger described the chaos: “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus.” The driver had fallen asleep. The MTA pulled him from service. The sidewalk was almost not empty.

      Who Pays the Price?

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. In three years, SUVs alone took five lives and left fourteen with serious wounds. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, and bikes add to the toll. The dead are not numbers. They are children, elders, workers, and parents. They are you, if you walk or ride here.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Back

      Council Member Vickie Paladino has voted for some safety bills. She backed laws to remove abandoned vehicles and clear sightlines, and to require pavement markings. She co-sponsored bills for speed humps near parks and for faster removal of sidewalk hazards. But she also voted against decriminalizing jaywalking, a move that would have protected pedestrians from police, not cars. She co-sponsored a bill to require e-bike registration, a law that burdens delivery workers and does nothing to slow cars.

      Paladino celebrated new open school streets, calling it a win for safe pick-up, drop-off, and play. But the work is not done. The streets are not safe. The dead do not come back.

      What Comes Next

      Every crash is preventable. Every death is a policy failure. Call Council Member Paladino. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people outside cars. Do not wait for another name on the list. Act now.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 19 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, assembly district AD 26 and state senate district SD 11.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 19?
      It includes the College Point, Whitestone-Beechhurst, Bay Terrace-Clearview, Murray Hill-Broadway Flushing, Fort Totten, Auburndale, Bayside, Douglaston-Little Neck, Alley Pond Park, and Queens CB7 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 26, AD 27, and AD 40, and State Senate Districts SD 11 and SD 16.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 19?
      Cars and trucks (SUVs, sedans, trucks, vans, taxis, buses) caused the most harm to pedestrians in District 19, with five deaths and fourteen serious injuries over the past three years. Motorcycles and mopeds were involved in two injuries, and bikes in five minor injuries.
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Every death and serious injury on the street is a result of policy choices and can be stopped with better design, enforcement, and leadership.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws that lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, remove hazards, and focus enforcement on the drivers and vehicles that do the most harm.

      Int 0262-2024
      Paladino co-sponsors bill to require speed humps near parks, improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill orders speed humps on roads beside parks over one acre. DOT can skip spots if safety or rules demand. Law aims to slow cars where families walk, run, and play.

      Int 0262-2024 sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after introduction on February 28, 2024. The bill reads: “requiring the installation of speed humps on roadways adjacent to any park equal or greater than one acre.” Lincoln Restler leads as primary sponsor, joined by eighteen co-sponsors. The Department of Transportation must install speed humps unless the commissioner finds a risk to safety or a conflict with DOT guidelines. The law would take effect 180 days after passage. The measure targets streets where parks meet traffic, aiming to slow cars and shield people outside vehicles.
