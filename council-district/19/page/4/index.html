      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 19?

      Three Dead, Fifteen Broken: District 19 Bleeds While Leaders Stall

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 29, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and cleaned citations list.)

      District 19: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Toll on Our Streets

      Three dead. Fifteen left with injuries that will not heal. In the last year and a half, this is the price paid by people in District 19 for crossing a street, riding a bike, or just being in the wrong place when a driver made a mistake. The numbers do not tell you about the silence after the sirens fade. They do not show you the blood on the curb, or the way a family waits for news that will break them.

      Just last month, a 74-year-old man was killed crossing at Northern Boulevard and 217th Street. The SUV kept going straight. The man did not. He died at the scene.

      On July 11, eight people were hurt when an MTA bus jumped the curb in Flushing. The driver, just 25, told investigators he “misjudged the curb.” Video showed more. He had fallen asleep at the wheel. The MTA pulled him from service. “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus,” said one rider. The crash missed two people at the stop by inches.

      The Record of Leadership

      Council Member Vickie Paladino has voted for some safety bills. She backed laws to clear abandoned vehicles from streets, to require better pavement markings, and to speed up the removal of utility poles and wires. She co-sponsored a bill to put speed humps near parks. These are steps, but they are not enough. She also voted against a bill that would have made it safer for people to cross the street by legalizing safe crossings outside the crosswalk. The dead do not care about technicalities. They are just dead.

      Paladino has supported new school streets, calling them a win for children. But she has also pushed for more e-bike registration, a move that does little to stop the cars and trucks that do most of the killing.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every death and injury is a choice made by those in power. The city now has the power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not done so. The council can demand more protected crossings, more daylighted corners, more enforcement against reckless drivers—not just cyclists and delivery workers.

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people outside cars. The blood on the street is not an accident. It is a warning.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents local districts like District 19. Source
      ▸ Where does District 19 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, assembly district AD 26 and state senate district SD 11. Source
      ▸ Which areas are in District 19?
      It includes the College Point, Whitestone-Beechhurst, Bay Terrace-Clearview, Murray Hill-Broadway Flushing, Fort Totten, Auburndale, Bayside, Douglaston-Little Neck, Alley Pond Park, and Queens CB7 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 26, AD 27, and AD 40, and State Senate Districts SD 11 and SD 16. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 19?
      Most injuries and deaths were caused by SUVs and Cars (5 deaths, 193 minor injuries, 64 moderate injuries, 14 serious injuries), followed by Trucks and Buses (no deaths, 19 minor injuries, 7 moderate injuries, 2 serious injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 3 minor injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 5 minor injuries). Source
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      These crashes are not random accidents. They are the result of policy choices—about speed limits, street design, and enforcement—that can be changed to save lives. Source
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, install speed humps and daylight corners, and focus enforcement on reckless drivers—not just cyclists or pedestrians. Source

      Int 0262-2024
      Paladino co-sponsors bill to require speed humps near parks, improving street safety.
      ▸

      Council bill orders speed humps on roads beside parks over one acre. DOT can skip spots if safety or rules demand. Law aims to slow cars where families walk, run, and play.

      Int 0262-2024 sits with the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure after introduction on February 28, 2024. The bill reads: “requiring the installation of speed humps on roadways adjacent to any park equal or greater than one acre.” Lincoln Restler leads as primary sponsor, joined by eighteen co-sponsors. The Department of Transportation must install speed humps unless the commissioner finds a risk to safety or a conflict with DOT guidelines. The law would take effect 180 days after passage. The measure targets streets where parks meet traffic, aiming to slow cars and shield people outside vehicles.
