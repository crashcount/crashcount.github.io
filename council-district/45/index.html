      Left Turns, Broken Lives: How Many More Must Die on District 45 Streets?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline, tightened prose, fixed link formatting, and cleaned citations.)

      District 45: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll in Blood and Bone

      A 74-year-old woman, crossing with the light at Avenue D and New York Avenue, was struck and killed by a left-turning sedan. A 79-year-old woman, walking with the signal at Glenwood and Flatbush, was killed by a bus making a left. A 53-year-old cyclist died after being ejected on Cortelyou Road. A 72-year-old man, crossing with the signal at Flatbush, was killed by a turning bus. These are not numbers. They are lives ended in the crosswalk, on the way home, in the middle of the day. In three and a half years, seven people have been killed on District 45 streets. Fifty have been seriously injured. Over 3,000 have been hurt. The pain does not fade. The street remembers every name. NYC Open Data

      Who Pays the Price

      The old. The young. The ones on foot, on bikes, on scooters. Pedestrians and cyclists bear the brunt. Cars and SUVs killed three. Buses killed two. Trucks and motorcycles left others broken. The dead crossed with the signal. The drivers turned left, looked away, or did not yield. The city calls these accidents. But the pattern is clear. The cost is paid by the most vulnerable.

      What Has Been Done—And What Has Not

      Council Member Farah N. Louis has voted for and sponsored bills that chip at the edges. She backed laws for more protected bike lanes, raised speed reducers, better pedestrian lighting, and legalizing jaywalking. She supported truck route redesigns and crash investigations. But the pace is slow. She raised concerns about speeding up bike lane installation, asking for more community input while cyclists keep dying. raised concerns The city has the power to lower speed limits. It has not used it. The council can demand more. It has not.

      What Comes Next

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every delay is another family broken. Call Council Member Louis. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protected bike lanes, not just paint. Demand action, not excuses. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s lawmaking body. It passes local laws, oversees agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 45 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 45?
      It includes the East Flatbush-Farragut and Holy Cross Cemetery neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 41, AD 42, and AD 58, and State Senate District SD 21.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 45?
      Cars and SUVs were involved in 3 deaths and 12 serious injuries. Trucks and buses caused 2 deaths and 4 serious injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused no deaths but 3 moderate injuries. Bikes were involved in 1 moderate injury. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are crashes just accidents, or can they be prevented?
      Crashes are not random. Patterns show that street design, speed, and enforcement decisions can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, demand protected bike lanes, redesign dangerous intersections, and pass laws that put safety over delay.

      Int 1160-2025
      Louis votes yes to require quick pavement markings, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      City Council passed a law forcing DOT to repaint pavement markings within five days after resurfacing. Streets stripped bare by fresh asphalt must get lines back fast. Delays require public notice. The law aims to keep crosswalks and lanes visible, protecting walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It was introduced January 8, 2025, passed March 15, and became Local Law 28 of 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law orders DOT to install pavement or temporary markings within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must post notices explaining the delay and set a new timeline. DOT must also report yearly on compliance. The law responds to a simple fact: missing markings mean danger for people on foot and bike. With this law, the city moves to close that deadly gap.

      Louis sponsors PAWS Act resolution, increasing penalties for drivers hitting pets.
      ▸

      Council calls for Albany to pass the PAWS Act. The bill adds pets to protected victims in traffic law. It hikes fines for drivers who hit animals and flee. Lawmakers push for real consequences when cars strike dogs, cats, and other companions.

      Resolution 0723-2025 sits in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced January 23, 2025, it urges the state to pass A10660/S9915, the Protecting Animals Walking on the Street (PAWS) Act. The resolution states: 'add the term companion animals to the list of possible victim classes should a driver fail to exercise due care to avoid striking pedestrians and to increase the fine for striking a pet with a vehicle and leaving the scene without reporting the incident.' Council Members Farah N. Louis (primary sponsor), Lynn C. Schulman, Carlina Rivera, and Justin L. Brannan back the measure. The move targets a gap in current law, where penalties for striking animals are minor—on par with an illegal U-turn. The Council’s action signals a push for stronger accountability when vehicles harm the city’s most vulnerable, including its animals.

No safety analyst assessed the direct impact on vulnerable road users. The law’s effect: more agencies weigh in, but the process for safer streets grows longer and more complex.
