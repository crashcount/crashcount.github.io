      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 22?

      Astoria’s Streets Are Killing Us—Who Will Stop the Bleeding?

      Written August 9, 2025 (Revised August 11, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 22: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 9, 2025

      The Death Count Grows

      In District 22, the numbers do not lie. Thirteen people killed. Over 2,100 injured. Sixteen left with wounds so deep they are called serious, not just by doctors but by the city itself. These are not numbers. They are lives ended or broken on the streets of Astoria, Ditmars, and Queensbridge.

      Just last month, a cyclist was killed at 37th Street and 34th Avenue. A bus, a truck, and a bike. The cyclist did not walk away. The city recorded her as another fatality, another body lost to the road. NYC Open Data.

      The Usual Suspects

      Cars and SUVs did the most harm. They killed three. They left 219 with minor injuries, 91 with moderate, and four with wounds that will not heal. Trucks and buses killed one and hurt dozens more. Motorcycles and mopeds, ten more injuries. Bikes, too, are in the count, but the toll is lower. The street is not safe for anyone who is not behind a windshield.

      Leadership: Words, Votes, and the Slow Grind

      Council Member Tiffany Cabán has spoken after tragedy. When a teenage cyclist was killed on 21st Street, she called it “wave after wave of grief and heartbreak” and demanded safer streets for those on foot and bike. Streetsblog NYC. She has called for “bigger and bolder” traffic-calming measures, saying, “if it saves lives, it’s worth doing.” Streetsblog NYC.

      Cabán has co-sponsored bills to ban parking near crosswalks, to daylight intersections, and to add high-visibility markings where blood has already been spilled. She voted to remove abandoned vehicles and to require taxi warning decals. But the pace is slow. The deaths do not wait for committee meetings.

      The Next Step Is Yours

      Every day, the street takes another. Call Council Member Cabán. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand protected bike lanes and daylighted corners. Demand action, not delay. The dead cannot speak. You must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents local districts like District 22.
      ▸ Where does District 22 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, assembly district AD 34 and state senate district SD 11.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 22?
      It includes the Astoria (North)-Ditmars-Steinway, Old Astoria-Hallets Point, Astoria (Central), Astoria (East)-Woodside (North), Queensbridge-Ravenswood-Dutch Kills, Rikers Island, St. Michael’S Cemetery, Astoria Park, and Queens CB1 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 34, AD 35, AD 36, and AD 85, and State Senate Districts SD 11, SD 12, SD 13, and SD 59.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 22?
      Cars and SUVs were responsible for the most harm, with 3 deaths and 317 injuries. Motorcycles and mopeds caused 26 injuries, while bikes caused 10 injuries but no deaths. Trucks and buses killed one and caused 32 injuries.
      ▸ Are these crashes just accidents, or are they preventable?
      Most crashes are preventable. Street design, speed limits, and enforcement can save lives if leaders act.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, protected bike lanes, daylighted intersections, and rapid removal of abandoned vehicles. They can also push for faster, bolder safety changes instead of waiting for tragedy.

      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.

      Int 0857-2024
      Cabán votes yes to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council orders swift removal of abandoned, derelict cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. No plates, no stickers, no excuses. Police and sanitation must act. Safer crossings for all who walk, ride, or wait.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council on June 30, 2025. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to vehicles reported as abandoned to the department of sanitation," requires the Department of Sanitation to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours of report. The NYPD must tow cars lacking valid plates or stickers. Prime sponsor Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led, joined by Crystal Hudson, Amanda Farías, Lincoln Restler, and others. The law targets street hazards, clearing blocked sightlines and crosswalks. It aims to cut risks for pedestrians and cyclists by removing abandoned vehicles fast.

      Int 0193-2024
      Cabán votes yes on taxi dooring warning decals, no major safety change.
      ▸

      Council passes law. Taxis and for-hire cars must post bold warnings on rear doors. Riders face the message: look for cyclists before swinging the door. A move to cut dooring. City acts. Cyclists stay in the crosshairs.

      Int 0193-2024, now law, cleared the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and passed City Council on May 1, 2025. The bill, titled 'A Local Law...requiring taxis and for-hire vehicles to display a decal warning passengers to look for cyclists when opening the door,' demands clear warning signs on all rear passenger doors. Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Gutiérrez, Hudson, Rivera, Won, and others. The Taxi and Limousine Commission must provide decals at no cost. The law aims to fight dooring, a threat to cyclists citywide. Enacted May 31, 2025, it takes effect in 120 days.

The hearing referenced the city’s long record of racially biased enforcement, including jaywalking laws once used to target Black and Latinx New Yorkers. Experts, including the NYCLU, say the Adams administration’s surge in traffic stops continues a pattern of racist policing.
