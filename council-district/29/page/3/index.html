      About these crash totals

      Counts come from NYC police crash reports (NYC Open Data). We sum all crashes, injuries, and deaths for this area across the selected time window shown on the card. Injury severity follows the official definitions in the NYPD dataset.

      • Crashes: number of police‑reported collisions (all road users).
      • All injuries: total injured people in those crashes.
      • Moderate / Serious: subcategories reported by officers (e.g., broken bones vs. life‑threatening trauma).
      • Deaths: people who died due to a crash.

      Notes: Police reports can be corrected after initial publication. Minor incidents without a police report are not included.

      Late night on 101 Street, a life ends. The pattern does not.

      District 29: Jan 1, 2022 - Sep 18, 2025

      Late at night on Jul 31, 2025, on 101 Street near 103rd Avenue, a driver in a sedan killed a 23-year-old man who was outside an intersection. Police recorded unsafe speed by the driver in that crash. Source.

      He was one of 11 people killed on District 29 streets since 2022. Another 2,942 were hurt. Source.

      The clock and the cost

      Deaths pile up at night. The district’s data shows the worst hours include 2 AM and 11 PM. Source.

      Corners that don’t forgive

      Wide corridors carry the toll. On 101 Avenue, crashes injured 62 people and killed one. On Atlantic Avenue, 90 injured and one dead. Source.

      At 120 Street and 101 Avenue, a pickup driver making a left turn hit a 47-year-old man who was crossing with the signal. He died. Police recorded driver inattention. Crash record.

      At 109 Street and 103 Avenue, a driver in an SUV turned and injured a girl who was crossing with the signal. Police recorded failure to yield and an improper turn. Crash record.

      Who gets hurt, and by whom

      In this district, drivers of sedans injured 215 people walking and drivers of SUVs injured 183. Trucks and buses added more. Source.

      What leaders did — and didn’t

      Council Member Lynn C. Schulman voted to speed up pavement markings after repaving (Int 1160-2025). She also voted for taxi door decals warning about dooring (Int 0193-2024) and to remove abandoned cars faster (Int 0857-2024). Legislative record.

      Citywide, families and advocates say licenses are a privilege. As State Sen. Jessica Ramos put it after a Queens child was killed, “driving is a privilege that can, and should be, taken away.” Streetsblog.

      District 29’s Assembly Member is David Weprin. Its State Senator is Leroy Comrie. The Legislature has a bill to fit the danger: require speed limiters for repeat camera violators and high-point drivers. Will they back it? Details and contacts.

      Slow the cars. Stop the repeats.

      The fixes are not mysteries:

      • Daylight corners and harden left turns on 101 Avenue and Atlantic Avenue.
      • Give leading pedestrian intervals and protect crossings where turn crashes recur.
      • Target late-night speeding where deaths cluster.

      Citywide tools exist. Lower the default speed limit and pass the Stop Super Speeders Act to force the worst drivers to slow down. The data here shows why. Sources · How to help

We aggregated by injury severity, hour, location (street/corridor), person type, and vehicle type. Data was extracted on Sep 17, 2025.       Int 1160-2025 Schulman votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Feb 13 - Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.
