      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 31?

      District 31: Broken Bodies, Broken Promises—How Many More Must Die?

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised July 10, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 31: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Shattered

      In District 31, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-four people killed. Over 3,100 injured. Twenty-nine suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again. In the last year alone, eight people died and more than a thousand were hurt. The dead include the young and the old. A 27-year-old woman died on the Belt Parkway. Police said, “Since both occupants were out of the car at the time of the crash, investigators haven’t figured out who was driving” according to NY Daily News. A woman in her seventies never made it home from a trip with her senior center. “The minivan veered off the road and struck a tree, according to police” reported NY Daily News.

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. Trucks and vans take their share. The numbers are steady. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises, Laws, and Missed Chances

      Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers has acted. She voted to end jaywalking tickets, making it legal to cross where you need to see the vote. She called for more red light cameras, but warned, “We need to ensure cameras improve communities and street safety” she told Streetsblog NYC. She pushed for daylighting at intersections, better lighting, and more speed humps. She pressed the city to publish its progress on street redesigns and called out the Department of Transportation for failing to deliver. “DOT gives us their word every hearing and we are not getting results” she said to Streetsblog NYC.

      But the streets are still deadly. Cameras and laws help, but they are not enough. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not. The council can demand more protected bike lanes and crossings. Progress is slow. The bodies pile up.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Every death is preventable. Every injury is a failure. Call Council Member Brooks-Powers. Demand she fight for a 20 mph speed limit, more daylighted intersections, and protected space for people on foot and bike. Join Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Speak at your community board. Do not let another name become a number. Act now.

      Half Of NYC Taxis Now Wheelchair Accessible
      ▸

      Half the city’s taxis now take wheelchairs. Ten years of pressure forced change. The fleet stands as the most accessible in the nation. Policy, not luck, drove this. Disabled riders gain ground. Streets shift. Access grows.

      amNY reported on June 5, 2025, that New York City’s Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) reached a milestone: 50% of active taxis are now wheelchair accessible. TLC Commissioner David Do said, “We made that today. It is the most accessible fleet in the entire country.” This achievement follows a decade-long push by disability advocates and a 2013 settlement requiring half the medallion fleet to be accessible. The transition faced delays, including the COVID-19 pandemic and medallion owner financial struggles. Policy changes, such as alternating accessible vehicles and funding support, drove the shift. The article highlights systemic progress, not individual driver actions, and underscores the impact of sustained advocacy and regulatory pressure.

      Richards Criticizes Conduit Boulevard Safety and Design Failures
      ▸

      Five dead. Forty badly hurt. The Conduit slices through Queens and Brooklyn, fast and wide. DOT will study a fix. Borough presidents called for urgent change. The city will listen to neighbors. The old highway’s days are numbered. Lives hang in the balance.

      ""The current state of the Conduit falls significantly short of meeting the needs of drivers, cyclists and pedestrians alike. It's confusing, it's poorly designed ... and we know the lack of sufficient pedestrian and bike infrastructure makes it even more dangerous for the neighbors of Queens and Brooklyn."" -- Donovan J. Richards

      On May 13, 2025, the Department of Transportation (DOT) announced a city-funded public engagement process for a major safety redesign of Conduit Boulevard, a three-mile corridor linking Atlantic Avenue to the Belt Parkway and JFK Airport. The project follows urgent requests in 2023 from Queens Borough President Donovan Richards and Brooklyn Borough President Antonio Reynoso. DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez said, "Brooklyn and Queens deserve a Conduit Boulevard that improves daily life, not a roadway that puts pedestrians and drivers at risk and physically divides entire neighborhoods." The corridor has seen five deaths and 40 severe injuries in five years. The median’s dirt paths show heavy pedestrian use, but only 15 crosswalks span the stretch. The public process begins in June and may run into 2026. The redesign aims to end decades of danger for vulnerable road users.

      Int 0193-2024
      Brooks-Powers votes yes on taxi dooring warning decals, with neutral safety impact.
      ▸

      Council passed a bill forcing taxis and for-hire cars to post warnings on rear doors. The decals tell riders to watch for cyclists before swinging open. The law aims to cut dooring. Signs come free from the city. Cyclists face less risk.

      Int 0193-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, moved through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and passed the Council on May 1, 2025. The bill reads: 'requiring taxis and for-hire vehicles to display a decal warning passengers to look for cyclists when opening the door.' Lincoln Restler led as primary sponsor, joined by Jennifer Gutiérrez and a long list of co-sponsors. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law orders all taxis and for-hire vehicles to post warning decals on rear passenger doors, alerting riders to check for cyclists before opening. The Taxi and Limousine Commission will provide the signs at no cost. The measure targets dooring, a common threat to cyclists. The bill takes effect 120 days after becoming law.
