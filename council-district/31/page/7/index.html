      Delay Is Deadly: How Many More Lives Will District 31 Lose?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 5, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      District 31: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      The numbers do not flinch. In the last 12 months, District 31 saw 8 people killed and 1,013 injured in 1,523 crashes. Ten of those injuries were serious. The dead include a 27-year-old woman on the Belt Parkway, a 21-year-old man on Laurelton Parkway, and a woman in her seventies in a minivan on Brookville Boulevard. Each crash leaves a hole. A seatbelt left dangling. A name erased from the mailbox.

      A police officer stood by the wreck on Brookville Boulevard. “A woman was killed and three other people were hospitalized when a trip from a Queens senior residential home turned deadly early Friday,” police said. The minivan veered off the road and hit a tree. The woman in the back seat did not make it out alive.

      On the Belt Parkway, a white Toyota hit an underpass. Two people crawled from the wreck. “Since both occupants were out of the car at the time of the crash, investigators haven’t figured out who was driving, police said” after the crash. The woman died at the hospital. The man survived.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers has backed bills to daylight intersections, expand red light cameras, and lower speed limits on Open Streets. She voted to legalize crossing mid-block, ending jaywalking enforcement that often targeted the vulnerable. She has pressed the city to track its progress on street redesigns, saying, “DOT gives us their word every hearing and we are not getting results” she told the DOT.

      But the pace is slow. Promises pile up. Streets remain dangerous. Bills to ban parking near crosswalks and add speed humps wait in committee. The city’s own numbers show the cost of delay.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Speed kills. Delay kills. Residents must demand more. Call Brooks-Powers. Call the Mayor. Demand the city use its new power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. Demand every intersection be made safe to cross. Demand cameras stay on. Every day of delay is another family changed forever.

      Take action now.

      Int 0854-2022
      Brooks-Powers votes yes on daylighting bill, boosting intersection safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to daylight at least 100 intersections a year. No parking near corners. Physical barriers like planters or bike corrals will block cars. High-crash spots get priority. The city must report progress. Streets change. Lives depend on it.

      Int 0854-2022, now enacted as Local Law 66 of 2023, passed the City Council on April 27, 2023, after review by the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The law requires the Department of Transportation to 'implement daylighting at a minimum of 100 intersections a year,' meaning no parking within 15 feet of corners. High-crash intersections must be prioritized unless deemed infeasible. DOT must also install physical daylighting features, such as planters or bike corrals, where possible. Council Member Farah N. Louis sponsored the bill, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hudson, Ossé, and others. The law took effect 90 days after enactment. DOT must report annually on locations and reasons for any exceptions. This measure targets the blind spots that kill and injure pedestrians and cyclists at intersections. The city’s streets will see more open corners and fewer hiding places for danger.

The matter summary states: 'The city will launch a pilot program testing neighborhood delivery microhubs across the five boroughs, aimed at reducing the surge of truck traffic.' Councilmember Selvena N. Brooks-Powers, representing District 31, emphasized the need for sustainable infrastructure to curb environmental justice impacts from truck congestion. The program will allow large trucks to unload at designated hubs, shifting the final delivery leg to vans, cargo bikes, or carts. Details on locations and management are pending. After a year, DOT will review data and consider expansion, with a final report due in 2026. Environmentalists and advocates support the move but note it does not address warehouse siting in vulnerable communities.
