      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 31?

      District 31: Broken Bodies, Broken Promises—How Many More Must Die?

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 20, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 31: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 20, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Families Shattered

      In District 31, the numbers do not lie. Twenty-four people killed. Over 3,100 injured. Twenty-nine suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again. In the last year alone, eight people died and more than a thousand were hurt. The dead include the young and the old. A 27-year-old woman died on the Belt Parkway. Police said, “Since both occupants were out of the car at the time of the crash, investigators haven’t figured out who was driving” according to NY Daily News. A woman in her seventies never made it home from a trip with her senior center. “The minivan veered off the road and struck a tree, according to police” reported NY Daily News.

      SUVs and sedans do most of the killing. Trucks and vans take their share. The numbers are steady. The pain is not.

      Leadership: Promises, Laws, and Missed Chances

      Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers has acted. She voted to end jaywalking tickets, making it legal to cross where you need to see the vote. She called for more red light cameras, but warned, “We need to ensure cameras improve communities and street safety” she told Streetsblog NYC. She pushed for daylighting at intersections, better lighting, and more speed humps. She pressed the city to publish its progress on street redesigns and called out the Department of Transportation for failing to deliver. “DOT gives us their word every hearing and we are not getting results” she said to Streetsblog NYC.

      But the streets are still deadly. Cameras and laws help, but they are not enough. The city can lower speed limits to 20 mph. It has not. The council can demand more protected bike lanes and crossings. Progress is slow. The bodies pile up.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      Every death is preventable. Every injury is a failure. Call Council Member Brooks-Powers. Demand she fight for a 20 mph speed limit, more daylighted intersections, and protected space for people on foot and bike. Join Transportation Alternatives and Families for Safe Streets. Speak at your community board. Do not let another name become a number. Act now.

      Brooks-Powers Criticizes NYPD for Reduced Traffic Enforcement
      ▸

      NYPD claimed bike lane enforcement soared. Data showed the opposite. Tickets for blocking bike lanes fell. Truck violations dropped. Council pressed for answers. NYPD dodged specifics. Vulnerable road users left exposed. Accountability missing. The numbers do not lie.

      On May 11, 2022, the City Council's Transportation Committee, chaired by Selvena Brooks-Powers, held an oversight hearing on NYPD traffic enforcement. NYPD Transportation Bureau Chief Kim Royster testified, stating, "So far this year, bike lane enforcement is up 148 percent." Streetsblog fact-checked this claim and found enforcement was actually down 6.4 percent from the previous year. Brooks-Powers questioned enforcement against illegally parked tractor-trailers. Royster cited fine amounts but gave no enforcement numbers. Data showed code 78 and code 6 violations both dropped sharply. Royster's answers were vague, lacking specifics on enforcement or safety. The hearing exposed a gap between NYPD claims and reality, leaving vulnerable road users at risk.
