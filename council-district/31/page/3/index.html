      Delay Is Deadly: How Many More Lives Will District 31 Lose?

      Written June 4, 2025 (Revised June 6, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      District 31: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 4, 2025

      The Toll: Lives Lost, Bodies Broken

      The numbers do not flinch. In the last 12 months, District 31 saw 8 people killed and 1,013 injured in 1,523 crashes. Ten of those injuries were serious. The dead include a 27-year-old woman on the Belt Parkway, a 21-year-old man on Laurelton Parkway, and a woman in her seventies in a minivan on Brookville Boulevard. Each crash leaves a hole. A seatbelt left dangling. A name erased from the mailbox.

      A police officer stood by the wreck on Brookville Boulevard. “A woman was killed and three other people were hospitalized when a trip from a Queens senior residential home turned deadly early Friday,” police said. The minivan veered off the road and hit a tree. The woman in the back seat did not make it out alive.

      On the Belt Parkway, a white Toyota hit an underpass. Two people crawled from the wreck. “Since both occupants were out of the car at the time of the crash, investigators haven’t figured out who was driving, police said” after the crash. The woman died at the hospital. The man survived.

      Leadership: Action and Delay

      Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers has backed bills to daylight intersections, expand red light cameras, and lower speed limits on Open Streets. She voted to legalize crossing mid-block, ending jaywalking enforcement that often targeted the vulnerable. She has pressed the city to track its progress on street redesigns, saying, “DOT gives us their word every hearing and we are not getting results” she told the DOT.

      But the pace is slow. Promises pile up. Streets remain dangerous. Bills to ban parking near crosswalks and add speed humps wait in committee. The city’s own numbers show the cost of delay.

      What Next: No More Waiting

      Speed kills. Delay kills. Residents must demand more. Call Brooks-Powers. Call the Mayor. Demand the city use its new power to lower speed limits to 20 mph. Demand every intersection be made safe to cross. Demand cameras stay on. Every day of delay is another family changed forever.

      Take action now.

      Brooks-Powers Calls for Safety-Boosting Red Light Camera Expansion
      ▸

      City Council pushed Albany to expand red light cameras from 150 to 600 intersections. The move falls short of the original goal but marks the largest growth since 1994. Advocates say cameras save lives. Debate continues over equity and coverage.

      On June 6, 2024, the City Council passed a resolution urging the state to expand New York City's red light camera program from 150 to 600 intersections. The measure, led by Council Member Lincoln Restler, follows internal debate and negotiation, with some members pushing for 1,325 intersections and others preferring fewer. The final number represents a four-fold increase, covering about 4 percent of city intersections. The Council's resolution, described as a 'home rule' message, aims to prevent the program from sunsetting and to improve safety for all road users. Restler stated, 'This will expand it to about 4 percent of intersections, and it will make our city safer.' Assembly Member Jeff Dinowitz called the expansion a victory, while advocates like Sara Lind of Open Plans noted no deaths at camera-protected intersections since 1994. Transportation Committee Chair Selvena Brooks-Powers highlighted the need to ensure cameras improve communities and street safety, raising concerns about fairness in enforcement. The Department of Transportation called the Council's action a critical step.

      Brooks-Powers Supports Safety-Boosting Speed Limit Reduction
      ▸

      Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can now set lower speed limits. Lawmakers carved out wide roads, leaving many deadly corridors untouched. Advocates fought for years. Families of crash victims cheered. The fight for safer streets is not over.

      Sammy’s Law, passed on April 22, 2024, as part of New York’s $273-billion state budget, grants New York City the authority to set its own speed limits on most roads. The bill, long blocked by Assembly Speaker Carl Heastie, finally moved forward under Governor Kathy Hochul’s push. The measure, described as 'a significant legislative achievement,' excludes roads outside Manhattan with three or more travel lanes in one direction—a concession to car-centric lawmakers. The law requires community board notification and comment, but their input is only advisory. Families of road violence victims and street safety advocates celebrated the win. Council Member Selvena Brooks-Powers, Transportation Chair, conditioned her support on street redesigns in underserved neighborhoods. The law’s carve-out leaves many of the city’s deadliest roads unchanged, withholding proven safety benefits from major thoroughfares.
