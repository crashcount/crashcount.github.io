      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 31?

      District 31: Deadly Night on Conduit Demands Safer Streets

      Written August 15, 2025 (Revised August 15, 2025 – Finisher pass)

      District 31: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 15, 2025

      Just This Week

      On 2025-08-13 a driver struck a 52‑year‑old man at 155th Street and South Conduit Avenue near JFK and fled. “The operator of the vehicle fled the scene after hitting the man,” police told ABC7. The victim was taken to Jamaica Hospital, where he died. No arrests have been announced.

      This year in Council District 31 there have been 4 deaths and about 800 injuries so far, up from 1 death and 572 injuries at this point last year (NYC Open Data). Since 2022 the district’s recorded toll is 24 deaths, 3,537 injuries and 31 serious injuries (NYC Open Data).

      Where it happens

      Crashes concentrate on high‑speed corridors. Belt Parkway and South Conduit Avenue are prominent hotspots; Brookville Boulevard also shows repeated severe collisions. Fatalities rise overnight, with a clear spike between about 1 a.m. and 3 a.m., indicating the need for better night lighting and enforcement (NYC Open Data).

      Local data point to a few recurring causes: the largest contributing buckets are recorded as “other” and traffic‑related errors, and unsafe speed appears frequently in crash reports. Cars and SUVs account for most pedestrian deaths and injuries in the district.

      Concrete, local fixes are straightforward and proven: targeted nighttime speed enforcement and improved lighting along the Conduit; hardened crossings and refuge islands at high‑risk spots; daylighting and leading pedestrian intervals (LPIs) at slip lanes and intersections; and rapid installation of protected crossings where demand is clear.

      Leadership and politics

      Council Transportation Chair Selvena Brooks‑Powers has publicly pressed DOT for results: “DOT gives us their word every hearing and we are not getting results,” she told Streetsblog on 2025-01-22. Brooks‑Powers also co‑sponsored Int 1347‑2025, a bill addressing unlicensed commuter vans now in committee (Legistar), and has supported greenway planning to move people off dangerous roads (amNY).

      Queens Borough President Donovan Richards has called Conduit Boulevard “confusing” and poorly designed, urging a redesign to serve pedestrians and cyclists (Streetsblog, 2025-05-13).

      What must change now (local + citywide)

      • Lower NYC’s default speed limit to 20 mph using Sammy’s Law. Slower limits reduce crash severity and buy time for people to avoid collisions.
      • Require intelligent speed‑assistance devices for habitual speeders and repeat camera violators to stop the same drivers from killing again.
      • Fix the Conduit and nearby hotspots now: add LPIs, daylighting, hardened turns, refuge islands, better lighting, and targeted night enforcement rather than more press releases.

      Citywide laws and equipment rules can make local fixes stick: a citywide default 20 mph limit and mandated speed limiters for repeat offenders would reduce the most lethal crashes across neighborhoods like District 31.

      Act

      Call Council Member Selvena N. Brooks‑Powers and the Mayor. Demand lower default speeds, mandatory speed limiters for repeat offenders, and rapid infrastructure fixes on South Conduit Avenue and Belt Parkway. Start here: /take_action/.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Which areas are in District 31?
      It includes the Laurelton, Springfield Gardens (South)-Brookville, Rosedale, Montefiore Cemetery, Far Rockaway-Bayswater, Rockaway Beach-Arverne-Edgemere, Rockaway Community Park, John F. Kennedy International Airport, and Jamaica Bay (East) areas, plus Queens CB13. It overlaps parts of AD 23, AD 29, SD 10, and SD 14.
      ▸ Where does District 31 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 31?
      Cars and SUVs cause most pedestrian harm: vehicle rollups show 7 pedestrian deaths and about 402 injuries tied to SUVs/cars. Trucks and buses account for additional deaths and injuries. Motorcycles, mopeds, and bikes cause far fewer pedestrian deaths in the district (NYC Open Data).
      ▸ Are these crashes just “accidents”?
      No. District 31’s harm clusters by place and time — Belt Parkway and South Conduit Avenue, and late‑night hours around 1–3 a.m. — patterns that point to fixable design and speed problems (NYC Open Data). These patterns can be changed with engineering and enforcement.
      ▸ What can local politicians do right now?
      Lower the default city speed limit to 20 mph using Sammy’s Law, require speed limiters for repeat camera violators and habitual speeders, and harden crossings on the Conduit and Belt Parkway with LPIs, refuge islands, and daylighting. Start here: /take_action/.
      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The City Council is New York’s lawmaking body. Members introduce bills that go to committees—like Transportation and Infrastructure—then to a full Council vote. Int 1347‑2025 on unlicensed commuter vans is a current example (Legistar).
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      CrashCount tracks traffic violence and holds officials accountable. We publish data and reporting on local crashes, safety patterns, and policy responses so residents can demand concrete fixes.

      Brooks-Powers Supports Safety-Boosting Slow Zones and Speed Limits
      ▸

      DOT will drop speed limits to 20 mph on 49 streets this September. Only a sliver of city roads get safer. Regional slow zones start in lower Manhattan. Council Member Marte backs the move. Advocates want faster, broader action. Most streets stay risky.

      On June 27, 2024, the NYC Department of Transportation announced it will use new powers from Sammy's Law to lower speed limits from 25 to 20 mph on 49 street segments starting September 2024, with 201 more to follow by late 2025. The measure covers just 0.13% of city streets, possibly reaching 0.8% if regional slow zones expand. The first such zone will be south of Canal Street in lower Manhattan. DOT can only lower limits street by street unless the City Council acts. Council Transportation Chair Selvena Brooks-Powers confirmed no citywide bill exists. Council Member Christopher Marte welcomed the slow zone in his district, citing rampant speeding off the bridges. Advocates and officials demand faster, broader action, saying, 'Twenty is plenty wherever you'll find people, and in New York that's everywhere.' DOT says it will prioritize areas near schools and neighborhoods with more non-white and low-income residents. The rollout is slow. Most streets remain unchanged.

      Brooks-Powers Supports Safety-Boosting Speed Limit Reduction
      ▸

      Albany passed Sammy’s Law. The city can now set lower speed limits. Lawmakers carved out wide roads, leaving many deadly corridors untouched. Advocates fought for years. Families of crash victims cheered. The fight for safer streets is not over.

The law requires community board notification and comment, but their input is only advisory. Families of road violence victims and street safety advocates celebrated the win. Council Member Selvena Brooks-Powers, Transportation Chair, conditioned her support on street redesigns in underserved neighborhoods. The law’s carve-out leaves many of the city’s deadliest roads unchanged, withholding proven safety benefits from major thoroughfares.

      Int 0745-2024
      Brooks-Powers sponsors bill to require DOT to report micromobility activity data.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to reveal bike and micromobility numbers. Streets and bridges get counted. Riders’ paths mapped. City must show where safety fails and where it works. Data goes public. No more hiding the truth.

      Int 0745-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. It was introduced April 11, 2024, and became law September 14, 2024, as Local Law 88. The law’s title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...requiring the department of transportation to provide information about bicycle and other micromobility device activity.' Council Member Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led as primary sponsor, joined by Farías, Schulman, Hudson, Restler, and others. The law forces DOT to publish monthly and annual data on bike and micromobility use, plus crash and safety project details. The city must show where riders go, where danger lurks, and what it does to fix it. The mayor returned the bill unsigned, but it stands.
