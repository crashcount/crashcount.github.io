      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 38?

      No More Excuses: Blood on Brooklyn Asphalt, Blood on the Council’s Hands

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 38: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      The Toll: Death on Familiar Streets

      In District 38, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people killed. Over 3,100 injured. Twenty-eight left with wounds so deep they are called serious injuries. These are not just numbers. They are neighbors, elders, children. They are the man crushed crossing 4th Avenue, the woman struck down by a turning sedan, the cyclist thrown from his bike. The street does not care who you are. It only takes.

      Just days ago, two men were killed at 52nd Street and Third Avenue. A BMW ran the red, hit them, and sped away. Police found the driver later. The bodies stayed behind. “EMS pronounced both men dead at the scene. Their identities are being withheld pending family notification,” reported amNY. The corner where they died has seen 59 crashes and 80 injuries in five years—a slow-motion slaughter.

      Who Bears the Brunt?

      Pedestrians and cyclists pay the price. Cars and trucks did most of the killing: 5 deaths, 248 minor injuries, 121 moderate, 7 serious. Motorcycles and mopeds took more. Bikes left dozens hurt. The old, the young, the ones on foot—they are the ones who die first. The street is not fair.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Stalled

      Council Member Alexa Avilés has backed bills to clear abandoned cars, fix curbs, and daylight crosswalks—measures that help, but do not end the bloodshed. She voted yes to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours, aiming to clear blocked sightlines and crosswalks (see bill summary). She co-sponsored a law to ban parking near crosswalks, demanding the city install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections a year. But the bill sits in committee, waiting. The dead do not wait.

      The Voices Left Behind

      After the hit-and-run, the charges stacked up: manslaughter, negligent homicide, reckless driving. “Florentino was charged with two counts each of manslaughter, criminally negligent homicide, leaving the scene of an accident without reporting, and reckless endangerment,” wrote amNY. The law comes after the fact. The families are left with silence.

      What Now? Demand More

      This is not fate. This is policy. Every day the Council delays, another family is broken. Call Council Member Avilés. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand daylight at every crosswalk. Demand action, not excuses. The dead cannot speak. You must.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 38 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 49 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 38?
      It includes the Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Sunset Park (West), Sunset Park (Central), Green-Wood Cemetery, Dyker Heights, Bensonhurst, Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Brooklyn CB7, and Brooklyn CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 49 and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17 and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 38?
      Cars and Trucks: 5 deaths, 248 minor injuries, 121 moderate injuries, 7 serious injuries. Motorcycles and Mopeds: 1 death, 14 minor injuries, 4 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries. Bikes: 0 deaths, 27 minor injuries, 11 moderate injuries, 0 serious injuries.
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are not random. Most deaths and injuries in District 38 happen in the same places, year after year. Better street design, lower speeds, and enforcement can prevent them.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to prevent traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, daylighting crosswalks, and safer street design. They can push for action, not delay, and make sure every street protects the most vulnerable.

      Int 0857-2024
      Avilés votes yes to remove abandoned vehicles, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Council orders swift removal of abandoned, derelict cars. Streets clear in 72 hours. No plates, no stickers, no excuses. Police and sanitation must act. Safer crossings for all who walk, ride, or wait.

      Bill Int 0857-2024, now at the Mayor's desk, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council on June 30, 2025. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to vehicles reported as abandoned to the department of sanitation," requires the Department of Sanitation to remove derelict vehicles within 72 hours of report. The NYPD must tow cars lacking valid plates or stickers. Prime sponsor Selvena N. Brooks-Powers led, joined by Crystal Hudson, Amanda Farías, Lincoln Restler, and others. The law targets street hazards, clearing blocked sightlines and crosswalks. It aims to cut risks for pedestrians and cyclists by removing abandoned vehicles fast.

      Judge Blocks Bedford Avenue Bike Lane Removal
      ▸

      A Brooklyn judge stopped the city from tearing out a protected bike lane. Cyclists and children face danger when lanes vanish. The city acted fast, without notice. The fight for safe passage on Bedford Avenue continues in court.

      Streetsblog NYC reported on June 18, 2025, that Judge Carolyn Walker-Diallo halted New York City's plan to remove three blocks of the Bedford Avenue protected bike lane. The court found the city may have acted "arbitrarily, capriciously, and illegally" by ordering the removal without proper legal notice. City law requires the Department of Transportation to notify local officials and allow time for public discussion, but, as the court papers state, "DOT did not provide the required notice." Plaintiffs argued that removing the lane would "irreparably" harm cyclists, especially children. The city claimed it would only shift the lane, not remove it, but advocates countered that any removal increases danger. The case highlights the need for transparent processes and the risks when safety infrastructure is hastily altered.
