      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 38?

      No More Names: End the Killing on District 38 Streets

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 38: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: The Recent Toll

      In District 38, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people are dead. Twenty-eight more are left with serious injuries. Over 3,100 have been hurt since 2022. Every number is a name, a family, a life cut or broken. The dead are not just numbers. They are elders, children, neighbors. A 70-year-old woman, crossing with the signal, killed by a turning sedan at 7th Avenue and 44th Street (NYC Open Data). An 82-year-old man, struck by a motorcycle on 60th Street. A 66-year-old woman, ejected from her moped, died on 39th Street. The list goes on. The street does not forgive.

      The Machines That Kill

      Cars and trucks are the main threat. They account for most deaths and injuries to people on foot. SUVs and sedans alone have killed five pedestrians and injured hundreds more. Trucks and buses have claimed another life. Motorcycles and mopeds are not far behind, with one death and dozens of injuries. Bikes have hurt, but not killed. The danger is not spread evenly. It is heavy, fast, and often unseen until too late.

      Leadership: Steps Forward, Steps Not Taken

      Council Member Alexa Avilés has moved on some fronts. She voted to clear abandoned vehicles from the streets, a step to open sightlines and protect those on foot and bike. She backed bills for curb repair, speed humps near parks, and more transparency on police vehicle force. She co-sponsored the SAFE Streets Act. She voted to decriminalize jaywalking, a move to stop blaming the dead for their own deaths. But the work is not done. Every day of delay is another day of risk.

      The Human Cost

      A neighbor said it plain after a recent crash: “It’s devastating. It’s affecting everyone in our family, especially (Ruiz’s) mom. Maddy was her only daughter.” The pain does not fade. The tire marks linger in the parking lot. The empty chair at the table stays empty.

      What Comes Next: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. This is policy. The city can lower speed limits. It can redesign streets. It can end the reign of reckless drivers. Call Council Member Avilés. Demand action. Demand 20 mph limits, protected crossings, and enforcement that targets the real danger—drivers, not the people they kill. The dead cannot speak. The living must.

      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      These deaths and injuries are preventable. Safer street design, lower speed limits, and better enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass laws to lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, and target reckless drivers—not people walking or biking.

      Int 1105-2024
      Avilés votes yes, boosting street safety transparency and project accountability.
      ▸

      Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.' Council Member Julie Won led sponsorship, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law demands DOT post annual and monthly updates on every project tied to the master plan—listing details, timelines, funding, and setbacks. This law brings sunlight to street safety work, making the city answer for every mile and missed deadline.

      Int 1160-2025
      Avilés votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.
