      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 38?

      District 38: Crosswalk deaths, truck streets, and a clock that won’t stop

      Written August 25, 2025 (Revised August 30, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 38: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 25, 2025

      Fourth Avenue runs hot. Five people died along it since 2022. The crash logs show 259 injured there too. The BQE and Gowanus feeder roads add more blood. Two deaths on the Brooklyn‑Queens Expressway. Two more along the Gowanus Expressway. The count keeps climbing in small lines of type.

      Since 2022: 24 people killed, 3,373 injured in District 38. Twelve were pedestrians. Four rode bikes. Heavy vehicles hit hard: trucks and buses figure in dozens of pedestrian injuries and two deaths. The worst hours stack up at school let‑out and the afternoon rush. Three deaths at 3 p.m. Two at 4 p.m. Another two at 1 p.m. The street doesn’t care.

      Two men in the crosswalk, 52nd and 3rd

      Before dawn on July 11, a 2011 BMW went south on 3rd Avenue and hit two people crossing with the signal at 52nd Street. Both died. The city record lists “Traffic Control Disregarded” and “Unsafe Speed.” It notes a right‑front bumper and two bodies broken. The line for each man ends the same: “Apparent Death.” Source.

      On 44th Street at 7th Avenue, a left‑turning Toyota struck a 70‑year‑old woman crossing with the signal. She died. The file calls it “Failure to Yield Right‑of‑Way.” Another clean field, another life gone. Source.

      Trucks on Fourth Avenue, a body on the asphalt

      At 4th Avenue and 60th Street, a box truck going straight crushed a 30‑year‑old man. He died where he fell. The form reads “Crush Injuries.” No damage noted for the truck. Source.

      A 65‑year‑old man died on Columbia and Sigourney near the waterfront. Two box trucks in the file. One parked. One “Avoiding Object in Roadway.” The pedestrian’s entry says only “Apparent Death.” Source.

      The hot streets and the hot hours

      Here, walking and biking draw blood. Pedestrians: 12 dead, 500 injured. Cyclists: 4 dead, 498 injured. SUVs and sedans lead the harm to people on foot. Trucks and buses are few but devastating. Fourth Avenue is a top danger zone. So are the BQE and Gowanus lanes feeding it. In the midday and afternoon, injuries spike. Three deaths at 9 a.m. Three more at noon and 3 p.m. Two at 4 p.m. Two at 1 p.m. The pattern is simple and cruel.

      What City Hall has — and hasn’t — done

      The Council moved some pieces. It passed a bill to remove derelict cars fast to clear sightlines and crosswalks. Your member voted yes. Record. Another law now requires warning decals in Ubers and taxis to help prevent doorings. Your member voted yes. Record. A daylighting bill to ban parking at crosswalk corners was laid over in committee, even as people kept dying at turns. Your member co‑sponsored it. Record.

      Advocates point to the worst drivers. A small group does the most harm. “Just 1.5% of motorists cause 21% of pedestrian deaths.” “16 camera tickets in a year doubles the risk of killing or seriously injuring someone,” and “30+ multiplies it fifty‑fold.”

      Fix the corners. Slow the cars. Stop the repeats.

      Concrete things save lives here:

      • Daylight every corner on Fourth Avenue and the truck routes. Harden the turns. Install leading pedestrian intervals. The Council’s daylighting bill spells it out; it needs to move. Bill history.
      • Clear junked and illegal‑plate cars that block sightlines and crossings. The new derelict‑vehicle law is on the books; enforce it. Law.
      • Target trucks on Fourth, Third, and the BQE feeders. Enforce failure‑to‑yield and speeding at peak injury hours.

      Citywide, two steps would cut the harm that washed over 4th Avenue and 52nd Street:

      • Lower the default speed. Slower cars mean survivors, not obituaries. The city now has the power to set safer limits, and advocates urge it to use it. Act now.
      • Force the worst repeat speeders to obey the limit with speed limiters. The data on repeat offenders is clear. Details.

      The forms keep filling. Names turn to numbers. Fourth Avenue is still wide.

      Sources and hotspots

      • Top danger corridors in this district include 4th Avenue and the Gowanus/BQE.
      • Deadliest hours cluster around midday to late afternoon with spikes at 1–4 p.m. and 3 p.m. especially. District totals since 2022: 24 dead, 3,373 injured, with pedestrians and cyclists making up 1,0oo‑plus injuries combined.

      Voices from the record

      “A driver struck and killed a 47‑year‑old pedestrian… then left the scene,” police said.

      “Criminal charges for him were still pending,” Gothamist reported.

      “No criminality is suspected in either case,” police said, after two separate train deaths in the system.

      One call can matter. Tell City Hall to drop speeds and rein in repeat speeders. Take action.

      Int 1160-2025 Avilés votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Feb 13 - Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.

      Int 1138-2024 Avilés co-sponsors bill to ban parking near crosswalks, boosting street safety.
      ▸

      Dec 5 - Council bill bars cars from blocking crosswalks. No standing or parking within 20 feet. City must install daylighting barriers at 1,000 intersections yearly. Streets clear. Sightlines open. Danger cut.

      Int 1138-2024, now laid over in the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, was introduced December 5, 2024. The bill states: “prohibiting standing or parking a vehicle within 20 feet of a crosswalk at an intersection.” Council Member Erik D. Bottcher leads, joined by Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and over two dozen co-sponsors. The law orders the Department of Transportation to install daylighting barriers at a minimum of 1,000 intersections each year, up from 100. The city must also run outreach and education. The bill aims to keep crosswalks clear, improve visibility, and protect people on foot and bike. No more hiding behind parked cars. The committee laid the bill over on April 21, 2025.
