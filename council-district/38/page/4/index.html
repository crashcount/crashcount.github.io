      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 38?

      Blood on Third Avenue. When Will City Hall Care?

      Written July 18, 2025 (Revised July 18, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body, cleaned and deduped citations, and improved citation formatting in FAQ.)

      District 38: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 18, 2025

      The Deaths Keep Coming

      In District 38, the numbers do not lie. Twenty people killed. Over 3,100 injured. Twenty-eight left with wounds so deep they will never heal. These are not just numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. They are the faces you pass on the avenue, the hands you hold at home.

      Just days ago, two men were killed crossing Third Avenue in Sunset Park. The driver left them in the street and drove away. “I had a six pack of Modelos and two drinks. I was driving the car by myself last night. It’s my fault. After I hit them, I came home,” said the driver. The blood alcohol was below the legal limit. The law did not save them. The street did not save them. No one did.

      A few blocks away, a young woman sat on a curb in a parking lot. Her boyfriend spun his car in circles. He lost control. She died before sunrise. “He lost control when he was doing donuts with the vehicle,” said the police. The tire marks stayed for days. The loss will last longer.

      Who Pays the Price

      SUVs and cars did the most harm: 5 deaths, 7 serious injuries, 381 people hurt. Trucks and buses: 1 death, 36 injured. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 death, 19 injured. Bikes: 38 injured. The deadliest machines keep rolling. The most vulnerable keep falling.

      What Has Been Done, What Must Be Done

      Council Member Alexa Avilés has voted for and sponsored bills to clear abandoned vehicles, repair curbs, and add speed humps near parks. She voted to decriminalize jaywalking and to require better pavement markings. She co-sponsored a bill to ban parking near crosswalks, but it sits in committee, waiting. She voted yes to remove abandoned vehicles within 72 hours—a step toward clearer sightlines and safer crossings. But the deaths keep coming.

      Every day without action is another day of loss.

      Call to Action

      Do not wait for another name on the list. Call Council Member Avilés. Call the Mayor. Demand a citywide 20 mph speed limit. Demand real protection for people walking and biking. Demand streets where no one is left to die alone. The time for waiting is over.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 38 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Brooklyn, assembly district AD 49 and state senate district SD 17.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 38?
      It includes the Carroll Gardens-Cobble Hill-Gowanus-Red Hook, Sunset Park (West), Sunset Park (Central), Green-Wood Cemetery, Dyker Heights, Bensonhurst, Sunset Park (East)-Borough Park (West), Brooklyn CB7, and Brooklyn CB6 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 49 and AD 51, and State Senate Districts SD 17 and SD 26.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 38?
      SUVs and cars: 5 deaths, 7 serious injuries, 381 injured. Trucks and buses: 1 death, 36 injured. Motorcycles and mopeds: 1 death, 19 injured. Bikes: 38 injured. NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes just 'accidents'?
      No. Most crashes are preventable with safer street design, lower speeds, and stronger enforcement. They are not acts of fate.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce laws for lower speed limits, safer crossings, protected bike lanes, and strict penalties for reckless driving. They can also push for citywide 20 mph limits and demand real accountability for dangerous drivers.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
