      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 28?

      District 28: Nights of sirens, days of mourning

      Written August 25, 2025 (Revised August 25, 2025 – Minor stats refresh)

      District 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Aug 4, 2025

      Street by street, the count climbs. Since 2022, District 28 logged 12 dead and 3,850 hurt in 5,932 crashes. Pedestrians took six of those deaths. SUVs and cars did most of the damage. The hours that cut deepest sit at 8 p.m., 9 p.m., and 10 p.m.

      JFK’s edge: a man down and a driver gone

      A 52‑year‑old man tried to cross at 155th Street and South Conduit Avenue at 2:30 a.m. The driver hit him and left. Police said, “The operator of the vehicle fled the scene after hitting the man.” Detectives searched nearby cameras as the body reached Jamaica Hospital and was pronounced dead. “No arrests have been made.”

      Queens South has carried a heavy toll this year. Reporters counted 17 traffic deaths there by Aug. 10. Police confirmed the man died where the road meets the airport’s shadow.

      The corridors that won’t stop taking

      The Belt Parkway is a factory for harm in this district: 405 injured and one killed. North Conduit Avenue shows another fatal mark and 198 injured. Lefferts Boulevard took a life. The data calls out unsafe speed, inattention, and failure to yield as steady triggers. After dark, the injuries spike; the death count rises at 8, 9, 10, and 11 p.m.

      In April, a 57‑year‑old SUV driver died on the Belt. The record lists unsafe speed and an unsafe lane change. The airbag went off. He did not get home. Crash record 4807281.

      On Christmas night, at 158th Street and 111th Avenue, a driver’s inattention met a woman in the crosswalk. She died there. Crash record 4781816.

      Wrong-way on the Clearview: intent and metal

      A Queens jury heard the words. Joseph Lee told police he entered the expressway “in the wrong direction because I wanted to hurt people and I felt ‘liberated’ by what I had done.” “You want to fight?” He hit five cars and left two motorists badly hurt. A judge gave him eight years. It happened outside this district, but the lesson runs through every fast lane: the margin is thin, and the body pays.

      Simple fixes on deadly blocks

      Daylight every corner near North Conduit and Lefferts. Harden turns and add leading pedestrian intervals at the crosswalks along Rockaway Boulevard and 111th Avenue. Build raised crossings and speed cushions on approaches to Belt Parkway service roads. Clear abandoned cars within 72 hours; the Council already voted for it, aiming to open sightlines and crosswalks. Int 0857‑2024 passed on June 30, 2025.

      Slow every street; stop the worst repeaters

      Lower speeds save lives. The city can set safer limits under Sammy’s Law. Make 20 mph the default where people walk. Install intelligent speed controls on cars that rack up tickets and points, as proposed in Albany. Fewer fast cars. Fewer funerals.

      Take one step now. Tell City Hall and your Council Member to back lower speeds and rein in repeat speeders. Start here: Take Action.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ Where are the worst crash spots in District 28?
      Belt Parkway and North Conduit Avenue top the list for injuries and deaths. Lefferts Boulevard also shows a fatal crash in the data.
      ▸ When are crashes most severe here?
      Injuries are highest late afternoon into night, with deaths peaking around 8–10 p.m. and remaining high at 11 p.m.
      ▸ What’s causing the most harm?
      Unsafe speed, inattention, and failure to yield appear again and again in district crashes, with SUVs and cars driving most pedestrian injuries and deaths.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
      ▸ What can be done right now on local streets?
      Daylighting, hardened turns, and LPIs at key crossings; raised crosswalks and speed cushions near service roads; and rapid removal of abandoned cars, as required by Int 0857‑2024.

      Int 1105-2024 Adams votes yes, boosting street safety transparency and project accountability.
      ▸

      Apr 10 - Council passed a law forcing DOT to post sharp, regular updates on street safety projects. Progress on bike lanes, bus lanes, and signals must go public. No more hiding delays or cost overruns. The city must show its work.

      Bill Int 1105-2024, now enacted, came through the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. Introduced November 13, 2024, it passed Council on April 10, 2025, and became law May 10, 2025. The bill's title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code...in relation to tracking progress made towards the requirements of the streets master plan.' Council Member Julie Won led sponsorship, joined by Brooks-Powers, Hanif, Ayala, and others. The law demands DOT post annual and monthly updates on every project tied to the master plan—listing details, timelines, funding, and setbacks. This law brings sunlight to street safety work, making the city answer for every mile and missed deadline.

      Int 1160-2025 Adams votes yes on pavement markings bill, boosting street safety citywide.
      ▸

      Feb 13 - Council orders DOT to repaint pavement lines within five days after resurfacing. Delays must be explained to the public. Clear markings mean fewer deadly gaps for walkers and riders.

      Int 1160-2025, now enacted, passed the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and full Council in February 2025. The law demands the Department of Transportation install pavement markings or temporary lines within five business days after any street resurfacing. If DOT misses the deadline, it must notify the public and explain the delay. The bill’s matter title: 'A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to installing pavement markings.' Council Member Farah N. Louis led as primary sponsor, joined by Brannan, Schulman, Banks, Farías, Carr, and Ariola. The law took effect March 15, 2025. Timely markings close the deadly window when streets lack crosswalks and lanes, protecting people on foot and bike.
