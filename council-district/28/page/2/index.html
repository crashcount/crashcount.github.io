      Deadly Miles, Empty Promises: Demand Safe Streets Now

      Written June 17, 2025 (Revised June 19, 2025 – Removed duplicate headline from body and deduped citations.)

      District 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jun 17, 2025

      Blood on the Boulevard

      In District 28, the numbers do not lie. Ten dead. Thirty-six left with serious injuries. More than 3,300 hurt since 2022. The streets are not safe for the old, the young, or anyone on foot or bike. Linden Boulevard, Belt Parkway, Rockaway, Lefferts—names that echo with sirens and loss.

      A man with a cane tried to cross Linden. He never made it home. “Today is a sad day. We lost a brother, father, son, uncle, and cousin. [He] has been snuffed from us by a hit and runner driver,” said his family. The driver kept going. The street kept moving.

      A cyclist was left broken and alone on 115th Avenue. Police found him unconscious. The driver vanished into the night. “They are now looking for evidence to help them track down the driver,” reported ABC7.

      Who Pays the Price?

      Pedestrians and cyclists bear the brunt. Cars and SUVs killed or maimed most. Trucks, buses, motorcycles, and mopeds added to the toll. Children, elders, workers—none are spared. The numbers are relentless: in the last year alone, 8 killed, 16 seriously hurt, over 1,000 injured. Every number is a name erased from a family table.

      What Has Been Done—and What Hasn’t

      Council Member Adrienne Adams has voted for some safety bills. She backed the citywide greenway plan and supported legalizing jaywalking, a move that ends the old habit of blaming the victim. She called for more traffic calming in senior zones: “It’s important that the Council advance equitable policies… to ensure that all New Yorkers can live, work and commute on safer streets.”

      But the council has not brought a citywide 20 mph speed limit to a vote. The streets remain fast, wide, and deadly. Promises are made. The dead stay dead.

      What Next? Demand Action

      This is not fate. It is policy. Call Council Member Adrienne Adams. Demand a vote for a citywide 20 mph limit. Demand protected bike lanes and crosswalks that put people first. Join Families for Safe Streets. Stand up. Do not wait for another name to be added to the count.

      Take action now.

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district. Source

      It belongs to borough Queens. Source
      ▸ Which areas are in District 28?
      It includes the South Ozone Park and Baisley Park neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, AD 31, and AD 32, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 14, and SD 15. Source
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 28?
      Most injuries and deaths were caused by Cars and SUVs (4 deaths, 71 moderate injuries, 5 serious injuries), followed by Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 1 moderate injury, 2 serious injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 1 moderate injury, no serious injuries). Source
      ▸ Are crashes just 'accidents' or are they preventable?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes and crosswalks, and support laws that hold dangerous drivers accountable.

      Int 0346-2024
      Adams votes yes to legalize jaywalking, improving overall pedestrian safety.
      ▸

      Council passed a law letting pedestrians cross streets anywhere, at any time. Jaywalking is now legal. No more tickets for crossing outside crosswalks or against signals. The law shifts blame from walkers to drivers. Streets belong to people again.

      Int 0346-2024, now Local Law 98 of 2024, was enacted by the City Council on October 26, 2024, after passing the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. The bill, titled "A Local Law to amend the administrative code of the city of New York, in relation to pedestrian crossing guidelines and right of way," lets pedestrians cross roadways at any point, even against traffic signals. It erases penalties for so-called jaywalking. Sponsors include Tiffany Cabán (primary), Lincoln Restler, Crystal Hudson, Shahana K. Hanif, Julie Won, Mercedes Narcisse, Darlene Mealy, Erik D. Bottcher, and Farah N. Louis. The Council voted overwhelmingly in favor. The law requires the Department of Transportation to educate all road users about these new rights. By ending jaywalking enforcement, the city removes a tool often used to blame victims and target the vulnerable. The law marks a shift: streets are for people, not just cars.
