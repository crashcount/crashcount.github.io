      Who’s Injuring and Killing Pedestrians in District 28?

      Ten Dead, Thirty-Six Broken—Council Inaction Is Killing District 28

      Written July 1, 2025 (Revised July 1, 2025 – Fixed duplicate headline in body, deduped and sorted citations, improved FAQ citation.)

      District 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 1, 2025

      Blood on the Streets

      In District 28, the toll does not let up. Ten people are dead. Thirty-six more are left with serious injuries. In the last twelve months alone, crashes have climbed. Injuries are up by 20%. Four people have died since January. These are not just numbers. They are neighbors, children, elders. The man on Linden Boulevard, crushed by an SUV. The scooter rider on 116th Avenue, thrown to the ground and gone before sunrise. The moped riders in Jamaica, broken in the street. The cyclist, Amanda Servedio, struck and killed while coming home from a ride. She “went airborne. She flew like 30 feet. It was a lot of force,” a witness told the Daily News.

      The System Fails the Vulnerable

      Most deaths come at the hands of cars and SUVs. In three years, cars and SUVs killed four, left five with grave wounds, and hurt hundreds. Trucks and buses add to the toll. Motorcycles and mopeds, too. Pedestrians and cyclists pay the price. The city’s own data shows the pattern: drivers turning left, speeding, not yielding, not seeing. The city blames the dead for crossing wrong. But only one person ends up in the morgue.

      Leadership: Words, Laws, and Silence

      Council Member Adrienne Adams has voted for some safety bills. She backed the law that legalized jaywalking, ending a policy that punished the vulnerable. She called for more traffic calming in senior zones: “It’s important that the Council advance equitable policies… to ensure that all New Yorkers can live, work and commute on safer streets.” But when it comes to the citywide 20 mph speed limit, she has not led. She has called out the city for missing safety targets, but the pace is slow. The blood keeps coming.

      What Now: No More Waiting

      This is not fate. It is policy. Every day the council delays, another family is broken. Call Council Member Adrienne Adams. Demand a citywide 20 mph limit. Demand protected bike lanes, daylighted corners, and real enforcement against reckless drivers. Join with others. Do not wait for another name to be added to the list. The street will not forgive silence.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents neighborhoods across the five boroughs.
      ▸ Where does District 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 28?
      It includes the South Ozone Park, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, Queens CB10, and Queens CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, AD 31, and AD 32, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 14, and SD 15.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 28?
      Most injuries and deaths came from cars and SUVs (4 deaths, 5 serious injuries), followed by trucks and buses (no deaths, 2 serious injuries), motorcycles and mopeds (no deaths, 2 serious injuries), and bikes (no deaths or serious injuries). NYC Open Data
      ▸ Are these crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are preventable. Lower speeds, safer street design, and real enforcement can save lives.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to stop traffic violence?
      They can pass and enforce lower speed limits, fund protected bike lanes, daylight intersections, and hold reckless drivers accountable.

Without mayoral commitment, the plan’s promise for safer streets remains unfulfilled.

      Adams Supports State Action Against Reckless Drivers
      ▸

      DOT will let the Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program die. The program failed to curb reckless driving. Few drivers faced consequences. Most kept breaking the law. City leaders blame weak enforcement and legal hurdles. Streets remain unsafe for those on foot and bike.

      The Dangerous Vehicle Abatement Program (DVAP), introduced in 2018 and passed in 2020, will expire after its pilot ends on October 26, 2023. The Department of Transportation (DOT) recommends ending DVAP, citing 'uncertain effects, high cost per participant, and the complexity of its implementation.' City Comptroller Brad Lander, who drafted the bill, criticized DOT for 'slow and limited implementation,' calling the program a failure. Only 885 drivers took the mandated safety course, with little impact on violations. Transportation Committee Chair Selvena Brooks-Powers said, 'Holding reckless drivers accountable and keeping our streets safe for all New Yorkers remains a priority.' DOT now supports state-level bills to suspend registrations for repeat red-light violators. No Council legislation will reauthorize or expand DVAP.
