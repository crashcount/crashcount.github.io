      Ten Dead, No Excuses: Demand Action on District 28’s Killer Streets

      Written July 17, 2025 (Revised July 17, 2025 – Fixed broken link, cleaned citations)

      District 28: Jan 1, 2022 - Jul 17, 2025

      Blood on the Asphalt: Recent Crashes in District 28

      District 28’s streets do not forgive. In the past year, 10 people died and over 1,100 were injured in crashes here. Eighteen suffered injuries so severe they may never walk the same again (NYC Open Data). The dead include mothers, sons, and a child. These are not just numbers—they are families left waiting for a door that will never open.

      Just after Christmas, a woman was killed crossing 111th Avenue. The driver kept going. She never made it to the other side (NYC Open Data CrashID 4781816). On Rockaway Boulevard, a 23-year-old man was struck and killed at the intersection. The car hit him head-on. He did not get up (NYC Open Data CrashID 4788744).

      The Human Cost: Voices from the Scene

      When a bus jumped the curb in Flushing, eight people were hurt. “I was all the way in the back and all of a sudden the bus hit the curb, I guess, jumped the curb, I went this way and that way and banged into the side of the bus,” said Ken Baur. The driver had fallen asleep at the wheel. The MTA pulled him from service. The investigation continues (ABC7).

      A chain-reaction crash on the Belt Parkway left two dead. The car lost control, hit the median, and caught fire. There were no arrests. The road was quiet again, but the loss remains (amny).

      Leadership: What Has Been Done, What Must Come Next

      Council Member Adrienne Adams has voted for bills to clear abandoned vehicles from streets, boost transparency, and decriminalize jaywalking. These steps matter. Removing derelict cars clears sightlines for children and elders crossing the street. But the carnage continues. Ten dead in a year is not safety.

      The city now has the power to lower speed limits. It can redesign streets. It can end the reign of repeat speeders. But these tools mean nothing if they are not used. Every day of delay is another day a mother does not come home.

      Call your council member. Demand a 20 mph speed limit. Demand streets where children can cross and live. Do not wait for another name to join the list.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      ▸ What is the New York City Council and how does it work?
      The New York City Council is the city’s legislative body. It passes laws, oversees city agencies, and represents the interests of New Yorkers in each district.
      ▸ Where does District 28 sit politically?
      It belongs to borough Queens, assembly district AD 23 and state senate district SD 10.
      ▸ Which areas are in District 28?
      It includes the South Ozone Park, South Jamaica, Baisley Park, Springfield Gardens (North)-Rochdale Village, Queens CB10, and Queens CB12 neighborhoods. It also overlaps parts of Assembly Districts AD 23, AD 24, AD 31, and AD 32, and State Senate Districts SD 10, SD 14, and SD 15.
      ▸ What types of vehicles caused injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 28?
      Most injuries and deaths to pedestrians in District 28 were caused by Cars and Trucks (5 deaths, 377 injuries), followed by SUVs (3 deaths, 145 injuries), Taxis (1 death, 12 injuries), Motorcycles and Mopeds (no deaths, 7 injuries), and Bikes (no deaths, 3 injuries).
      ▸ Are crashes preventable or just 'accidents'?
      Crashes are not random. Lower speeds, safer street design, and enforcement can prevent deaths and injuries.
      ▸ What can local politicians do to make streets safer?
      They can lower speed limits, redesign dangerous streets, remove abandoned vehicles, and pass laws that protect people walking and cycling.
      ▸ What is CrashCount?
      We’re a tool for helping hold local politicians and other actors accountable for their failure to protect you when you’re walking or cycling in NYC. We update our site constantly to provide you with up to date information on what’s happening in your neighborhood.
